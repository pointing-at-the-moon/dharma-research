## Abstract

This paper presents the formal argumentative core for an original scholarly thesis within the S8 series. Taking as its pivot the eighty-four-year gap between the *parinirvāṇa* of Fazang (法藏, 643–712) and Prajñā Tripiṭaka's (般若三藏, c. 734 – c. 810) translation of fascicle 40 of the *Forty-Fascicle Avataṃsaka* (《四十華嚴》, 796–798), it advances the metaphor of "the socket and the plug"[^1]: the Gate of the Distinct Completion of Dharmas Across the Ten Times (十世隔法異成門) is the **socket** Fazang installed on the doctrinal side, and Samantabhadra's Ten Great Vow-Kings constitute the **plug** Prajñā Tripiṭaka connected on the cultivational side. The two specifications match perfectly, yet they belong to two translation lineages with no direct contact. §I–§III argue the doctrinal resonance between the threefold turn of dedication (commonsense direction → loss of directional sense → the position called Samantabhadra) and the folding of the ten times; §IV offers a drop-of-water-returning-to-the-sea metaphor as intuitive resonance; §V corrects three slopes of misreading (energy-transfer reification, utilitarian object-fixation, annihilationist collapse); §VI is the formal argumentative core, sealing the "Total Vow facing Total Gate" claim through a ten-by-ten correspondence table, the bilateral symmetry structure, and the negative evidence furnished by Zongmi's *Commentary on the Practices and Vows of Samantabhadra* (《華嚴經行願品疏鈔》, X229), the *Yuanren lun* (《原人論》, T1886), and the *Preface to the Collected Writings on the Sources of Chan* (《禪源諸詮集都序》, T2015). At the cross-series level, this paper also delivers the *Avataṃsaka*-style closure for S1-P02 〈Initial Arising of Bodhicitta and Dharma-Flow〉, paving the way for the final sealing in S8-P11 and S8-P12.

本篇為 S8 系列原創學術命題之正式論證篇。藉由法藏（643–712）圓寂與般若三藏（Prajñā，734?–810?）譯出《四十華嚴》卷四十（796–798）之間八十四年的時間差，提出「插座與插頭」這一隱喻：十世隔法異成門是法藏在論述面裝好的插座，普賢十大願王是般若三藏在修證面接上的插頭，兩者規格完全吻合，卻分屬兩個不相往來的譯出脈絡。本篇以十條一一對應表、雙端對稱結構、與宗密《華嚴經行願品疏鈔》（X229）等所構成的反面證據，完成「總願對總門」的命題收口。

## §I  Introduction: When You Dedicate, Where Does the Light Come From?

### 1.1  A question that gets glossed over

Almost every Han-Chinese monastery closes its morning and evening services with the dedication verse. *May this merit adorn the Buddha's pure land; may it repay the four kinds of grace above and relieve the three sufferings below.* By the third recitation, most people are already putting away their cushions.

But if one stops on this single line and asks: **when you dedicate, where does that light come from?**

If the answer is "from me" — then who is "me"? Is merit some kind of substance that can be sent out from one's hand? If so, dedication reduces to an energy transfer, no different from power transmission in physics, only with the labels swapped. If not, then what exactly is the subject of the verb "to dedicate"?

If the answer is "from the Buddha; I am only the conduit" — then why does it have to pass through "me"? If the Buddha can give directly, what need is there for this single thought of mine?

If the answer is "I don't know — I just recite it" — then 84 years before, when Fazang wrote in fascicle 1 of the *Huayan jing tanxuan ji* (《華嚴經探玄記》) that "the three times each contain three, gathered into a single thought, hence the ten times" (T1733, 35.111c)[^2], **what he was processing was precisely this "I don't know."** And 84 years later, when Prajñā Tripiṭaka in the Luoyang translation bureau rendered the final fascicle of the *Forty-Fascicle Avataṃsaka*, where Samantabhadra says "thought after thought continuing without interruption; in body, speech, and mind, without weariness" (T0293, 10.847a)[^3], **what he was translating was also this "I don't know."**

What this paper takes up is the question that the morning and evening services slide past.

### 1.2  Socket and plug: the 84-year gap

Fazang entered parinirvāṇa in Chang'an in 712. His most important doctrinal contribution — the Ten Profound Gates (十玄門) — was already fully formed in the *Tanxuan ji* and the *Huayan yisheng jiaoyi fenqi zhang*. The tenth gate is the **Gate of the Distinct Completion of Dharmas Across the Ten Times** (十世隔法異成門). Its formula: "Past, present, and future — each of the three times contains its own past, present, and future, making nine times; the nine times are gathered into a single thought, hence ten times." This is the perfect-teaching architecture on the doctrinal side, folding the linear flow of time into the simultaneous presentation of "one thought — ten times."

Eighty-four years after Fazang's death, in 796, Prajñā Tripiṭaka opened the translation of the forty-fascicle *Mahāvaipulya-buddhâvataṃsaka-sūtra* (T0293, conventionally the *Forty-Fascicle Avataṃsaka*) at Chongfu Temple in Chang'an. It was completed in 798. This translation is only an expanded version of the *Gaṇḍavyūha* — the "Entry into the Dharmadhātu" chapter — but its **final fascicle, fascicle 40**, contains material absent from both the Jin-dynasty 60-fascicle and the Tang 80-fascicle versions: **Samantabhadra's Ten Great Vow-Kings**. The tenth vow is Universal Dedication (普皆迴向). After the vow text, a refrain appears ten times: "thought after thought continuing without interruption; in body, speech, and mind, without weariness."

Eighty-four years separate the two events. Fazang could not have read the Ten Great Vow-Kings — in his lifetime the text had not yet been translated. Prajñā Tripiṭaka's bureaus at Chongfu Temple in Luoyang and Ximing Temple in Chang'an had no direct lineage relation to Fazang's Huayan school; Prajñā Tripiṭaka belonged to the Indian esoteric transmission, and his co-translation team was led by Yuanzhao and Zhirou, not by disciples of Fazang.

Yet when one lays the structural formula of the Ten-Times Profound Gate flat on the table, and lays the refrain of the tenth of Samantabhadra's Ten Great Vow-Kings beside it — **the two specifications match perfectly**. The Ten-Times Profound Gate says "the three times each contain three, gathered into one thought"; the refrain says "thought after thought continuing without interruption." The Profound Gate says "the nine times gathered into one thought, hence ten times"; the refrain says "in body, speech, and mind, without weariness." Extend "thought" into the continuity of the three karmas of body, speech, and mind; unfold "one thought" into the unwearying performance of those three karmas; then fold this unfolding back into "without interruption" — and you have the cultivational enactment of the Ten-Times Profound Gate.

This is not a doctrinal resemblance. It is a structural interlock. One is a socket; one is a plug.

The principal task of this paper is to argue that the interlock is not coincidence — it is the same perfect-teaching architecture self-disclosing in two registers, the doctrinal and the cultivational.

### 1.3  Division of labor between this paper and S8-P11

S8-P11 〈The Ten Great Vows Beneath Ten Windows〉 has already completed the doctrinal correspondence table between the Ten Great Vows and the Ten Profound Gates, and has established the pedagogical–cultivational framework of "Total Vow facing Total Gate, bilateral symmetry." The work of P11 is **pedagogical use**: to give the reader an operable correspondence table that shows from which Profound Gate each vow may be entered.

### 1.4  A first-round preview of the failure point

The core thesis of this paper — "the position called Samantabhadra is precisely where the directional sense of dedication dissolves" — has one obvious failure point: it slides easily into annihilationism. If "without their light, my light does not stand" gets read as "I have no light of my own to emit at all," then dependent arising has been read as nihilism; if "dedication is not my giving" gets read as "dedication is not happening," then emptiness of self-nature has been read as non-existence.

§V, Middle-Way Correction, will treat these two slopes formally. Here I give only the preview: **"the directional sense dissolves" is not "direction does not exist"; it is "direction no longer belongs to a fixed point of origin."** The light is still flowing; only the topology of the flow has changed, from "point to point" to "net to net." This is the middle way, not annihilationism.

## §II  Scripture Basis

### 2.A  Āgama leakage: the faint signal of dedication

Dedication (*pariṇāmanā / pariṇāma*) is not a core concept at the Āgama stage. In the *Saṃyukta-āgama* and the *Madhyama-āgama*, the Buddha speaks of giving and speaks of merit, but the action of "transferring merit to another" has not yet been systematized. The closest signal appears in fascicle 27 of the *Ekottara-āgama* and in similar passages: after an act of giving, the donor wishes "may this merit reach all beings equally." This is only the seed-form of dedication attached to alms-giving — not yet unfolded into an independent category of practice.

Dedication at the Āgama stage is linear, unidirectional, and subordinate to giving. It has no "thought after thought continuing" temporal structure, no horizontal "universal" unfolding, and certainly no perfect-teaching dimension of "directional sense dissolving." This signal matters not because it already contains the full face of dedication, but because it proves: **dedication was not invented ex nihilo by the Mahāyāna; it was the faint Āgama-level signal, subsequently amplified, structured, and at the Avataṃsaka stage folded into the perfect teaching.**

### 2.B  Revealed in the Avataṃsaka

**2.B.1  T0279, fascicle 33: "such a one is called Samantabhadra"**

Eighty-four years before Samantabhadra's Ten Great Vow-Kings were translated, the 80-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* had already supplied the definitional line for the name "Samantabhadra." The *Mahāvaipulya-buddhâvataṃsaka-sūtra*, fascicle 33, "Chapter on the Ten Dedications," reads: <cite>"one who well abides in this dedication — such a son of the Buddhas is called Samantabhadra"</cite> (T0279, 10.176b)[^4].

This line must be read precisely. It is not saying "Samantabhadra excels at dedication," nor "dedication is one among Samantabhadra's many virtues." It is a definitional statement: **the one who is able to abide in dedication is Samantabhadra.** In other words, Samantabhadra is not a particular historical person; it is the title for the position of "one complete in dedication." Whoever comes to stand in that position is Samantabhadra.

This definitional line was sealed 84 years later when Prajñā Tripiṭaka translated the Ten Great Vow-Kings: the last of the Ten Great Vows is precisely Universal Dedication, and the refrain repeatedly stresses that this vow is "thought after thought continuing without interruption" — meaning that Samantabhadra is Samantabhadra precisely because he stands continuously in the position of dedication, never leaving for a single instant.

**2.B.2  T0293, fascicle 40: the Ten Great Vows and the refrain**

The structural core of fascicle 40 of the *Forty-Fascicle Avataṃsaka* is the Ten Great Vows. Each vow opens with the formula "furthermore, good son," unfolds through a passage of vow-text, and then attaches the refrain. The Ten Great Vows are: (1) reverence to all Buddhas, (2) praise of the Tathāgatas, (3) extensive offerings, (4) repentance of karmic obstructions, (5) rejoicing in merit, (6) requesting the turning of the Dharma-wheel, (7) requesting the Buddhas to remain in the world, (8) constantly following the Buddha's learning, (9) ever according with sentient beings, (10) Universal Dedication (T0293, 10.844c–847a)[^5].

The text of the tenth vow, Universal Dedication, reads: <cite>"as for Universal Dedication: from the initial prostration down through every act of according, all the merit thereby produced is wholly dedicated to all sentient beings throughout the dharma realm and the realm of space"</cite> (T0293, 10.846b)[^6]. Note the internal structure: the passage gathers the merit of the preceding nine vows and dedicates the entirety of it. The tenth vow is not the tenth item laid out in parallel with the first nine; it is the sealing of the first nine — the first nine are "the doing," and the tenth is "handing the entire doing back."

What follows is the refrain, repeated with striking regularity ten times across fascicle 40, once after each vow-text. The complete refrain reads: <cite>"thought after thought continuing, without interruption; in body, speech, and mind, without weariness."</cite>

These sixteen characters of refrain are **the only genuinely new material** in fascicle 40 of the *Forty-Fascicle Avataṃsaka* relative to both the 80-fascicle and the 60-fascicle versions. The individual vow-titles have scattered correspondences in earlier scriptures, but welding the Ten Great Vows to the refrain — and making the refrain repeat ten times after the ten vows — is a structural innovation unique to Prajñā Tripiṭaka's translation bureau.

The function of the refrain will be argued in detail in §III: it converts the doctrinal propositions of the Ten Profound Gates into bodily injunctions on the cultivational side.

### 2.C  The patriarchal expositions

**2.C.1  Fazang, T1733 / T1866: the *locus classicus* of the Ten-Times Profound Gate**

In fascicle 1 of the *Huayan jing tanxuan ji*, Fazang gives the formula of the Ten Profound Gates: <cite>"past, present, and future — each of the three times contains its own past, present, and future, making nine times; the nine times are gathered into a single thought, hence ten times"</cite> (T1733, 35.111c)[^2]. The same formula reappears, with isomorphic structure and minor verbal variation, in fascicle 4 of the *Huayan yisheng jiaoyi fenqi zhang*: <cite>"since the nine times mutually interpenetrate one another, they form a single total phrase; total and partial together compose the ten times"</cite> (T1866, 45.505a)[^7].

The crux of this formula lies neither in "nine" nor in "ten" but in the four characters "gathered into a single thought." Linear time has past, present, and future — three times — and each of these three internally contains its own past, present, and future, yielding nine times. But the nine times are not nine parallel segments. They are "gathered" — ingathered, folded — into "a single thought." The final count is therefore not nine but ten: the nine times plus the very act of "gathering into one thought" together constitute the tenth time.

The tenth time *is* the act of gathering. It is "folding" as an action, registered inside the temporal structure itself.

This is the real thesis of the Ten-Times Profound Gate: **the deepest structure of time is not flow but folding; not sequence but simultaneous presentation.** Fazang had this thesis fully in place before his parinirvāṇa in 712.

**2.C.2  Chengguan, T1735 / T1736: the groundwork for "dedication is Samantabhadra"**

Chengguan (738–839), in the *Huayan jing shu* and the *Huayan jing suishu yanyi chao*, lays recurring groundwork in fascicles 26, 30, 48, and 51 for the move "dedication is Samantabhadra" — whenever he comments on the "Chapter on the Ten Dedications" of the 80-fascicle *Avataṃsaka*, he repeatedly links the practice of dedication with the position of Samantabhadra. In fascicle 11 of the *Suishu yanyi chao*: <cite>"here we merely take the three times as mutually arising in dependence, thereby forming nine times; because they do not leave a single thought, they make ten times"</cite> (T1736, 36.85b)[^8] — which is almost a mirror restatement of Fazang's formula.

But Chengguan has a curious blank. In the *Dafangguang fo huayan jing shu* (T1735), he **scarcely unfolds the Universal Dedication vow of fascicle 40 of the *Forty-Fascicle Avataṃsaka* anywhere in any dedicated treatment.** The proper commentarial treatment appears in his separately authored commentary on the *Forty-Fascicle Avataṃsaka* (extant as the single-text commentary in X227). The zero-treatment of Universal Dedication in T1735, set against Chengguan's repeated preparatory groundwork elsewhere for "dedication is Samantabhadra," forms a tension: he knew this vow was the summit, he had laid down every preparatory thread, but he did not formally seal the point in his main commentary.

The tension is real. It is not an oversight on Chengguan's part but a trace, visible at the commentarial layer, of the "socket and plug" structure being read — Chengguan is the first person to feel the interlock exists without yet having a name by which to call it.

**2.C.3  Zongmi, X229: the zero-commentary as negative evidence**

Zongmi (780–841), in the *Huayan jing xingyuanpin shuchao* (《華嚴經行願品疏鈔》, X229), comments on the *Practices and Vows of Samantabhadra* chapter, but his treatment of the tenth vow — Universal Dedication — is **extremely brief, almost absent**. For each of the first nine vows he provides an unfolding; the tenth alone he passes over.

This zero-commentary will be formally argued as negative evidence in §VI, 6.5. Here I note only this: Zongmi did not fail to grasp the importance of dedication. As a Huayan patriarch with a Chan background, he knew this vow was the summit; but he chose not to unfold it at the commentarial level — and the choice is itself a signal.

## §III  Core Argument: The Threefold Turn of Dedication and the Folding of the Ten Times

### 3.1  First turn: I emit light toward another (linear dedication)

The dedication structure at the beginner's stage of cultivation is linear. I have recited a fascicle of scripture; I have held a Dharma service; I have produced some merit — these merits are mine, and then I send them out to sentient beings. The direction of time is clear: done now, flowing into the future, benefitting others. The subject is "I," the verb is "give," the object is "another."

This turn corresponds to the linear face of the Ten-Times Profound Gate — the "past / present / future" surface. The three linear times here serve as cultivation's opening move: I concede that time has direction, I concede that merit has an owner, I concede that giving requires an object. Without this turn, the other two cannot follow — you must first have "I give" before you can discover how the directional sense of "giving" dissolves.

The first turn is not something to be abolished. It is real; it is elementary; it is foundational. The problem lies only in this: **if you stop at the first turn, dedication is nothing more than an extension of giving, and it never touches the position called Samantabhadra.**

### 3.2  Second turn: the mutual illumination of Indra's Net (horizontal interfusion)

When cultivation enters its second stage, you discover something: **at the very moment you are emitting light, the buddhas of the ten directions, the good teachers of the past, and the sentient beings of the present are also emitting light.** Your single thought of dedication is not an isolated act of giving — it is one node in the resonance of the dharma realm.

This is the true face of Indra's Net. Each jewel emits light while simultaneously reflecting the light of every other jewel; the light of all other jewels is reflected back into each individual jewel — light illuminating light, layer upon layer, without end. The single ray of dedication-light you send forth, at the very moment it goes out, is already being received, reflected, and reciprocated by the countless lights of others.

This turn corresponds to the horizontal structure of the refrain "thought after thought continuing, without interruption." Why does thought continue upon thought? Because no thought is ever isolated — at the moment of its arising, each thought is instantly received by every other thought in the dharma realm, and so it "continues"; it does not hold itself upright in continuity, it is held up by the whole net. Why without interruption? Because interruption can only occur at isolated points, and within the structure of the net there are no isolated points.

In this second turn, the boundary between "I give" and "they give" begins to blur. The instant I emit light is the instant I receive light; the instant I dedicate is the instant I am dedicated to. But this is not yet the deepest turn — it only opens the linear direction into a reticular one; the subject "I" is still present, merely no longer alone.

### 3.3  Third turn: without their light, my light does not stand (vertical folding)

The deepest turn is this: **I am able to emit this single thought of light only because, across immeasurable aeons past, the light of all buddhas, good teachers, and countless sentient beings has already been poured into me.** Without their light preceding mine, this single thought of mine could never have arisen.

To say it through: you can recite this dedication-verse today because someone taught you to recite it; the one who taught you was taught in turn by someone else; trace the line back far enough and you come to Śākyamuni Buddha, to the Seven Buddhas of the Past, to some sentient being who first roused bodhicitta countless aeons ago, and to the still-earlier conditions that caused that being to rouse bodhicitta. Your single thought of dedication-light is the local manifestation, in this single thought, of a lamp-chain without beginning and without end.

So the true face of the action "dedication" is not "I give." **It is returning a light that was never mine to its own proper belonging.** The light in my hand has never been mine; I am merely the momentary holder of this lamp-chain in this thought. Dedication is not sending my light out; it is recognizing that this light has always belonged to the entire dharma realm, and returning to the dharma realm what was only temporarily entrusted to my hand.

This turn corresponds to "gathered into a single thought, hence ten times" in the Ten-Times Profound Gate — the nine times of "each of the three having three" are all folded into the present single thought. Why can they be folded? Because this single thought already contains the light of all past buddhas and good teachers. Why must they be folded? Because only when the past has been wholly gathered into the present can "dedication" cease to be a linear transmission and become a simultaneous return.

After the third turn, the directional sense of dedication dissolves. There is no "from me to him," no "from now to later" — only "the light inherent to the dharma realm recognizing itself in this thought." This is the true face of the position called Samantabhadra: whoever can stand there is Samantabhadra; whoever cannot, is not.

### 3.4  The refrain as the conversion from proposition to injunction

Return now to the refrain introduced in §2.B.2: <cite>"thought after thought continuing, without interruption; in body, speech, and mind, without weariness."</cite>

Fazang's "the three times each contain three, gathered into a single thought, hence ten times" is a doctrinal proposition — it tells you what the true structure of time is. But doctrinal propositions cannot be cultivated directly — you cannot "cultivate a proposition." The proposition has to be converted into a bodily injunction.

The refrain Prajñā Tripiṭaka translated is precisely this conversion. "Thought after thought continuing" renders "gathered into a single thought" as a thought-level injunction: not "think of a single thought," but "let this thought be followed by the next without rupture." "Without interruption" renders "hence ten times" as a temporal-continuity injunction: not "imagine ten times," but "leave no gap between thought and thought." "Body, speech, and mind" unfolds "a single thought" into the three karmas that can be enacted bodily: not stopping at the level of consciousness, but bringing all three karmas into synchrony. "Without weariness" locks the whole structure into ongoingness: not "done and done," but "never stop."

The four phrases together translate Fazang's doctrinal proposition entirely into an enactable cultivational injunction. **The folding of the ten times on the doctrinal side is, on the cultivational side, the sixteen characters of the refrain.** This is the interlock of socket and plug.

And the refrain in fascicle 40 repeats ten times — once after each vow. The meaning of this structure is this: the Ten Great Vows are not ten parallel tasks but ten training grounds for the same refrain. From reverencing all Buddhas down to Universal Dedication, each vow trains your capacity for "thought after thought continuing without interruption," until at the tenth vow that capacity itself is called "dedication," is called "Samantabhadra."

### 3.5  Where the directional sense dissolves — the position called Samantabhadra

In §2.B.1 I cited the definitional line from fascicle 33 of the *Avataṃsaka*: <cite>"one who well abides in this dedication — such a son of the Buddhas is called Samantabhadra."</cite> After the threefold turn of §III, this line can be read again.

"Well abides in" is not "is good at doing"; it is "can rest in" — the capacity to not leave this position. "This dedication" is not any particular act of dedication; it is "the true face of dedication itself," that is, the structure in which directional sense has dissolved after the threefold turn. "Such a son of the Buddhas is called Samantabhadra" — whoever can rest in this position receives the name Samantabhadra.

Samantabhadra, then, is not a historical figure, nor is he one particular bodhisattva among the buddhas of the ten directions. Samantabhadra is a **position** — the position at which the light inherent to the dharma realm recognizes itself in a single thought, and from which that recognition can occur. Whoever stands at that position is Samantabhadra: while standing there, he is; the moment he steps away, he is not.

This reading also explains why the refrain insists on "thought after thought continuing, without interruption": because the position called Samantabhadra is not a certificate earned once and for all, but a sustained state that must be re-entered thought by thought, never leaving for an instant. Break the continuity by even one thought and Samantabhadra is no longer Samantabhadra — this is the true weight of the name "Samantabhadra."

### 3.6  Sealing S1-P02: the Avataṃsaka-style closure of "Initial Arising of Bodhicitta and Dharma-Flow"

S1-P02 〈Initial Arising of Bodhicitta and Dharma-Flow〉 handled a problem: how can the Dharma be transmitted across time? That paper's answer was given at the level of dependent arising — the Dharma is not some thing being passed down; it is the principle of dependent arising itself self-disclosing as the Dharma within each temporal segment.

At S8-P10, that answer is re-sealed under the perfect-teaching structure of the *Avataṃsaka*. The true face of "the Dharma-stream crossing the three times" is precisely the Gate of the Distinct Completion of Dharmas Across the Ten Times: **past, present, and future are not three segments laid out in flat succession, but a folded structure gathered simultaneously into "a single thought"; the Dharma-stream does not flow from past to present — the past has always already been in the present of this single thought.**

S1-P02 answered "why can the Dharma-stream cross the three times" (a doctrinal-level explanation at the layer of dependent arising); S8-P10 answers "how does the Dharma-stream cross the three times" (the structural answer at the perfect-teaching level). The former is the reply given at the level of teaching; the latter is the enacted reply at the level of cultivation. Taken together, they complete the cross-series sealing from "Faith" (信) to "Manifestation" (顯) — which is also one of the functions of S8 as the framing final volume.

## §IV  Cross-disciplinary Resonance: A Drop of Water Returning to the Sea

⚠️ This section is a metaphorical resonance only. No scientific model is invoked. The metaphor cannot replace doctrinal argument; it serves only as an intuitive entry.

Imagine a drop of water that has evaporated from the sea, drifted high into the air, condensed into cloud, fallen as rain, and — through stream and river — returned to the sea. At the instant it rejoins the sea, ask: did this drop of water "dedicate" itself to the sea?

From the vantage of commonsense directional sense, the answer is yes — it came from the sea, it returns to the sea, and this looks like a complete act of dedication. But think one step further and you will see: the drop never actually left the sea. While it was in the cloud it was part of the sea (only in a different state); while it was in the stream it was part of the sea (only in transit); and the instant it rejoins the surface it is part of the sea (only, now, recognized again). "Returning to" is not a displacement in space; it is the instant at which "already belonging" is recognized.

Dedication has the same structure. ⚠️ This metaphor does not prove anything; it only opens one more intuitive door onto the doctrine of "directional sense dissolving": you thought you were sending your merit out, but in fact you have finally recognized that this merit was, from the very start, neither yours nor the recipient's, and belonged only to the dharma realm that was already there. Samantabhadra as a position is the position at which that recognition takes place.

## §V  Middle-Way Correction

### ❌ #58  Dedication read as energy transfer (a physicalist misreading)

To read dedication as "I transmit my merit to somebody else" is to reify merit into a transmissible object. This is the textbook error of fitting a cultivational term to physical intuition. §3.1–3.3 have already argued: the terminus of the threefold turn of dedication is the dissolving of directional sense, not a closed loop between an emitter and a receiver. Failure-point tagged a second time: **"dedication = energy transfer" is the first slope of S8-P10; any explanation of dedication as "merit transmission" has already slid, doctrinally, out of the Avataṃsaka perfect-teaching structure.**

### ❌ #59  Dedication requires an explicit object to be effective (a utilitarian misreading)

The second misreading binds dedication to the specificity of its object: dedicating to a named person is effective; unnamed dedication has no effect. This demotes dedication to the filling-out of a wish-list. The three characters "Universal Dedication" (普皆迴向) in the scripture already refuse this reading — "universal" means that one need not, and cannot, select an object, because the moment an object is selected one has returned to the directional-sense structure of §3.1 and has not yet entered the second turn.

### ❌ #60  "Without their light, my light does not stand" sliding into annihilationism

When §3.2 speaks of "the directional sense dissolving," a tempting direction of misreading is to take it as "neither I nor the other exists," thereby sliding into annihilationism. The correction: what dissolves is not existence itself, but the substantialist grasping of the three-part structure of "dedicator / dedicatee / thing dedicated." The dharma realm is still there; the light is still there; the action is still happening — what dissolves is only the grasped layer of "who sent to whom." Precisely this is the middle way: one neither posits an emitter nor cancels the light itself.

## §VI  Socket and Plug: The Bilateral Symmetry of the Ten-Times Profound Gate × Universal Dedication

This section is the formal argumentative core of the original S8 thesis, and it carries two functions: (1) it completes the doctrinal interlock of S8-P10 under the symmetric structure of "Total Vow facing Total Gate"; (2) it proposes the 84-year gap as positive evidence — evidence of non-design — for the overall framework of the S8 series.

### 6.1  The 84-year gap as evidence

Fazang (643–712) entered parinirvāṇa in the third year of Jingyun, under Emperor Ruizong of the Tang. The doctrinal framework of the Ten-Times Profound Gate had been fully formed in the *Huayan jing tanxuan ji* (T1733) and the *Huayan yisheng jiaoyi fenqi zhang* (T1866) — the Ten Profound Gates, the Six Characteristics, the Four Dharmadhātus, the entire perfect-teaching socket on the doctrinal side was installed by his hand. But he never saw the complete translation of fascicle 40 of the *Forty-Fascicle Avataṃsaka* in his lifetime, because that fascicle would not appear until 84 years later[^9], when Prajñā Tripiṭaka (c. 734 – c. 810) translated it during the twelfth to the fourteenth years of Zhenyuan (796–798).

The first appearance in Chinese of Samantabhadra's Ten Great Vow-Kings together with the refrain "thought after thought continuing without interruption; in body, speech, and mind, without weariness" dates from 796–798. Neither the 60-fascicle nor the 80-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* contains the sealing structure of fascicle 40 — Samantabhadra's Ten Great Vow-Kings and this refrain were brought onto Chinese soil, as the sealing passage on the cultivational side, only when Prajñā Tripiṭaka translated the *Forty-Fascicle Avataṃsaka*.

In other words: when Fazang installed the socket, the plug was not yet in this world; when Prajñā Tripiṭaka connected the plug, the installer of the socket had been gone for 84 years. No known channel of human coordination lies between the two events — Prajñā Tripiṭaka's translation bureau, the base text on which he worked (the Sanskrit manuscript presented by the king of Uḍra/Oḍra), and his translation team belong to a different line from the Huayan school's translation-transmission lineage after Fazang.

### 6.2  A specification match at the doctrinal level

But §III has already argued that the refrain Prajñā Tripiṭaka brought over interlocks completely, at the doctrinal level, with Fazang's Ten-Times Profound Gate. "Thought after thought continuing" matches "gathered into a single thought"; "without interruption" matches "hence ten times"; "body, speech, and mind" unfolds the consciousness-level folding into the cultivational injunction of the three karmas in synchrony; "without weariness" locks the folded temporal structure into ongoingness. The sixteen characters of the refrain are not an arbitrary set of four closing phrases; they are the only possible unfolding, on the cultivational side, of the Ten-Times Profound Gate.

More crucial still: the refrain repeats ten times in fascicle 40 — once after each vow. This tenfold structure corresponds exactly to the "ten" of the Ten-Times Profound Gate: not a numerical coincidence of "also happens to be ten," but each of the Ten Great Vows mapping onto one posture of the Ten Profound Gates, and each repetition of the refrain locking that vow back into the structure of the ten-times folding.

### 6.3  The ten-by-ten correspondence table

The table below is fully synchronized with S8-P11 §3.11.乙, and functions as the doctrinal anchor for the "Total Vow facing Total Gate" bilateral symmetry:

| # | Great Vow | Profound Gate Posture | Doctrinal Interlock |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Reverence to all Buddhas | Simultaneous Complete Correspondence | A single prostration is at once a prostration to all Buddhas of the ten directions and the three times |
| 2 | Praise of the Tathāgatas | Unobstructed Freedom of Broad and Narrow | A single phrase of praise gathers boundless merit |
| 3 | Extensive Offerings | Mutual Inclusion of One and Many | A single stick of incense is every stick of incense; a single flower is every flower |
| 4 | Repentance of Karmic Obstructions | Mutual Identity and Freedom of All Dharmas | The nature of transgression is empty; karmic obstruction is bodhi |
| 5 | Rejoicing in Merit | Hidden and Manifest Jointly Establishing | Self's merit and others' merit mutually hide and reveal, yet are established simultaneously |
| 6 | Requesting the Turning of the Dharma-Wheel | Subtle Mutual Containment and Establishment | A single request gathers every turning of the Dharma-wheel across the three times |
| 7 | Requesting the Buddhas to Remain in the World | The Realm of Indra's Net | The Buddhas inter-contain and inter-penetrate — one abiding is every abiding |
| 8 | Constantly Following the Buddha's Learning | Revealing Dharma through Phenomena | Following a single event, a single practice, the whole of Buddha-virtue is seen |
| 9 | Ever Accord with Sentient Beings | Host and Retinue Perfectly Illuminating All Virtues | Every sentient being is a fully perfect node in the host-retinue mutual inclusion |
| 10 | Universal Dedication | Distinct Completion of Dharmas Across the Ten Times | The three times fold into a single thought; the directional sense of dedication dissolves |

All ten interlock. Not one requires a forced pairing, and not one is left over.

### 6.4  Total Vow facing Total Gate: bilateral symmetry

At a deeper layer: the first vow, "reverence to all Buddhas," and the first gate, "Simultaneous Complete Correspondence," form the entry-end symmetry — reverence is the first action of cultivation, simultaneous complete correspondence is the first structure of doctrine, and both are saying "the whole is present in this single thought." The tenth vow, "Universal Dedication," and the tenth gate, "Distinct Completion of Dharmas Across the Ten Times," form the exit-end symmetry — dedication is the final action of cultivation, the ten-times gate is the final structure of doctrine, and both are saying "the directional sense dissolves within folded time."

Entry-end symmetry and exit-end symmetry together compose a fully bilateral closed loop: beginning at "the whole present," ending at "the directional sense dissolved"; entering through "reverence," exiting through "dedication." The eight vows and eight gates in between interlock one by one, saturating the loop. **This is not a forced fit; it is two translators, across an 84-year gap, each completing one half of the same structure.**

### 6.5  The zero-commentary of Zongmi's X229 as negative evidence

More pointed still is the negative-evidence side. Zongmi (780–841) was the fifth patriarch of the Huayan school and a major synthesizer of *Yuanjue* and Chan thought. He authored the *Huayan jing xingyuanpin shuchao* (《華嚴經行願品疏鈔》, X229) specifically as a commentary on fascicle 40, and he repeatedly cited Samantabhadra's Ten Great Vow-Kings in the *Preface to the Collected Writings on the Sources of Chan* (《禪源諸詮集都序》, T2015). But in his most systematic Huayan doctrinal-classification work, the *Yuanren lun* (《原人論》, T1886), and in related commentaries, he **nowhere sets out an explicit one-to-one correspondence between the Ten Great Vow-Kings and the Ten Profound Gates**[^10].

Chengguan (738–839), in the *Huayan jing shu* (T1735) and the *Suishu yanyi chao* (T1736), produced detailed exegesis of fascicle 40 and repeatedly invoked the language of the Ten Profound Gates when discussing Samantabhadra's practice and vows — but likewise never proposed an explicit pairing table for the ten-by-ten correspondence.

Put differently: Fazang installed the socket; Prajñā Tripiṭaka connected the plug; Chengguan walked up to the edge of the correspondence; Zongmi cited every relevant source and yet did not take the final step. From the Tang onward, through Song, Yuan, Ming, and Qing, a ten-by-ten correspondence table is absent from the traditional commentarial lineage.

This absence is itself negative evidence: if the correspondence had been designed by a particular translator or school, the designer or his direct lineage would be expected to leave a correspondence table behind. None exists — which supports the judgment that **the correspondence emerges from the doctrinal structure itself rather than from human design.** Fazang could not have designed a match for a plug that would not exist for another 84 years; Prajñā Tripiṭaka could not have designed a match for a socket whose installer had been dead for 84 years; and while Chengguan and Zongmi had every material at hand, neither produced the table.

### 6.6  Pointing at the Moon as "completing the step Chengguan implied and Zongmi did not take"

What the S8 series as a whole does can be located this way: not inventing a new framework, but completing — in the twenty-first century — the step Chengguan had already implied and for which Zongmi had the materials but did not take. The ten-by-ten correspondence table is determined at the doctrinal level by the structure itself; anyone who reads Fazang and fascicle 40 in earnest will see it. What Pointing at the Moon does is simply to write it down, and to point out the 84-year gap as evidence of non-design.

This also answers the multi-tier audience thesis proposed in S8-P02: the real readership of the perfect teaching is not some group of people belonging to a particular historical period, but the process through which the structure of the whole dharma realm gradually recognizes itself across time. Fazang, Prajñā Tripiṭaka, Chengguan, Zongmi, and today's reader are all nodes within this process of recognition. Pointing at the Moon is not supplying "what they missed"; it is supplying the step at which "the structure's self-recognition of itself" happens, as it happens, to fall to the present.

### 6.7  Cultivational prompt: tomorrow morning's dedication verse

The argument could close here, but the Avataṃsaka perfect-teaching structure does not permit argument to leave cultivation behind. So this section ends with one very concrete prompt: **tomorrow morning, when you reach the last line of the dedication verse, try not to send the merit out.** Try only to stand there and let the refrain recite itself through you — thought after thought continuing, without interruption; in body, speech, and mind, without weariness. If there is even one thought in which you forget "I am dedicating" and simply let the action happen by itself, in that thought you are standing in the position of Samantabhadra. The next thought forgets — and you are not. The thought after that steps back in — and again you are.

The position called Samantabhadra — the instant at which the socket installed 84 years earlier and the plug connected 84 years later lock together — is waiting for you, tomorrow morning, inside the dedication verse.

## §VII  Conclusion: The Instant the Socket and the Plug Lock

### 7.1  Doctrinal sealing of the bilateral symmetry

S8-P10 completes the formal argument for the "Total Vow facing Total Gate" thesis of the S8 series. Fazang's Ten Profound Gates and Prajñā Tripiṭaka's translation of Samantabhadra's Ten Great Vow-Kings, across an 84-year gap, form a bilateral symmetry for which no avenue of human design is available. The ten-by-ten correspondence, the double-ended closed loop, and the zero-commentary of Zongmi — three layers of evidence taken together — advance "socket and plug" from a mere metaphor into a thesis anchored doctrinally and supported textually.

### 7.2  Samantabhadra as a position

The reading proposed in §3.5 — that Samantabhadra is not a historical figure but a position — can now, after §VI, be said more plainly: that position is the instant at which socket and plug lock, the instant at which the light inherent to the dharma realm recognizes itself in a single thought. The Pure Land and Tibetan traditions each have their own modes of entry into this position; this paper does not adjudicate among them. It notes only that the entry-path of Han-Chinese Huayan runs through the repeated practice of the refrain of Samantabhadra's Ten Great Vow-Kings, until "thought after thought continuing without interruption" itself becomes the capacity to stand in this position.

### 7.3  Paving the way for P11 / P12

S8-P11 has already treated the unfolded correspondence of "Ten Great Vows × Ten Profound Gates" beneath the frame of the Ten Windows. S8-P10 here formally argues the grounding of the correspondence, the 84-year gap, and the correspondence table itself. S8-P12 will address the S8 series' final sealing — the reverse ingathering of the *Avataṃsaka*, as "framing final volume," back into the eight-series whole (Faith, Understanding, Practice, Questioning, Cutting, Illumination, Embrace, Manifestation). The place of S8-P10 in this trilogy is to lock the symmetric structure of "Total Vow facing Total Gate" from both cultivational and doctrinal ends at once.

### 7.4  Sealing verse

> For eighty-four years the socket stood empty;<br>
> in one fascicle — the fortieth — the plug arrived.<br>
> Thought after thought continuing, no direction remains,<br>
> and Samantabhadra is only in this one.
