# The Diamond Sūtra's Self-Replay

**The §17 Pivot, Dual-Layer Structure, and the Two Obstructions**

## Abstract

In §17 of the Diamond Sūtra — the "Section on Ultimate Non-Self" — Subhūti re-asks virtually the same question he posed in §2, yet the Buddha responds at an entirely different level. This paper argues that this is not literary repetition but the sūtra's structural pivot: the text folds into a dual-layer architecture where the first half (§1–16) addresses person-emptiness (*pudgala-śūnyatā*), deconstructs the four "marks" (*xiāng* 相), and counters afflictive obstruction (*kleśāvaraṇa* 煩惱障), while the second half (§17–32) addresses dharma-emptiness (*dharma-śūnyatā*), deconstructs the four "views" (*jiàn* 見), and counters cognitive obstruction (*jñeyāvaraṇa* 所知障).

Three text-internal evidences confirm this progression: the subtle word-order shift between the two questions, the upgrade from four "marks" to four "views," and the shift in *jífēi* objects from external referents to the teaching itself. The mapping is then grounded in the two-obstruction definitions from the *Cheng weishi lun* (成唯識論, T1585), the obstruction-sequence from the *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra* (解深密經, T0676), and Kuiji's dual-attachment framework in the *Zànshù* (讚述, T1702). Vasubandhu's pivotal verse in the *Jingang bore boluomi jing lun* (金剛般若波羅蜜經論, T1511) — "harboring 'I am a bodhisattva' — this itself obstructs the mind, violating the path of non-abiding" (於內心修行，存我為菩薩，此即障於心，違於不住道) — provides the most critical textual evidence.

This paper serves as the structural map for the entire S5 series: every subsequent paper analyzing individual passages must first locate that passage in the first or second half and identify whether it cuts self-attachment or dharma-attachment.

**Keywords:** Diamond Sūtra, §17 pivot, dual-layer structure, two obstructions, *kleśāvaraṇa*, *jñeyāvaraṇa*, *ātma-grāha*, *dharma-grāha*, person-emptiness, dharma-emptiness

## I. Introduction

### 1.1 The Problem

Among the Diamond Sūtra's thirty-two sections, §17 — "Ultimate Non-Self" — has long been recognized as the sūtra's watershed. Yet the nature of this watershed admits two fundamentally different readings.

The first reading is literary: §17 is rhetorical recapitulation. The Buddha has Subhūti re-ask the question for pedagogical reinforcement, then provides a more thorough answer. Under this reading, §17 is a review session.

The second reading is doctrinal: §17 is not repetition but progression. Subhūti re-asks not because he failed to understand the first half, but precisely because he understood it — and that understanding generated a deeper perplexity that the first half cannot resolve. The Buddha's second answer is therefore not "let me say it again" but "let me go deeper."

This paper adopts the second reading and advances a specific thesis: the §17 pivot folds the Diamond Sūtra into a dual-layer structure — the first half addressing person-emptiness (*pudgala-śūnyatā*), the second half addressing dharma-emptiness (*dharma-śūnyatā*) — and this dual-layer structure precisely maps the Yogācāra doctrine of two obstructions: afflictive obstruction (*kleśāvaraṇa* 煩惱障) and cognitive obstruction (*jñeyāvaraṇa* 所知障).

This is not the imposition of an external theory. The paper will identify three independent text-internal evidences confirming that the Diamond Sūtra itself has encoded this progression.

### 1.2 Paper Positioning

This is the third paper in the S5 series by writing order (P01→P02→P07), but functionally it is the series' structural map. S5-P01 established the micro-level doctrinal framework (the *jífēi* formula as trisvabhāva mapping); S5-P02 established the pedagogical framework (the kōan mirror). This paper reveals the macro-level structure — the sūtra's dual-layer architecture pivoting at §17.

Every subsequent paper — from S5-P03 "All marks are illusory" through S5-P09 "All conditioned phenomena are like a dream" — must first locate its passage in the first or second half and identify whether it addresses self-attachment (afflictive obstruction) or dharma-attachment (cognitive obstruction). This paper provides that positioning tool.

The relationship to S5-P01: P01 reveals *jífēi* = trisvabhāva (micro-structure); P07 reveals dual-layer = two obstructions (macro-structure). Together they constitute the complete Yogācāra hermeneutic framework for the Diamond Sūtra.

## II. Scriptural Foundations

### Part A: Sūtra Sources

#### 2.1 Subhūti's Two Questions

Shortly after the Diamond Sūtra opens, Subhūti poses the central question of the entire text:[^1]

> "Good men and good women who have generated the mind of *anuttara-samyak-saṃbodhi* — how **should** they abide? How should they subdue their minds?" (善男子善女人，發阿耨多羅三藐三菩提心，**應**云何住？云何降伏其心？)

[^1]: *Vajracchedikā Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra* (金剛般若波羅蜜經) §2, T08, No. 235.

The Buddha answers for fifteen sections. Then, in §17, Subhūti opens his mouth again:[^2]

> "Good men and good women who have generated the mind of *anuttara-samyak-saṃbodhi* — **how** should they abide? How should they subdue their minds?" (善男子善女人，發阿耨多羅三藐三菩提心，云何**應**住？云何降伏其心？)

[^2]: *Vajracchedikā Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra* §17, T08, No. 235. Note the word-order shift: §2 reads "*yīng* yún hé zhù" (應云何住), §17 reads "yún hé *yīng* zhù" (云何應住).

The two questions are nearly identical — but with a subtle word-order difference. In §2, the character *yīng* (應, "should") leads the phrase; in §17, it shifts to mid-position. This is unlikely to be a casual translation variation from Kumārajīva, one of the most precise translators in Chinese Buddhist history. More importantly, the Buddha's answers to the two questions differ fundamentally in depth — if the question had not changed, the answer would not have shifted so radically. The word-order adjustment signals that the questioner's consciousness has itself progressed.

§2's phrasing emphasizes "should" — *what should I do?* This is the practitioner's quest for external norms, the typical question of a beginner.

§17's phrasing emphasizes "how" — *after learning everything in the first half, what does it actually mean to truly abide?* This is no longer asking for rules but for a mode of being.

#### 2.2 The Two Answers: A Difference in Depth

The answer to §2 unfolds across §3–§16. Its core teaching revolves around deconstructing the four marks:[^3]

> "If a bodhisattva has a mark of self, a mark of person, a mark of sentient being, or a mark of life-span, they are not a bodhisattva." (若菩薩有我相、人相、眾生相、壽者相，即非菩薩。)

[^3]: *Vajracchedikā* §3, T08, No. 235.

Everything in the first half — non-abiding generosity, "all marks are illusory," "even the dharma should be abandoned" — approaches from the perspective of *sentient beings*. The Buddha cuts the practitioner's projections of "I": I am giving, I see the Tathāgata, I am adorning the buddha-field.

The answer to §17 enters an entirely different plane:[^4]

> "There is truly no dharma called *anuttara-samyak-saṃbodhi*." (實無有法發阿耨多羅三藐三菩提者。)

> "The Tathāgata means the suchness of all dharmas." (如來者，即諸法如義。)

> "The Tathāgata's attainment of *anuttara-samyak-saṃbodhi* — in this there is neither reality nor unreality. Therefore the Tathāgata says: 'All dharmas are buddha-dharma.' Subhūti! What is called 'all dharmas' is not all dharmas; therefore it is called 'all dharmas.'" (如來所得阿耨多羅三藐三菩提，於是中無實無虛……即非一切法，是故名一切法。)

[^4]: *Vajracchedikā* §17, T08, No. 235.

The first half says "a bodhisattva should not cling to the mark of self" — it negates the practitioner's self-identification. The second half says "there is truly no dharma called bodhi" and "all dharmas are buddha-dharma yet not all dharmas" — it negates the substantiality of the teaching itself. This is a fundamental upgrade in dimension: from deconstructing person to deconstructing dharma.

#### 2.3 The Language Upgrade: From Four "Marks" to Four "Views"

The expression "mark of self, mark of person, mark of sentient being, mark of life-span" appears six times across the sūtra, revealing a clear linguistic progression:[^5]

[^5]: Statistical analysis based on the complete text of *Vajracchedikā*, T08, No. 235. First half: §3 (L39), §6 (L63), §14 (L155) use 相 (*xiāng*, "mark"); §15 (L197) first introduces 見 (*jiàn*, "view") at the boundary; §17 (L222) reverts to 相 for continuity; §31 (L346-347) uses 見 with the full *jífēi* structure.

In the first half (§1–§16), the term used three times is *xiāng* (相, "mark"): §3, §6, §14.

At §15 — the boundary zone — the character *jiàn* (見, "view") appears for the first time: "clinging to self-**view**, person-**view**, sentient-being-**view**, life-span-**view**."

In the second half, §17 still uses *xiāng* — maintaining continuity with the §2 answer. But by §31, the sūtra delivers its most radical *jífēi* formulation:[^6]

> "Self-view, person-view, sentient-being-view, life-span-view — these are not self-view… they are called self-view…" (我見人見眾生見壽者見，即非我見人見眾生見壽者見，是名我見人見眾生見壽者見。)

[^6]: *Vajracchedikā* §31, T08, No. 235.

The shift from *xiāng* to *jiàn* is not merely lexical. *Xiāng* (mark/lakṣaṇa) refers to the object of cognition — the features you perceive in external phenomena. *Jiàn* (view/dṛṣṭi) refers to the subject of cognition — the very way you look at the world. The first half deconstructs what you see (the grasped, *grāhya*); the second half deconstructs how you see (the grasping, *grāhaka*).

This is the sūtra's own linguistic evidence: the upgrade from "marks" to "views" maps the progression from person-emptiness to dharma-emptiness, from deconstructing the grasped to deconstructing the grasping. The text does not require external theory to explain this — it has encoded the two-obstruction structure in its own language.

#### 2.4 The Upgrade in *Jífēi* Objects

S5-P01 analyzed the distribution of the *jífēi* formula across the sūtra. This paper adds the distinction between the two halves.

The first half (§1–§16) contains approximately eight *jífēi* instances, focused on external referents:[^7] adorning the buddha-field, the great body, atoms, worlds, the thirty-two marks. These cut *dharma-names* — specific designations, physical forms, and cosmological objects.

[^7]: First-half *jífēi* instances from T08, No. 235: §10 "adorning is not adorning, it is called adorning" (L108); §10 "not-body is called the great body" (L113); §13 "not atoms, called atoms; not world, called world" (L137); §13 "not marks, called thirty-two marks" (L141), among others.

The second half (§17–§32) contains over eleven instances, upgraded to the teaching itself:[^8] all dharmas, mind, the complete form-body, the act of teaching, sentient beings, wholesome dharmas, *bodhi*, the dharma-mark, the four views. The second-half *jífēi* cuts not just names but *dharma-categories* — prajñāpāramitā itself, all dharmas, wholesome dharmas, *bodhi*, the very act of teaching the dharma. This is not the first half's operation repeated at the same level, but a recursive structure operating at a deeper dimension. The first half cuts your attachment to external objects; the second half cuts your attachment to the tools of practice.

[^8]: Second-half *jífēi* instances from T08, No. 235 include: §17 "not all dharmas, therefore called all dharmas" (L241); §18 "not mind, called mind" (L264); §20 "not complete form-body, called complete form-body" (L275-276); §21 "no dharma to teach, called teaching" (L283); §22 "not sentient beings, called sentient beings" (L287); §23 "not wholesome dharmas, called wholesome dharmas" (L295); §31 four views *jífēi* (L346-347); §31 "not dharma-mark, called dharma-mark" (L351), among others.

#### 2.5 The Two-Obstruction Framework in the Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra

The sūtra-level definition of the two obstructions appears across multiple fascicles of the *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra* (解深密經). Fascicle 2 states:[^9]

> "Having been liberated from afflictive obstruction, if they receive the awakening of the buddhas, their minds can also become liberated from cognitive obstruction." (解脫煩惱障已……於所知障其心亦可當得解脫。)

[^9]: *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra* (解深密經), fasc. 2, T16, No. 676.

Fascicle 4 further establishes the sequence:[^10]

> "All afflictions no longer manifest; only cognitive obstruction remains as the basis." (一切煩惱不復現行，唯有所知障為依止故。)

[^10]: *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra*, fasc. 4, T16, No. 676. Description of bodhisattvas at the eighth stage and above.

This describes bodhisattvas from the eighth stage onward: afflictive obstruction no longer manifests, yet cognitive obstruction persists. The buddha-stage completes the process:[^11]

> "The most extremely subtle afflictive and cognitive obstructions — unattached, unobstructed — [the buddha] directly and fully awakens to all domains of the knowable." (最極微細煩惱及所知障……現正等覺。)

[^11]: *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra*, fasc. 4, T16, No. 676.

The sūtra establishes a clear sequence: afflictive obstruction is coarser and ceases first; cognitive obstruction is subtler and ceases last. This mirrors the Diamond Sūtra's progression from the coarser first half (deconstructing the four marks) to the subtler second half (deconstructing the four views and the teaching itself).

### Part B: Treatise Sources

#### 2.6 The Two-Obstruction Definitions in the Cheng weishi lun

The *Cheng weishi lun* (成唯識論, *Vijñaptimātratāsiddhi*), fascicle 9, provides the most technically precise definitions of the two obstructions.

On afflictive obstruction:[^12]

> "Afflictive obstruction means: grasping the imagined nature as a real self, with *satkāya-dṛṣṭi* (identity-view) as its chief, the 128 root afflictions, and their derivative secondary afflictions. All of these disturb the body and mind of sentient beings and obstruct nirvāṇa; hence they are called afflictive obstruction." (煩惱障者，謂執遍計所執實我……能障涅槃名煩惱障。)

[^12]: *Cheng weishi lun* (成唯識論), fasc. 9, T31, No. 1585.

On cognitive obstruction:[^13]

> "Cognitive obstruction means: grasping the imagined nature as a real dharma, with *satkāya-dṛṣṭi* as its chief — views, doubt, ignorance, craving, aversion, pride, and so forth. These cover the unerring nature of the knowable and obstruct *bodhi*; hence they are called cognitive obstruction." (所知障者，謂執遍計所執實法……能障菩提名所知障。)

[^13]: *Cheng weishi lun*, fasc. 9, T31, No. 1585.

The two definitions share a perfectly parallel structure, with one critical difference: afflictive obstruction roots in grasping a "real self" (*shí wǒ* 實我), cognitive obstruction in grasping a "real dharma" (*shí fǎ* 實法). Afflictive obstruction "obstructs nirvāṇa" — it blocks liberation; cognitive obstruction "obstructs *bodhi*" — it blocks full awakening.

Fascicle 10 further establishes the correspondence between the two obstructions and the two emptinesses:[^14]

> "[The two vehicles] can only penetrate person-emptiness suchness and sever afflictive seeds." (唯能通達生空真如斷煩惱種。)

> "[Bodhisattvas] can fully penetrate the suchness of both emptinesses, severing both cognitive and afflictive obstruction seeds simultaneously, and suddenly realize unsurpassed *bodhi* and nirvāṇa." (具能通達二空真如雙斷所知煩惱障種頓證無上菩提涅槃。)

[^14]: *Cheng weishi lun*, fasc. 10, T31, No. 1585.

The two vehicles (śrāvaka and pratyekabuddha) penetrate only person-emptiness and sever only afflictive obstruction. Bodhisattvas penetrate both emptinesses and sever both obstructions. This provides the most direct Yogācāra framework for the Diamond Sūtra's dual-layer structure: the first half's teaching suffices for śrāvaka liberation (penetrating person-emptiness); the second half's teaching is the core of the bodhisattva path (penetrating dharma-emptiness).

Fascicle 9 also distinguishes three kinds of prajñā:[^15]

> "Prajñā has three kinds: person-emptiness non-discriminating wisdom, dharma-emptiness non-discriminating wisdom, and both-emptinesses non-discriminating wisdom." (般若有三種：謂生空無分別慧、法空無分別慧、俱空無分別慧。)

[^15]: *Cheng weishi lun*, fasc. 9, T31, No. 1585.

The first half's prajñā leans toward person-emptiness non-discriminating wisdom; the second half's prajñā leans toward dharma-emptiness non-discriminating wisdom; the sūtra read as a whole embodies both-emptinesses non-discriminating wisdom. The three kinds of prajñā unfold sequentially across the Diamond Sūtra's dual-layer architecture.

#### 2.7 Vasubandhu's Pivotal Verse — The Key Evidence

In his *Jingang bore boluomi jing lun* (金剛般若波羅蜜經論, T1511), Vasubandhu uses "cutting doubt" (*duàn yí* 斷疑) as the structural principle for the entire sūtra. When analyzing the §17 re-questioning, he delivers the treatise's most critical verse:[^16]

> "Cultivating within the mind, / Harboring 'I am a bodhisattva' — / This itself obstructs the mind, / Violating the path of non-abiding." (於內心修行，存我為菩薩，此即障於心，違於不住道。)

[^16]: *Jingang bore boluomi jing lun* (金剛般若波羅蜜經論), fasc. 2, T25, No. 1511. Vasubandhu's verse analyzing the §17 re-questioning.

Vasubandhu explains: the first half's teaching cuts external practices — "not abiding in objects when practicing generosity." But after learning non-abiding, the practitioner may retain a more covert attachment — "I abide in the great bodhisattva vehicle, I practice in this way, I subdue my mind in this way, I liberate sentient beings."

This is the doctrinal rationale for §17's existence. Subhūti did not fail to understand the first half. On the contrary — he understood it. But understanding itself generated a new problem: "I am a bodhisattva who understands prajñā" — this self-identification is itself dharma-attachment. Vasubandhu's four characters *cún wǒ wéi púsà* (存我為菩薩, "harboring 'I am a bodhisattva'") diagnose the covert pathology with surgical precision.

#### 2.8 Asaṅga's Structural Analysis

Asaṅga's commentary (T1510a/b) uses "seven meaning-gates" and "eighteen abiding-places" as the sūtra's structural framework. Analyzing the re-questioning section, Asaṅga notes:[^17]

> "The bodhisattva sees himself attaining a superior position… 'I abide thus, I practice thus, I subdue my mind thus, I liberate sentient beings.'"

> If the bodhisattva still harbors notions of sentient beings, this reveals either active self-grasping or latent tendencies.

[^17]: *Nengduan jingang bore boluomiduo jing lun* (能斷金剛般若波羅蜜多經論), fasc. 2, T25, No. 1510b.

Asaṅga confirms Vasubandhu's analysis: the second half targets the practitioner's attachment to their own practice — "I am practicing" is itself the obstruction. Notably, Asaṅga does not use the paired terminology "two obstructions" (*èr zhàng* 二障), instead employing "discrimination" (*fēnbié* 分別, Skt. *vikalpa*) as the core obstruction-concept. But the substance fully corresponds: the first half deconstructs the *objects* of discrimination; the second half deconstructs the *mind* that discriminates.

#### 2.9 Kuiji's Dual-Attachment Framework

Kuiji (窺基, 632–682), in his *Jingang bore boluomi jing zanshu* (金剛般若波羅蜜經讚述, T1702), provides the most systematic Yogācāra doctrinal assessment.

Fascicle 1 establishes the logical relationship between self-grasping and dharma-grasping:[^18]

> "Ordinary beings who have self-grasping necessarily also have dharma-grasping; the two vehicles have dharma-grasping but do not necessarily have self-grasping. Moreover, the two vehicles without self-grasping do not necessarily lack dharma-grasping; bodhisattvas without dharma-grasping necessarily lack self-grasping." (凡夫有我執必兼有法執；二乘有法執不必具我執。又二乘無我執則未必無法執；菩薩無法執則必無我執。)

[^18]: Kuiji, *Jingang bore boluomi jing zanshu* (金剛般若波羅蜜經讚述), fasc. 1, T33, No. 1702.

These four propositions form a tight logical matrix: self-grasping entails dharma-grasping, but dharma-grasping does not entail self-grasping. Removing dharma-grasping is more fundamental — once dharma-grasping is eliminated, self-grasping necessarily vanishes.

Fascicle 2 further establishes the causal structure of the two obstructions:[^19]

> "The two graspings of self and dharma — when these two graspings are eliminated, the two obstructions are removed." (我法二執，二執若遣二障即除。)

> "No mark of self, person, sentient being, or life-span = eliminating self-grasping. No dharma-mark and no non-dharma-mark = eliminating dharma-grasping." (無我相人相眾生相壽者相 = 除我執；無法相亦無非法相 = 除法執。)

[^19]: Kuiji, *Zànshù*, fasc. 2, T33, No. 1702.

This is the Yogācāra doctrinal assessment of the Diamond Sūtra: the four marks correspond to self-grasping (afflictive obstruction); the dharma-mark and non-dharma-mark correspond to dharma-grasping (cognitive obstruction). Kuiji in fascicle 2 also establishes a four-fold distinction:[^20]

- Mind-liberated, wisdom-not-liberated = two vehicles (afflictive obstruction severed, cognitive obstruction remaining)
- Mind-not-liberated, wisdom-liberated = great-compassion bodhisattvas
- Both liberated = buddhas
- Neither liberated = ordinary beings

[^20]: Kuiji, *Zànshù*, fasc. 2, T33, No. 1702. The four-fold classification of liberation.

The first half moves the practitioner from "neither liberated" toward "mind-liberated" — a level attainable even by the śrāvaka vehicle. The second half pushes toward "both liberated" — the depth unique to the bodhisattva path.

Fascicle 7 explicitly confirms the two-layer deconstruction structure:[^21]

> "Previously, the sūtra deconstructed the *objects* of self-grasping and dharma-grasping, revealing their unreality. Now it deconstructs the *mind* that grasps self and dharma." (前破我法所緣之境令知不實，今破能緣我法見心。)

> "It is not without the two [emptinesses] that one attains the path — by distancing from self and dharma [grasping]." (非無二得道，遠離於我法。)

[^21]: Kuiji, *Zànshù*, fasc. 7, T33, No. 1702.

The first half deconstructs "the objects of grasping" (*suǒyuán zhī jìng* 所緣之境); the second half deconstructs "the mind that grasps" (*néng yuán jiàn xīn* 能緣見心). This precisely matches this paper's analysis: first half deconstructs the grasped; second half deconstructs the grasping.

#### 2.10 The Yogācārabhūmi's Supplementary Framework

The *Yogācārabhūmi-śāstra* (瑜伽師地論), fascicle 36, establishes an epistemological framework for the two obstructions through the "four kinds of reality":[^22]

- Reality cognized by wisdom purified of afflictive obstruction = the Four Noble Truths + person-selflessness (person-emptiness)
- Reality cognized by wisdom purified of cognitive obstruction = dharma-selflessness + beyond verbal expression + cognizable only by bodhisattvas and buddhas

[^22]: *Yogācārabhūmi-śāstra* (瑜伽師地論), fasc. 36, T30, No. 1579.

Fascicle 48 uses the metaphor of skin/flesh/marrow for the three vehicles' progressive severance of obstructions:[^23]

- Skin = coarse heaviness of evil-destiny dispositions
- Flesh = coarse heaviness of afflictive obstruction
- Marrow = coarse heaviness of cognitive obstruction

[^23]: *Yogācārabhūmi-śāstra*, fasc. 48, T30, No. 1579.

Skin is shed first, flesh next, marrow last. Cognitive obstruction is like bone marrow — deeply hidden. Not because it is intrinsically "harder," but because it is covert: you may not even know you have it. The second half of the Diamond Sūtra is necessary precisely because cognitive obstruction hides within "I understand the dharma."

## III. Core Argument

### 3.1 Thesis: §17 Is the Structural Pivot; the Dual-Layer Maps the Two Obstructions ✅

Based on the scriptural foundations above, this paper's core thesis is: the Diamond Sūtra's §17 is not literary repetition but a doctrinal pivot. Subhūti's re-questioning marks the sūtra's transition from the first layer (person-emptiness / afflictive obstruction) into the second layer (dharma-emptiness / cognitive obstruction), forming a dual-layer architecture that precisely maps the Yogācāra two-obstruction doctrine.

The argument unfolds across three dimensions.

### 3.2 First Dimension: Three Independent Text-Internal Evidences

This paper does not rely on external theory to "discover" the Diamond Sūtra's dual-layer structure. The text itself provides three independent internal evidences:

**Evidence 1: Word-order shift.** §2's "*yīng* yún hé zhù" → §17's "yún hé *yīng* zhù." This signals the questioner's consciousness has progressed — from "what should I do?" to "what does it really mean to abide?" The Buddha's response to the first question spans fifteen sections; his response to the second opens an entirely new dimension.

**Evidence 2: "Marks" → "Views."** The first half uses *wǒ xiāng* 我相 (mark of self — the object of cognition); the second half upgrades to *wǒ jiàn* 我見 (view of self — the subject's mode of cognition). §31 applies the full *jífēi* structure to the four "views." The transition from *xiāng* to *jiàn* is the transition from deconstructing the grasped to deconstructing the grasping — from person-emptiness to dharma-emptiness.

**Evidence 3: Upgrade in *jífēi* objects.** The first half's *jífēi* cuts "dharma-names" — buddha-fields, form-bodies, atoms, worlds. The second half's *jífēi* cuts "dharma-categories" — prajñāpāramitā, all dharmas, wholesome dharmas, *bodhi*, the act of teaching itself. The recursive structure operates at a deeper dimension in the second half: not only are individual concepts deconstructed, but the tools of deconstruction themselves are deconstructed.

Three evidences converge on a single conclusion: the Diamond Sūtra undergoes a qualitative progression at §17, not a quantitative repetition.

### 3.3 Second Dimension: The Precise Mapping to the Two Obstructions

The Diamond Sūtra's dual-layer structure corresponds to the two-obstruction doctrine as follows:

**First half (§1–§16) = Afflictive obstruction layer.** Objects deconstructed: four "marks" (self, person, sentient being, life-span). Root cause: grasping the imagined nature as a real "self" (*satkāya-dṛṣṭi* as chief). Counter-measure: non-abiding generosity, transcending all marks. Attainment: penetrating person-emptiness suchness, severing afflictive seeds. Corresponding realization: attainable by śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas (mind-liberation).

**Second half (§17–§32) = Cognitive obstruction layer.** Objects deconstructed: four "views," dharma-marks, non-dharma-marks. Root cause: grasping the imagined nature as a real "dharma." Counter-measure: "there is truly no dharma called *bodhi*," "no dharma to teach, called teaching," "all dharmas are buddha-dharma yet not all dharmas." Attainment: penetrating dharma-emptiness suchness, severing both obstruction-seeds simultaneously. Corresponding realization: attainable only by bodhisattvas and buddhas (both-liberation).

Kuiji's fascicle 2 assessment — "no marks of self through life-span = eliminating self-grasping; no dharma-mark and no non-dharma-mark = eliminating dharma-grasping" — is the most direct classical expression of this mapping.

### 3.4 Third Dimension: Vasubandhu's "Harboring 'I Am a Bodhisattva'" — Diagnosing the Root

Vasubandhu's verse — "Harboring 'I am a bodhisattva' — this itself obstructs the mind, violating the path of non-abiding" — is the most critical text for understanding the §17 pivot.

The first half's practitioner has learned non-abiding generosity and transcendence of the four marks. But precisely this "having learned" manufactures a new problem:

- I know I should not cling. → Seed of dharma-attachment.
- I am a bodhisattva who practices prajñā. → Metamorphosis of self-attachment.
- My practice is deeper than others'. → Classic symptom of cognitive obstruction.

Vasubandhu pinpoints it with four characters: *cún wǒ wéi púsà* — not that you have a mark of self (the first half already cut that), but that you have a subtler self-identification: "I am a practitioner." This identification is itself the obstruction — and it is more covert than "I am an ordinary being," because it wears the clothing of the dharma.

This is why §17 must exist. If the Diamond Sūtra had only the first half, practitioners would rest in the self-satisfaction of "I have already transcended marks" — and that self-satisfaction is precisely cognitive obstruction. Subhūti's re-questioning represents not confusion but awakening: he recognizes that the first half's teaching is not yet sufficient — a deeper layer needs to be opened.

### 3.5 An Everyday Analogy: Two Layers of Therapy

A person visits a therapist carrying anxiety. In the first phase, the therapist helps them see that the anxiety's root is their attachment to a "perfect self." They learn to release this attachment, and the anxiety genuinely diminishes.

Then they return and say: "I have learned to let go. I am now a person who knows how to let go."

The therapist smiles: "Did you notice? What you just said — 'I am a person who knows how to let go' — that is a new identity."

The first phase cut "I must be perfect" — a secular analogue of afflictive obstruction. The second phase cuts "I have already let go" — a secular analogue of cognitive obstruction. The second layer is more covert than the first, because it looks like the fruit of practice.

The Diamond Sūtra's §17 is that therapist's smile: "You have indeed learned to transcend the four marks — but did you notice? What you are now attached to is 'I have transcended the four marks.'"

### 3.6 Macro-Recursion: The Dual Layer as Structural Self-Reference

S5-P01 revealed the micro-level recursion of the *jífēi* formula — each "not X, called X" is a trisvabhāva mapping operating on a single concept. This paper reveals the macro-level recursion — the entire sūtra folds at §17, with the first half's entire teaching structure re-deploying at a deeper level in the second half.

Micro-recursion operates at the concept level (each *jífēi* cuts one designation); macro-recursion operates at the structural level (the entire sūtra's teaching repeats itself at different depths). The two are isomorphic in logic: continuously taking itself as the object of its own cutting.

This also explains why the second half's *jífēi* formulations are more radical than the first half's. The first half cuts individual concepts' imagined nature (buddha-fields, form-bodies); the second half turns the cutting tools themselves — prajñā, wholesome dharmas, *bodhi* — into objects of *jífēi*. By the second half, the recursive structure has incorporated its own instruments into its cutting field.

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Dialogue

### 4.1 Narratology: Mise en Abyme ⚠️ Heuristic Analogy

In narratology, *mise en abyme* (embedded self-reflection) refers to a story containing a miniature of itself — a painting within a painting, a play within a play. The Diamond Sūtra's §17 structure bears formal resemblance: the same question is re-posed within the text, and the second answer contains and surpasses the first. The sūtra becomes a "sūtra within a sūtra."

But in the Buddhist context, embedding is not an aesthetic device — it is pedagogical design. Literary *mise en abyme* is potentially endless — each embedded layer can continue embedding. The Diamond Sūtra's dual-layer structure has a clear doctrinal direction: from person-emptiness to dharma-emptiness, from afflictive obstruction to cognitive obstruction, from mind-liberation to both-liberation. Embedding is the means; progression is the purpose.

### 4.2 Developmental Psychology: Kegan's Orders of Consciousness ⚠️ Heuristic Analogy

Robert Kegan's constructive-developmental theory describes five orders of human consciousness.[^24] Its core dynamic: each order transforms the previous order's "subject" into an "object." When you inhabit a given order, its framework is invisible to you (it *is* your world); when you develop to the next order, the previous framework becomes something you can observe.

[^24]: Kegan, Robert. *The Evolving Self: Problem and Process in Human Development*. Harvard University Press, 1982.

The Diamond Sūtra's dual-layer structure displays a similar dynamic. The first half objectifies "self" — you were the subject of "self-mark" (I *am* this person); the first half teaches you to make it an observable object (this is a "mark," not the real me). The second half objectifies "dharma" — you were the subject of "buddha-dharma" (my practice, my prajñā-wisdom); the second half teaches you to make that, too, an observable object (this, too, is a "view," not the real dharma).

However, Kegan's model is developmental — it describes the natural stages of psychological maturation. The Diamond Sūtra's progression is not merely natural development; it contains a dimension absent from Kegan's model — realization (*zhèngliàng* 證量). The transition from person-emptiness to dharma-emptiness is not just "seeing more broadly" but a fundamental transformation of the cognitive ground (*āśraya-parāvṛtti*, 轉依). Psychology can describe the objectification of the subject, but cannot encompass the ultimate dimension of that transformation.

### 4.3 Wittgenstein's Double Ladder ⚠️ Heuristic Analogy

S5-P01 drew on Wittgenstein's ladder metaphor: propositions are a ladder that must be thrown away after climbing.[^25] This paper extends the analogy to a double-ladder structure.

[^25]: Wittgenstein, Ludwig. *Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus*, 6.54. 1921.

The first half is the first ladder: concepts (non-abiding, transcending the four marks) help you climb past the first layer. You finish using this ladder and prepare to discard it — but discover you are now holding a new ladder: "I have used and discarded the first ladder; I know what prajñā is."

The second half is the second ladder: it helps you discard the first ladder along with every trace it left behind — including the memory "I discarded a ladder."

Ultimately, both ladders must be thrown away. The Diamond Sūtra's §32 six similes — "All conditioned phenomena are like a dream, an illusion, a bubble, a shadow" — enact the final discard (to be analyzed in S5-P09).

But Wittgenstein's ladder metaphor terminates in silence ("Whereof one cannot speak, thereof one must be silent"). The Diamond Sūtra does not fall silent — after discarding ladders, it continues to speak. "Is called X" is not silence but re-establishment of designation within the consummated nature. This is the fundamental difference between Buddhist philosophy and linguistic philosophy: Buddhism has a realization on "the other side"; linguistic philosophy has only the boundary of silence.

## V. Middle Way Correction

### 5.1 ❌ "The First Half Is Enough" — Reading Only the First Half as the Complete Teaching

Many readers feel deeply benefited after the first half (transcending the four marks, non-abiding generosity) and consequently pay less attention to the second half's progression. This reading is not wrong — the first half's teaching is entirely valid, and even śrāvakas can attain liberation through it. But if one believes the first half is the Diamond Sūtra's entirety, one misses the sūtra's deepest teaching.

§17 exists to tell you: breaking self-grasping is only halfway. Śrāvakas sever afflictive obstruction and attain liberation, but the bodhisattva path must also sever cognitive obstruction. The Diamond Sūtra is not a text that teaches only person-emptiness — it is a complete teaching that walks from person-emptiness all the way to dharma-emptiness.

### 5.2 ❌ "The Second Half Negates the First" — Believing the Second Half Overturns the First Half's Teaching

The opposite misreading is equally common: does the second half's "there is truly no dharma" mean the first half's teaching (transcending the four marks, non-abiding generosity) has been invalidated?

It is not negation but upgrading in dimension. The first half's teaching is entirely valid — it helped you break self-grasping. The second half does not say "the first half was wrong"; it says "the first half was right, but not deep enough." The first half is the first layer of completion; the second half is the second layer. An analogy: after learning to swim, the coach says "now forget the technique and feel the water with your body" — this does not deny the technique you learned, but internalizes it to a level beyond conscious holding.

### 5.3 ❌ "Pretending Non-Attachment" — Claiming Non-Attachment While Attached to the Self-Image of Non-Attachment

This is P07's primary correction battlefield. Vasubandhu's verse — "harboring 'I am a bodhisattva,' this itself obstructs the mind" — diagnoses this condition precisely.

The covertness of cognitive obstruction lies in this: knowing you have afflictive obstruction is easy — you are angry, and you know you are angry. Knowing you have cognitive obstruction is nearly impossible — because cognitive obstruction hides within "I understand the dharma." A person says "I have already transcended the four marks" — that statement itself is a new version of a "mark." A person says "I am not attached" — "non-attachment" has become their deepest attachment.

The second half's function is to illuminate this blind spot. It does not tell you "you should be non-attached" — that was the first half's teaching. It tells you "even 'non-attachment' must not be attached to" — that is the second half's teaching. Every *jífēi* structure in the second half cuts the "practice residue" left behind by the first half.

### 5.4 ❌ Treating the Two Obstructions as a Practice Checklist

This paper's mapping of the two obstructions onto the Diamond Sūtra's two halves may invite an operationalized misreading: "First practice the first half to sever afflictive obstruction, then practice the second half to sever cognitive obstruction" — as if there were a clear practice roadmap.

At the doctrinal level, the two obstructions do progress from coarse to subtle — the Diamond Sūtra's dual-layer structure maps precisely this progression. But at the practice level, the two obstructions do not follow a strict sequence. Afflictive and cognitive obstructions often interweave in practice: you may have touched dharma-emptiness in some dimensions while not yet having penetrated person-emptiness in others.

The Diamond Sūtra's dual-layer structure is a pedagogical design — it separates the doctrine into two layers for teaching clarity. But practice does not follow the chapter order of the pedagogical design. You cannot "finish the first half before starting the second" — you need to traverse the entire sūtra repeatedly. With each traversal, what you see in the first and second halves will differ. This is the Diamond Sūtra's function as a kōan mirror (see S5-P02): your depth of understanding determines what you see in the mirror.

## VI. Conclusion

### 6.1 Core Findings

This paper's argument can be summarized in four points.

First, the Diamond Sūtra's §17 is the structural pivot of the entire text. Subhūti's re-asking of the same question and the Buddha's response at an entirely different depth fold the sūtra into a dual-layer structure: first half addressing person-emptiness, second half addressing dharma-emptiness. Three text-internal evidences — word-order shift, upgrade from four "marks" to four "views," and upgrade of *jífēi* objects from external referents to the teaching itself — independently confirm this progression.

Second, this dual-layer structure precisely maps the Yogācāra two-obstruction doctrine. The first half deconstructs self-grasping (afflictive obstruction), countering the practitioner's attachment to "self"; the second half deconstructs dharma-grasping (cognitive obstruction), countering the practitioner's attachment to "the teaching." Kuiji's assessment — "no marks of self through life-span = eliminating self-grasping; no dharma-mark and no non-dharma-mark = eliminating dharma-grasping" — is the most direct classical expression of this mapping.

Third, Vasubandhu's verse — "harboring 'I am a bodhisattva,' this itself obstructs the mind, violating the path of non-abiding" — provides the most critical textual evidence for the §17 pivot's doctrinal necessity. After the first half's teaching succeeds, "I am a practitioner" as a residual self-identification is precisely the typical form of cognitive obstruction. §17 exists not for repetition but to counter the more covert obstruction generated by the success of practice itself.

Fourth, S5-P01 reveals *jífēi* = trisvabhāva mapping (micro-structure); this paper reveals dual-layer = two-obstruction mapping (macro-structure). Together they constitute the complete Yogācāra hermeneutic framework for the Diamond Sūtra — at the micro-level, each *jífēi* formula is a compressed expression of the three natures; at the macro-level, the sūtra's dual-layer architecture is the canonical mapping of the two obstructions.

### 6.2 Series Outlook

This paper provides the positioning tool for subsequent S5 papers. When analyzing individual passages, one must first confirm their location in the dual-layer structure:

S5-P03 "All marks are illusory" (§5) — belongs to the first half, deconstructing the four "marks," countering afflictive obstruction. Yet the scope of "all marks" already hints at the second half's theme — "all" includes the marks of the dharma itself.

S5-P05 Merit comparison — a recursive structure spanning both halves, with qualitative differences before and after §17. The first half's merit comparisons use external measures (seven treasures); the second half's use internal measures (upholding, reciting, explaining to others).

S5-P06 "Abide with a mind that does not abide" (§10) — belongs to the first half, but "non-abiding" receives deeper treatment in the second half. Even attachment to "non-abiding" must be released.

S5-P09 "All conditioned phenomena are like a dream" (§32) — the second half's finale; the six similes cut not just self-grasping but dharma-grasping. "All conditioned phenomena" includes the buddha-dharma itself — this is the sūtra's most radical *jífēi* formulation.

S5-P10 From Hundred Dharmas to the Diamond — S3 spent fourteen papers constructing the hundred-dharma toolkit; S5's dual-layer structure reveals that toolkit's ultimate destination: the first half teaches you how to use the tools (the proper function of the dependent nature); the second half teaches you not to reify the tools (even tools must not be grasped). P07's two-obstruction mapping provides the framework for P10's bridge: S3's construction = the first half's teaching; S5's cutting = the second half's upgrade in dimension.
