# Do Not Seek Outside — Take Refuge in Your Own Nature

**The Complete Teaching from Āgama to Chan: External Conditions Were Never the Source of Satisfaction**

*心不向外求——依止自性*

## Abstract

This paper argues a core thesis taught repeatedly by the Buddha, transmitted consistently from the Āgamas through Chan: **the mind should not seek externally—because external conditions were never the source of satisfaction.** The argument proceeds: (1) an everyday entry point: the same bowl of rice tastes delicious when hungry and nauseating when full—what changed was not the food but your mind; from this arises the core question: if satisfaction does not come from the food, where does it come from? (2) In Yogācāra, vedanā (feeling) is a mental factor (caitta), not a property of external objects—"vedanā is characterized by the experience of agreeable, disagreeable, and neutral aspects"; two sticks rubbed together produce fire, but the fire resides in neither stick; (3) a systematic survey of the Buddha's complete teachings on not seeking externally in the Āgama corpus: from "the mind seeks externally, and then one must restrain it to seek the mind" to "be your own lamp, do not rely on another"; from "it is impossible for a stream-enterer to abandon the inner and seek externally" to "one who deeply sees dependent arising never seeks a teacher outside"; (4) hedonic adaptation as cross-disciplinary correspondence (⚠️ structural analogy only), demonstrating how modern psychology independently discovered the same phenomenon; (5) Middle Way corrections against asceticism and nihilism—after the Buddha attained awakening, he ate fine food when offered, and ate alms food when that was what came. This is Paper #8 in the series, following Paper #6 (awareness of outward-seeking) and Paper #7 (turning inward to plant seeds for the future), arguing the fundamental problem of external seeking: **it is not that good things don't exist outside, but that outside things cannot resolve your inner problem.**

**Keywords:** vedanā, caitta, external seeking, self-refuge, pañca-kāma, dukkha of not-obtaining, hedonic adaptation, satipaṭṭhāna

## I. The Problem: The Same Bowl of Noodles

When you are hungry, a bowl of plain noodles from a roadside stall is the most delicious thing in the world.

But if you have just finished an all-you-can-eat buffet, and that same bowl of plain noodles is set before you, you might not even want to look at it. One more sip of water and you feel like throwing up.

What changed?

The food did not change. The noodles are still the same noodles—the same broth, the same taste. What changed was **you**. More precisely, what changed was your vedanā—the way you experience that bowl of noodles shifted from "agreeable" to "disagreeable."

Now extend this logic to love.

You say "I love you." But if one day the other person says "I don't love you anymore," would you keep loving them? If the answer is no—then what you loved was never them. What you loved was the *feeling* of being loved. More precisely, what you loved was the pleasant vedanā you yourself produced when being loved.

Push the same logic further:

- You pursue wealth—what you chase is the sense of security (pleasant vedanā) that comes with "having money"
- You pursue power—what you chase is the sense of fulfillment (pleasant vedanā) that comes with "being obeyed"
- You pursue fame—what you chase is the sense of pleasure (pleasant vedanā) that comes with "being recognized"

All of these pursuits share a common structure: you believe satisfaction comes from the outside (food, a lover, money, status), but in reality satisfaction comes from your own mind—from your vedanā.

Here is the question: if satisfaction is not given to you by external things, why do you keep looking for it out there?

## II. Scriptural Foundations: What the Buddha Taught

### 2.1 Vedanā Is a Mental Factor — The Fire Is Not in the Wood

The Yogācāra definition of vedanā is precise to the point of leaving no room for ambiguity:

> 「受」謂領納順、違、俱非境相為性，起愛為業。[^1]

*"Vedanā is characterized by the experience of agreeable, disagreeable, and neutral aspects of objects; its function is to give rise to craving."*

The essence of vedanā is "experience" (領納)—it is the mind's **mode of responding** to external objects, not an intrinsic property of external objects themselves. Its function (karman) is to give rise to love—craving. Note: vedanā → tṛṣṇā—this is the mechanism of "feeling conditions craving" (vedanā-pratyaya-tṛṣṇā) in the twelve links of dependent arising. Your vedanā is the direct source of your grasping.

But where does vedanā come from? Not directly from external objects. In the *Saṃyuktāgama*, the Buddha used a brilliant analogy:

> 譬如兩木相磨，和合生火；若兩木離散，火亦隨滅。如是諸受緣觸集，觸生觸集，若彼彼觸集故，彼彼受亦集；彼彼觸集滅故，彼彼受集亦滅、止、清涼、息、沒。[^2]

*"Just as two sticks rubbed together produce fire through their conjunction, and when the two sticks are separated the fire ceases—so too, feelings arise dependent on contact; when contact arises and accumulates, feelings arise and accumulate; when contact ceases, feelings also cease, cool, and are extinguished."*

Fire is not in the first stick, nor in the second. Fire is the product of two sticks rubbing together—once they separate, the fire goes out.

Likewise: the feeling of "delicious" is not in the food, nor in your tongue. It is the product of the "conjunction of three" (三事和合)—sense faculty, sense object, and consciousness:

> 眼、色緣生眼識，三事和合觸，觸緣受，若苦、若樂、不苦不樂。[^3]

*"Dependent on eye and form, eye-consciousness arises. The conjunction of the three is contact. Contact conditions feeling—whether painful, pleasant, or neither-painful-nor-pleasant."*

Vedanā is a product of dependent arising. It is not an inherent property of any external object.

That bowl of plain noodles never had the property of "delicious"—"delicious" was your vedanā. And your vedanā depended on the conditions of your contact.

### 2.2 The Buddha's Direct Instruction: The Mind Seeks Externally

The Buddha did not merely analyze the vedanā-aggregate in theory. In the Āgamas, he repeatedly, explicitly, and directly taught his disciples: do not seek externally.

The *Saṃyuktāgama* contains three consecutive sūtras (615–617) that form a complete teaching unit:

**First sūtra (615) — Diagnosis:**

> 心於外求，然後制令求其心。[^4]

*"The mind seeks externally; therefore one must restrain it and seek the mind itself."*

The mind's default mode is to run outward—this is the ordinary person's baseline state. The first step of practice is not pursuing some lofty realization; it is **pulling the outward-running mind back in**.

**Second sūtra (616) — Method (the Cook Analogy):**

> 當取自心相，莫令外散。[^5]

*"One should grasp the characteristics of one's own mind; do not let it scatter outward."*

The foolish bhikṣu "does not grasp the characteristics of his own mind but grasps external characteristics"—he does not observe his own mind but chases after external appearances, and as a result he regresses. The wise bhikṣu "first grasps the inner mind, and only then grasps external characteristics"—he first settles his own mind, and only then deals with external conditions.

**Third sūtra (617) — Boundary (the Rāva Bird Analogy):**

> 自處父母境界者，謂四念處。[^6]

*"One's own ancestral territory refers to the four establishments of mindfulness."*

The four satipaṭṭhānas (observing body, feelings, mind, and dharmas) are your "home"—your own territory. The five sensory pleasures (form, sound, smell, taste, touch) are "another's territory"—someone else's ground. A bird on its own territory is safe; once it flies into a raptor's territory, it gets caught.

### 2.3 The Criterion for a Stream-Enterer: Seeking Externally Is Impossible

If the previous three sūtras are a practice guide, then this passage from the *Madhyamāgama* is a **criterion of judgment**:

> 若見諦人捨離此內，從外求尊、求福田者，終無是處。[^7]

*"It is impossible for a stream-enterer, having abandoned the inner, to seek a worthy one or a field of merit externally."*

A person who has truly seen the path (stream-enterer) **cannot possibly** abandon the inner Dharma to seek externally. Only ordinary people do that.

The sūtra continues: even if a stream-enterer suffers "extreme, severe pain," he will not abandon the inner Dharma and seek liberation from heterodox śramaṇas or spell-chanting brahmins. Because he has personally seen:

> 此實不可以欲而得。[^8]

*"This truly cannot be obtained through desire."*

What you want cannot be obtained simply by wanting it. This is the dukkha of not-obtaining (求不得苦), one of the eight sufferings—the more you seek, the more you suffer.

### 2.4 The Buddha's Final Teaching: Be Your Own Lamp

Before his parinirvāṇa, what was the Buddha's last teaching to his disciples? It was not some profound doctrine, but the simplest of instructions:

> 當自熾燃，熾燃於法，勿他熾燃；當自歸依，歸依於法，勿他歸依。[^9]

*"Be your own lamp; take the Dharma as your lamp; do not rely on another. Take refuge in yourself; take refuge in the Dharma; do not take refuge in another."*

Be your own lamp. Rely on yourself. Rely on the Dharma. Do not rely on anything else.

What is the method? Observe the body internally (觀內身). That is all.

What the Buddha taught in his final moments is the same thing he taught throughout the *Saṃyuktāgama*—**do not seek outside**.

## III. Core Argument: Why Seeking Externally Must Fail

### 3.1 The Nature of Vedanā: Satisfaction Is an Activity of the Mind

Chapter II established: vedanā is a mental factor, not a property of external objects. The same bowl of rice—pleasant vedanā when hungry, painful vedanā when full. The external object did not change; the vedanā changed.

This means: you can never achieve lasting satisfaction by changing external conditions. Because satisfaction (pleasant vedanā) does not reside in external conditions.

### 3.2 Impermanence × Impermanence = You Can Never Catch It

There is a deeper layer to this problem: it is not just that "vedanā is not in external objects," but that **both external objects and your mind are changing**.

External objects are impermanent—today's delicacy may not suit your palate tomorrow. The person who loves you today may leave tomorrow.

Your mind is also impermanent—what you crave today, you may feel indifferent about in time.

Between two impermanent things, there can never be a stable "equilibrium point." What you chase is changing, and you yourself are also changing—you can never catch up.

This is not pessimism. This is the **fact** the Buddha observed.

### 3.3 The Dukkha of Not-Obtaining: The More You Seek, the More You Lose

The Buddha listed "not-obtaining" as one of the eight sufferings not by accident.[^8] It is not the unfortunate fate of certain people—it is the **inevitable result** of seeking externally.

Chan Master Huangbo Xiyun put it even more penetratingly:

> 但是眾生著相外求。求之轉失。使佛覓佛，將心捉心，窮劫盡形終不能得。不知息念忘慮，佛自現前。[^10]

*"Sentient beings merely grasp at appearances and seek externally. The more they seek, the more they lose. Using Buddha to search for Buddha, using mind to grasp mind—exhaust every kalpa and every lifetime, and you will never obtain it. They do not know that by ceasing thought and letting go of deliberation, Buddha naturally appears before them."*

"The more they seek, the more they lose"—you seek more, and you lose more. Not because you are unlucky, but because the act of "seeking" is itself the problem. Using mind to catch mind is like using your hand to grab your own hand—it is structurally impossible.

### 3.4 From Āgama to Chan: A Single Unbroken Thread

This teaching is not the idiosyncratic view of one school or one tradition. From the Āgamas to Chan, across twenty-five hundred years, every generation of awakened beings has said the same thing:

| Lineage | Core Teaching |
|---------|--------------|
| Āgama (the Buddha) | Be your own island, take yourself as refuge; be the Dharma's island, take the Dharma as refuge[^11] |
| Prajñā (Nāgārjuna lineage) | Without attaining inner joy, one seeks pleasure in external things; therefore the mind is disturbed[^12] |
| Huayan | Seek liberation within the afflictions, not from outside[^13] |
| Chan (Sixth Patriarch) | Bodhi is sought only within the mind; why labor to seek the profound outside?[^14] |
| Chan (Mazu) | The very one who is asking me right now—that is your treasury. Everything is already complete; why seek externally?[^15] |
| Chan (Linji) | The sickness lies in lack of self-trust; because you do not trust yourself enough, you rush outward seeking[^16] |

Linji's diagnosis is the most precise: the root cause of seeking externally is **lack of self-trust**—you do not believe that you are already complete, so you go looking outside.

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Correspondence: Hedonic Adaptation

### 4.1 An Independent Discovery in Psychology

In 1971, psychologists Brickman and Campbell introduced the concept of the "hedonic treadmill": after experiencing major positive or negative events, a person's level of happiness returns to a baseline within a period of time.[^17]

Subsequent research confirmed: lottery winners showed no significant difference in happiness from control groups after one year.[^18] Pay raises, promotions, buying a house—virtually every improvement in external conditions gets "adapted to" within months. You return to your baseline, and then continue chasing the next thing.

This is exactly what the Buddha observed twenty-five hundred years ago: vedanā is a product of dependent arising; it does not reside in any external object; it changes as conditions change.

### 4.2 Structural Correspondence (⚠️ Strictly Delimited)

| Hedonic Adaptation | Yogācāra Vedanā Theory | Correspondence |
|-------------------|----------------------|----------------|
| Positive event → happiness rises → returns to baseline | Pleasant contact → pleasant vedanā → vedanā ceases | Impermanence of vedanā |
| Adaptation mechanism (habituation) | Contact arising is vedanā arising; contact ceasing is vedanā ceasing | Vedanā arises and ceases dependent on contact |
| Baseline unchanged | Vedanā is a mental factor, not a property of external objects | Satisfaction is not outside |

✅ Doctrinally sound: both systems describe the phenomenon that "external conditions cannot bring lasting satisfaction."
⚠️ Hypothetical analogy: hedonic adaptation is empirical psychology; vedanā theory is soteriological doctrine—the levels of inquiry differ.
❌ Logically flawed: "The Buddha discovered hedonic adaptation long ago"—this is an anachronistic projection.

## V. Middle Way Corrections

### ❌ Misreading One: Asceticism

"Since external things cannot satisfy you, then don't pursue anything at all."

Correction: The Buddha was not an ascetic. As a prince, he enjoyed the fullest luxuries; after renouncing, he underwent six years of extreme austerity, ultimately abandoned asceticism, and accepted the milky rice offered by the cowherd woman; after attaining Buddhahood, when disciples offered the finest food, he partook of it; when he received whatever came on his alms round, he partook of that too.[^19]

What the Buddha demonstrated was: **when merit brings abundance, enjoy it; when it does not, be equally at ease.** The point is not "having" or "not having," but non-attachment.

Many Buddhist practitioners are wealthy, prominent laypersons. You do not need to renounce the world to practice. Your abundance is the natural manifestation of your merit—understanding this is enough.

### ❌ Misreading Two: Nihilism

"External things are all illusory, all unreal."

Correction: The Buddha never said external objects do not exist. He said they are "impermanent"—they genuinely exist, but they are not permanent and unchanging. Food truly satiates you; a beloved truly warms you. The problem is not that they are unreal, but that **they are not the source of satisfaction**. The source of satisfaction is your mind.

### ❌ Misreading Three: Self-Enclosure

"Not seeking outside means not interacting with the external world."

Correction: The Buddha said "first grasp the inner mind, and only then grasp external characteristics"[^5]—first settle the mind, **then** deal with external conditions. It is not that you do not deal with external conditions; it is that the sequence cannot be reversed. You stabilize yourself first, and then your interaction with the world has quality.

## VI. Conclusion: Your Treasury, Already Complete

Mazu Daoyi said one sentence to Dazhu Huihai that can serve as the conclusion of this paper:

> 即今問我者，是汝寶藏。一切具足，更無欠少。使用自在。何假向外求覓。[^15]

*"The very one who is asking me right now—that is your treasury. Everything is already complete, nothing is lacking. Its use is free and unhindered. Why seek externally?"*

You do not need to add anything from the outside onto yourself. What you need is to **recognize what you have always had**.

This is not motivational fluff. This is the conclusion verified repeatedly over twenty-five hundred years—through the Āgamas, Prajñā, Yogācāra, Huayan, and Chan, by every generation of awakened beings: if you seek externally, you will inevitably fail—not because there is nothing good out there, but because **the good things out there cannot resolve your inner problem**.

Paper #6 told us: manaskāra (attention) allows you to become aware that you are seeking externally.[^20]

Paper #7 told us: after turning inward, you plant wholesome seeds for your future self.[^21]

This paper argues the more fundamental step: **why must you turn inward?** Because the path of seeking externally, followed to its end, is a dead end.

The person who knows this, the Buddha called a "stream-enterer" (見諦人).

The person who does not know this is still looking outside.
