# Twenty-Five Perfect Penetrations: The Door That Fits You

**From "All Dharmas Are Tathāgatagarbha" to "Which Door Do You Enter?": Practice Reports of Twenty-Five Sages and Mañjuśrī's Selection**

## Abstract

The first four papers of this series completed the Śūraṅgama Sūtra's inner-circle theoretical framework: the mind is not located inside or outside (P02), the seeing-nature does not perish with objects (P03), and all dharmas are originally tathāgatagarbha (P04). Yet knowing theoretically that "everything is tathāgatagarbha" does not tell the practitioner where to begin. This paper examines the chapter on Twenty-Five Perfect Penetrations (二十五圓通, èrshíwǔ yuántōng) in fascicles 5–6, arguing that its core message is not an encyclopedia of twenty-five methods but rather "your faculty-disposition determines your door." Twenty-five sages each report their path of realization; Mañjuśrī ultimately selects Avalokiteśvara's ear-faculty penetration (耳根圓通, ěrgēn yuántōng) as most suited to beings in the sahā world. Drawing on Zixuan's four-gate taxonomy, Jiaoguang's four criteria for selection, and the *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra*'s theory of faculty-disposition differences, this paper proposes that Mañjuśrī's selection is not an absolute doctrinal ranking but a pedagogical fit — just as first-language acquisition necessarily begins through hearing, the most natural entry point for practice in the sahā world is the hearing-nature (聞性, wénxìng).

**Keywords:** Twenty-Five Perfect Penetrations, ear-faculty penetration, Avalokiteśvara, Mañjuśrī's selection, faculty-disposition fit, reverse hearing of self-nature, root of perfect penetration

## 1. Introduction

Imagine twenty-five doors standing before you. Each one leads to exactly the same destination. But not every door is one you can open.

By the end of the previous paper, we had established that the five aggregates, six sense-entries, twelve sense-bases, eighteen sense-realms, and seven elements — all dharmas whatsoever — are originally the wondrous true suchness of the tathāgatagarbha (如來藏妙真如性).[^1] This was the conclusion the Śūraṅgama Sūtra hammered home across fascicles 3–4 with thirty iterations of "originally neither causes-and-conditions nor spontaneous nature" and seven iterations of the core formula: "pure in original nature, pervading the dharma-realm, arising in accordance with sentient beings' minds, manifesting in proportion to their understanding, appearing through the momentum of karma."

But after hearing all of this, Ānanda asked the question that any serious practitioner would ask:

> "World-Honored One, we in this assembly, the voice-hearers still in training, are like this as well. Since beginningless time, we have been born and perished along with ignorance. Though we have obtained such roots of good through much learning and bear the name of 'renunciants,' we are like those afflicted with alternating-day malaria. We humbly beseech the World-Honored One's great compassion: how are body and mind bound? And from what point do we begin to untie them?"[^2]

"Alternating-day malaria" (隔日瘧, gé rì nüè) — three words that constitute the most poignant confession in the entire sūtra. Ānanda, foremost in learning, could remember every word of the Buddha's teaching. Yet his afflictions cycled like malaria: one day better, the next day worse, never fully cured. This is precisely the chasm between knowing and realizing: one may recite every passage on the four categories and seven elements from memory, yet still flare with anger at the next perceived slight.

The Buddha's response was not another theory. He used the metaphor of a silk cloth tied into six knots to demonstrate that knots tied at different times must be untied sequentially. Then he spoke a single pivotal line — the hinge upon which the entire chapter on perfect penetrations turns:

> "Select a faculty for perfect penetration; enter the stream and realize true awakening."[^3]

Six characters in the original. Choose a faculty (根, gēn). Enter the stream (入流, rùliú). That is the path to awakening.

But which faculty? This is the question the chapter on Twenty-Five Perfect Penetrations sets out to answer. The Buddha did not prescribe the answer himself. Instead, he asked twenty-five accomplished sages — bodhisattvas, arhats, dharma-princes — to stand and report which entry-point each had used. Then he requested Mañjuśrī to make the final selection.

This is not an exhibition of twenty-five meditation techniques. It is an empirical report on faculty-disposition fit (根器適配, gēnqì shìpèi): each sage's path corresponds precisely to his particular karmic history, habitual tendencies, and innate faculty-orientation. Mañjuśrī's ultimate selection of Avalokiteśvara's ear-faculty penetration is not because "the ear-faculty is best" but because "the ear-faculty most closely fits the faculty-disposition of beings in the sahā world."

This paper develops this thesis at three levels. First (§2), it establishes the scriptural foundation: the Buddha's framing question, representative sage-reports, Avalokiteśvara's practice sequence, Mañjuśrī's selection verse, and the structural analyses of commentators Zixuan (子璿) and Jiaoguang (交光). Second (§3), it argues that the core logic of the twenty-five penetrations is not "comparison of merit" but "faculty-disposition fit," and analyzes both Avalokiteśvara's six-step sequence and Mañjuśrī's actual criteria. Third (§4), it draws a cross-disciplinary analogy with first-language acquisition to illuminate the structural features and boundaries of the claim that "the hearing-faculty is the default entry point."

## 2. Scriptural Foundations

### A. Sūtra Sources

#### 2.1 From Theory to Practice: "Select a Faculty for Perfect Penetration"

After completing the theoretical demonstration of the four categories and seven elements, the Śūraṅgama's narrative undergoes a pivotal turn. The Buddha concludes the four-categories-and-seven-elements section with the principle of mutual functioning of the six faculties: "Pull out any one faculty, free it from adhesion and subdue it inward; once it returns to its original truth and emanates its fundamental radiance, the remaining five adhesions will all be freed."[^4] He cites six members of the assembly — Aniruddha who sees without eyes, the Nāga Upananda who hears without ears — as living proof that the faculties can transcend their ordinary organ-dependence.

Ānanda's response was a confession of inadequacy. He acknowledged that "since beginningless time, we have been born and perished along with ignorance," then pressed the question: "How are body and mind bound? And from what point do we begin to untie them?" The Buddha answered with the silk-cloth metaphor: knots not tied simultaneously cannot be untied simultaneously. The resolution must proceed in sequence — find the entry point and work from there.

The verse that follows contains the structural pivot of the entire middle circle of the sūtra: "The six being untied, the one also vanishes. Select a faculty for perfect penetration; enter the stream and realize true awakening."[^5] Another line in the same verse foreshadows the depth at which this selection operates: "The ādāna consciousness is extremely subtle; its habitual energies form a raging torrent. Fearing confusion between the true and the not-true, I have customarily refrained from expounding it."[^6] The eighth consciousness is too subtle, too easily misconstrued. Yet "selecting a faculty for perfect penetration" is precisely an operation at this depth — the chosen faculty must be capable of penetrating that far.

At this point, Ānanda understood the principle ("I have grasped the meaning of the six and the one vanishing") but still did not know which faculty to select ("I have not yet reached the root of perfect penetration"). He requested further instruction. The Buddha's response was not to name the answer himself but to address the entire assembly:

> "You bodhisattvas and arhats, born within my Dharma and having attained the state beyond learning — I now ask you: at the time of your initial aspiration, when you awakened to the eighteen realms, which did you find to be a perfect penetration? By what skillful means did you enter samādhi?"[^7]

The pedagogical design is remarkable: let those who have already walked the path turn back and tell you which road they took. Not theoretical possibilities, but experiential reports.

#### 2.2 The Twenty-Five Penetrations: Representative Selection

Twenty-five sages reported their respective methods in sequence. Following Zixuan's four-gate taxonomy,[^8] the twenty-five can be classified into: penetrations through sense-objects (依塵顯悟門, six types), penetrations through sense-faculties (依根證入門, five types, with the ear-faculty reserved for last), penetrations through consciousness (依識修斷門, six types), and penetrations through the great elements (依大歸性門, seven types), plus Avalokiteśvara's ear-faculty penetration. One representative from each gate:

**Gate of Awakening through Sense-Objects — Ājñāta Kauṇḍinya (sound):**

Kauṇḍinya, one of the five bhikṣus who were the Buddha's first disciples, entered through the sense-object of sound:

> "At the Deer Park and at the Cock Garden, I beheld the Tathāgata's initial attainment of the Way. Through the Buddha's voice I awakened to the four noble truths.... The wondrous sound was secretly complete; through sound I attained arhatship."[^9]

His entry was through the object heard — not the hearing-nature itself, but the content of what was heard. This is characteristic of the "awakening through sense-objects" gate: discovering the original nature within the object of perception.

**Gate of Realization through Sense-Faculties — Aniruddha (eye-faculty):**

Aniruddha's story carries particular pedagogical weight in the Śūraṅgama. Rebuked by the Buddha for excessive sleeping ("You are like an animal"), he resolved to stay awake for seven days and lost his eyesight as a result. Yet it was precisely after losing his physical eyes that he realized a seeing that transcends them:

> "The World-Honored One showed me the Joyful-Seeing, Illuminating, Vajra Samādhi. Without relying on eyes, I see the ten directions — purely real and penetrating, like observing a fruit in my palm.... Reversing the seeing to trace its origin: this is foremost!"[^10]

"Without relying on eyes, I see the ten directions" — this is the most direct empirical confirmation of P03's thesis that the seeing-nature does not depend on the physical eye. Aniruddha's blindness proved the point. His entry was through the eye-faculty (眼根) — not the eyeball, but the seeing-nature itself. "Reversing the seeing to trace its origin" (旋見循元) precisely describes the "penetration through sense-faculties" operation: turning the outward-directed seeing back toward its source.

**Gate of Cultivation through Consciousness — Mahāmaudgalyāyana (mind-consciousness):**

Maudgalyāyana entered through the clarification of mind-consciousness:

> "I reversed the clear, still radiance of my mind-consciousness and let it shine forth, like a muddy stream gradually becoming clear and pure. This is foremost!"[^11]

"Like a muddy stream gradually becoming clear and pure" (如澄濁流久成清瑩) — this is characteristic of the "cultivation through consciousness" gate: not severing the operation of consciousness, but allowing its turbidity to settle until the inherent clarity reveals itself.

**Gate of Returning to Nature through the Great Elements — Mahāsthāmaprāpta (mindfulness of the Buddha):**

The念佛圓通章 (Chapter on Perfect Penetration through Mindfulness of the Buddha) is one of Pure Land Buddhism's most important scriptural foundations:

> "If sentient beings remember and are mindful of the Buddha, they will certainly see the Buddha, whether in this present life or in lives to come. They will not be far from the Buddha, and without relying on any other expedient means, their minds will open of themselves. Like a person who has been near incense, whose body carries the fragrance — this is called 'adornment with fragrance and light.'"[^12]

Mahāsthāmaprāpta's method is "gathering in all six faculties with pure mindfulness in continuous succession" (都攝六根，淨念相繼). The metaphor of "fragrance and light adornment" (香光莊嚴) captures his understanding: the fragrance on your body is not your own — it comes from prolonged exposure to the incense-laden environment. Mindfulness of the Buddha works similarly: the merit is not something you generate but something that permeates you through sustained contact.

These four representatives illustrate four fundamentally different approaches: entering through what is heard (sense-object), through the capacity of seeing itself (sense-faculty), through the clarification of consciousness (consciousness), and through the continuity of mindful recollection (great element). What they share is not method but result — all attained perfect penetration. What differentiates them is not the quality of their methods but the differences in their faculty-dispositions — each found the door that fit.

#### 2.3 Avalokiteśvara's Ear-Faculty Penetration: The Practice Sequence

After the twenty-four sages had reported, Avalokiteśvara rose to speak last. His report far exceeds the others in both length and depth — a structural signal in itself.

Avalokiteśvara first disclosed his causal-ground aspiration: "I recall that innumerable kalpas ago, a buddha appeared in the world named Avalokiteśvara. I generated the aspiration for bodhi under that buddha, who taught me to enter samādhi through hearing, contemplation, and cultivation (從聞思修入三摩地)."[^13] His teacher's name was also "Avalokiteśvara" — indicating a lineage transmission. The nine characters "entering samādhi through hearing, contemplation, and cultivation" constitute the overarching framework of the entire ear-faculty penetration.

Avalokiteśvara then described his practice in a precise six-step sequence:

**Step 1: Entering the stream, the object vanishes (入流亡所).** Within the operation of hearing, he reversed its direction — instead of flowing outward after sound, he turned inward to observe the hearing-nature itself. "Entering the stream" means reversing the flow; "the object vanishes" means the objects of hearing disappear.

**Step 2: Both movement and stillness cease to arise (動靜二相了然不生).** Once the objects of hearing vanished, the conceptual pair of "sound" and "silence" also ceased. Ordinarily one assumes "silence" is simply "the absence of sound," but here both "movement" (sound present) and "stillness" (sound absent) are recognized as objects grasped by the ear-faculty — both must be transcended.

**Step 3: Hearing and what is heard are exhausted (聞所聞盡).** Both the capacity to hear and its objects are exhausted. This is the first level of emptiness — emptying the subject-object duality of hearing.

**Step 4: Awareness and what is aware become empty (覺所覺空).** One does not rest in the awareness that "hearing has been exhausted." Even "awareness" itself and "what awareness is aware of" are emptied.

**Step 5: Emptiness and what is emptied are extinguished (空所空滅).** Even "emptiness" and "that which has been emptied" are extinguished. Emptiness pushed to its extreme — even emptiness itself cannot be found.

**Step 6: Arising-and-ceasing having ceased, quiescent extinction manifests (生滅既滅，寂滅現前).** The first five steps all belong to the realm of arising and ceasing. When all arising and ceasing comes to rest, quiescent extinction — the nature of neither arising nor ceasing — manifests of itself.

The structure is not linear advancement but progressive negation: first negate the heard (object), then negate the hearer (subject), then negate awareness, then negate emptiness, then negate even the act of negation. At each step, what seemed like the final ground is revealed to be yet another layer to let go. Only when "letting go" itself has been let go does what was always already present — manifest.

The fruit of this realization is "obtaining two supreme qualities": "Above, I merge with the fundamental wondrous awakened mind of all buddhas of the ten directions, sharing the same compassionate power as the tathāgatas. Below, I merge with all sentient beings of the six destinies in the ten directions, sharing the same aspiration of compassion."[^14] Compassionate power upward, compassionate aspiration downward — this is the core virtue of Avalokiteśvara: great compassion (大慈大悲).

From this fruit unfold the thirty-two response-bodies, fourteen kinds of fearlessness, and four inconceivable powers. All originate from what the text calls "the vajra samādhi of hearing-permeation and hearing-cultivation, with its effortless wondrous power" (聞薰聞修金剛三昧無作妙力) — a term that appears three times across Avalokiteśvara's report, threading together his causal cultivation, his fruit-qualities, and his wondrous functioning.[^15]

#### 2.4 Mañjuśrī's Selection: "The True Teaching-Substance of This Land Is Pure and Lies in Hearing"

After all twenty-five had spoken, the Buddha requested Mañjuśrī to select the most suitable penetration for Ānanda and beings of the Dharma-ending age. Mañjuśrī responded in verse, with a structure of extraordinary precision.

**Preamble:** "Returning to the source, the nature is non-dual; the expedient means are many. The nature of the holy is without obstruction; both the forward and the reverse are expedient methods. Yet for the beginner entering samādhi, the speed is not the same."[^16] The first four lines establish equality — the source is one, the methods are many, the holy nature admits all paths. The final two lines introduce differentiation — for the beginner, some doors open faster than others. "The speed is not the same" is the entire justification for the selection: from the perspective of the fruit, all doors are equal; from the perspective of initial practice, efficiency genuinely differs.

**Systematic elimination:** Mañjuśrī then eliminated the other twenty-four penetrations one by one, using a uniform four-line format ending with "how can this yield perfect penetration?" (云何獲圓通). Each was found wanting on different grounds: the sense-object of form "cannot be thoroughly penetrated," the sense-object of sound "does not encompass the whole," the sense-object of smell "does not persist in awareness," and so on through all six sense-objects, five sense-faculties, six consciousnesses, and seven elements.[^17]

**The three authenticities of the ear-faculty:** After the eliminations, Mañjuśrī established the ear-faculty's superiority on three grounds:

First, **circular authenticity** (圓真實): "Suppose a person sits quietly while drums are struck in all ten directions — all ten places are heard simultaneously. This is circular authenticity."[^18] The eye can only see what is in front; the ear hears in all directions simultaneously. This is "circular" (圓).

Second, **penetrating authenticity** (通真實): "One can hear sounds through walls, whether far or near. The other five faculties are not equal to this."[^19] Physical barriers do not obstruct sound as they obstruct sight. This is "penetrating" (通).

Third, **constant authenticity** (常真實): "When sound is absent, hearing does not thereby cease; when sound is present, hearing does not thereby arise. Arising and ceasing are both completely transcended."[^20] The hearing-nature does not arise with sound or perish with silence — it transcends the duality of presence and absence. This is "constant" (常).

Circular, penetrating, constant — these three together constitute the doctrinal meaning of the term "perfect penetration" (圓通) itself.

**The core practice instruction:** Mañjuśrī concluded with the essential directive: "Reverse the hearing to hear the self-nature; the nature realizes the unsurpassed Way — this is truly what perfect penetration is."[^21] Not hearing *something*, but reversing the hearing to hear *what hears*. "You use your hearing to receive the Buddha's teaching — why not use your hearing to hear [the nature of] hearing itself?"[^22] When one root returns to the source, all six faculties are liberated.

The verse reaches its climax: "This is the gate to nirvāṇa traversed by buddhas as numerous as atoms of dust. Past tathāgatas have already accomplished it through this gate; present bodhisattvas each enter perfect clarity through it; future practitioners should rely on this very Dharma."[^23]

The final assessment: "For the realization of the nirvāṇa-mind, Avalokiteśvara is supreme.... The perfect penetration that surpasses the rest — this is the true heart of the matter."[^24]

### B. Commentary Sources

#### 2.5 Zixuan: The Four-Gate Taxonomy and the Positioning of the Ear-Faculty

In his *Śūraṅgama Commentary* (首楞嚴義疏注經, T1799), Song-dynasty commentator Zixuan (長水子璿) classified the twenty-five penetrations into four gates: "gate of awakening through sense-objects," "gate of realization through sense-faculties," "gate of cultivation through consciousness," and "gate of returning to nature through the great elements."[^25] This taxonomy reveals that the twenty-five are not random case reports but a methodologically organized system — twenty-five approaches to realization, classifiable into four basic types corresponding to four different orientations of practice.

Zixuan positioned the ear-faculty penetration as "realization through reverse hearing of the authentic" (返聞真實證) and explained why Avalokiteśvara was arranged to speak last: "Because the meaning of perfect penetration is vast, and because celebration, instruction, and selection follow in sequence, it is placed last to indicate the ultimate authentic truth. If it were presented first, the buddhas of the ten directions would immediately emanate light in celebration of this gate, and the remaining sages would have no opportunity to present theirs."[^26]

On the phrase "entering the stream, the object vanishes" (入流亡所), Zixuan's commentary offers precise practice guidance: "Initially observing the hearing-nature, one illuminates in reverse, departing from conditions. One does not follow the sense-objects in their arising and ceasing. Therefore it is called 'entering the stream, the object vanishes.'"[^27] "Entering the stream" means "reversing the stream" — instead of following the direction of sound outward, one reverses the flow and returns to the hearing-nature itself.

Zixuan's analysis of Mahāsthāmaprāpta's mindfulness-of-Buddha penetration in relation to the selection deserves particular attention. He wrote: "The mindfulness-of-Buddha approach is the most essential for this land. Although it involves arising and ceasing, the focused concentration of thought, combined with the Buddha's vow-power, leads directly to birth in the Pure Land. Having been born there, progress accelerates and realization is assured. In the present context of revealing the perfect faculty, Avalokiteśvara is supreme. The method of elevation and subordination requires this selection."[^28] The mindfulness-of-Buddha approach is effective — but its "cause" is a thought (念), and thought is a dharma of arising and ceasing. Using an arising-and-ceasing cause to seek a non-arising-non-ceasing perfect penetration constitutes a category mismatch of cause and fruit. This selection does not deny the efficacy of mindfulness of the Buddha; it evaluates within the specific criterion of "perfect penetration."

#### 2.6 Jiaoguang: Four Criteria for Selection and the Precise Meaning of "Within Hearing"

Ming-dynasty commentator Jiaoguang (交光真鑒) in his *Śūraṅgama Sūtra: Commentary Following the Essential Pulse* (楞嚴經正脈疏, X0275) articulated four precise criteria for the selection: "First, not suited to the locality; second, not convenient for beginners; third, requiring special prerequisites; fourth, not a method for sustained cultivation. By reversal, the ear-faculty is suited to the locality, convenient for beginners, requires no special prerequisites, and is viable for sustained cultivation."[^29]

These four criteria, stated in the negative, directly yield the ear-faculty's four advantages: (1) suited to the sahā world's conditions; (2) accessible to beginners; (3) requiring no special prerequisites such as prior supernatural attainments or initiatory transmissions; (4) a method one can practice consistently over time rather than a one-time event of spontaneous awakening.

Jiaoguang's reading of "the true teaching-substance of this land is pure and lies in hearing" (此方真教體，清淨在音聞) is particularly penetrating. He observed: "Conventionally, sound alone is taken as the teaching-substance — this is the provisional substance following appearances. But the true teaching-substance of this land, pure in original nature and pervading the dharma-realm, lies not in the sound but solely in the hearing-nature that hears the sound."[^30] The teaching-substance is not sound itself but the hearing-nature. Sound is the provisional vehicle; the hearing-nature is the authentic substance.

On practical implementation, Jiaoguang defined "within hearing" (聞中) with exacting precision: "This 'within hearing' is the midst of one-thought-not-arising, illuminating the dharma-realm in completeness — it is also the ground of the one-vehicle quiescent extinction, the true araṇya, the correct place of cultivation." He further offered concrete guidance: "One may verify this during quiet sitting practice, especially when rising at the fifth watch [approximately 3–5 a.m.], when the night air is clear and the myriad sounds have just begun to stir. With not a single thought arising, one perceives the hearing-nature — vast and circular, clear and illuminating."[^31]

Jiaoguang also distinguished the practice of reverse hearing from Tiantai śamatha-vipaśyanā: "The three contemplations of [Tiantai] begin through the sixth consciousness's discursive thinking, but this sūtra begins by setting aside the sixth consciousness entirely.... When practice arises, understanding ceases; a single act of reverse hearing is supremely simple and direct. There is no need to establish elaborate systems of calming and contemplation. This is what is called 'entering directly with a single blade.'"[^32]

### C. Supporting Scriptures

#### 2.7 The Lotus Sūtra's "Universal Gate" Chapter: Causal Ground and Fruit-Quality

The *Lotus Sūtra*'s Universal Gate chapter (普門品, T0262 fascicle 7) and the Śūraṅgama's ear-faculty penetration chapter together constitute the complete causal-and-fruit portrait of Avalokiteśvara.

The Śūraṅgama's Avalokiteśvara speaks in the first person — "I generated the aspiration for bodhi under that buddha," "Initially within hearing, I entered the stream" — this is the practitioner's retrospective account of causal-ground cultivation. The *Lotus Sūtra*'s Avalokiteśvara is described in the third person by the Buddha for Akṣayamati Bodhisattva — this is the display of accomplished fruit-qualities.[^33]

Both sūtras describe Avalokiteśvara's response-bodies (thirty-two in the Śūraṅgama, thirty-three in the *Lotus Sūtra*) with extensive overlap.[^34] The core difference lies in the sentence pattern: the Śūraṅgama uses "If beings are such... I manifest such-a-body and teach the Dharma" — first-person practitioner's voice; the *Lotus Sūtra* uses "For those who should be liberated by such-a-body, [Avalokiteśvara] immediately manifests such-a-body and teaches the Dharma" — third-person description of perfected function. Both texts share the epithet "Bestower of Fearlessness" (施無畏者), but in the Śūraṅgama it arises from cultivation ("the vajra samādhi of hearing-permeation"), while in the *Lotus Sūtra* it arises from function ("observing their voices, all attain liberation").

For the purposes of this paper, the causal-and-fruit distinction matters: the Śūraṅgama teaches *how to practice*; the *Lotus Sūtra* shows *what the practice yields*. Our focus here is the former: the causal-ground path.

#### 2.8 The Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra: Faculty-Disposition Differences and Pedagogical Fit

The *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra* (解深密經, T0676), in its chapter on the "Character of Non-Self-Nature" (無自性相品), provides a systematic analysis of how the same definitive teaching produces radically different effects depending on the listener's faculty-disposition. The sūtra distinguishes four types of beings:[^35]

*Type 1:* Those who have planted superior roots of good and can understand as it truly is — they directly comprehend the teaching, generate deep faith, and swiftly attain realization.

*Type 2:* Those who have planted superior roots of good and are honest in nature but lack the strength for analytical discrimination — though they cannot fully comprehend, they generate excellent faith and clean conviction, and through devotion they accumulate merit.

*Type 3:* Those who are not honest in nature and who cling to their own views — though they hear the teaching, they grasp its words literally, concluding that "all dharmas definitely have no self-nature," thereby falling into the extreme of annihilationism.

*Type 4:* Those who have not planted roots of good and are not honest in nature — they can neither understand nor generate faith, and they develop the notion that the teaching is "not-Dharma."

All four types encounter the same teaching. The dramatically different outcomes arise not from differences in the teaching but from differences in faculty-disposition. This is precisely the theoretical foundation of the twenty-five penetrations: it is not that methods differ in quality, but that faculty-dispositions differ in compatibility. The same method that yields "perfect penetration" for one practitioner's disposition may function merely as "expedient means" for another's.

The *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra* adds a further distinction: "Because they rely on the Dharma to attain śamatha and vipaśyanā, I designate such bodhisattvas as 'those who follow the Dharma' — they are of sharp faculties. Because they attain śamatha and vipaśyanā without relying on the Dharma, I designate such bodhisattvas as 'those who follow faith' — they are of dull faculties."[^36] The difference between sharp and dull faculties is not a difference in the nature of the faculty (聖性無不通, "the holy nature is without obstruction") but a difference in the mode of entry — some enter through Dharma (hearing, contemplation, and cultivation), others through faith (recollection, devotion). Both paths arrive, but for different people, at different speeds.

## 3. Core Argument

### 3.1 A Classification System, Not an Encyclopedia

The most common misreading of the twenty-five penetrations is to treat them as "an introduction to twenty-five meditation techniques" — as though one could browse and select at leisure, like choosing from a menu. But this is not a menu. It is a classified report.

Zixuan's four-gate taxonomy reveals the report's internal structure: penetrations through sense-objects, sense-faculties, consciousness, and the great elements.[^37] This classification is not arranged by difficulty or by degree of sublimity, but by the *type of object upon which practice is based* — does one work from "the perceived object" (sense-object), "the perceiving capacity" (sense-faculty), "the act of perception" (consciousness), or "the nature of the constitutive elements" (great element)?

Jiaoguang further distinguished the structural orientations: "Following the current of sense-objects and consciousness, winding one's way to the Way — this is called 'forward entry.' Reversing the outward flow of the faculty-nature and turning back to enter the nature — this is called 'reverse entry.'"[^38] Penetrations through sense-objects and consciousness are "forward entries" — following the current of perception to arrive at the goal by a longer route. Penetrations through sense-faculties are "reverse entries" — reversing the outward direction and returning directly. Jiaoguang specifically observed that all the sense-faculty penetrations share the vocabulary of "reversing" and "turning back" (旋, 反): Aniruddha's "reversing the seeing to trace its origin," Maudgalyāyana's "reversing the clear, still radiance." This is not coincidence but the common structural signature of "penetration through sense-faculties."

Understanding this classification is prerequisite to understanding Mañjuśrī's selection: he is not picking the "best" from among twenty-five parallel options, but identifying which *type* of approach — and which specific faculty within that type — best matches the faculty-disposition characteristic of beings in this world.

### 3.2 The Precise Doctrinal Meaning of "Perfect Penetration"

"Perfect penetration" (圓通, yuántōng) is not a vague term of approbation. Mañjuśrī's three authenticities define each character precisely.

"Circular" (圓) = encompassing all without remainder. "Drums struck in all ten directions — all ten are heard simultaneously." The eye covers roughly 180 degrees forward; the nose detects only what is nearby; the body must make contact; the mind wanders. Only the ear-faculty receives from all spatial directions simultaneously. This is "circular authenticity."[^39]

"Penetrating" (通) = unobstructed freedom. "One can hear sounds through walls." Sight is blocked by a wall; hearing is not. The ear-faculty's function is not interrupted by material barriers. This is "penetrating authenticity."[^40]

And more fundamental than either is "constant" (常): sound arises and ceases, but the hearing-nature itself does not arise with the presence of sound or cease with its absence. This is precisely P03's thesis — "the seeing-nature does not perish with objects" — transposed into the auditory register.[^41]

The three together — circular (spatially encompassing), penetrating (materially unobstructed), constant (temporally unconditioned) — constitute the doctrinal content of "perfect penetration." The other faculties, sense-objects, consciousnesses, and elements each fall short on one or more of these dimensions — which is exactly why Mañjuśrī posed the question "how can this yield perfect penetration?" to each of them.

### 3.3 Faculty-Disposition Fit: The Same Method, Different Effects

The *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra*'s four-type analysis provides a crucial interpretive framework for the twenty-five penetrations: the efficacy of a method depends not on the "quality" of the method itself, but on the degree of "fit" between the method and the practitioner's faculty-disposition.[^42]

This framework applies directly to the twenty-five reports. Kauṇḍinya awakened through the sense-object of sound because his karmic history was bound to "sound" — he was the first person to hear the Buddha's voice at the Deer Park. Aniruddha awakened through the eye-faculty because his blindness forced him to confront the seeing-nature directly. Mahāsthāmaprāpta awakened through mindfulness of the Buddha because his deepest karmic connection was with Amitābha's lineage — recollection (念) was his most well-practiced mental activity.

Jiaoguang's analysis sharpens this point: "Chan practitioners base their realization on the specific point of the present life's awakening, but that is not necessarily their cultivated method. How could Lingyun have taken peach blossoms as his method of cultivation? It was simply that his investigation of the doubt had been pushed to the extreme, and he happened to break through at the moment of seeing the blossoms."[^43] Awakening may be *triggered* by a specific sensory contact, but the true "method" is the sustained cultivation that brought the practitioner to that tipping point. Similarly, the twenty-five sages' reports are not random triggers but the core axis of their long-term practice.

This answers the common question: if all methods are equal, why select at all? Because equality is from the fruit-perspective — "the holy nature is without obstruction." From the causal-ground perspective, different faculty-dispositions respond to different methods with genuinely different degrees of efficiency — "for the beginner entering samādhi, the speed is not the same." Mañjuśrī's selection does not claim the ear-faculty is "superior" but that it is "more efficient for the majority of beings in this world."

### 3.4 The Structure of Progressive Negation in Avalokiteśvara's Six Steps

Avalokiteśvara's six-step practice sequence has a deep structure that Zixuan accurately identified: progressive negation.

Step 1 (entering the stream, the object vanishes): negate the heard — cease following sound outward. Step 2 (movement and stillness cease): negate both poles of auditory experience — not only sound but even "the absence of sound." Step 3 (hearing and what is heard exhausted): negate the hearer — even the subjective capacity of hearing is emptied. Zixuan annotated this step with precision: "The conditions having vanished, the hearing-appearance does not arise. This hearing-appearance is the wisdom of hearing; subject and object are both still" — this corresponds to "the emptiness of persons" (人空).[^44]

Step 4 (awareness and what is aware become empty): negate awareness — one notices that "hearing has been emptied," but this very noticing is a fresh attachment. Step 5 (emptiness and what is emptied extinguished): negate emptiness — the sense that "everything is empty" is itself something. Step 6 (arising-and-ceasing having ceased, quiescent extinction manifests): negate negation itself — all five prior steps are operations within the realm of arising and ceasing; when even the activity of negation ceases, the nature of quiescent extinction — which was never absent — simply manifests.

Zixuan's summation: "Arising-and-ceasing having ceased — this concludes the three wisdoms and the three emptinesses. Since they progressively emptied, all belonged to arising and ceasing. Having reached the ultimate, this is what is meant by 'having ceased.' The truth of non-arising, the nature of quiescent constancy, clearly manifests — this is what is meant by 'quiescent extinction manifests.'"[^45]

This structure resonates deeply with S5's analysis of the Diamond Sūtra's "jífēi" (即非) formula: the Diamond Sūtra uses language to negate language ("X is precisely not-X; this is called X"); Avalokiteśvara uses hearing to negate hearing ("reverse the hearing to hear the self-nature"). The methods differ, but the operational logic is structurally parallel — both employ the practitioner's most familiar tool to deconstruct that very tool, until the distinction between tool and user dissolves.

### 3.5 The True Criteria of Mañjuśrī's Selection

The most frequent misinterpretation of Mañjuśrī's selection is to read it as "the ear-faculty penetration is better than all twenty-four others." But Mañjuśrī himself opened his verse with: "Returning to the source, the nature is non-dual; the expedient means are many. The nature of the holy is without obstruction; both the forward and the reverse are expedient methods."[^46] If the ear-faculty were genuinely "best" in absolute terms, these four lines would be unnecessary.

Jiaoguang resolved this tension by observing that "their cultivation is truly without superiority or inferiority" can be understood at three levels: each sage had multi-lifetime habituation suited to his particular nature; each sage's specific afflictions were addressed by the most apt counter-method; and each had the Buddha's spiritual power enhancing the process.[^47] In other words, within each sage's own karmic context, his method was optimal. A different person using the same method would not necessarily achieve the same result.

What then did Mañjuśrī actually select for? Jiaoguang's four criteria provide the precise answer: the other twenty-four penetrations are subject to one or more of the following limitations — "not suited to the locality, not convenient for beginners, requiring special prerequisites, not a method for sustained cultivation."[^48]

*Not suited to the locality:* The sahā world's teaching-substance is sound — the Buddha teaches through speech, beings receive through hearing. Other buddha-lands may employ light, fragrance, or silence as their medium. But in this world, sound is the medium.[^49]

*Not convenient for beginners:* Some penetrations require extraordinary karmic conditions (such as Aniruddha's blindness) or prior attainments (such as Maudgalyāyana's supernatural powers) that beginners cannot replicate.

*Requiring special prerequisites:* Some methods presuppose specific transmissions, initiations, or preliminary practices not universally available.

*Not a method for sustained cultivation:* Some realizations are one-time events triggered by unique circumstances, not methods one can practice daily.

The four limitations, reversed, yield the ear-faculty's four strengths: suited to this world, accessible to beginners, requiring no special prerequisites, and viable for daily sustained practice. What Mañjuśrī selected was not "the most sublime method" but "the method the most people can use" — pedagogical fit, not doctrinal ranking.

### 3.6 "Reverse the Hearing to Hear the Self-Nature": The Ultimate Orientation

The chapter's ultimate destination is not "choose the ear-faculty" — it is "reverse the hearing to hear the self-nature; the nature realizes the unsurpassed Way."[^50]

The operative meaning of "reverse hearing" (反聞) is: redirect the hearing-nature. Ordinarily, the hearing-nature flows outward — hearing sounds, discriminating meanings, generating reactions. "Reverse hearing" means ceasing to chase after auditory objects and instead turning back to investigate: what is it that hears?

This corresponds precisely with P03's line of inquiry. P03 asked: what do you see when you close your eyes? You answer "nothing" — but you have forgotten that you are seeing. P05's version: what do you hear in a perfectly silent room? You answer "nothing" — but you have forgotten that you are hearing. The judgment "I hear nothing" is itself proof that the hearing-nature is operating. If the hearing-nature were not present, you would not even know that there was no sound.

Jiaoguang's practical guidance is concrete: "Especially when rising at the fifth watch, when the night air is clear and the myriad sounds have just begun to stir — with not a single thought arising, one perceives the hearing-nature: vast and circular, clear and illuminating, unobstructed by mountains or walls, undimmed by darkness."[^51] No special environment or ritual is required. The early morning, the quietest moment, with not a single thought — right there, recognize the hearing-nature that hears everything. It is vast (圓, circular in all directions), clear (通, unobstructed), and constant (常, neither arising with sound nor ceasing with silence).

This is why Mañjuśrī pronounced: "This is the gate to nirvāṇa traversed by buddhas as numerous as atoms of dust." Not because the ear is more important than the eye, but because the operation of reverse hearing most directly points toward the ultimate goal shared by all penetrations: returning to what was always already present. Regardless of whether one enters through the eye-faculty, ear-faculty, nose-faculty, or any other — one must eventually "reverse" and investigate the nature of the faculty itself. The ear-faculty is simply the reversal-point that is easiest to operate for the majority of beings in this world.

## 4. Cross-Disciplinary Dialogue: First-Language Acquisition and the Default Status of the Hearing-Faculty

⚠️ **The following analogy is a heuristic analysis, not a theoretical equivalence. The Buddhist concept of hearing-nature (聞性) and the linguistic-cognitive concept of auditory processing belong to fundamentally different ontological orders.**

Human first-language acquisition has a characteristic widely acknowledged in linguistics: it begins almost entirely through hearing. Infants begin receiving the prosodic patterns of their mother's speech while still in utero.[^52] After birth, long before they can produce meaningful speech, they are already hearing — distinguishing phonemic boundaries, perceiving intonational contours, building categorical representations of speech sounds. Vision plays a supporting role (lip-reading, gesture), but audition is the overwhelmingly dominant channel. Congenitally deaf children without access to sign language experience severely delayed language development — a fact that demonstrates, by negation, hearing's default status in language acquisition.

This structure bears a comparable similarity to Mañjuśrī's logic for selecting the ear-faculty.

**Similarity: the default reception channel.** The "teaching-substance" (教體) of the sahā world is sound — the Buddha teaches through voice, beings receive through hearing. Just as infants prioritize the auditory channel among all sensory modalities for language reception, sahā-world beings prioritize the ear-faculty among all six faculties for receiving the Dharma. Jiaoguang's assessment — "Beings of this land have a particularly sharp ear-faculty, able through the hearing-nature to penetrate immeasurable distinctions of principle and phenomena"[^53] — structurally parallels the observation in linguistics that hearing is the default channel for language acquisition.

Mañjuśrī's criterion of "suited to the locality" resonates here as well: different "buddha-lands" may have different teaching-substances — "In some buddha-lands, the Buddha uses light to carry out the Buddha's work... In the Fragrant Accumulation buddha-land, there are no words; only through various fragrances do the devas attain discipline."[^54] This is analogous to how different sensory channels can serve as the primary vehicle for language under different conditions — in signing communities, vision replaces hearing as the primary channel. But under "standard conditions" (the sahā world), hearing is the default.

**Fundamental difference: symbol systems versus the hearing-nature itself.** Linguistics deals with symbol systems — language is a structured system of phonemes, morphemes, and grammatical rules; hearing is the sensory channel that receives this system. The Buddhist "entering samādhi through hearing, contemplation, and cultivation" deals not with symbol systems but with the hearing-nature itself — not what one hears, but what the capacity for hearing *is*. The endpoint of first-language acquisition is mastery of a linguistic tool; the endpoint of reverse hearing is the dissolution of the distinction between tool and user.

Language acquisition depends on the *content* of hearing (discriminating phonetic differences). The practice of reverse hearing depends precisely on relinquishing the *content* of hearing (entering the stream, the object vanishes) and returning to the *capacity* of hearing itself. The directions are exactly opposite: one moves outward to receive signals; the other moves inward to investigate the signal-receiver.

**Non-reducibility statement:** The analogy with first-language acquisition illuminates only one thing: under specific conditions of existence, one sensory channel becomes the most natural, most efficient mode of information reception. Mañjuśrī's selection of the ear-faculty carries a similar "default channel" logic. But the analogy ends there. The hearing-nature is not the auditory nerve, not the function of the auditory cortex, not any component of language processing. Reverse hearing of the self-nature points toward a reality that transcends all sensory functions — a domain beyond the scope of linguistics. ⚠️

## 5. Middle Way Corrections

### ❌ 5.1 Absolutizing the Selection: "The Ear-Faculty Penetration Is Always Best"

The most common misreading treats Mañjuśrī's selection as meaning "the ear-faculty penetration is the best method under all circumstances." But Mañjuśrī's own opening lines — "Returning to the source, the nature is non-dual; the expedient means are many. The nature of the holy is without obstruction; both the forward and the reverse are expedient methods"[^55] — preclude any such absolute reading. The selection is an optimal recommendation under three specific conditions: "this land" (the sahā world), "the beginner" (初心), and "the Dharma-ending age" (末劫). It is not a ranking that transcends all conditions. Jiaoguang explicitly stated that the other twenty-four sages' cultivation "is truly without superiority or inferiority"[^56] — within each one's own karmic context, each method was optimal.

### ❌ 5.2 Conflating Devotion to Avalokiteśvara with Avalokiteśvara's Method

Veneration of Avalokiteśvara is pervasive in Chinese Buddhist culture — reciting the name, chanting sūtras, prostrations, and petitionary prayers. These are all meritorious practices. But they are a different level of practice from the ear-faculty penetration sequence that Avalokiteśvara himself described in the Śūraṅgama. Reciting "Namo Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva" is outward-directed faith — relying on Avalokiteśvara's vow-power as a contributory condition. "Entering the stream, the object vanishes" and "reverse the hearing to hear the self-nature" are inward-directed cultivation — taking one's own hearing-nature as the object of practice. The two can complement each other, but should not be confused. Equating devotion to Avalokiteśvara with Avalokiteśvara's own practice obscures the precise operational instructions of the ear-faculty penetration.

### ❌ 5.3 Selection Does Not Equal Rejection: The Status of Mindfulness of the Buddha

Mañjuśrī's elimination of Mahāsthāmaprāpta's mindfulness-of-Buddha penetration — "All conditioned activities are impermanent; the nature of mindfulness is originally arising-and-ceasing. Cause and fruit are of different categories — how can this yield perfect penetration?" — is most easily misread as "the mindfulness-of-Buddha method is ineffective." But Zixuan's clarification is essential: "The mindfulness-of-Buddha approach is the most essential for this land.... Through focused concentration combined with the Buddha's vow-power, one is born directly in the Pure Land. Having been born there, progress accelerates and realization is assured."[^57] Jiaoguang's analysis is more precise: "Using this as the cause, being born and seeing the Buddha — cause and fruit correspond, and there is nothing objectionable. But using this arising-and-ceasing cause to seek the present realization of the non-arising-non-ceasing perfect penetration — cause and fruit are of different categories, and this is decidedly unattainable."[^58] The "thought" (念) in mindfulness is an arising-and-ceasing dharma; seeking a non-arising-non-ceasing fruit through an arising-and-ceasing cause constitutes a category mismatch — but this judgment holds only within the specific criterion of "perfect penetration," not as a comprehensive assessment of the mindfulness-of-Buddha approach.

### ⚠️ 5.4 Linearizing the Six-Step Sequence

Avalokiteśvara's six steps — entering the stream, movement-and-stillness ceasing, hearing-and-heard exhausted, awareness-and-aware emptied, emptiness-and-emptied extinguished, quiescent extinction manifesting — are easily read as a strict linear ladder, like the four dhyānas and eight samādhis in fixed ascending sequence. But the sūtra's phrasing is "gradually increasing in this way" (如是漸增) — "gradually increasing" implies a logical sequence but not necessarily sharply demarcated stages. In actual practice, these steps may alternate, overlap, and deepen recursively rather than proceeding once-through in fixed order. Reifying the six steps into fixed stages produces attachment to "which step am I on?" — precisely contrary to the six steps' core spirit of progressively letting go, including letting go of keeping score.

### ⚠️ 5.5 Two Errors Concerning "Within Hearing"

Jiaoguang specifically warned against two common errors regarding the phrase "within hearing" (聞中).[^59] The first error: taking "within hearing" to mean "within the physical ear" — understanding reverse hearing as concentrating attention on one's ears. This is a materialistic misunderstanding. The second error: taking "within hearing" to mean "within ear-consciousness" — understanding reverse hearing as observing one's psychological process of auditory discrimination. This falls into the discursive thinking of the sixth consciousness. The true "within hearing" is "the midst of one-thought-not-arising, illuminating the dharma-realm in completeness" — transcending both the physical ear and the ear-consciousness, working directly at the level of the hearing-nature. If the practitioner is still thinking "I am performing reverse hearing," what is being reversed is not the hearing-nature but the sixth consciousness's conceptualization.

## 6. Conclusion

Twenty-five doors, all leading to the same destination. You need only open one.

This paper has argued that the core thesis of the Śūraṅgama Sūtra's chapter on Twenty-Five Perfect Penetrations is not "which method is best" but "which method best fits you." Each of the twenty-five sages' practice reports constitutes empirical evidence for faculty-disposition fit — each found realization through the sensory, cognitive, or elemental dimension with which he was most familiar, most practiced, most karmically connected. Mañjuśrī's selection is not a rejection of the other twenty-four penetrations but an optimal recommendation under three specific conditions — this land, the beginner, the Dharma-ending age — based on four criteria: the ear-faculty is suited to the locality, convenient for beginners, requires no special prerequisites, and is viable for sustained cultivation.

Avalokiteśvara's practice sequence — entering the stream / movement-and-stillness ceasing / hearing-and-heard exhausted / awareness-and-aware emptied / emptiness-and-emptied extinguished / quiescent extinction manifesting — is a structure of progressive negation: first negate the heard, then the hearer, then awareness, then emptiness, then negation itself. When all arising and ceasing comes to rest, the nature of quiescent extinction — which was never absent — manifests of itself. The starting point of this entire sequence — "entering the stream, the object vanishes" — is "reverse hearing": not hearing something, but turning back to hear what hears.

Yet understanding all of this remains "knowing" (知). Ānanda's "alternating-day malaria" persists. Knowing about reverse hearing is not the same as performing reverse hearing; knowing about the hearing-nature is not the same as realizing the hearing-nature. The chasm between knowing and realizing that S5's Diamond Sūtra series repeatedly emphasized remains fully in force here. The Śūraṅgama's response is: now that you know where the door is — walk through it.

After walking through, what you need is no longer theory but protection. Having found your door and begun to practice — what then? How do you avoid going astray? How do you distinguish genuine states from illusory ones?

The next paper — *The Three Gradual Stages and the Ground of Dry Wisdom* — enters the Śūraṅgama's middle-circle deployment of practice: after the practitioner has found his or her perfect penetration, three gradual stages of purification must be traversed (removing contributory causes, excising the core nature, counteracting present karma) before formal stages of realization can begin. The four categories and seven elements told you "everything is originally tathāgatagarbha" (the principle). The twenty-five penetrations told you "where to start" (the door). The three gradual stages tell you "how to walk after entering the door" (the path).

If you have read this entire paper and developed a profound understanding of and resonance with Avalokiteśvara's practice sequence — that is good. But please remember: understanding is not the endpoint of practice. It is not even the starting point. The starting point of practice is when you actually sit down, in the quietest moment, stop all thoughts, and listen — not for any sound, but for what hears.

What hears has always been there.
