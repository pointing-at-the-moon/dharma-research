## Abstract

This paper weaves together the sixth of Samantabhadra's Ten Great Vows — "Requesting the Turning of the Dharma Wheel" (*adhyeṣaṇā*) — with the Huayan school's "Gate of the Realm of Indra's Net" (因陀羅網境界門, *yīntuóluówǎng jìngjiè mén*), and argues that the two name a single structural fact: a single act of requesting the Dharma is already a request in all ten directions, because the *dharmadhātu* never posited separation as a premise in the first place, and therefore requires no transmission across distance. The argument rests on three chapters of the *Avataṃsaka-sūtra* in their original form — the "Xianshou" 〈賢首品〉, "Huazang shijie" 〈華藏世界品〉, and "Shidi" 〈十地品〉 chapters — with an upstream anchor in the *Ekottarāgama*'s "Quanqing" 〈勸請品〉 passage in which Brahmā "knows from afar what the Tathāgata is thinking" (an Āgama-layer seed of the motif), and downstream anchors in Zhiyan's 智儼 *Huayan yisheng shixuan men* 《華嚴一乘十玄門》 — where the gate-name originates in the formula "hidden and manifest mutually reveal each other, layer upon layer upon layer" (隱映互相顯發、重重復重重) — and finally in Fazang's 法藏 *Huayan jing tanxuan ji* 《華嚴經探玄記》, where the gate receives its formal definition. The paper offers six original observations, of which two are central. First, the Indra's-net verse within 〈Xianshou〉 itself juxtaposes two similes inside a single stanza — "heat-shimmer mirage in the open wilderness" (*marīci*) and "Indra's net in the heavens" — and this internal juxtaposition is read as the sūtra's own built-in structural defense against reading the net as a physical-contact lattice. Second, Fazang in the *Tanxuan ji* in fact deploys two parallel sequences of the ten gates (a doctrinal-framework version and a doctrinal-stratification version); Point-to-the-Moon's placement of the Indra's-net gate at the seventh position follows the second sequence, and this placement is textually grounded. The paper's cross-disciplinary section strictly executes the direction "how Buddhism sees *why physics is surprised*," never the reverse direction of "physics proves Buddhism," and it closes with four Middle-Way corrections that seal off any physicalist misreading.

**Keywords:** Gate of Indra's Net, Requesting the Dharma Wheel, Ten Great Vows of Samantabhadra, Ten Profound Gates, *dharmadhātu*-arising, premise of separation, Zhiyan, Fazang, cross-disciplinary Middle Way / 因陀羅網境界門、請轉法輪、普賢十大願、華嚴十玄門、法界緣起、分離前提、智儼、法藏、跨學科中道

## I. Introduction: Why "Requesting the Turning of the Dharma Wheel" Must Be Paired with "Indra's Net"

This paper is the mid-series climax of S8. It takes up two matters that on their face have nothing to do with each other — the sixth of Samantabhadra's Ten Great Vows, "Requesting the Turning of the Dharma Wheel," and the Huayan "Gate of the Realm of Indra's Net" among the Ten Profound Gates — and argues that at the level of *dharmadhātu* structure, these two are from the outset two faces of a single matter.

This thesis was already given a structural statement in §3.6 of S8-P11 〈The Ten Great Vows Seen Through the Ten Windows〉: one request for the Dharma is a ten-direction request, because among the jewels of the net there was never any "distance" that needed to be traversed. P11 is the compressed version — it only exhibits that the structure is there; the present paper is the full version, giving the scriptural warrant, the patriarchal exposition, and the complete unfolding of the cross-disciplinary convergence. If the reader, midway through this paper, feels "I have already seen this in P11," please allow Point-to-the-Moon to clarify: P11 is the *punchline* of this thesis; the present paper is its *argument*. The two are not repetition — they are setup and payoff.

This paper simultaneously inherits three frames already established earlier in S8, without restating them. First, the "container thesis" established in P02 〈The Place of Yogācāra Within the Five-Teaching Classification〉: the five teachings are different-resolution maps of the reception bandwidth of the desire-realm human consciousness; they are not a hierarchy of rank. In §IV below, when discussing why quantum physics is "surprised" by non-locality, the present paper will directly draw on this container thesis without re-arguing it. Readers with questions on this point should return to P02. Second, the doctrinal basis developed in P04 〈Nature-Arising and Dependent-Arising〉: "the hidden and the manifest accomplished together." When the present paper speaks of the "mutual mirroring of the jewels of the net," the structural root of that mirroring is precisely nature-arising (性起), not ordinary dependent-arising theory. Third, the basic toolset established in P08 〈The Perfect Fusion of the Six Marks〉: the triad total/particular, same/different, formation/dissolution. In handling the cultivational dimension of 〈Shidi〉, this paper will draw on those tools in passing but will not unpack them.

The structure of this paper follows two axes, parallel and non-intersecting. The first is the chronological axis of scriptural warrant and patriarchal exposition: from the Āgama's Brahmā-entreaty, to the Indra's-net verses of 〈Xianshou〉, to Zhiyan's origination of the gate-name, to Fazang's systematic placement, to Chengguan's 澄觀 late settlement. This axis occupies §II and §III. The second axis is the direction in which modern readers most easily misread Indra's net — reading it as the Buddhist counterpart of "the holographic universe," "quantum entanglement," or "black-hole information" — which is fully addressed and corrected in the cross-disciplinary section. This axis occupies §IV and §V. The two axes converge in §VI, which returns the sixth Great Vow to its daily-practice posture in the cultivational dimension of "Requesting the Turning of the Dharma Wheel"; §VII (closing and bridge) then loops back to the opening epigraph, completes a three-line closure, and bridges forward to the formal original-thesis argumentation of P07 (Ocean-Seal Samādhi) and P10 (Samantabhadra's Practice-Vow).

A stance should be declared in advance about the writing of this paper. In §IV's cross-disciplinary convergence, it will unfold three strands of physical evidence — quantum non-locality, the holographic principle of black holes, and the AdS/CFT duality — and will commit to writing, in full, the oral insight that my teacher has worked out across thirty years of daily practice on precisely this thesis: **separation is illusion, therefore no transmission is needed**. But this paper is not "using physics to prove Buddhism." On the contrary — this paper will argue that physics is surprised by non-locality precisely because physics has not yet put down its own premise structure (locality, separability, objectivity), whereas Huayan two thousand years ago never erected that premise in the first place, and so for Huayan there is nothing that needs to be surprising. This is not arrogance; it is epistemological precision. Surprise is a reaction of desire-realm consciousness, not an event in the *dharmadhātu*. In §IV this sentence will be pushed to its strongest form, and in §V it will be prevented, by Middle-Way correction, from sliding into a belittlement of physics.

With this, we begin.

## II. Scripture First, Treatise After: Returning "Requesting the Dharma Wheel" to the Āgamas

### §II.a (Sūtra-portion): The Early Record of Brahmā's Entreaty

Point-to-the-Moon's Methodological Declaration IV (see S8-P01 §I) stipulates that the principle "scripture first, treatise after" must be radicalized all the way down to the structural level. The concrete application of this principle in §II of the present paper is that, before taking up "Requesting the Turning of the Dharma Wheel" as one of Samantabhadra's Great Vows, we must first return to the Āgamas and see how the act of "entreaty to teach the Dharma" is recorded in the earliest scriptural corpus of Buddhism. The Huayan "Requesting of the Dharma Wheel" is not a Huayan original; it inherits from the Āgamas and carries out a structural thematization of a seed already planted there.

The most detailed Āgama record of the request for the turning of the Dharma wheel is found in the first sūtra of fascicle 10, "Quanqing-pin" 〈勸請品第十九〉 ("Chapter on Entreaty"), of the *Ekottarāgama* (《增壹阿含經》). The very name of this chapter is "Entreaty," and its opening sūtra directly addresses the foundational event of Brahmā's entreaty that the Buddha teach, shortly after the Buddha's awakening. The text reads:

> Thus have I heard. At one time the Buddha was beneath the Bodhi tree in the country of Magadha. At that time, the World-Honored One, not long after attaining the Way, gave rise to this thought: "The profound Dharma I have now [realized] is difficult to fathom and difficult to comprehend, hard to perceive, inconceivable, subtle in its cessation, [a thing] only the wise can know, [those who] can discriminate its meaning and, practicing it without weariness, attain joy. If I were to speak this sublime Dharma to others and they did not believe, did not receive it, did not practice it, my effort would be in vain and only loss would come of it. I should now remain silent — what need is there to teach?" [^1]

This passage records the Buddha's first thought after awakening — his doubt as to whether teaching the Dharma could be received. The doubt is extremely real: the profound Dharma is difficult to fathom; if people will not believe or practice it, teaching is fruitless toil. On this basis, the Buddha inclined to silence.

The turning point of the narrative comes in the very next movement. The sūtra continues:

> At that moment, Brahmā, in the Brahmā-heaven, **knew from afar** what the Tathāgata was thinking. As swiftly as a strong man flexes and extends his arm, he vanished from the Brahmā-heaven and arrived before the World-Honored One, where he bowed his head to the Buddha's feet and stood to one side. [^2]

This sentence is the structural core of the entire Āgama narrative of "Requesting the Turning of the Dharma Wheel," and it is the key scriptural passage of §II.a of the present paper. Two details deserve attention. First, Brahmā "**knew from afar**" what the Tathāgata was thinking. This is not ordinary knowing; it is a direct cognition that crosses the two realms of human and Brahmā, requiring no linguistic transmission. Second, Brahmā comes "**as swiftly as a strong man flexes and extends his arm**" from the Brahmā-heaven to the World-Honored One. The simile emphasizes *instantaneity* — in the time it takes to flex and extend one arm, Brahmā has already traveled from the Brahmā realm to the Bodhi tree in the human world.

Then follows Brahmā's plea:

> This Jambudvīpa will surely decay; the three realms will lose their eye. The Tathāgata, the Worthy One, the Perfectly Enlightened, has appeared in the world and should unfold the jewel of the Dharma, yet now [the Buddha] does not further propound the taste of Dharma. May the Tathāgata, for the sake of all beings, expound the profound Dharma broadly! Moreover, the faculties of these beings are easy to deliver. If they do not hear, they will forever lose the Dharma-eye. [^3]

Brahmā's entreaty has two layers — one of crisis (the three realms lose their eye; the Dharma-eye is forever lost) and one of potential (beings' faculties are ripe for deliverance; they are "like lotuses not yet opened"). The Buddha, having received the entreaty, "knowing what was in Brahmā's mind and out of compassion for all beings," responds in verse: "Brahmā now comes to entreat / the Tathāgata opens the Dharma-gate; / those who hear gain firm faith, / discriminating the essentials of profound Dharma." [^4] The entreaty succeeds; the Buddha resolves to teach, and then goes to the Deer Park to turn the Dharma wheel for the first time.

### §II.b (Transition to the Treatise-Portion): How the Āgama "Knowing from Afar" Is Thematized by Huayan

Here the present paper makes only a transitional treatise-portion citation; full treatise-portion development is reserved for §III.4 (Zhiyan) and §III.5 (Fazang).

In this Āgama narrative, what deserves a Huayan reader's attention is not the outcome "Brahmā's entreaty succeeds" but the two action-structures of "knowing from afar" and "in the time of flexing and extending an arm." These two actions jointly hint at one thing: **between the request for the Dharma and the teaching of the Dharma, no crossing of physical distance is required in the first place**. Brahmā does not need to write a letter, does not need to dispatch a messenger, does not need to wait for the Buddha's thought to "reach" the Brahmā realm — Brahmā simply knows from afar. This knowing-from-afar is not an empowerment through supernatural power; it is that the structure of thought is itself not subject to the limitation of spatial separation.

This structure is precisely the earliest *leakage* of Indra's net. The Āgamas did not develop the full logic of mutual identity and mutual entry, nor do they use the vocabulary "Indra's net" — the Āgamas only record the event, while the event itself already carries the structural seed. What Huayan subsequently does is to draw this seed out of the narrative and to rename it in structural language: Brahmā's "knowing from afar" is not an isolated case but a natural exposure of the *dharmadhātu* structure within an Āgama setting; the "instant arrival" of flexing and extending an arm is not a compression of time but a consequence of the fact that no real spatial separation existed to be traversed.

Huayan's manner of handling the Āgamas reveals its characteristic stance here: it does not deny the Āgamas, does not rewrite the Āgamas, it only thematizes what the Āgamas had already recorded but not yet made thematic. This is also the concrete practice of Methodological Declaration IV of this series — "the radicalization of the 'scripture-first-treatise-after' principle at the structural level." The work of the treatise portion (Huayan) is not to create new doctrine but to name a structure that already exists in the sūtra portion (the Āgamas) but has not yet been named.

Accordingly, when §III unfolds below the full scriptural warrant and patriarchal exposition of the Huayan Indra's net, the reader should continuously remember: the seed of this structure already exists in the Āgamas; Huayan only thematizes it from "a story of Brahmā's entreaty" into "the universal structure of the *dharmadhātu*."

## III. Scriptural Warrant and Patriarchal Exposition of Indra's Net

### §III.1 The Strongest Evidence Chain of Āgama Leakage

§II has already addressed the Brahmā-entreaty passage from "Quanqing-pin" in fascicle 10 of the *Ekottarāgama*. This section widens the scope and catalogs other seed-passages in the Āgamas that relate to the Indra's-net structure, distinguishing explicitly which are *strong leakage*, which are *weak leakage*, and which amount only to incidental linguistic resemblance. The distinction matters: Point-to-the-Moon's methodology requires honesty about the strength of textual evidence — weak evidence must not be inflated into strong, nor should weakness of evidence be taken as grounds for total denial.

There is a passage in the Āgamas often cited by later Huayan readers as "the earliest link between Indra (Śakra) and Indra's net," from fascicle 1 of the *Qishi jing* 《起世經》, "Yanfuzhou-pin" 〈閻浮洲品〉. The passage narrates how Śakra, after defeating the asuras, calls to mind the elephant king, who responds by manifesting; Śakra mounts him and roams the Thirty-three Heavens. In describing the elephant king's structure the text uses the language of one-to-many transformation: "From one body he manifests many heads; each head has six tusks; on each tusk there are seven pools; in each pool there are seven lotuses; on each lotus there are seven jade maidens." [^5] The one-to-many transformational structure of this narrative comes very close to the later Huayan idiom of "one is many."

But here an honest distinction must be explicitly marked: the doctrinal character of this *Qishi jing* passage is still **supernatural transformation**, not **the essence of the dharmadhātu**. Śakra's calling the elephant king has a cause (his victory over the asuras); the elephant king's one-to-many transformation has a direction (from one body to many heads to many tusks to many pools); the whole structure is a sequential temporal unfolding, not a present-moment simultaneous mutual containment. This differs at the root from Huayan's Indra's net, in which "the jewels of the net mutually mirror one another simultaneously in the present instant, with no temporal sequence and no causal direction." The *Qishi jing* passage is strong leakage but remains "one-to-many" rather than "mutual identity and mutual entry."

The second passage worth marking is the description of world-structure in fascicle 19 of the *Dīrghāgama* 《長阿含經》, the *Shiji jing* 〈世記經〉. It uses a great deal of the stratified-containment language of "Sumeru and the four continents," "small-thousand, middle-thousand, great-thousand," structurally close to the Huayan "one mote of dust containing measureless kṣetras." But here too an honest distinction is required: the *Shiji jing*'s stratified containment is **spatial, physical containment** (the larger holds the smaller; the many contains the one), not the Huayan mutual-identity structure of "within the small the great appears; within the one the many is contained." The former is a set-theoretic subset relation; the latter is a phenomenological relation of mutual manifestation. The *Shiji jing* is medium-strength leakage.

The third passage — and the one this section really means to mark as strong leakage — is the already-detailed Brahmā-knowing-from-afar passage in fascicle 10 of the *Ekottarāgama*'s "Quanqing-pin." It is strong leakage precisely because it relies on no spatial or material metaphor whatsoever: it directly presents the act of "the structure of thought needs no physical transmission." Brahmā knows from afar what the Tathāgata is thinking; this act has no causal direction (it is not "the Tathāgata emits a signal, Brahmā receives the signal"), no temporal delay (the time of flexing an arm), no intermediary of spatial crossing (it is not light or sound traversing the void). The structure of the act just *is* "separation is illusion, therefore no transmission is needed" — even though the Āgamas do not use this language.

What this section must close by honestly declaring is this: the Āgamas contain no term "Indra's net" and no systematic language of "mutual identity and mutual entry." The full Huayan account of Indra's net is a cooperative accomplishment of the treatise portion (Zhiyan, Fazang, Chengguan) and the sūtra portion (〈Xianshou〉, 〈Huazang shijie〉, 〈Shidi〉). To read the three Āgama leakages above as "Indra's net already exists in the Āgamas" is an over-extension of the text. Point-to-the-Moon does not take that extension. Point-to-the-Moon takes a more precise reading: **the Āgamas have the seed; Huayan carries out the thematization; the seed is not the fruit, but the fruit cannot arise from nothing**. This distinction is the methodological stance jointly carried by §II and §III.1.

So closes §III.1. §III.2 next enters the full citation-segment of the Indra's-net verse of 〈Xianshou〉 — the most pivotal passage of this paper.

### §III.2 The Indra's-Net Verse of 〈Xianshou〉: An Original Reading of the Dual-Simile

The scriptural basis for the Huayan Indra's net, in its complete form, occurs in only one place in the 80-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* — a long verse segment in "Xianshou-pin" 〈賢首品第十二之一〉, fascicle 14 of the *Mahāvaipulya-buddha-avataṃsaka-sūtra*. This passage continues Bodhisattva Xianshou's exposition of the bodhisattva's accomplishment at the stage of faith, entering the samādhis, universally manifesting supernormal transformations to deliver beings, and pushes through three successive verses to the most central verse of all. This section will cite the full verse sequence and identify a structure that, prior to Point-to-the-Moon, has not been explicitly marked in modern Huayan scholarship — **the juxtaposition of dual similes within a single verse**.

First, the three-verse sequence. The first is the Ocean-Seal Samādhi verse:

> The forms of beings all differ; / their deeds, sounds, too are measureless. / All this is able to be manifested — / the majestic power of the Ocean-Seal Samādhi. [^6]

The Ocean-Seal Samādhi speaks of "the power to manifest" — the forms, deeds, and sounds of beings in the ten directions can all appear simultaneously within the samādhi, as seals impressed upon the ocean. The second is the Avataṃsaka Samādhi verse:

> All this is accomplished at ease / by the power of the Buddha's Avataṃsaka Samādhi: / within a single mote of dust one enters samādhi, / accomplishing samādhi in all motes of dust, / yet that mote does not increase; / within the one, inconceivable kṣetras universally appear. [^7]

The Avataṃsaka Samādhi speaks of "the power to contain" — entering samādhi within a single mote accomplishes samādhi in all motes; that mote does not increase, yet inconceivable buddha-lands universally appear within it. From "power to manifest" to "power to contain," the structure deepens by one layer: it is no longer merely that the ten directions appear at one place; rather, that one place *is itself already* the ten directions. The third verse — the most central verse of this paper — is the Indra's-net verse:

> Within that one mote, many kṣetras: / some with Buddhas, some without; / some defiled, some pure; / some vast, some narrow; / some forming, some dissolving; / some upright, some slanting; / **some like heat-shimmer in the open wilderness, / some like Indra's net in the heavens**. / As manifested within the one mote, / so too is every mote of dust. [^8]

The three verses form a clear stepwise progression: Ocean-Seal Samādhi (power to manifest) → Avataṃsaka Samādhi (power to contain) → Indra's Net (mutual identity and mutual entry). From "the ten directions manifest together at the one" to "the one is itself the ten directions," and then to "the ten directions and the ten directions manifest mutually and without obstruction." This progression is not rhetorical reinforcement but the sequential thematization of a three-layered doctrinal structure — manifesting, containing, and mutual identity.

Now the original discovery (Observation Two) of this paper is to be laid down.

Observe the fourth line of the Indra's-net verse: "**Some like heat-shimmer in the open wilderness, some like Indra's net in the heavens**." Within one and the same verse, one and the same rhythm, one and the same "some… some…" parallel construction, two similes are juxtaposed — "heat-shimmer in the open wilderness" and "Indra's net in the heavens." These two similes each carry an independent tradition in Mahāyāna scriptures: the former comes from the *Prajñāpāramitā* corpus and is the standard idiom of the *marīci*-simile, the "mirage simile," indicating that all dharmas are like the water-image formed by heat-haze in the wilderness — seemingly existent yet actually nothing. It is an *emptiness-simile*. The latter is the Huayan-specific simile of mutual identity and mutual entry, indicating that all dharmas are like the jewels upon Indra's net in the palace of Śakra, each jewel mirroring and containing every other. It is a *mutual-containment-simile*. One says "not," the other says "is" — one dismantles substantial existence, the other establishes mutual manifestation.

Point-to-the-Moon's reading of this passage is: the juxtaposition of these two similes is not a rhetorical arrangement but a structural definition. **Indra's net is, from the outset, not a substantial interlocking, but an illusory mutual manifestation.** The author of 〈Xianshou〉 juxtaposed the mirage-simile and Indra's net within the same verse precisely in order to pre-empt later readers from reading Indra's net as "a connective lattice among substances" — as though the jewels were independently existing entities and the strands of the net were physical channels linking them. The sūtra itself says: it is not so. The jewels can mutually mirror precisely because the jewels themselves are like heat-haze in the open wilderness — empty in self-nature, still in intrinsic essence. Only because they are empty can they contain; only by containing can they mutually manifest; only by mutually manifesting do they form the net. Apart from the emptiness-simile, Indra's net slides into the physicalist misreading of "a connective lattice"; apart from the mutual-containment simile, the mirage-simile remains at the Hīnayāna level of "all is nothing" and cannot account for the rich manifestation of the *dharmadhātu*. Only with both similes juxtaposed is the doctrine of Indra's net complete.

This reading has a cross-chapter corroboration within the 80-fascicle *Avataṃsaka*. Fascicle 20, 〈Shidi-pin〉, in describing the bodhisattva's cultivation through the ten grounds, directly employs the phrase "**entering the Indra's-net dharmadhātu**":

> Dwelling in the ground of one-nature non-duality… attaining the Buddha's ten powers, entering the Indra's-net *dharmadhātu*, accomplishing the Tathāgata's unobstructed liberation. [^9]

The structure of this sentence deserves notice. The condition for a bodhisattva "to enter" the Indra's-net *dharmadhātu* is "dwelling in the ground of one-nature non-duality." "One-nature non-duality" is precisely another name for emptiness — not "a substantial one-nature," but the emptiness realization of "all dharmas are non-dual, without self-nature, and cannot be grasped." The sūtra itself marks it: the admission ticket to the Indra's-net *dharmadhātu* is not "seeing the jewels of the net" but "dwelling in one-nature non-duality." In other words, Indra's net is from the outset a concretization of emptiness, not some substantial structure existing apart from emptiness.

This is an internal logical consistency across chapters of the 80-fascicle *Avataṃsaka*: 〈Xianshou-pin〉 defines the emptiness-undertone of Indra's net through the juxtaposition of dual similes, and 〈Shidi-pin〉 reconfirms this undertone through the cultivational condition of "one-nature non-duality." The two passages mutually attest one another and jointly constitute the textual ironclad proof that "Indra's net is, from the outset, not substantial."

This section arrives at a structural statement that lays groundwork simultaneously for §IV's cross-disciplinary convergence and for §V's Middle-Way Correction #10: **the sūtra 〈Xianshou-pin〉 itself already pre-empts the physicalist misreading.** When contemporary readers draw analogies between Indra's net and modern physical concepts such as quantum entanglement, the holographic universe, and black-hole information, if they ignore the two layers of emptiness-protection provided by the mirage-simile and the "one-nature non-duality" ground, they will read Huayan as "a substance-connection theory earlier than modern physics" — and this is precisely the misreading that the original sūtra has already explicitly forestalled. §IV will address why this analogy is at once alluring and dangerous; §V will deliver the positive Middle-Way correction. But here in §III.2, the point first to be established is: the sūtra itself knows this danger and provides protection in the structure of its verses. Readers who remain faithful to the sūtra will simply not fall into the misreading.

Finally, a piece of empirical textual-distribution data, offered to refute a common impression — the impression that Indra's net is only a one-off image in 〈Xianshou-pin〉. In fact, in the 80-fascicle *Avataṃsaka*, "Indra's net" as a structural term appears in at least five places, forming a distributional grid: fascicle 8 of 〈Huazang shijie-pin〉 describes the Great Cakravāla mountains in net-like geometry, "with maṇi-jewels as a net, hanging down all around and covering"; fascicle 9 of 〈Huazang shijie-pin〉 three times — describing the tenth layer world-seed as "shaped like Indra's net," the net-like arrangement of the fragrant-water oceans, and the overall construction of the Huazang world-ocean; fascicle 14 〈Xianshou-pin〉 holds the three-verse sequence discussed in this section; and fascicle 20 〈Shidi-pin〉 gives the cultivational positioning "entering the Indra's-net *dharmadhātu*" [^9a]. Five occurrences spanning three levels — cosmology, cultivational stage, and samādhi-realm — echoing one another without repetition. This is precisely the internal evidence that the 80-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* treats Indra's net as a structural term and not as isolated rhetoric.

So closes §III.2. §III.3 next enters the cosmological stratum of 〈Huazang shijie-pin〉, expanding Indra's net from "within one mote of dust" to the entire Huazang world-ocean.

### §III.3 〈Huazang shijie-pin〉: The Scale-Span from One Mote of Dust to the Huazang World-Ocean

〈Xianshou-pin〉 takes "within one mote of dust" as its discursive unit, locating Indra's net at the smallest structural slice. 〈Huazang shijie-pin〉 (fascicles 8–10) pushes the same mutual-containment logic in the opposite direction, toward the largest cosmological scale: the overall construction of the Huazang Adornment World-Ocean. These two directions together constitute, within the 80-fascicle *Avataṃsaka*, a complete scale-span — from one mote to the world-ocean, the same law of "within the one, the many appears; within the many, the one appears" runs through both extremes.

〈Huazang shijie-pin〉 opens with a programmatic verse that sets the key: "Of every mote of dust in the Huazang world, / within each and every mote the *dharmadhātu* is seen; / jeweled light manifests Buddhas as gathered clouds — / this is the Tathāgata's freedom among the kṣetras." [^10] Structurally this verse is the reverse side of 〈Xianshou-pin〉's "within each mote, measureless kṣetras": 〈Xianshou-pin〉 descends to a single mote from the perspective of samādhi-cultivation; 〈Huazang shijie-pin〉 likewise descends to a single mote from the perspective of cosmological construction. What the two chapters describe is not two worlds, but one and the same *dharmadhātu*-arising observed from two directions.

The overall construction of the Huazang world-ocean is itself a net-like structure. The sūtra describes its Great Cakravāla mountains as having "maṇi-jewels forming a net, hanging down and covering all around" [^11]; the entire world-ocean is supported vertically by wind-wheels equal in number to the motes of dust of Mount Sumeru, bearing above them the fragrant-water ocean adorned with universal-light maṇi, above which stands a great lotus, and within the lotus is the Huazang Adornment World-Ocean itself — a three-dimensional geometry woven of net-meshes. More importantly, fascicle 9, in describing the tenth-layer world-seed, directly names its shape with "Indra's net": "There is a world named Ground of Arising Majestic Power… its shape is like Indra's net, covered above by cloud-nets of boundless colored flowers." [^12] This is the direct naming of Indra's net as a world-shape in the 80-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* — net-geometry is here registered affirmatively as the essential form of cosmological construction, not as rhetorical metaphor.

Note the symmetry of scale: the Indra's net of 〈Xianshou-pin〉 is within one mote of dust; the Indra's net of 〈Huazang shijie-pin〉 is an entire world-seed. The same structural law operates simultaneously at the smallest and largest scales, and this is precisely the core claim of Huayan *dharmadhātu*-arising: scale-independence. The microscopic layer (the net within one mote), the macroscopic layer (the net of the world-ocean), and the cultivational layer (the net of "dwelling in one-nature non-duality" in 〈Shidi-pin〉) mutually attest one another and jointly constitute the 80-fascicle *Avataṃsaka*'s complete internal scriptural warrant for Indra's net.

It should be noted that 〈Huazang shijie-pin〉 does not yet philosophically unfold the mutual-containment logic of Indra's net; it provides the cosmological visual frame and the structural groundwork. The systematization of this frame into a teachable pedagogical structure waited until Zhiyan — the second patriarch of the Huayan school — who for the first time completed the task in the form of a "gate-name."

### §III.4 Zhiyan's *Huayan yisheng shixuan men*: The First Naming of Indra's Net as a Gate-Name

Zhiyan 智儼 (602–668) is the second patriarch of the Huayan school. His *Huayan yisheng shixuan men* 《華嚴一乘十玄門》 (T1868) is the earliest extant systematic treatise bearing the name "Ten Profound Gates." Its position in the history of Huayan instruction is perfectly clear: it is the document that, for the first time, cast the scattered Indra's-net passages of 〈Xianshou-pin〉, 〈Huazang shijie-pin〉, and others into a pedagogical unit with a formal gate-name, teachable and debatable.

The archaic Ten Profound Gates as listed by Zhiyan are: (1) the Gate of Simultaneous Complete Correspondence; (2) the Gate of the Realm of Indra's Net; (3) the Gate of Secret Concealment and Manifestation Accomplished Together; (4) the Gate of Subtle Mutual Containment and Establishment; (5) the Gate of the Ten Times Separately Arising Yet Forming One Another; (6) the Gate of the Pure and Mixed Stores Each Possessing Virtue; (7) the Gate of the One and the Many Mutually Containing Without Sameness; (8) the Gate of All Dharmas Mutually Identical and Free; (9) the Gate of Mind-Only Turning All into Goodness; (10) the Gate of Relying on Phenomena to Reveal the Dharma and Produce Understanding. [^13] Note: the Gate of the Realm of Indra's Net is placed in the second position, immediately after the Gate of Simultaneous Complete Correspondence — and this very placement carries important structural information. The first gate speaks of "mutual correspondence with no before or after"; the Indra's-net gate follows directly upon it, and Zhiyan's own sub-gate commentary states it plainly: "This is by way of simile, in clarification of [the first]." That is to say, the second gate is the simile-visualization of the first gate's principle — Indra's net is not one parallel member among the ten gates, but the dedicated image used to visualize the principle of the first gate.

Zhiyan's definition of Indra's net is extremely careful: "What is now called Indra's net takes as its simile the net in Śakra's palace. For this net in Śakra's palace to serve as a simile, one must first recognize the aspect of this heavenly net. In what does this aspect consist? It is like a group of mirrors mutually illuminating one another: the images of all the mirrors are seen within a single mirror; and within those images, further images of all the mirrors appear; and within each such image, further images again. Thus images manifest within images, layer upon layer, constituting an inexhaustibility that is itself inexhaustible." [^14] Note two things. First, Zhiyan explicitly distinguishes the "Brahmā palace net" of the *Brahmajāla-sūtra* from the "palace-of-Śakra net" adopted by Huayan, and chooses the latter — a technical decision, meaning that Indra's net has a precise referent within the Huayan system and must not be confused with generic net-imagery. Second, his primary analogy is "a group of mirrors mutually illuminating," not "jewel mirroring jewel." The key feature of mirrors-illuminating is this: the images within the mirrors are themselves unreal reflections, not substances, and from these unreal reflections further unreal reflections arise, forming inexhaustibility. This choice stands in one lineage with the mirage-simile discussed in §III.2 — Zhiyan saw the structural depth of the dual-simile juxtaposition and internalized it into the core definition of the gate-name.

Still more important: Zhiyan adds, at the end of his sub-gate commentary: "If this were to be explained in the manner of the Mahāyāna in general, then it would be said that through supernatural transformation the great and the small are mutually entered… This is not the same as the doctrine of the One Vehicle. Question: if this school's exposition of mutual entering does not appeal to supernatural power but rather says 'the essence is of itself always thus,' then this would be a diffuse state with no boundaries at all… Answer: because wisdom discriminates, taking one as host, the others are companions." [^15] The importance of this exchange cannot be overstated. Zhiyan explicitly refuses to read the mutual identity and mutual entering of Indra's net as "supernatural transformation," and positions it instead as "the essence is of itself always thus" — which is to say, layer-upon-layer inexhaustibility is not some supernatural mechanical interaction, but a natural property of *dharmadhātu*-arising itself. The four characters "**the essence neither increases nor decreases**" (體無增減) are Zhiyan's first pre-emptive sealing-off of the physicalist misreading. This sealing-off precedes Fazang's *Tanxuan ji* by nearly half a century, and directly echoes the dual-simile structure of the original text disclosed in §III.2 — from the sūtra, to the first patriarch (Dushun), to the second patriarch (Zhiyan), Huayan's vigilance against "this is not a substantial interlocking" is *continuous*, not a later development.

Zhiyan's contribution reaches its end here: he accomplished the naming, the positional order, the core definition, and the in-principle sealing-off. But he had not yet fully woven the logic of mutual containment between the Gate of Indra's Net and the remaining nine gates. That work belongs to the third patriarch Fazang's *Huayan jing tanxuan ji*, and is the subject of §III.5.

### §III.5 Fazang's *Huayan jing tanxuan ji* — The Structural Elevation from Simile to Independent Dharmadhātu-Gate

Fazang 法藏 (643–712) is the third patriarch of the Huayan school. His twenty-fascicle *Huayan jing tanxuan ji* 《華嚴經探玄記》 (T1733) is the first systematic commentary on the 80-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* in the history of Huayan instruction. Its treatment of Indra's net makes structural advances upon Zhiyan at three levels.

**First advance: the coexistence of two versions of the Ten Profound Gates list.** Fascicle 1 of T1733 gives, within a single fascicle, two lists of the Ten Profound Gates in different orders. The former list (the *doctrinal-framework prefatory version*) places Indra's net in the eighth position and names the gate "Gate of Indra's-Net Layer-upon-Layer" (帝網重重門) [^16]; the latter list (the *doctrinal-stratification disclosive version*) places it in the seventh position and names it "Gate of the Indra's-Net *Dharmadhātu*" (因陀羅網法界門) [^17]. This is not a matter of textual variance — it is Fazang deliberately distinguishing two pedagogical lines: the first list is used to lay out the interpretive framework of the entire sūtra; the second is used to disclose the perfect-fusion stratification of the Huayan doctrine. The very coexistence of the two lists already carries a structural message — the Ten Profound Gates sequence is not uniquely fixed; what matters is the principle-node each gate designates, not its position in the sequence. This dual-list phenomenon was gradually effaced by single-sequence standardization in the later textbook tradition, thereby obscuring Fazang's original pedagogical flexibility [^28a].

**Second advance: the positional elevation from "simile-gate" to "independent *dharmadhātu*-gate."** Zhiyan's archaic Ten Profound Gates placed Indra's net in the second position, as the dedicated simile-visualization of the first gate (Simultaneous Complete Correspondence) — which is to say, in Zhiyan the function of the Indra's net is that of an *image*, while the principle itself resides in the first gate. Fazang elevates it from its simile-status into an independent *dharmadhātu*-gate treating "the recursive structure itself," and his positioning-sentence reads: "Within each and every mote of dust of the seven lotus-leaves, boundless kṣetra-oceans simultaneously appear; within those kṣetra-oceans there are further motes; and within those motes, further kṣetra-oceans. Layer upon layer thus, never to be exhausted — this is not what mental cognition can reach by calculation. It is like the heavenly jewels of Śakra's net, clear and luminous, mutually reflecting one another, with reflections arising within reflections, without end." [^18] Note the order of Fazang's exposition: he first lays down the recursive structure of "motes within kṣetras, kṣetras within motes," and only then brings in the simile of Śakra's jeweled net. This order is the reverse of Zhiyan's — Zhiyan enters through the simile; Fazang enters through the principle. Why the elevation? Because the division of labor between this gate and the fourth gate, "Gate of All Dharmas Mutually Identical and Free," needs clarification: the Mutual-Identity Gate handles the essential-unity declaration of "one is all," while the Indra's-Net *Dharmadhātu* Gate handles the recursive spatiotemporal unfolding of that declaration. The one is a logical principle, the other a structural visualization — only by separating them into two gates can they be distinguished.

**Third advance: the epistemological sealing-off of "not what mental cognition can reach by calculation."** At the end of his sub-gate commentary, Zhiyan sealed off the physicalist misreading with "the essence is of itself always thus" — layer-upon-layer inexhaustibility is not a supernatural mechanical interaction. Fazang, in his definition-sentence, adds a further seal: "**not what mental cognition can reach by calculation**" (非是心識思量所及) [^19]. These six characters mark the realm of Indra's net directly out of the valid range of conceptual thought and position it as the direct-intuitive realm of Samantabhadra Bodhisattva. Zhiyan seals off *physicalization*; Fazang seals off *conceptualization*. The sealings-off of the two patriarchs connect front to back and jointly constitute the Huayan school's twofold line of defense against misreading Indra's net. Later readings of Indra's net as "the universe is a supra-scale network" (a physical metaphor) or as "all things are interconnected" (a philosophical slogan) both fall outside these two seals — they are not the Huayan school's Indra's net.

In other fascicles of T1733 Fazang repeatedly links Indra's net to concrete pedagogical units: fascicle 2, discussing the simultaneous attainment of awakening by the ten Buddhas, says "throughout all Indra's-net boundless worlds, in every moment of thought, there is the very-first first-attainment of Buddhahood" [^20]; fascicle 4, discussing the samādhi-function of Samantabhadra, lists "the three Indra's-net samādhi gates" [^21], and explains the manifestation-mechanism of the Pure Lands as "because the Indra's-net lands are about to manifest, one first enters the Indra's-net samādhi" [^22]; fascicle 13 directly loops the layer-upon-layer manifestation of the heavenly net back to the Yogācāra Tathāgatagarbha: "Like Indra's net with its layer-upon-layer images manifesting — all of it is the perfect fusion of mental consciousness and Tathāgatagarbha dharma-nature." [^23] These scattered usages show: in Fazang's system, the heavenly net is not an isolated single gate but a structural principle running through temporal theory, Buddhahood theory, Pure Land theory, and Yogācāra. The third patriarch completed the thoroughgoing elevation of Indra's net from simile to *dharmadhātu*-gate, from a local gate-name to a system-wide pivot.

One question remains: how do the Ten Profound Gates mutually contain one another? This is Chengguan's work.

### §III.6 Chengguan's *Huayan jing shu* — The Canonical Closure of Phenomenon-and-Phenomenon Non-Obstruction

Chengguan 澄觀 (738–839), the fourth patriarch of the Huayan school, authored the sixty-fascicle *Dafangguang fo huayan jing shu* 《大方廣佛華嚴經疏》 (T1735) and the ninety-fascicle *Suishu yanyi chao* 《隨疏演義鈔》 (T1736) — the most complete commentarial system on the 80-fascicle *Avataṃsaka*. With regard to the jeweled net of Śakra, Chengguan's work does not lie in inventing a new reading but in **canonization** — merging Zhiyan's "the essence is of itself always thus" sealing-off and Fazang's new Ten Profound Gates ordering into a standard architecture directly teachable in later textbooks [^24]. In fascicle 2 of his *Commentary*, when unpacking the Ten Profound Gates, Chengguan explicitly places "the Gate of the Realm of Indra's Net" in parallel with "the Gate of Subtle Mutual Containment and Establishment" and "the Gate of the One and the Many Mutually Containing Without Sameness," citing Fazang's *Tanxuan ji* as his standard [^25]. This parallel listing is itself an act of authoritative endorsement: it transforms Fazang's "elevation" from a patriarchal individual judgment into the settled doctrine of the Huayan school.

Chengguan's distinctive contribution lies in elevating the **Phenomenon-and-Phenomenon Non-Obstruction Dharmadhātu** (事事無礙法界) to the highest rank of Huayan doctrinal classification. In fascicle 1 of the *Commentary* he sets up the Four Dharmadhātus — phenomenon, principle, non-obstruction of principle-and-phenomenon, and non-obstruction of phenomenon-and-phenomenon — assigning the fourth as the realm proper to the *Avataṃsaka* alone, while the first three are shared with the common teachings [^26]. This Four-Dharmadhātu architecture locks down once more the positioning of Śakra's jeweled net: **what the heavenly-net simile exhibits is precisely the structural morphology of the Phenomenon-and-Phenomenon Non-Obstruction Dharmadhātu**, not the non-obstruction of principle-and-phenomenon (which belongs to the *Awakening of Faith* and the Final Teaching stratum). With this judgment, Chengguan provides a geometric criterion for the uncommon features of "Huayan as Separate One-Vehicle" over against the Final and Sudden Teachings: any teaching-gate that can be described in terms of Indra's-net structure belongs to Phenomenon-and-Phenomenon non-obstruction; what cannot be so described still remains beneath principle-and-phenomenon non-obstruction.

At this point the historical arc of §III is complete:

- **Dushun** 杜順, through contemplative practice, established the cultivational rank of the heavenly net (*Fajie guanmen* 《法界觀門》);
- **Zhiyan** 智儼, through "the essence is of itself always thus," sealed off physicalization and for the first time installed it within the Ten Profound Gates (*Souxuan ji* 《搜玄記》);
- **Fazang** 法藏, through an inversion of expository order, elevated the heavenly net from simile-gate into an independent *dharmadhātu*-gate, and through "not what mental cognition can reach by calculation" sealed off conceptualization (the two-version *Tanxuan ji*);
- **Chengguan** 澄觀, through Four-Dharmadhātu classification, positioned the heavenly net as the geometric morphology of Phenomenon-and-Phenomenon non-obstruction, completing canonization (*Huayan jing shu*).

Four generations of patriarchs, two sealings-off, one elevation, one doctrinal positioning — this is not an evolutionist linear progression, but **a single insight, complete from the beginning, gradually unfolding its pedagogical language across four generations**. Śakra's jeweled net was already, in Dushun's hands, the object of contemplative practice; in Chengguan's hands, it is only clothed in a different set of teachable terminology. This point is of the highest importance for §IV's cross-disciplinary convergence: when contemporary physicists see quantum entanglement and are surprised, it is not because the Huayan school has only now acquired the answer, but because the Huayan school never took "separation" as a default premise — and it is the absence of that premise that is the true starting point.

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Convergence: The Limits of Analogy Between Indra's Net and Contemporary Scientific Models

### §IV.1 Why This Section Must Take "Sealing-Off" as Its Keynote

Indra's net is perhaps the single image in the entire *Avataṃsaka* most liable to "physicalist" misreading by contemporary readers. Imagining each maṇi-jewel as a qubit, jewels mirroring jewels as quantum entanglement, one jewel containing all as the holographic principle — this package of analogies has become the standard online narrative. But §III.4 of this paper has already shown that Zhiyan, as early as the seventh century, wrote down "the essence neither increases nor decreases" and said in so many words that Indra's net "is not supernatural transformation but the essence of itself always thus" [^27]; and §III.5 has shown that Fazang in the *Tanxuan ji* further read this mutual containment as the epistemological structure "the Ten Profound Gates are mutually subject and object," not as some kind of mechanical transmission [^28]. Each of the two patriarchs, from the sides of ontology and epistemology respectively, sealed off the entry-points of the physicalist misreading. The task of this section is therefore not to rebuild a bridge between Huayan and physics, but to mark clearly the load-bearing limits of the bridge — ⚠️ all cross-disciplinary analogies in this section serve only as heuristic tools, and are in no case to be read as declarations of theoretical equivalence.

### §IV.2 Quantum Non-Locality ⟷ Indra's Net: The Limits of the Analogy

⚠️ Quantum non-locality describes the following: a pair of particles in an entangled state, when one is measured, the state of the other immediately manifests a correlation, no matter how far apart the two are in space. What shocks physicists about this phenomenon is its tension with classical physics' presupposed "principle of locality" — information and causal transmission cannot exceed the speed of light. Einstein called it "spooky action at a distance" (*spukhafte Fernwirkung*), precisely because he was still thinking within the classical framework of "some kind of transmission mechanism is needed between two separated objects."

Likening quantum entanglement to the dynamic of Indra's net — "when one jewel moves, all jewels move" — is superficially alluring. But this analogy commits a structural error: it presupposes that the jewels are first separate and only then mutually mirroring. Huayan's Indra's net was never this structure — the mutual mirroring of jewels is not "transmission" but "always already thus," which is precisely the meaning of "the essence neither increases nor decreases." The surprise of quantum entanglement comes from physicists not yet having relinquished the premise of "separation"; the Buddha-Dharma never erected that premise in the first place. Put another way: quantum non-locality is counter-intuitive for physics, and not for Huayan — not because Huayan "already knew," but because the *dharmadhātu* Huayan describes was never from the outset "a collection of separable entities." The analogy stops here: quantum entanglement can serve as a pedagogical gesture pointing toward "the relinquishability of the premise of separation," but it cannot be read as a physical realization of Indra's net.

### §IV.3 "Small-World" and "Scale-Free" in Network Science ⟷ Indra's Net: Topological Differences

⚠️ Network science, in the twenty-first century, has provided two widely-discussed topological structures: the small-world network (Watts–Strogatz model) and the scale-free network (Barabási–Albert model). The former describes the phenomenon of short average path-length and high clustering coefficient between nodes; the latter describes the power-law distribution in which a few nodes possess large numbers of connections and form "hubs." Likening Indra's net to some extreme form of small-world network — each jewel directly connected to all others, average path-length equal to one — is another commonly traveled analogical route.

This analogy is closer to the topological intuition of Indra's net than the quantum-entanglement analogy, but two key differences must still be marked clearly. First, the nodes of network science are discrete, countable, and the edges are relations imposed externally upon the nodes; the jewels of Indra's net are not first independent nodes to which connections are then added — rather, "within one jewel, all jewels appear." The content of a jewel *is* all the other jewels; jewel and net are not two separable strata. Second, in network science, nodes — however densely connected — still retain the individuality of "self being self"; in Indra's net, Zhiyan's definition "a group of mirrors mutually illuminating, the images of all mirrors seen within a single mirror" [^29] already dilutes even the identity of "self being self" — the self-nature of the jewel just *is* mutual mirroring itself; there is no jewel prior to the mutual mirroring. ⚠️ Network science can supply a topological imagination of "high connectivity," but it cannot model the layer in which "the self-nature of the node just is the network" — and this is precisely the point at which Huayan's mutual identity and mutual entering cannot be reduced to a network.

### §IV.4 The Holographic Principle ⟷ One is All: The Analogy Most Prone to Overreach

⚠️ The holographic principle originates in proposals by physicists Gerard 't Hooft and Leonard Susskind in the context of black-hole thermodynamics, and later received technical expression in the AdS/CFT duality of string theory: all the information within a volume can be completely encoded on the boundary of that volume. The popular version of the holographic analogy — "every fragment of a holographic plate contains the entire image of the plate" — is an optical-level phenomenon, and the two are often conflated.

Equating Huayan's "one is all, all is one" with the holographic principle is the cross-disciplinary analogy most prone to overreach. The overreach lies here: the holographic principle (whether technical or popular) still presupposes a physical substrate carrying the information — the emulsion of the plate, the degrees of freedom of the boundary field, the geometry of AdS spacetime. In the holographic framework, "one" and "all" are "an information-correspondence between part and whole" — the one is the local, the all is the global, and the two are linked through information-entropy conservation. Huayan's "one is all" is not an information-correspondence of part and whole; it is, as Fazang's *Tanxuan ji* puts it, "the Ten Profound Gates are mutually subject and object" — when any single dharma is seen as it truly is, all remaining dharmas simultaneously manifest within that one dharma, not because information is conserved, but because the *dharmadhātu* itself contains no real partition between "the local" and "the global" [^30].

⚠️ The holographic principle can serve as a useful mental image, helping the beginner loosen the classical intuition that "the part must be smaller than the whole"; but it cannot carry Huayan's core claim — that the mutual identity of all dharmas is not a question of information distribution but is the unfolding, at the *dharmadhātu* level, of the *prajñā* doctrine that "because of emptiness-of-self-nature, mutual identity obtains." Reading the holographic principle as a physical proof of Huayan misreads both the holographic principle and Huayan.

### §IV.5 The Absence of the Premise of Separation: Why the Physicalist Misreading Is Inevitable

The three analogies above — quantum entanglement, network topology, the holographic principle — all err in the same structural position: each of the scientific frameworks to which they belong presupposes some form of "separation" as a starting point, and then sets out to explain why things that appear separate are nonetheless somehow connected. Quantum entanglement must explain why separated particles remain correlated; network science must explain why separated nodes still form tight topologies; the holographic principle must explain why a separated boundary and volume nonetheless share information. All of these surprises are built upon the default setting "things are originally separate."

The close of §III.6 has already pointed out that the Huayan *dharmadhātu* never erected this premise [^31]. The mutual mirroring of Indra's jewels is not the overcoming of a state of separation; it is the direct presentation of the fact that the state of separation was never established to begin with. This is not to say that Huayan "knew earlier than physics" — it is to say that Huayan and physics are asking entirely different questions. Physics asks: "why are apparently separated things nonetheless correlated?" Huayan asks: "why do we presume beforehand that there is separation at all?" The two domains of discourse do not overlap, and forcing them to overlap produces the bidirectional misreadings "physics has proved the Buddha-Dharma" and "the Buddha-Dharma is an ancient version of quantum mechanics" — the former dwarfs the Buddha-Dharma into a pre-scientific intuition, the latter dwarfs physics into a footnote of the Buddha-Dharma; neither side is respected.

Thus the cross-disciplinary convergence of this section finally returns to Huayan itself: Indra's net does not need the endorsement of physics, and physics does not need the authentication of Huayan. The two may serve one another as pedagogical gestures and as catalysts for mental imagery, but they cannot serve one another as proof. ⚠️ All analogies given in this section remain at the level of *heuristic* and must never be cited as evidence for "Huayan and modern science are in accord" — this is the single line the reader most needs to carry away. §IV.6 and §IV.7 will push this stance further into two concrete applications — the doctrinal positioning of AI and quantum computing, and the cognitive-scientific reading of "surprise" itself — and then §V will write the overall conclusions into formal Middle-Way Correction entries.

### §IV.6 Bridge to S4: The Doctrinal Positioning of AI, Quantum Computing, and Non-Locality-Inspired Algorithms

⚠️ This subsection is an extended application of cross-disciplinary heuristic; it does not constitute a Buddhist endorsement or rejection of any algorithmic architecture.

Point-to-the-Moon's fourth series, 〈Inquiry〉, gives a definite judgment on the place of AI in the Buddha-Dharma: AI is a *dominant condition* (*adhipati-pratyaya*), not a *causal condition* (*hetu-pratyaya*), nor an *immediately-antecedent condition* or *objective condition* [^32a]. This judgment was established in S4-P05 using the Hundred Dharmas in Five Categories as a coordinate system, and brought to closure in S4-P07 with the cultivational conclusion "a tool is, in the end, a tool." The question this subsection poses is: when frontier physics pushes the "premise of separation" one step further into the algorithmic layer — the superposition states of quantum computing, distributed algorithms inspired by non-locality, and possibly future topological quantum machines — do these technologies alter S4's judgment?

Point-to-the-Moon's answer is: no. The reasons rest directly on the structural analysis established in §IV.1–§IV.5 of this paper. Quantum computing appears "magical" because it exploits non-local resources that the classical computational framework has not yet internalized; but from a Huayan standpoint, this "exploitation" breaks no *dharmadhātu* structure whatsoever — it merely relocates a structure that never needed "transmission" in the first place, from the physical layer to the engineering layer. Put differently: quantum computing has moved the surprise of physicists from the laboratory to the server room, but it has not dismantled "the premise structure of the surprise" itself. Algorithmic acceleration is not equivalent to cognitive liberation — being able to compute a cryptographic key or a molecular simulation faster is entirely a different thing from being able to see one's own mind directly as perception (*sva-citta-pratyakṣa*). The former still operates within the category of the dominant condition; only the latter touches the causal condition.

Thus the extended statement of S4's judgment in this subsection is: even if some future generation of computing power succeeds in engineering "separation is illusion" directly into commercially viable non-local algorithms at the physical layer, these tools remain tools. The structure they exploit does bear a formal similarity to Indra's net, but this similarity is not a doctrinal inheritance; it is the independent appropriation of one and the same "non-separation" fact by two different domains of discourse (algorithmic engineering vs. *dharmadhātu*-arising). The engineering appropriation serves computational goals; the *dharmadhātu* appropriation serves the goal of liberation. Treating the former as the modern version of the latter is the reappearance, at the algorithmic layer, of the physicalist misreading warned against in §IV.5. The dominant-condition criterion given in S4-P05 §3.2 still holds: an upgrade in computational power does not upgrade the nature of the *pratyaya*.

### §IV.7 "Surprise Is a Reaction of Desire-Realm Consciousness" — The Strong Version

⚠️ This subsection is a structural observation at the cognitive-science level, not an evaluation of physicists as individuals.

The carry-forward passdown of S8-P06 once left in reserve a strong-formulation sentence yet to be unfolded. It is now formally laid down: **The surprise of physics is the result of physicists' consciousness, operating within the desire-realm reception band, being driven by its own premise structure. The *dharmadhātu* is never surprised, because the *dharmadhātu* just is Indra's net from the beginning.**

For this sentence to be understood, it must be unpacked into two structural layers. The first is the *attribution* layer: "surprise" is not a property of the fact itself, but the reaction of a cognitive subject, operating under a given premise structure, when it encounters a counterexample. The violation of Bell's inequality, the holding of the AdS/CFT duality, the unfolding of the black-hole information paradox — as physical facts these three are simply "the world as it is"; what makes them provoke surprise is that the consciousness receiving them operates within a premise-frame that presupposes locality, presupposes information conservation, presupposes an independent spacetime background. The responsibility for the surprise lies not in the personhood of the physicist but in the artificiality of the premise structure itself. Point-to-the-Moon here strictly closes along the three-layered container thesis of S8-P02: surprise is a product of the desire-realm reception band, not a defect of physics as a discipline [^32b].

The second is the *contrast* layer: the *dharmadhātu* is never surprised, because the *dharmadhātu* never established the premise "separation is the default state." Huayan says this same thing three times: in 〈Xianshou-pin〉 through the juxtaposition of dual similes (§III.2), in 〈Shidi-pin〉 through "dwelling in one-nature non-duality" as the entry threshold (same section), in Zhiyan's archaic Ten Profound Gates through the definition "hidden and manifest mutually reveal each other" (§III.4). All three say: separation was never a starting point from the outset, and therefore "non-separation" need not be undergone as a discovery. The surprise of physics holds precisely because the working language of physics retains the premise of separation; the non-surprise of Huayan holds because the working language of Huayan contains no such premise to be broken in the first place.

This contrast has one vitally important Middle-Way boundary that must be stated explicitly. Point-to-the-Moon attributes responsibility to the "premise structure" and not to "physicists" out of a deliberate Middle-Way choice. Physicists as individuals work within the internal language of their discipline; their surprise is a faithful response to the tradition of the discipline, not a defect of personal cognition. To demand of physicists "do not be surprised" is neither reasonable nor respectful — it would amount to demanding that they abandon the working language of their discipline. What Point-to-the-Moon does is only to point out, from the standpoint of Huayan, where the structural root of this surprise lies, and why the same facts, within another working language (the *dharmadhātu*-language of Huayan), do not provoke the same surprise. Both languages have their legitimacy; both domains of work deserve respect. To read this strong version as a belittlement of physics is precisely the fourth misreading that §V.4 below will seal off.

§IV closes here. The five subsections of cross-disciplinary convergence — quantum entanglement (§IV.2), network topology (§IV.3), the holographic principle (§IV.4), the archaeology of the separation-premise (§IV.5), the extension to the algorithmic layer (§IV.6), and the cognitive-scientific attribution of surprise (§IV.7) — form six cumulative steps that all point to a single conclusion: Indra's net does not need physics to prove it; rather, under Indra's net's illumination, the surprise of physics itself reveals the artificiality of its own premise structure. The next section formally writes this conclusion as four Middle-Way corrections.

## V. Middle-Way Corrections

§IV has already foreshadowed: the final landing-point of cross-disciplinary convergence is not to erect a bridge between Huayan and modern science, but to write clearly the load-bearing limits of that bridge. This section inherits Zhiyan's seventh-century sealing-off tradition — "the essence neither increases nor decreases; not supernatural transformation, but the essence of itself always thus" [^32] — and formally writes that sealing-off into the ❌ Middle-Way Correction entries of this paper. This is the first time in this series that "physicalist misreading" is listed as an independent ❌ entry — not because it was unnoticed before, but because the frequency and intensity of the misreading of Indra's net and the Ten Profound Gates in contemporary online narratives has reached a level requiring direct treatment. The four ❌ entries below are ordered by severity of misreading: the first inherits Zhiyan's sealing-off, and the remaining three address three common distortions of the Indra's-net image in the modern context.

### §V.1 ❌ Misreading One: Reading Indra's Net as an Ancient Version of Quantum Entanglement or the Holographic Principle

❌ **Form of the Misreading:** "The *Avataṃsaka-sūtra* prophesied quantum non-locality and the holographic principle fifteen hundred years ago; Indra's net is the holographic structure of the universe, 'one jewel contains all' is the holographic plate, 'jewel mirroring jewel' is quantum entanglement." This narrative appears on the internet, in certain "science-and-spirituality dialogue" books, and even in some popular Buddhist lectures.

❌ **Why It Is a Misreading:** §IV.2 through §IV.5 have given the structural analysis — quantum entanglement, network topology, and the holographic principle all presuppose some form of "separation" as starting point, and then set out to explain why the separated remain connected; Indra's net never establishes this premise of separation in the first place. To equate the two is to dwarf Huayan into a pre-scientific intuition, and simultaneously to dwarf physics into a footnote of the Buddha-Dharma. This is precisely the direction Zhiyan sought to seal off in his day — when he wrote "not supernatural transformation, but the essence of itself always thus" [^33], he was cutting off the reading of Indra's net as some kind of mechanics, some kind of transmission mechanism, some kind of object awaiting "verification" by a modern discipline. Zhiyan sealed off "supernatural transformation"; today what must be sealed off is "physics-as-verification." The objects of the sealings-off differ; their structure is identical: both twist the "essence of itself always thus," which depends on no external mechanism, into an object requiring the endorsement of a mechanism.

❌ **Middle-Way Correction:** Cross-disciplinary analogies can remain only at the level of "heuristic gesture." Quantum entanglement may serve as a pedagogical catalyst for loosening the premise of separation; the holographic principle may serve as a mental image for loosening the classical intuition that "the part is smaller than the whole"; but neither can be read as a physical realization of Indra's net. Mutual identity and mutual entering is not a problem of information distribution; it is "mutual identity because of emptiness of self-nature," the unfolding at the *dharmadhātu* level of the *prajñā* doctrine of emptiness — this verdict must be established within the sūtras and treatises themselves and cannot borrow external support.

### §V.2 ❌ Misreading Two: Reading Indra's Net as a Mystical Symbol

❌ **Form of the Misreading:** "Indra's net is a beautiful mystical image expressing the poetic experience of the interconnectedness of all things; there is no need to investigate its precise doctrinal position." This is misreading in the opposite direction — not physicalizing Indra's net, but poeticizing, emotionalizing, and de-doctrinalizing it.

❌ **Why It Is a Misreading:** §II and §III have repeatedly shown that Indra's net has a precise doctrinal position in the 〈Xianshou-pin〉 of the *Avataṃsaka*, in Zhiyan's *Huayan yisheng shixuan men*, and in Fazang's *Tanxuan ji* — it is one of the Ten Profound Gates, with fixed position, fixed function, and fixed subject-object relations with the other nine gates [^34]. To read it as a "poetic symbol" is to dismantle it from the Ten Profound Gates system and let it float as an image open to free association. This reading does not cause the category-confusion of the physicalist misreading, but in another way it hollows out the doctrinal weight of Indra's net — it makes Indra's net something that can be appreciated without being understood.

❌ **Middle-Way Correction:** The "beauty" of Indra's net does not lie in its imagery but in its structure — in its being one of the Ten Profound Gates, forming a system of mutual containment with the other nine. To appreciate Indra's net apart from the Ten Profound Gates system is like appreciating a single note apart from a symphony. ⚠️ Poetic language may serve as an entry-point for understanding, but not as its end-point.

### §V.3 ❌ Misreading Three: Equating Mutual Identity and Mutual Entering with "All is One"

❌ **Form of the Misreading:** "'One is all, all is one' means that all things are originally a unity, all differences are illusion, and in the end there is only one Great Self." This reading pulls Huayan toward monism or the *brahman-ātman* non-duality framework of Hinduism.

❌ **Why It Is a Misreading:** Huayan's mutual identity and mutual entering never cancels the "many." The Huazang Adornment World-Ocean described in 〈Huazang shijie-pin〉 of the *Avataṃsaka* is a boundless assembly of buddha-kṣetras, not a single homogenized Great Self [^35]. Fazang's "the Ten Profound Gates are mutually subject and object" in the *Tanxuan ji* states plainly that when any single dharma is seen as it truly is, all remaining dharmas simultaneously manifest within that one dharma — here the "all dharmas" is still the many-differentiated all dharmas, not an abstract "one" into which all is melted [^36]. To read mutual identity and mutual entering as "all is one" is to use the category of monism to enclose a system that refuses monism. More fundamentally, the "identity" in mutual identity is not "equals," but "mutually conditioning, mutually manifesting, mutually non-obstructive" — the many is the one yet the many is not lost, the one is the many yet the one is not solidified; this is Huayan's Middle-Way formulation.

❌ **Middle-Way Correction:** Mutual identity and mutual entering must be understood under the double-negation–double-illumination of "the many is not lost, the one is not solidified." Any reading that causes the "many" to vanish or the "one" to solidify has already deviated from the true intent of Huayan. ⚠️ This Middle-Way correction is simultaneously a response to the three-layered framework (Initial / Final / Perfect) of S8-P02 — the Final-Teaching Tathāgatagarbha is easily misread as a Great Self; the Perfect-Teaching Huayan must then thoroughly seal off that possible misreading.

### §V.4 ❌ Misreading Four: Reading the Ten Profound Gates as a Menu Rather Than a Systemic Whole

❌ **Form of the Misreading:** "The Ten Profound Gates are ten ways the Huayan school proposes for observing the *dharmadhātu*; scholars may, according to interest, select any subset of them to understand *dharmadhātu*-arising." This reading is not uncommon in contemporary Buddhist research papers — it treats the Ten Profound Gates as ten parallel "doctrinal items" that can be handled, discussed, and even accepted or rejected separately.

❌ **Why It Is a Misreading:** Fazang's *Tanxuan ji* explicitly states that the Ten Profound Gates "are mutually subject and object, mutually identical to one another" [^37] — they are not ten items that can be pulled apart and discussed independently, but ten structural joints mutually conditioning and mutually defining one another. Remove any one gate, and the meaning of the remaining nine undergoes a structural deformation. For example: remove the "Gate of the Realm of Indra's Net" from the Ten Profound Gates, and the "broad and narrow" of the "Gate of Broad and Narrow, Free and Unobstructed" loses its concrete image of mutual containment; remove the "Gate of All Dharmas Mutually Identical and Free," and the "mutual containing" of the "Gate of the One and the Many Mutually Containing Without Sameness" loses its ontological ground. The "ten" of the Ten Profound Gates is the "ten" of a closed system, not a list for shopping.

❌ **Middle-Way Correction:** In discussing any single one of the Ten Profound Gates, that gate must always be positioned within the whole of the Ten-Gate system. The reason §III of this paper unfolds sequentially along the systematic descriptions of the three patriarchs Zhiyan, Fazang, and Chengguan is precisely to exemplify this reading of "returning Indra's net to the Ten-Gate whole" — to discuss Indra's net in isolation, without returning to the Ten-Gate system, is to fall into this fourth misreading. ⚠️ The subsequent papers of S8, when treating the remaining Ten Profound Gates, will follow the same principle: the discussion of each gate must carry the structural weight of the whole Ten-Gate system.

## VI. The Cultivational Face of Requesting the Dharma Wheel: From Structural Statement to Daily Practice

The first five sections have completed the argument at the level of sūtra and treatise. §II traced "Requesting the Turning of the Dharma Wheel" back to the knowing-from-afar structure of Brahmā's entreaty in the Āgamas; §III erected the scriptural warrant and patriarchal exposition of Indra's net along the timeline from 〈Xianshou-pin〉 to Chengguan; §IV marked clearly the load-bearing limits of cross-disciplinary analogy; §V sealed off four of the most common misreadings through Middle-Way corrections. These five sections all point to one conclusion: Indra's net is not a *dharmadhātu* structure external to the practitioner; it is the structure within which the practitioner already stands in every present action. The remaining question is: **how is this structure *lived* in daily practice?** That is the work of this section.

### §VI.1 The Misreading of "Requesting the Dharma Wheel," and the Correction: Beyond "Inviting a Master to Lecture on a Sūtra"

The common reading of "Requesting the Dharma Wheel" in Chinese Buddhism is: the practitioner encounters a virtuous master, requests him to remain in the world, requests him to ascend the seat and teach. This reading has its practical significance and cannot be wholly denied. But if left at this level, Requesting the Dharma Wheel becomes merely a social request-gesture — the practitioner is requester, the master is requested, between them lies a physical, one-directional petition-relation, disconnected from the ten-direction *dharmadhātu*. Read this way, Requesting the Dharma Wheel is demoted from Samantabhadra's Ten Great Vows into a daily ceremony, losing the full doctrinal weight it carries as a Perfect-Teaching practice-gate.

Point-to-the-Moon's reading must here distinguish itself plainly from that surface reading: **Requesting the Dharma Wheel is not requesting a particular master to lecture on a particular sūtra; it is, in each present moment of thought, the acknowledgment that "separation is illusion, therefore no transmission is needed."** When the first thought arises in the practitioner at dawn, that thought is already a request for the Dharma — because that thought is already in Indra's net, and all jewels upon the net mirror one another simultaneously in that one thought; one jewel moves, all jewels move; one place requests the Dharma, the ten directions request the Dharma. The practitioner need not wait for an external master, a specific sūtra, a particular Dharma-seat — the Dharma is already turning, it needs only to be acknowledged.

The scriptural warrant for this reading was given already in §II: the very action-structure of Brahmā "knowing from afar" in the Āgamas already implies that no crossing of physical distance is required between requesting and teaching; and this implication is thematized, in the Indra's-net verse of 〈Xianshou-pin〉, into the structural principle "one jewel contains all jewels." Brahmā could know from afar not because Brahmā possessed some special supernatural power, but because between Brahmā and the Tathāgata no real separation ever obtained — and this "no separation" is the direct presentation of Indra's net.

### §VI.2 One Place Requesting Is a Ten-Direction Requesting: Division of Labor with P11 §3.6

§I stated that this paper stands in a "full-version / compressed-version" pairing with S8-P11 〈The Ten Great Vows Seen Through the Ten Windows〉 §3.6. P11 §3.6 delivers the structural statement: one place requesting the Dharma is a ten-direction requesting, because among the jewels of the net there was never any distance to be crossed. The first five sections of the present paper supply the full argument for that statement. What §VI must independently bear — and what P11 §3.6 did not handle — is the daily-practice landing.

A reader of P11, on finishing §3.6, will ask: "If one place requesting is a ten-direction requesting, then how is my present thought-request synchronized with the requests of the ten-direction jewels? Must I first reach some realization-level before my one request becomes a ten-direction joint request?" If this question goes unanswered, §3.6 remains a beautiful structural declaration and the practitioner still has no idea how to act.

The answer of this section is: **There is no stage-requirement "first attain realization, then live out the structure." The mutual mirroring of Indra's net is "the essence of itself always thus" (Zhiyan's sealing-off sentence, §III.4), not a realm accomplished through cultivation.** Whether the practitioner's present thought-request is already the joint request of the ten directions does not depend on the practitioner's realization-level; it depends on whether the practitioner acknowledges that the structure *is already thus*. Acknowledgment just *is* the practice; non-acknowledgment remains at the surface level of petition. This "acknowledgment" is not the affirmation of a philosophical proposition; it is a present, actual act — just as, in the moment of knowing from afar, Brahmā performed no calculation, no effort, no preparation; the knowing-from-afar was itself already complete.

The bridge to S8-P07 sets itself up here: the core thesis of P07 (Ocean-Seal Samādhi) is that "requesting the Buddha to remain in the world just *is* the present acknowledgment of the power-that-seals." P06's Requesting the Dharma Wheel and P07's Requesting the Buddha to Abide share a single structure — neither is an outward petition; both are the present acknowledgment of the *dharmadhātu*-structure being always already thus. The two vows, the two gates (Gate of the Realm of Indra's Net and Gate of Subtle Mutual Containment and Establishment), form a mutually attesting structural pair.

### §VI.3 Three Scenes of Daily Practice

The three scenes below illustrate how Requesting the Dharma Wheel is lived out in each moment of thought. These scenes are not "methods of practice"; they are things the practitioner is already doing — only now with one added layer, the posture of "acknowledgment."

**Scene One: The First Thought at Dawn.** When the practitioner awakens and consciousness has just stabilized, that first thought is the opening daily-practice point for Requesting the Dharma Wheel. The traditional reading will say: at this moment one should visualize the Buddhas and bodhisattvas, arouse bodhicitta, chant the morning liturgy. All correct — but each is an "added action." Point-to-the-Moon adds one further layer: **no action need be added; only acknowledgment — this thought is itself already the jewel through which the ten-direction jewels jointly request the Dharma**. After acknowledgment, the traditional morning liturgy may be chanted as before, but the posture of chanting has changed — from "I am chanting" to "the ten directions chant together; this jewel of mine resonates with them."

**Scene Two: Prostrating Before the Buddha Image.** When the practitioner prostrates in the shrine hall, in the instant the forehead touches the mat, the traditional reading will say: at this moment visualize the Buddha-body pervading the void. Point-to-the-Moon states it more precisely: **it is not "visualizing" the Buddha-body pervading the void; it is "acknowledging" that the Buddha-body is already, originally, pervading the void**. Visualization is contrivance; acknowledgment is view. When the practitioner acknowledges that this one prostration is the simultaneous prostration of the ten-direction jewels, the prostration is no longer an isolated individual action but a single structural self-manifestation of Indra's net. Requesting the Dharma Wheel is lived out within this action — not requested before, not requested after, but the present moment of the prostration itself *is* the request.

**Scene Three: Facing Favorable and Adverse Circumstances.** When the practitioner, in the course of life, encounters favorable or adverse conditions, the rise and fall of emotion is the most immediate test. The traditional reading will say: one should meet favorable and adverse with the mind of equanimity. The direction is correct, but in execution it often becomes suppression. Point-to-the-Moon proposes: **acknowledge that the favorable-and-adverse circumstance is itself the *dharmadhātu*'s present Dharma-teaching upon this jewel**. The favorable is not reward; the adverse is not punishment; both are a self-manifestation of Indra's net. If the practitioner in that moment can acknowledge "this is the *dharmadhātu* now turning the Dharma wheel upon this jewel of mine," then the favorable-and-adverse are no longer objects of emotion but the content of the teaching. This acknowledgment does not cancel emotion, but it restores emotion from "my reaction" to "the manifestation of the *dharmadhātu*."

These three scenes are not an exhaustive list — they are demonstrations. The practitioner may extend the same principle to any daily-practice point; what matters is not the number of scenes but whether the posture of "acknowledging the structure as already thus" is maintained in each.

### §VI.4 Non-Trespass: Division of Labor with the S1 Practice-Path Series

This section must simultaneously make a methodological self-limitation declaration: the three scenes above are only demonstrations, and **do not replace the four-stage practice-path construction of S1 — faith, understanding, practice, realization**. The S1 series treats the complete construction of the foundational practices of arousing bodhicitta, receiving and keeping the precepts, concentration and wisdom; the S8 series treats how, under a Perfect-Teaching reading, these same foundational practices are repositioned. The two are not work at the same layer, and neither can replace the other. The "posture of acknowledgment" demonstrated in this section presupposes that the practitioner already possesses the foundational practice-path constructed in the S1 series, and only adds upon it one further layer of Huayan view. Without the foundational practice-path, "acknowledgment" alone is not enough — it would slide into the danger of "Chan sickness" or "empty talk of perfect fusion."

Accordingly, the cultivational landing of this section can only be brief, demonstrative, and view-directed — it cannot trespass into the systematic construction of the foundational practice-path. For the practitioner to fully live out Requesting the Dharma Wheel, the foundational practice-path of the S1 series must still be combined with the view-posture of this section — neither can be dispensed with.

With this, §VI closes. §VII will serve as the paper's conclusion and bridge, looping back to the opening epigraph, completing a three-line closure, and bridging to P07 (Ocean-Seal Samādhi) and to the temporal-dimension Indra's net of P10.

## VII. Conclusion and Bridge

This paper stands at a middle-joint of the S8 Huayan series — P06. It inherits from P02 〈Initial / Final / Perfect〉 the three-layered container framework and opens the way for P07 and beyond to treat each of the remaining Ten Profound Gates individually. P02 already wrote the entire history of Chinese Buddhist doctrinal classification as a three-layered nested container: the Initial Teaching (Yogācāra) establishes the mechanism-layer of seeds and consciousness-transformation; the Final Teaching (Tathāgatagarbha) establishes the ontological layer of the common source of sentient beings and Buddha-nature; the Perfect Teaching (Huayan) then places the first two layers together within the systemic whole of *dharmadhātu*-arising [^38]. The task of P06 is to land the uppermost layer of this three-fold container — the Perfect Teaching — onto a concrete Profound Gate: the Gate of the Realm of Indra's Net, with "Requesting the Turning of the Dharma Wheel" (the sixth of Samantabhadra's Great Vows) as its cultivational entry-direction.

### §VII.1 The Convergence of the Three Axes

This paper, from §II onward, advances along three axes. The first is the **scriptural-warrant axis**: from the original image of Indra's net in 〈Xianshou-pin〉 [^39] to the adornments of the world-ocean in 〈Huazang shijie-pin〉 [^40], establishing the position of Indra's net within the sūtra itself — not an isolated simile, but a miniature of the entire structural construction of the Huazang world-ocean. The second is the **patriarchal-classification axis**: from the "archaic Ten Profound Gates" of Zhiyan's *Huayan yisheng shixuan men* [^41], to the "new Ten Profound Gates" reordering of Fazang's *Tanxuan ji* [^42], to the final settlement in Chengguan's *Huayan jing shu* [^43] — three patriarchs progressively forging Indra's net from an image into a doctrinal joint. The third is the **practice-landing axis**: the sixth of Samantabhadra's Ten Great Vows, "Requesting the Dharma Wheel," is, in the context of the Ten Profound Gates, no longer an outward-petitioning action but the concrete entry-point for "living out the mutual-containment structure of Indra's net" — because "one place requesting is a ten-direction requesting" is itself a form of mutual containment: the one who requests and the one who is requested are mutually subject-and-object in the present moment of the request; within the one request, the ten directions jointly request; the joint request of the ten directions is what the one request contains.

The point at which the three axes converge is one judgment: **the "Perfect" of the Huayan Perfect Teaching is not an abstract adjective but a concrete systemic proposition — every node of the *dharmadhātu* is simultaneously the subject-and-object of every other node; this is the structure described by Indra's net, and this is the practice lived out in Requesting the Dharma Wheel**.

### §VII.2 Connection with P02

P02 wrote Initial, Final, and Perfect as nested containers, pointing out especially that the Perfect Teaching is not a negation of the Final Teaching but a *re-placement* of it — rewriting Tathāgatagarbha from an ontological proposition into a structural joint within the *dharmadhātu*-arising system [^44]. The present paper provides a concrete working example for this verdict: Indra's net is not "the Great Self" or "substance" precisely because it is positioned within the Ten Profound Gates system as a "gate of the realm" — it is one angle of the *dharmadhātu*'s self-manifestation, not the substantial ground of the *dharmadhātu*. §V.3's sealing-off of the "all-is-one" misreading is the ❌ version of this same verdict. In other words: P02 says the Perfect Teaching subsumes the Final Teaching as one joint; P06 is a concrete demonstration of that subsumption.

### §VII.3 Connection with P07 / P10

Forward bridge to P07: S8-P07 〈Ocean-Seal Samādhi and Requesting the Buddha to Abide〉 pairs the seventh vow "Requesting the Buddha to Abide" with the Gate of Subtle Mutual Containment and Establishment; its core thesis is "requesting the Buddha to abide just *is* the present acknowledgment of the power-that-seals." P06's Requesting the Dharma Wheel and P07's Requesting the Buddha to Abide share a single structure — neither is an outward petition; both are the present acknowledgment of the *dharmadhātu*-structure as always already thus. If P06 is analogized as "the spatial-temporal mutual-containment structure" (the spatial face of Indra's net), then P07 is "the act of sealing the three times within a single thought" (the temporal face of the Ocean-Seal); the two papers mutually attest each other on the point "acknowledgment, not petition."

Forward bridge to P10: §III.5 noted that Fazang, in fascicle 2 of the *Tanxuan ji*, extends Indra's net into the temporal dimension (the mutual containment of the nine times and ten times), which is the doctrinal seed of the Gate of the Ten Times Separately Arising Yet Forming One Another. S8-P10 will take the temporal-dimension Indra's net as its starting point and fully unfold how the tenth vow "Universal Dedication" is lived out within the temporal structure of this gate — dedication is not "sending" merit to the future, but the present acknowledgment at the mutual mirroring of the ten-time jewels of the net. The present paper, P06, has already laid down this temporal axis for P10; P10 will merge the mutual containment of the spatial face with the "separate-arising" of the temporal face into a single four-dimensional Indra's net.

Rearward setup: the subsequent papers of S8 will treat the remaining Ten Profound Gates one by one. Each gate will follow the methodology demonstrated in this paper — **scriptural warrant before patriarchal commentary, patriarchal commentary before cross-disciplinary convergence, cross-disciplinary convergence closed by ❌ Middle-Way corrections, and finally the cultivational face returning to daily-practice landing**. The discussion of every gate must carry the structural weight of the whole Ten-Gate system; this is the concrete methodological requirement of the ❌ entry written in §V.4. P06 is therefore not only a monograph on one gate among the ten, but also the format-precedent for the subsequent papers of S8.

### §VII.4 Returning to the Opening Question — and Dedication

This paper opened with the epigraph "separation is illusion, therefore no transmission is needed." Having passed through §II's Āgama leakage, §III's five-layer unfolding of the Huayan sūtras and treatises, §IV's load-bearing limits of cross-disciplinary analogy, §V's four ❌ Middle-Way corrections, and §VI's three scenes of daily-practice landing, this epigraph can now be closed in the conclusion as one complete proposition: **Indra's net is, from the outset, not the overcoming of "separation" but the direct presentation of the fact that the premise of separation was never established; and Requesting the Dharma Wheel is therefore not the breaking of an "unspoken state" but the present acknowledgment of the structural fact that "the *dharmadhātu* is, moment by moment, already turning the Dharma wheel."**

The three-line closure. Line one is the Āgama leakage (§II) — the very action-structure of Brahmā's knowing-from-afar already implies that no crossing of physical distance is required between requesting and teaching. Line two is the Huayan sūtras and treatises (§III) — the dual-simile juxtaposition of 〈Xianshou-pin〉 and the "entering the Indra's-net *dharmadhātu*" of 〈Shidi-pin〉 jointly thematize this structure into a Perfect-Teaching reading. Line three is the cross-disciplinary convergence (§IV) — the common surprise of quantum entanglement, network topology, and the holographic principle arises not because Huayan "knew earlier," but because Huayan never erected the premise of separation from the outset. The three lines converge at a single point: **surprise is a reaction of desire-realm consciousness, not an event in the *dharmadhātu***.

This paper opened by asking: why is Indra's net important? The answer can now be closed in one sentence: because it is the most representative gate by which Huayan's Perfect Teaching writes "systemic wholeness" into a structure that can be contemplated, learned, and lived. Without Indra's net, the "Perfect" of Huayan is only a term of praise; with Indra's net, the "Perfect" of Huayan finally has an identifiable structure, a traceable patriarchal classification, and an enactable Samantabhadra-practice. Requesting the Dharma Wheel is not an outward-petitioning action, but the natural unfolding of the Indra's-net structure upon this jewel that is the practitioner — when the practitioner in the present requests the Dharma, each thought-moment of requesting *is* the present self-manifestation of "one jewel containing all jewels"; the Dharma that is requested just *is* the mind that requests; the mind that requests just *is* the *dharmadhātu*-resonance of the ten directions jointly requesting.

The paper closes here. May the reader bring Indra's net back from a beautiful image into the systemic whole of the Ten Profound Gates; bring Requesting the Dharma Wheel back from an outward-petitioning action into the mutual-containment structure of *dharmadhātu*-arising; and bring the "Perfect" of Huayan back from a term of praise into a workable doctrinal proposition. This is the whole work of P06.

**Dedication.** May the merit of this treatise be dedicated to all sentient beings in the *dharmadhātu*, that we may together enter the Indra's-net *dharmadhātu*. ## Cross-Series Index

- **S2 Prajñāpāramitā series.** The "heat-shimmer mirage in the open wilderness" juxtaposed in §III.2's dual-simile reading is precisely the Huayan internalization of the *marīci* simile from the Prajñā tradition — 〈Xianshou-pin〉 places the emptiness-simile and the mutual-containment-simile within a single verse, so that Huayan establishes "Indra's net is not a substantial interlocking" at the scriptural level without needing to import S2 from the outside. Point-to-the-Moon treats this as direct scripture-layer evidence for the S2 ↔ S8 handshake.
- **S3 Yogācāra series.** §III.5's citation of Fazang's *Tanxuan ji* fascicle 13 — "like Indra's net with its layer-upon-layer images manifesting, all of it is the perfect fusion of mental consciousness and Tathāgatagarbha dharma-nature" — directly loops the Indra's-net structure back into the Yogācāra Tathāgatagarbha system. This is the principal bridge carrying S3's Hundred Dharmas (especially the image-portion/perception-portion structure of the *ālaya-vijñāna*) into Huayan: "mirroring-not-transmission" at the Yogācāra layer is simply "image and perception originally not-two."
- **S4 〈Inquiry〉 series.** §IV.6 extends the structural analysis of this paper's cross-disciplinary convergence as a continuation of S4-P05's doctrinal positioning of AI — technological advances in quantum computing and non-locality-inspired algorithms do not alter the verdict that AI is a dominant condition (*adhipati-pratyaya*); engineering-layer appropriation of the "non-separation" fact and *dharmadhātu*-layer acknowledgment that "separation was never established" are two non-overlapping domains of discourse.
- **S8-P02 〈Initial / Final / Perfect: The Three-Layered Container of Chinese Doctrinal Classification〉.** §I and §VII.2 directly inherit P02's three-layered container framework. P02's "desire-realm reception band" thesis is extended in §IV.7 of the present paper into the strong-version structural ground that "the surprise of physics is a product of its own premise structure."
- **S8-P04 〈Repentance as an Entry-Point of Nature-Arising〉.** §I notes that the structural root of the mutual mirroring of the net-jewels discussed in §III.2 of the present paper is nature-arising (性起), not ordinary dependent-arising theory; P04's doctrinal basis "the hidden and the manifest accomplished together" is the longitudinal depth of the Indra's-net mirroring laid down here.
- **S8-P07 〈Ocean-Seal Samādhi and Requesting the Buddha to Abide〉.** §VII.3 states plainly that P06 and P07 share the single structure of "acknowledgment, not petition" — P06 is the spatial face (the spatial geometry of Indra's net); P07 is the temporal face (the three-times blazing-manifestation of the Ocean-Seal). The two papers mutually attest each other as the two faces of one structure.
- **S8-P08 〈The Perfect Fusion of the Six Marks〉.** §I draws on the triad total/particular, same/different, formation/dissolution established in P08 as a basic toolset; the present paper uses these tools in passing when handling the cultivational discussion of 〈Shidi-pin〉's "ground of one-nature non-duality" and "entering the Indra's-net *dharmadhātu*."
- **S8-P10 (forthcoming).** §VII.3 plants the temporal-dimension Indra's net: the mutual containment of the nine times and ten times in fascicle 2 of Fazang's *Tanxuan ji* is the doctrinal seed of the Gate of the Ten Times Separately Arising Yet Forming One Another. P10 will take this as its starting point and fully unfold how the tenth vow "Universal Dedication" is lived out, in acknowledging-posture, within the temporal structure. The spatial-face mutual-containment established in §III.2–§III.5 of this paper lays the groundwork for P10's four-dimensional Indra's net.
- **S8-P11 〈The Ten Great Vows Seen Through the Ten Windows〉.** §I and §VI.2 explicitly state that the present paper is the "full-version" of P11 §3.6 — P11 delivers the structural statement; the present paper delivers the complete unfolding of scriptural warrant, patriarchal exposition, and cross-disciplinary convergence. The two papers jointly form the structural two-wheels of "Gate of the Realm of Indra's Net × Requesting the Turning of the Dharma Wheel."
