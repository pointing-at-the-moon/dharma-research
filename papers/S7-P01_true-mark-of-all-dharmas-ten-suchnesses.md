## Abstract

Every paper in the Pointing-at-the-Moon research series closes with the phrase *zhūfǎ shíxiàng, běnlái rúshì*—"the true mark of all dharmas, inherently so." This phrase originates from the Lotus Sūtra (*Saddharmapuṇḍarīka Sūtra*, T0262), Chapter 2 (Skillful Means), where the Buddha declares that "only a Buddha together with a Buddha can exhaustively fathom the true mark of all dharmas—namely, their such-like marks, such-like natures, such-like substances, such-like powers, such-like functions, such-like causes, such-like conditions, such-like effects, such-like retributions, and such-like ultimate equality of beginning and end." These are the *ten suchnesses* (十如是, *shí-rúshì*) upon which generations of East Asian commentators have built their doctrinal systems. This paper, opening the twelve-paper S7 series on the Lotus Sūtra, argues that the interpretive history of this single passage discloses three distinct registers across the Indian-to-Chinese reception lineage: Vasubandhu's *devotional register* of "dharma-body equality" (*fǎshēn píngděng*) in the *Saddharmapuṇḍarīkopadeśa* (T1519); Jizang's *ontological register* of "the unchanging substance of the tathāgatagarbha dharma-body" (*rúláizàng fǎshēn zhī tǐ bùbiàn*), which explicitly cites the *Upadeśa* in the *Lotus Commentary* (T1721); and Kuiji's *analytical register* of the trisvabhāva mapping onto the one vehicle in the *Profound Commentary on the Lotus* (T1723). The three are not competing readings but complementary disclosures of the same reality, sharing the dependent nature (*paratantra-svabhāva*, 依他起性) as their common pivot. The paper traces this lineage through both primary sūtra sources and major commentaries, including an unexpected finding concerning Vasubandhu's systematic avoidance of trisvabhāva language in the *Upadeśa*, and establishes the doctrinal foundation for the entire S7 series.

**Keywords:** true mark of all dharmas (*zhūfǎ shíxiàng*), ten suchnesses (*shí-rúshì*), one vehicle (*ekayāna*), three natures (*trisvabhāva*), dharma-body equality, tathāgatagarbha, Lotus Sūtra interpretation

## 1. The Question

From its first paper, the Pointing-at-the-Moon research series has closed every installment with the same phrase: *zhūfǎ shíxiàng, běnlái rúshì*—"the true mark of all dharmas, inherently so." These eight characters have traveled with the project through S1 (Faith), S2 (Understanding), S3 (Practice), S4 (Questioning), S5 (Cutting), and S6 (Illumination). Every paper, regardless of topic, returns to this closing signature.

But ask a long-time Buddhist reader: *Where does this phrase come from? Which sūtra, which chapter?* Many cannot answer—not because they never learned, but because the phrase is so ubiquitous (on hand-copied manuscripts, on Dharma-hall calligraphy, in teachers' casual speech) that it has become invisible, like the air. This paper stops to ask the question.

The most dignified source of *zhūfǎ shíxiàng* is the Lotus Sūtra, Chapter 2 (Skillful Means, *Upāya-kauśalya-parivarta*). After emerging from *samādhi*, Śākyamuni addresses Śāriputra: "The wisdom of the Buddhas is profound and measureless; the gate of that wisdom is difficult to understand and difficult to enter. None of the śrāvakas or pratyekabuddhas can know it." After a threefold refusal-and-request exchange, he finally declares:

> "Stop, Śāriputra, there is no need to say more. Why? Because what the Buddha has accomplished is the foremost, rarest, most difficult to understand of all dharmas. Only a Buddha together with a Buddha can exhaustively fathom the true mark of all dharmas—namely, their such-like marks, such-like natures, such-like substances, such-like powers, such-like functions, such-like causes, such-like conditions, such-like effects, such-like retributions, and such-like ultimate equality of beginning and end."[^1]

These are the *ten suchnesses* (*shí-rúshì*, 十如是) that generations of East Asian commentators have parsed, debated, and systematized. Ten parallel phrases, each beginning with *rúshì* ("such-like"), mirror the same object from ten angles. The object is the *true mark of all dharmas* (*zhūfǎ shíxiàng*).

This paper, opening the twelve-paper S7 series on the Lotus Sūtra, sets this passage as the doctrinal keystone for everything that follows. S7 within the Pointing-at-the-Moon eight-series architecture serves the function of "gathering" (*shè*, 攝)—drawing back into the one Buddha-vehicle (*ekayāna*) everything that S1 through S6 constructed, questioned, dismantled, and verified. Such gathering presupposes a clear view of what the Lotus Sūtra's "true mark" actually is. Without this foundation, the remaining eleven papers would stand on sand.

The paper advances three propositions:

**Proposition 1**: The interpretive history of the Lotus Sūtra's "true mark of all dharmas" discloses three distinct registers across the Indian-to-Chinese lineage: Vasubandhu's *devotional register* of dharma-body equality, Jizang's *ontological register* of tathāgatagarbha dharma-body (citing Vasubandhu), and Kuiji's *analytical register* of trisvabhāva mapped onto the one vehicle. These three are not competing readings but complementary disclosures of the same reality. This is the series's primary scholarly contribution.

**Proposition 2**: The Lotus Sūtra's "opening the provisional to reveal the real" (*kāi quán xiǎn shí*) and the *Avataṃsaka Sūtra*'s "mutual non-obstruction of phenomena upon phenomena" (*shì-shì wú'ài*) constitute a gathering-revealing dual (*shè-xiǎn duì'ǒu*)—the Lotus as a star radiating outward, the Avataṃsaka as a black hole collapsing inward. The same true mark is described in forms sentient beings can receive (the ten suchnesses: ten concrete facets) in the Lotus, and presented in its own native density ("one is all, all is one") in the Avataṃsaka. This paper only previews Proposition 2; its full development belongs to S7-P12.

**Proposition 3** (auxiliary): The seven parables of the Lotus Sūtra are not Mahāyāna inventions *ex nihilo* but the dramatic completion of seeds already present in the Āgama literature. This present paper focuses on Proposition 1; the systematic Āgama-seed work begins with S7-P03 (the parable of the burning house) and unfolds paper by paper.

> **【Retrospective Note from S7-P08 · Upgrade of Proposition 1】**: The three-register lineage established in this paper is structurally upgraded in S7-P08 "Non-Self-Existent Prophecy Displayed at Both Ends" §6.2. Re-reading the three registers — Vasubandhu's dharma-body equality (devotional register) / Jizang's tathāgatagarbha (ontological register) / Kuiji's trisvabhāva analysis (analytical register) — the first two (Vasubandhu's "dharma-body equality" plus Jizang's "body of the tathāgatagarbha dharma-body") in fact constitute the ***tathāgatagarbha* track**, while the third (Kuiji's *ālayavijñāna*-based three-nature system) constitutes the **Yogācāra track**. Paratantra as the common pivot is precisely the first convergence point of the two tracks in the history of Lotus exegesis. Proposition 1 is therefore formally upgraded: **the Lotus is the first confluence of the *tathāgatagarbha* track and the Yogācāra track**. The *tathāgatagarbha* track (dharma-body equality + *Mahāparinirvāṇa* T0374 + Lotus's fundamental-gate) carries the metaphysical basis of dharma-body equality, primordial buddhahood, and directional reversal; the Yogācāra track (Vasubandhu's six prophecy-types + Kuiji's seed doctrine) carries the pragmatic mechanism of seed-perfuming, gradual manifestation, and prophecy-as-statement. The full display of this dual-track confluence occurs in the Devadatta Chapter (see S7-P08 §3, §6). This paper's three-register lineage is the early articulation of the dual-track structure in the "one great cause" passage of *Skillful Means*; S7-P08 completes the dual-track confluence argument on the two wings of the Devadatta Chapter.

Before entering the argument, one unexpected finding deserves preliminary announcement. This research originally expected that Vasubandhu's *Saddharmapuṇḍarīkopadeśa* (T1519) would interpret the ten suchnesses through trisvabhāva language—after all, Vasubandhu is among the founding figures of Yogācāra. Systematic string searches across the full *Upadeśa*, however, returned zero hits for all core trisvabhāva terms: *zhūfǎ shíxiàng*, *rúshì xiàng*, *sān xìng* (three natures), *biànjì* (parikalpita), *yītā* (paratantra), *yuánchéng* (pariniṣpanna). The word "seed" (種子) appears only once, in a parable context referring to wholesome-root seeds in a non-technical sense.[^9] This is not an oversight—Vasubandhu's interpretive framework in the *Upadeśa* is demonstrably something else: *dharma-body equality*, *correction of seven inverted graspings through seven parables*, and *three equalities* (of vehicle, of saṃsāra-nirvāṇa, of body). The question becomes: *why does the founding figure of Yogācāra systematically avoid trisvabhāva language when commenting on the Lotus?* The three-register lineage proposed in this paper is the author's answer.

## 2. Textual Foundations

### 2.1 Primary Sūtra Sources

#### 2.1.1 The Lotus Sūtra (T0262) — Core Passage

The core passage of Kumārajīva's translation (406 CE) reads:

> "Stop, Śāriputra, there is no need to say more. Why? Because what the Buddha has accomplished is the foremost, rarest, most difficult to understand of all dharmas. Only a Buddha together with a Buddha can exhaustively fathom the true mark of all dharmas—namely, their such-like marks, such-like natures, such-like substances, such-like powers, such-like functions, such-like causes, such-like conditions, such-like effects, such-like retributions, and such-like ultimate equality of beginning and end."[^1]

The subsequent verse section reiterates: "Only I know this mark; the Buddhas of the ten directions likewise."[^2] Structurally, this passage sits at the doctrinal center of Chapter 2, which itself stands at the center of the "trace gate" (*jìmén*, Chapters 1–14) of the Lotus Sūtra according to the standard Tiantai division. It is followed by a series of parables (Chapters 3–7) and leads eventually to the "origin gate" (*běnmén*, Chapters 15–28) opened by the Sūtra's revelation of Śākyamuni's primordial enlightenment. From the viewpoint of textual structure, the ten-suchnesses passage is the anchor of the Lotus Sūtra's entire doctrinal edifice.

#### 2.1.2 The Zhèng-fǎhuá Jīng (T0263) — Philological Comparison

Dharmarakṣa's earlier translation, the *Zhèng-fǎhuá jīng* (286 CE), predates Kumārajīva by 120 years. The corresponding passage reads:

> "What the Sage has taught is unprecedented and loftily immeasurable. The Tathāgata fully understands the origins of all dharmas, whence they come, their natural being, the distinctions of their appearances and the roots of their marks, knowing the naturalness of dharmas."[^3]

Two differences merit notice. First, Dharmarakṣa's translation contains **no phrase equivalent to "the true mark of all dharmas"** (*zhūfǎ shíxiàng*); instead it has "the origins of all dharmas" (*zhūfǎ suǒyóu*) and "the naturalness of dharmas" (*fǎ zìrán*). *Suǒyóu* is a more neutral term pointing to the sources or dependent origination of phenomena, while *shíxiàng* ("true mark") carries ontological weight. Second, Dharmarakṣa's version contains **no parallel ten-fold structure** corresponding to the ten suchnesses. The ten parallel *rúshì* phrases are a distinctive feature of Kumārajīva's translation.

This does not imply that the Sanskrit *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka* lacks the relevant material—Sanskrit witnesses in fact have corresponding descriptive language at this juncture. Rather, the two translators made different renderings of the same Sanskrit passage. The scholarly consequence is significant: **the ten suchnesses as a crystalline doctrinal category is a product of Kumārajīva's translation practice**. Zhiyi's (智顗) later "three-turn reading" and the famous *yīniàn sānqiān* ("three thousand worlds in one instant of thought") doctrine of Tiantai Buddhism both rest upon Kumārajīva's specific rendering. This observation does not diminish Kumārajīva—his translation has clear Sanskrit warrant and offers greater pedagogical clarity—but it does remind us that the doctrinal structure of the East Asian Lotus tradition is partly a product of translation history.

#### 2.1.3 The Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra (T0676) on Trisvabhāva

The *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra*, in Chapter 6 (*Lakṣaṇa-parivarta*, "Chapter on Characteristics of All Dharmas"), provides the most authoritative sūtra-level definition of the three natures:

> "The characteristics of all dharmas are briefly of three kinds. What three? First, the imagined characteristic (*parikalpita-lakṣaṇa*); second, the dependent characteristic (*paratantra-lakṣaṇa*); third, the perfected characteristic (*pariniṣpanna-lakṣaṇa*). What is the imagined characteristic? It is that which consists in the conventional designation of own-being and differences in all dharmas, down to the conditions that give rise to verbal expression. What is the dependent characteristic? It is the arising of dharmas through conditions—this being present, that arises; this arising, that arises—from ignorance as condition for volitional formations, up to the arising of the entire aggregate of suffering. What is the perfected characteristic? It is the equality suchness (*samatā tathatā*) of all dharmas."[^4]

This passage provides the fundamental sūtra-level warrant for Proposition 1. Trisvabhāva is not a commentarial fabrication but a doctrinal framework taught directly by the Buddha. The three-register lineage argued below—especially Kuiji's analytical register—rests ultimately on this textual foundation.

#### 2.1.4 The Avataṃsaka Sūtra on the Single True Mark

To foreshadow Proposition 2, consider a passage from the eighty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka Sūtra*:

> "Only in order to know the single true mark of all Buddha-dharmas does one practice vigorous effort."[^5]

Equally relevant is the Avataṃsaka's characteristic statement of temporal mutual entry: "Entering all eons is not-an-eon; entering non-eons is all eons; entering all eons is a single thought-moment."[^6] The contrast is stark. The Lotus Sūtra presents the true mark of all dharmas through *ten parallel phrases*—a **radiative** description that unfolds multiple facets of reality for the hearer's reception. The Avataṃsaka presents the same reality through mutual entry—a **collapsive** description that compresses all facets back into a single density center. Both are truthful presentations of reality, but their pedagogical postures are entirely different. This contrast will be developed in full in S7-P12.

### 2.2 Commentarial Sources

#### 2.2.1 Vasubandhu's Saddharmapuṇḍarīkopadeśa (T1519)

Vasubandhu's *Upadeśa* on the Lotus Sūtra is a rare instance of Indian Yogācāra commentary on the Lotus. Its core framework orbits around the equality meaning of the one vehicle:

> "Innumerable vehicles are only the one Buddha-vehicle, because there is no second vehicle.… As for the one Buddha-vehicle, it is spoken of by reference to four meanings.… As for its substance as a dharma, this is because it has only one-vehicle-substance. The substance of the one vehicle is the *equal dharma-body of all Tathāgatas*."[^7]

"The substance of the one vehicle is the equal dharma-body of all Tathāgatas." This is Vasubandhu's most essential definition of the one Buddha-vehicle. The one vehicle is not pedagogical unification on the surface level; it is the fact that **the dharma-bodies of all Tathāgatas are intrinsically a single equal body without differentiation**. What all sentient beings will eventually realize is precisely this equal dharma-body.

Vasubandhu elaborates further through the notion of "seed-unsurpassability":

> "First, to manifest the unsurpassability of seeds, the rain parable is spoken. 'What you practice is the bodhisattva path' means: for one who had aroused bodhicitta, then regressed, and has now aroused it again, the roots of goodness previously cultivated do not perish, but together form the subsequent fruit."[^8]

This passage carries two significant implications. First, Vasubandhu uses "seed" in his exposition of the rain parable (the parable of the medicinal herbs, *auṣadhī-parivarta*)—but not in the technical Yogācāra sense of *ālayavijñāna*-seeds. It is the everyday meaning of "seeds of wholesome roots." Second, the one who regresses and then re-arouses bodhicitta finds that "the roots of goodness previously cultivated do not perish"—a strong guarantee that no cultivation is ever wasted, which is also one of the theoretical foundations of S7-P09 on the Never-Disparaging Bodhisattva.

**Crucial finding**: A complete string search of T1519 returns zero hits for the core trisvabhāva terminology: *zhūfǎ shíxiàng*, *rúshì xiàng*, *sān xìng*, *biànjì*, *yītā*, *yuánchéng*.[^9] Vasubandhu's interpretive framework for the Lotus is "dharma-body equality + seven inverted graspings corrected by seven parables + three equalities," fundamentally different from the framework he employs in the *Mahāyānasaṃgraha-bhāṣya*, the *Triṃśikā*, and his other trisvabhāva-centered Yogācāra writings. This is not oversight but a deliberate *register selection*—the reason for which the next section addresses directly.

#### 2.2.2 Jizang's Citation of the Upadeśa in the Lotus Commentary (T1721)

This is the unexpected discovery of the present research. Jizang (吉藏, 549–623 CE), the Sanlun (Madhyamaka) master, cites Vasubandhu's *Upadeśa* in his *Lotus Commentary* (*Fǎhuá yìshū*, T1721), fascicle 3, to define the true mark of all dharmas:

> "As for 'the true mark of all dharmas' (*zhūfǎ shíxiàng*): the *Upadeśa on the Dharma Flower* states that it means 'the unchanging substance of the tathāgatagarbha dharma-body' (*rúláizàng fǎshēn zhī tǐ bùbiàn*). Therefore it is also called the true mark and also called Buddha-nature. 'Correct dharma' is another name for correct contemplation."[^10]

Jizang explains *zhūfǎ shíxiàng* as "the unchanging substance of the tathāgatagarbha dharma-body." Whether this definition is a verbatim quotation from Vasubandhu's *Upadeśa* itself is uncertain: direct searches of T1519 and T1520 do not return "tathāgatagarbha" (*rúláizàng*) as a core term. Yet Jizang explicitly attributes the definition to the *Upadeśa*.

Whether this is direct citation or interpretive paraphrase, it constitutes a precious witness to the **Chinese reception history** of Vasubandhu's framework. By the Sui dynasty (late 6th to early 7th century), Vasubandhu's "dharma-body equality" framework was being read by Chinese commentators through a tathāgatagarbha lens. The shift from "equal dharma-body" to "the unchanging substance of the tathāgatagarbha dharma-body" introduces two elements not prominent in Vasubandhu's original: *tathāgatagarbha* and *unchangingness*. These add significantly stronger ontological weight to what in Vasubandhu is a relational claim about the non-differentiation of all Buddhas' dharma-bodies.

Jizang also offers his own distinctive four-stage dialectic of provisional and real:

> "First, the three [vehicles] are provisional and the one is real, in order to make beings abandon the three and enter the one vehicle. Second, both the three and the one are provisional; neither-three-nor-one is called real. Third, duality and non-duality are both provisional; neither-duality-nor-non-duality is real. Fourth, these four options still fail to escape verbal designation, and so are all called provisional; 'the quiescent mark of all dharmas which cannot be proclaimed in words'—this alone is real."[^11]

Each stage re-classifies the previous stage's "real" as yet another provisional, eventually arriving at the wordless quiescent mark of all dharmas as the sole unconditioned real. This is a paradigmatic Madhyamaka gesture: employing recursive linguistic negation to approach what language cannot reach. The present paper will argue in §3 that Jizang's four-stage dialectic, Vasubandhu's equal-dharma-body framework, and Kuiji's trisvabhāva mapping are all engaged with the same "true mark of all dharmas" but operate in three different registers.

#### 2.2.3 Kuiji's Trisvabhāva Mapping in the Profound Commentary (T1723)

The Tang-dynasty Faxiang master Kuiji (窺基, 632–682 CE), in fascicle 4b of the *Profound Commentary on the Lotus* (*Fǎhuá xuánzàn*), provides an explicit trisvabhāva analysis of the one vehicle:

> "Now this dependent nature is bodhi, and the perfected nature is nirvāṇa; together they form the one vehicle. Because the perfected nature is superior, it alone is called the one vehicle. By severing the contaminated dependent nature and expelling the imagined grasping, one realizes these two fruits. By understanding the dependent and the imagined … [this is] skillful means. Now all three are spoken together; therefore it is called real. The meaning of the three natures is treated elsewhere."[^12]

This passage is the central Chinese reference for Proposition 1. Kuiji unambiguously maps the one vehicle onto the trisvabhāva structure:

- **Dependent nature** (*paratantra*) = bodhi (the path of cultivation)
- **Perfected nature** (*pariniṣpanna*) = nirvāṇa (the ultimate fruit)
- **Expulsion of imagined grasping** (*parikalpita*) = condition of liberation
- **The one vehicle** = "all three spoken together, therefore called real"

"All three spoken together" is the crucial phrase. The one vehicle is not constituted by any single nature alone—not by the dependent, not by the perfected, not by the imagined—but by the *simultaneous and integral articulation of all three*. This is Kuiji's precise Yogācāra definition of the one Buddha-vehicle.

Between Vasubandhu and Kuiji lie several centuries and a cross-cultural translation. Vasubandhu speaks of "equal dharma-body"; Kuiji dissects this equal dharma-body into the structured practice of "expelling the imagined upon the dependent to realize the perfected." The two are not contradictory—but neither are they identical. §3 argues that they are the same reality expressed in different registers.

#### 2.2.4 Zhiyi's Three-Turn Reading of the Ten Suchnesses (T1716)

The Tiantai founder Zhiyi (智顗, 538–597 CE), in the *Profound Meaning of the Lotus Sūtra* (*Fǎhuá xuányì*), offers a celebrated "three-turn reading" (*sān zhuǎn dú*) of the ten suchnesses:

> "The Tiantai Master said: 'Reading according to meaning, there are three turns in all. One: "is-mark-thus, is-nature-thus," and so on through "is-retribution-thus." Two: "thus-mark, thus-nature," and so on through "thus-retribution." Three: "mark-thus-is, nature-thus-is," and so on through "retribution-thus-is." If all are read as "thus," the name "thus" is not different—this is the meaning of emptiness. If read as "thus-mark, thus-nature," then applying emptiness to marks and natures, nominal designation lays them out differently—this is the meaning of the provisional. If read as "mark-thus-is," "thus" refers to the "is" of the middle-way true mark—this is the meaning of the middle.'"[^13]

Zhiyi proposes that the same ten-suchnesses phrase admits three distinct grammatical reorganizations in the Chinese, each corresponding to one of the three foundational Tiantai contemplations: emptiness (*kōng*), the provisional (*jiǎ*), and the middle (*zhōng*). This is not a literary game but a deliberate exegetical device—mapping the sūtra's linguistic surface onto the threefold contemplation at the heart of Tiantai practice. It constitutes another *analytical register*, distinct from Yogācāra's trisvabhāva: its tools are Madhyamaka's three truths rather than Yogācāra's three natures. §3.6 below argues that Zhiyi's three-turn reading and Kuiji's trisvabhāva mapping are complementary rather than competitive grids imposed upon the same doctrinal passage.

#### 2.2.5 Dharmapāla's Cheng Weishi Lun on the Dependent as Pivot (T1585)

The *Cheng Weishi Lun* (*Vijñaptimātratā-siddhi*, T1585), compiled by Xuanzang from Dharmapāla and other Indian commentators on the *Triṃśikā*, provides in fascicle 9 a decisive theoretical grounding for the dependent nature's pivotal role:

> "'Support' (*āśraya*) means that which supports—namely, the dependent nature, since it serves as the support for both defiled and purified dharmas. The defiled is the illusory imagined grasping; the purified is the true perfected nature. 'Turning' (*parāvṛtti*) means the two aspects: turning-away and turning-toward. Through repeated cultivation of non-discriminating wisdom, cutting off the coarse heaviness of the twofold obstruction in the root consciousness, one is able to *turn away from the imagined grasping upon the dependent nature, and turn toward the perfected nature within the dependent nature*."[^14]

The structural clarity of this passage is remarkable:

- Both defiled and purified dharmas take the **dependent nature as their support**.
- Cultivation does not eliminate the dependent nature; rather, one *turns away from* the imagined grasping on the dependent nature, and *turns toward* the perfected nature within the dependent nature.

The dependent nature thus emerges as the **dynamic center** of the entire trisvabhāva structure. It is intrinsically neutral, dependently arisen, and open to either the defiled or the pure reading—what is attached to it decides whether it manifests as defiled or purified dharma.

This provides the śāstric foundation for a key claim advanced by the present paper for the S7 series: **the parables (*upāya*) of the Lotus Sūtra belong to the dependent nature as pedagogical manifestations**. The parable of the burning house, the prodigal son, the medicinal herbs, the phantom city—these parables have no intrinsic truth-value question. They are dependently-arisen teachings that the Buddha manifests for pedagogical purposes. The question lies with the receiver:

- If the receiver grasps the parable as substantially real (believing there is literally a burning house, literally three carts), this is **imagined grasping**.
- If the receiver understands the parable as a dependently-arisen pedagogical medium (comprehending it as the Buddha's skillful arrangement), this constitutes **turning toward the perfected nature**.

This structural principle forms the methodological backbone for the five parable papers S7-P03 through S7-P07, each of which will return to this pivot: the parable is dependent nature; the receiver's mode of reception determines whether it functions as defiled or purified dharma.

## 3. Core Argument

### 3.1 The Ten Suchnesses as a Ten-Facet Analysis of the Dependent Nature

Begin with the text itself. The ten items of the ten suchnesses are:

| # | Term | Referent |
|---|------|----------|
| 1 | such-like mark (*rúshì xiàng*) | outwardly visible appearance |
| 2 | such-like nature (*rúshì xìng*) | intrinsic nature, disposition |
| 3 | such-like substance (*rúshì tǐ*) | essential being |
| 4 | such-like power (*rúshì lì*) | potential capacity |
| 5 | such-like function (*rúshì zuò*) | actual manifested activity |
| 6 | such-like cause (*rúshì yīn*) | primary cause |
| 7 | such-like condition (*rúshì yuán*) | supporting condition |
| 8 | such-like effect (*rúshì guǒ*) | immediate result |
| 9 | such-like retribution (*rúshì bào*) | karmic maturation |
| 10 | such-like ultimate equality of beginning and end (*rúshì běn-mò jiūjìng děng*) | totality of the foregoing as one |

Structurally, these ten items are not arbitrary. The first three (mark, nature, substance) address the *intrinsic characteristics* of a phenomenon—outer, inner, and essential. Items four and five (power, function) address *dynamics*—potency and actualization. Items six through nine (cause, condition, effect, retribution) address the *causal structure*—primary cause, supporting condition, immediate fruit, and karmic maturation. The tenth item is a *totality statement*—from beginning to end, all of the above form one equal continuum.

What is this? It is a *complete phenomenological articulation* of a phenomenon. If one asks: what dimensions must be analyzed to describe a dependently-arisen phenomenon (any dharma) completely? The ten suchnesses offer a precise answer: mark, nature, substance, power, function, cause, condition, effect, retribution, and the equality of their beginning and end. Neither too many nor too few.

From the Yogācāra standpoint, this is precisely the *dependent nature* (*paratantra-svabhāva*) described along ten facets. The dependent nature is defined as "all dharmas whose own-being is conditioned arising,"[^4] encompassing everything that arises through causes and conditions. A complete description of any dependently-arisen dharma must simultaneously address its appearance, nature, essence, potential, function, primary cause, supporting condition, immediate effect, karmic maturation, and overall identity from beginning to end. These ten, no fewer, no more.

This is the paper's first claim: **the ten suchnesses constitute a ten-facet analysis of the dependent nature**. Not that the Lotus Sūtra treats the dependent nature as ten different things, but that the ten items together form a paradigmatic schema for the complete description of a conditioned phenomenon.

### 3.2 Layer One: Vasubandhu's Devotional Register of Dharma-Body Equality

Return now to the unexpected finding. Why does Vasubandhu, founder of systematic Yogācāra, decline to use trisvabhāva language in the *Upadeśa*?

A common explanation is chronological: that the *Upadeśa* predates the full maturation of trisvabhāva theory. This explanation fails: Vasubandhu's *Mahāyānasaṃgraha-bhāṣya*, *Madhyānta-vibhāga-bhāṣya*, and other works that explicitly employ trisvabhāva are roughly contemporaneous with the *Upadeśa*. Temporal sequence is not the explanation.

The deeper explanation is that **Vasubandhu selected a register appropriate to the Lotus Sūtra**. The Lotus's pedagogical posture is gathering: drawing all sentient beings down and in—this is the S7 series' central theme of *shè* (攝, "gathering"). The most effective register for such gathering is the *devotional register*: emphasizing the equal dharma-body of all Tathāgatas, emphasizing that all sentient beings will realize this equal dharma-body, emphasizing that there are no second or third vehicles but only the one Buddha-vehicle. This is the language of faith and feeling, not of cool conceptual analysis.

Vasubandhu's formulation "the substance of the one vehicle is the equal dharma-body of all Tathāgatas"[^7] is precisely the core statement of this devotional register. He did not miss the trisvabhāva—he selected a different register for the task at hand. Contrast: when processing Yogācāra doctrine in the *Mahāyānasaṃgraha-bhāṣya*, Vasubandhu uses trisvabhāva as his core instrument; when processing the Lotus in the *Upadeśa*, he uses equal dharma-body as his core instrument. **The same Vasubandhu, two registers.**

This offers an important methodological insight: *the doctrinal content of a sūtra may be expressed in multiple registers, each serving different pedagogical purposes*. The devotional register serves to draw in all beings and to awaken faith; the analytical register serves to provide detailed cultivation guidance and to forestall misinterpretation. Both are necessary.

The Lotus Sūtra's own register is devotional—it deploys extensive parables, miracles, predictions of buddhahood, emanations of light, and earthshakings, all elements that work on the emotional register. As an acute commentator, Vasubandhu responded to this register when commenting on the Lotus. This is *register awareness*.

### 3.3 Layer Two: Jizang's Tathāgatagarbha Mediation

Between Vasubandhu and Kuiji lie several centuries. In this middle zone, Jizang leaves a pivotal trace: citing Vasubandhu's *Upadeśa* in his *Lotus Commentary* to define "the true mark of all dharmas" as "the unchanging substance of the tathāgatagarbha dharma-body."[^10]

This step is more important than it first appears. Vasubandhu said "equal dharma-body"; Jizang extends the formulation to "unchanging substance of the tathāgatagarbha dharma-body," adding two elements:

1. **tathāgatagarbha**: the buddha-nature intrinsic to sentient beings, the latent potential for buddhahood. The term does not figure prominently as a core concept in Vasubandhu's *Upadeśa* itself.
2. **unchangingness** (*bùbiàn*): a strong ontological claim about the stability of the dharma-body's substance.

Together these elements transform "equal dharma-body" from a *devotional proclamation* into an *ontological thesis*. Originally, Vasubandhu's "the equal dharma-body of all Tathāgatas" was a relational claim: all Buddhas share one dharma-body. Jizang's reading advances it to "each sentient being has an unchanging tathāgatagarbha dharma-body substance"—a substantial claim about intrinsic being.

Why did this transition occur in the Sui dynasty? Because the Chinese Buddhist doctrinal climate of the Sui had come to be dominated by tathāgatagarbha thought. The *Awakening of Faith in the Mahāyāna* (*Dàshèng qǐxìn lùn*), the *Śrīmālā-devī Sūtra*, and the *Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra*—these texts together shaped a habit of thinking about buddha-nature as an intrinsic substrate. Jizang, though a Sanlun (Madhyamaka) master, could not entirely escape this ambient influence. When reading Vasubandhu's *Upadeśa*, he naturally reached for tathāgatagarbha vocabulary.

This paper does not argue that the transition is erroneous. On the contrary, the transition reveals an important fact: **the same "true mark of all dharmas" can be discussed in different registers**. Vasubandhu's devotional register is unsuited to addressing questions about sentient beings' intrinsic potential for buddhahood—his frame is outward-facing (the equality of all Buddhas' dharma-bodies). Jizang supplies an inward-facing ontological layer (sentient beings' intrinsic buddha-nature substance) through tathāgatagarbha. These are not contradictory; they are complementary.

### 3.4 Layer Three: Kuiji's Trisvabhāva Completion

Advancing another step from Jizang arrives at Kuiji. Kuiji was Xuanzang's closest disciple and the systematizer of the Faxiang school, possessing the full toolkit of Yogācāra trisvabhāva analysis. In the *Profound Commentary*, fascicle 4b, he performs an operation neither Jizang nor Vasubandhu undertook: **explicitly mapping the one vehicle onto the trisvabhāva structure**.

Kuiji's procedure can be unpacked in four steps:

1. **Dependent nature = bodhi**: the dependent nature is the entirety of dependently-arisen dharmas, including the causal factors of the path. Identifying the dependent nature with bodhi frames the whole path as the unfolding of dependent arising.
2. **Perfected nature = nirvāṇa**: the perfected nature is suchness in its equality aspect. Identifying the perfected nature with nirvāṇa frames ultimate realization as the manifestation of suchness.
3. **Expulsion of imagined grasping**: the essence of cultivation is not the elimination of the dependent nature (since the dependent nature is conditioned arising itself and cannot be eliminated), but the expulsion of the imagined grasping that adheres to the dependent nature.
4. **"All three spoken together, therefore called real"**: the one vehicle is real precisely because it articulates all three natures simultaneously—not one alone.

This is an extraordinarily precise analysis. It transforms the Lotus Sūtra's "one Buddha-vehicle" from a declaration of faith into a structurally decomposable, analytically specifiable mapping onto concrete cultivation. This is the *analytical register*.

One might object: does such analysis not rob the Lotus of its devotional dignity? No—because the analytical register and the devotional register serve different readers. A newcomer to the Lotus Sūtra needs proclamations like "the equal dharma-body of all Tathāgatas" to arouse faith. A practitioner deep in Yogācāra cultivation needs guidance like "expel the imagined upon the dependent in order to realize the perfected" to understand concrete practice. Both are necessary.

### 3.5 The Dependent Nature as Pivot: Dharmapāla's Decisive Argument

Kuiji's trisvabhāva mapping stands on a deeper theoretical foundation within Yogācāra: **the dependent nature is the pivot between defiled and pure**. Dharmapāla, in *Cheng Weishi Lun* fascicle 9, provides the definitive argument:

> "'Support' means that which supports—namely, the dependent nature, since it serves as the support for both defiled and purified dharmas. … Thus one is able to turn away from the imagined grasping upon the dependent nature, and turn toward the perfected nature within the dependent nature."[^14]

The passage's structure is crystalline:

- Both defiled and purified dharmas take the dependent nature as their support.
- Cultivation is not the elimination of the dependent nature, but the transformation *upon* the dependent nature—turning away from the imagined and turning toward the perfected.

This makes the dependent nature the *dynamic center* of the entire trisvabhāva structure. The dependent nature in itself is neutral, dependently arisen, capable of bearing either defiled or purified content. What is attached to it determines whether it functions as defiled or purified dharma.

This provides the doctrinal support for a key claim the present paper advances for S7: **the parables (*upāya*) of the Lotus Sūtra belong to the dependent nature as pedagogical manifestations**. The parables of the burning house, the prodigal son, the medicinal herbs, and the phantom city have no intrinsic truth-or-falsity question. They are dependently-arisen pedagogical manifestations. The question is the receiver's mode of reception:

- Grasping the parable as substantial reality (taking the burning house and three carts to be literally present) = **imagined grasping**.
- Understanding the parable as a dependently-arisen pedagogical medium (comprehending the Buddha's skillful arrangement) = **turning toward the perfected nature**.

This structural principle forms the methodological backbone of S7-P03 through S7-P07.

### 3.6 Tiantai Counterpoint: Zhiyi's Three-Turn Reading

Before presenting the synthesis, one comparative reading requires acknowledgment. Zhiyi's three-turn reading is the most celebrated analytical tool in the Chinese Lotus tradition; no serious research on the Lotus can bypass it.

Zhiyi's three-turn reading redeploys the same ten-suchnesses passage through three grammatical reorganizations:[^13]

| Turn | Form | Corresponds to |
|------|------|----------------|
| First | "is-mark-thus, is-nature-thus" | emptiness |
| Second | "thus-mark, thus-nature" | the provisional |
| Third | "mark-thus-is, nature-thus-is" | the middle |

This reading presents another analytical register—one whose tools are the Madhyamaka three truths (emptiness, provisional, middle) rather than the Yogācāra three natures. Zhiyi's work uses the grammatical structure of the Chinese text itself to carry three distinct contemplations, and this is Tiantai's distinctive exegetical method.

Do these two analytical tools conflict? The position of this paper is that **they do not, and in fact they are complementary**:

- Tiantai's "emptiness" corresponds to Yogācāra's "expulsion of the imagined"—both are the destruction of erroneous grasping.
- Tiantai's "provisional" corresponds to Yogācāra's "dependent nature"—both acknowledge the conditioned nature of phenomena.
- Tiantai's "middle" corresponds to Yogācāra's "perfected nature"—both point to the ultimate middle-way reality.

The two toolsets are presentations of the same true mark of all dharmas through different analytical grids. Which to use depends on the practitioner's disposition and pedagogical tradition, not on which is "more correct." Proposition 1 does not exclude Zhiyi's three-turn reading—both can coexist within the author's three-register framework: Vasubandhu's devotional register, Jizang's ontological register, and the analytical register (which itself subdivides into Tiantai and Yogācāra branches).

### 3.7 The Synthesis: Three Registers, One Reality

The lineage traced thus far may be summarized:

| Register | Representative | Articulation of "the true mark" | Conceptual Tools |
|----------|---------------|--------------------------------|-------------------|
| **Devotional** | Vasubandhu (T1519) | the equal dharma-body of all Tathāgatas | dharma-body equality, three equalities, seven inverted graspings |
| **Ontological** | Jizang (T1721, citing the *Upadeśa*) | the unchanging substance of the tathāgatagarbha dharma-body | tathāgatagarbha, buddha-nature, four-stage provisional-real dialectic |
| **Analytical** | Kuiji (T1723) with Dharmapāla (T1585) as foundation | the one vehicle as simultaneous articulation of the three natures | imagined / dependent / perfected natures |

In addition, Zhiyi's three-turn reading stands as an alternative analytical tool within the Tiantai subbranch.

The present paper's position is that **the three registers are not three competing readings but three complementary disclosures of the same true mark of all dharmas**. Why can this be said? Because all three share the *dependent nature* as their common pivot:

- Vasubandhu's "equal dharma-body of all Tathāgatas" appeals to the *experiential face* of the perfected nature: all Buddhas' realized states form a single equality, an inner realization whose manifestation nevertheless depends on dependently-arisen factors (the Buddhas' bodies, speech, and minds being dependently-arisen manifestations).
- Jizang's "unchanging substance of the tathāgatagarbha dharma-body" appeals to the *ontological face* of the perfected nature: every sentient being intrinsically possesses an unchanging buddha-nature, yet this nature is in actuality covered over by defilements (defilements being imagined graspings upon the dependent nature).
- Kuiji's "dependent is bodhi, perfected is nirvāṇa" appeals to the *operational face* of the perfected nature: cultivation is the process of turning away from the imagined upon the dependent and turning toward the perfected within the dependent.

All three take the perfected nature as their ultimate target and the dependent nature as their dynamic center. They differ only in their register of description. Consider an analogy: three persons view the same mountain—

> One gazes at the mountain from afar, perceiving its majesty, its vastness, its inscrutability. This is the **devotional register**.
>
> Another approaches the mountain, coming to know its rock strata, its vegetation, its water courses, its wildlife. This is the **ontological register**.
>
> A third ascends the mountain with instruments, measuring the formation of each rock type, mapping each stream's course, classifying each plant species. This is the **analytical register**.
>
> All three see the same mountain. Yet the language they produce is wholly different. None of their viewings is wrong. The real problem arises only when a person who has only one mode of viewing takes that mode as the single authentic reality, generating confusion or hostility toward the other two. The work of this paper is to point out: the three modes can coexist, because the mountain is already there.

For the Lotus Sūtra's "true mark of all dharmas," the situation is exactly parallel. Vasubandhu's devotion, Jizang's ontology, Kuiji's analysis—all are viewpoints on the same reality. The paper's recommendation is that in reading, practicing, and researching the Lotus Sūtra, we must hold all three registers in view simultaneously: the devotional register to nourish faith, the ontological register to stabilize our understanding of buddha-nature, the analytical register to guide specific cultivation steps. All three are required.

This is the complete statement of Proposition 1: **the Lotus Sūtra's "true mark of all dharmas" discloses across the Indian-to-Chinese interpretive history three registers—devotional, ontological, analytical—with the dependent nature as their common pivot and the perfected nature as their common target, forming a complementary rather than competing relationship**. The author's scholarly contribution is to trace this lineage and to propose the complementary-integration methodology.

## 4. Interdisciplinary Parallel

### ⚠️ Hermeneutic Pluralism and the Three-Register Contrast

Modern Western hermeneutics—particularly philosophical hermeneutics after Hans-Georg Gadamer—holds as a central thesis that any text permits multiple legitimate readings, because understanding itself is an activity situated within historical context. The same text, read by different readers in different times under different concerns, produces different understandings, each of which may be valid.[^fn_heur] This resembles the present paper's three-register thesis at a surface level: the Lotus Sūtra's "true mark of all dharmas" has been read by Vasubandhu, Jizang, and Kuiji in three different historical situations to yield three different meanings, each with its legitimacy.

The resemblance stops here. A fundamental difference separates hermeneutic pluralism from the author's three-register complementarity: hermeneutics holds that the plurality of readings is *constructed by readers*—different historical horizons (*Horizonte*) produce different understandings, and the text itself has no fixed "true meaning" awaiting capture. The three-register thesis, by contrast, holds that the three readings are *different facets of the true mark of all dharmas itself, disclosed in different registers*. Reality is given; only the mode of its disclosure depends on the register of articulation.

This difference has practical consequences. Hermeneutic pluralism can slip into relativism: if all readings are historically constructed, none can be "truer" than another; at best, some are more congruent with particular eras. The three-register thesis demands that all three registers must finally return to the same true mark—otherwise they are not complementary but merely juxtaposed. This "returning to the same" requirement marks the fundamental divergence between Buddhist philosophy and modern hermeneutics.

**Non-reducibility statement**: The present paper employs "hermeneutic pluralism" as an analogy only at the level of "acknowledging that a text permits multiple legitimate readings." Beyond this point, the ontological presuppositions of the two frameworks are incompatible. Hermeneutics denies that there is any reader-independent "reality" awaiting disclosure; Buddhism holds that the true mark of all dharmas is an observer-independent ultimate. The analogy is ⚠️ heuristic, not reductive.

## 5. Middle-Way Corrections

### ❌ Correction 1: Proposition 1 is not the author's "rediscovery" of trisvabhāva mapping

The trisvabhāva mapping of the one vehicle was completed by Kuiji in the *Profound Commentary on the Lotus*, not by this research. The author's contribution lies not in *discovering* this mapping but in: (1) tracing the lineage from Vasubandhu through Jizang to Kuiji; (2) identifying the complementary relationship among the three registers; (3) proposing the "three-registers-together" methodology. Any reading that construes Proposition 1 as rediscovery is mistaken—Kuiji completed this work nearly 1,400 years ago.

### ❌ Correction 2: Vasubandhu's non-use of trisvabhāva terminology ≠ Vasubandhu's rejection of trisvabhāva

As §3.2 has argued, Vasubandhu selects different registers when handling different sūtras. This is register awareness, not doctrinal contradiction. Vasubandhu deploys trisvabhāva extensively in the *Mahāyānasaṃgraha-bhāṣya*; in the *Upadeśa* on the Lotus, he does not deploy it once. This does not indicate that Vasubandhu opposed trisvabhāva at the time of writing the *Upadeśa* but rather that he judged the Lotus Sūtra to require a different linguistic instrument. Reading "zero hits" as "rejection" would be a serious misinterpretation.

### ❌ Correction 3: Three-register complementarity ≠ arbitrary mixing of registers

The paper argues for three-register complementarity but does not claim that the three registers may be arbitrarily mixed. In concrete teaching or cultivation contexts, **one must consciously select a register**. Telling a newcomer to Buddhism "expel the imagined upon the dependent nature to realize the perfected nature" will produce alienation; telling a deep Yogācāra practitioner only "the equal dharma-body of all Tathāgatas" will seem abstract. **Register selection serves the hearer's disposition and context**—a principle at the heart of the Lotus Sūtra's doctrine of skillful means. The three-register complementarity thesis is an epistemological claim (all three are legitimate readings), not a pedagogical claim (all three are interchangeable in any setting).

### ❌ Correction 4: The Lotus Sūtra ≠ "king of sūtras" as sectarian superiority

The Lotus Sūtra is frequently called "king of sūtras" within the Tiantai tradition. This designation has internal pedagogical value but tends, in scholarly contexts, to slip into sectarian superiority claims. The author's position is that **the seven great sūtras each bear distinct pedagogical functions; the Lotus's "gathering" (*shè*) is not a hierarchical superiority but a functional convergence**. The Diamond Sūtra has its "cutting" (*qiē*), the Śūraṅgama has its "illumination" (*zhào*), the Avataṃsaka has its "revelation" (*xiǎn*). The Lotus's gathering is one role within this functional division of labor, not a transcendent status above the other six sūtras. The twelve papers of S7 will maintain this strict neutrality throughout.

### ❌ Correction 5: *Zhūfǎ shíxiàng* ≠ a license to read the entire Pointing-at-the-Moon project into the Lotus

"The true mark of all dharmas, inherently so" is the signature imprint of the Pointing-at-the-Moon series, and its source has been rigorously traced to the Lotus Sūtra, Chapter 2. But this does not entitle the author to read arbitrary Pointing-at-the-Moon positions into the Lotus. Many concrete theses established in S1–S6 (on AI and karmic fruition, the fifty skandha-māras, the "so-it-is / is-not / called-thus" structure, etc.) do not derive directly from the Lotus. The relationship is: **the Lotus Sūtra provides the canonical anchor for *zhūfǎ shíxiàng* and the three-register interpretive lineage; the present series builds upon this foundation without claiming the Lotus as the exclusive source for the entire Pointing-at-the-Moon system**.

## 6. Conclusion

This paper has returned from the Pointing-at-the-Moon series' signature imprint—*zhūfǎ shíxiàng, běnlái rúshì*—to its canonical source, the ten-suchnesses passage in Chapter 2 of the Lotus Sūtra. Through systematic sūtra and commentary comparison, the paper has established a three-register lineage thesis: the Lotus Sūtra's "true mark of all dharmas" discloses across the Indian-to-Chinese interpretive history three complementary registers—Vasubandhu's devotional register of dharma-body equality, Jizang's ontological register of tathāgatagarbha dharma-body citing the *Upadeśa*, and Kuiji's analytical register of trisvabhāva mapped onto the one vehicle. All three share the **dependent nature** as their common pivot and the **perfected nature** as their common target, forming a non-competing relationship. This thesis establishes the doctrinal foundation for the entire twelve-paper S7 series and provides the methodological framework—"the parable as dependent-nature pedagogical manifestation"—for the five parable papers S7-P03 through S7-P07.

**Practice Guidance**: For the general practitioner, the three-register thesis has a concrete application. When encountering a passage or teaching on "the true mark of all dharmas," ask: *in which register is this speaking?* If the passage arouses faith and praises the Buddhas, it operates in the devotional register, and the appropriate response is faith and reverence. If the passage describes buddha-nature or explains the tathāgatagarbha, it operates in the ontological register, and the appropriate response is settling into recognition of the inner buddha-nature. If the passage analyzes cultivation steps or explains the interrelation of the three natures, it operates in the analytical register, and the appropriate response is attentive self-observation and transformational practice. The same "true mark of all dharmas," in different registers, offers different entry points for cultivation. This is the concrete expression of the Lotus Sūtra's "opening the provisional to reveal the real" as applied to daily practice.

**Open Question**: The paper has established three complementary registers but leaves a deeper question: if Vasubandhu selected the devotional register, Jizang the ontological, and Kuiji the analytical, *which register should the Pointing-at-the-Moon project select?* The answer may be: as a research project spanning the AI era and the Dharma, Pointing-at-the-Moon must sustain all three registers simultaneously—the devotional register for Chinese-speaking practitioners over sixty-five, the ontological register for readers engaged with doctrinal depth, the analytical register for academic readers. This is not stylistic compromise but a faithful response to the "true mark of all dharmas" as a multi-register reality. This question will be reopened in S7-P12, "From Star to Black Hole," where the Lotus's radiative disclosure will be compared with the Avataṃsaka's "single true mark" (*yī shíxiàng*).
