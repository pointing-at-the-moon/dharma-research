## 摘要 Abstract

本文承續 S6-P09《受陰十魔——第二支箭的精微化》,進入照妖鏡的第三篇——想陰十魔的鑑別診斷。本文核心命題:**想陰十魔不是十個不同的精魅問題,而是「貪求」結構的十種對象化。每一種貪求都召出一個「鏡像他者」——一個看起來已經擁有你所貪求之物的人——通過這個他者的形象、聲音與教導,魔完成對行者的惑亂。**「飛精附人」不是天魔的隨機策略,而是貪求結構本身的必然顯現:因為想陰處理的是對外在能力的貪求,而外在能力需要一個「持有者」作為對象。子璿說「想陰未盡猶引外魔」,交光說「葢受盡者不能入其心腑,故假旁人惑之,轉令自亂耳」——二家共同宣告:受陰已破之後,魔不能再從內部進入,只能從外部、透過旁人來惑亂。本文最重要的獨創貢獻是「十貪→十鏡像他者」的現象學配對結構分析,並從中提煉出鏡像他者的四條基本法則。

This paper continues S6-P09 (*The Ten Māras of the Receptive Aggregate: The Refinement of the Second Arrow*) and constitutes the third installment in the demon-revealing mirror series — the differential diagnosis of the ten māras of the perception aggregate (*saṃjñā-skandha*, 想陰). The central thesis: **the ten perception-aggregate māras are not ten distinct problems of demonic possession but ten objectifications of a single structure — craving (*tṛṣṇā*). Each craving conjures a "mirror-other" — a person who appears to already possess what the practitioner craves — and through that other's form, voice, and teaching the māra completes its disturbance of the practitioner.**

"Dispatching essence to possess a person" (*fēi jīng fù rén* 飛精附人) is therefore not a random tactic deployed by the celestial māra (*deva-māra*) but the necessary phenomenological unfolding of the craving structure itself: because the perception aggregate processes craving for *external capacities*, and external capacities require a *possessor* as their object. The receptive-aggregate māras can "turn the practitioner upon himself" (Jiāoguāng's *zì diān* 自顛) precisely because their object is the practitioner's own internal state; the perception-aggregate māras must "possess a person" because their object is an external capacity, and capacity demands a venue of display, a display demands a performer, and the performer is the possessed person.

Zǐxuán and Jiāoguāng arrive at a single judgment on this point. Zǐxuán: "While the perception aggregate is not yet exhausted, external māras are still drawn in" (*xiǎngyīn wèijìn yóu yǐn wàimó* 想陰未盡猶引外魔). Jiāoguāng: "Because once the receptive aggregate is exhausted [the māra] cannot enter the practitioner's heart-organs, it borrows a bystander to confuse him, turning him to disturb himself" (*gài shòujìn zhě bùnéng rù qí xīnfǔ, gù jiǎ pángrén huò zhī, zhuǎn lìng zì luàn ěr* 葢受盡者不能入其心腑，故假旁人惑之，轉令自亂耳). Both commentators agree: once the receptive aggregate has been broken through, the māra can no longer enter from within and must work from without, through a third party. This is the structural reason why "dispatching essence to possess a person" must exist.

The paper proceeds along four axes. **First**, it establishes "seeking the great teacher" (*qiú dàshī* 求大師) as the orthodox structure for severing the three cravings, drawing on *Saṃyukta Āgama* 895 and the "boil" (*yōng* 癰) simile of *Madhyama Āgama* fascicle 28 — and then exposes its mirror inversion: when the object of craving flips from "severing craving" to "obtaining capacity," the structural seat of the great teacher is filled by a mirror-other. **Second**, it grounds the analysis in the early-text doctrine of the three cravings, the eleven-link "cravings-for-existence proceed in successive completion" chain, and the *Dharmacakra-pravartana Sūtra*'s treatment of *samudaya-satya*. **Third**, it advances the paper's most original contribution: a phenomenological pairing of the ten cravings with the ten kinds of demonic-essence (怪鬼, 魃鬼, 魅鬼, 蠱毒鬼, 癘鬼, 大力鬼, 山林鬼神, 精魅, 日月薄蝕精氣, 自在天魔) and the four laws of the mirror-other distilled from this pairing. **Fourth**, it traces the axis of transition from *external māra* (*wàimó* 外魔) through the perception aggregate to *mind-māra* (*xīnmó* 心魔) in the formations aggregate (S6-P11), drawing on Zǐxuán and Jiāoguāng's joint adjudication.

**關鍵詞 Keywords:** 想陰十魔、貪求、鏡像他者、飛精附人、融通妄想、三愛、求大師、年老成魔、想陰盡、心魔 / ten māras of the perception aggregate, craving (*tṛṣṇā*), mirror-other, dispatching essence to possess a person, the false thought of pervasive transformation (*róngtōng wàngxiǎng* 融通妄想), three cravings, seeking the great teacher, aged into demonhood, exhaustion of perception aggregate, mind-māra

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Seeking the Great Teacher — The Orthodox Structure for Severing the Three Cravings

In the fifth century BCE, a brief exchange was recorded in *Saṃyukta Āgama* 895. The Buddha addressed the *bhikṣus*:[^1]

> "There are three cravings. What three? *Kāma-tṛṣṇā* (craving for sensual pleasure), *rūpa-tṛṣṇā* (craving for form), *arūpa-tṛṣṇā* (craving for the formless). For the sake of severing these three cravings, *one should seek a great teacher*."[^2]

"For the sake of severing the three cravings, one should seek a great teacher" — these ten characters (*wèi duàn sān ài gù, dāng qiú dàshī* 為斷三愛故，當求大師) compress one of the basic structures of the Buddhist path: severing craving is the goal, seeking a great teacher is the means. The same structure recurs throughout the Āgamas, and in *Madhyama Āgama* fascicle 10 it is unfolded into a complete therapeutic chain of eleven links: associating with a true person (*sappurisa-saṃseva*) → spiritual friend → hearing the true Dharma → faith → right reflection → right mindfulness and right knowing → guarding the sense-faculties → the three wholesome conducts → the seven factors of awakening → knowledge and liberation.[^3]

The chain begins with "associating with a true person" — that is, finding the right teacher and the right environment. It ends in "knowledge and liberation" — the three cravings severed, *saṃsāra* transcended. Each link presupposes the previous one. If the starting point is wrong — if the "true person" is not in fact a true person but something that *looks like* a true person — the entire chain reverses direction, running from liberation back into bondage.

*Madhyama Āgama* fascicle 28 supplements this with one of the most direct similes in the early canon for the location of the three cravings within the body. The Buddha says:[^4]

> "The boil — this means the body. Coarse form composed of the four great elements, born of father and mother, nourished by food and drink, supported by clothing and massage, by bathing and forbearance, subject to impermanence, decay, and dissolution: this is called the boil. The root of the boil — this means the three cravings. *Kāma-tṛṣṇā*, *rūpa-tṛṣṇā*, *arūpa-tṛṣṇā*: this is called the root of the boil. The discharges of the boil — these mean the six contact-bases."

The body is a boil, the three cravings are the root of the boil, and the six sense faculties are where the pus drains out. The simile's sharpness lies in locating the three cravings as the *root* of the body — not as some incidental property of the body but as the fundamental cause of the body's arising. To sever the three cravings is to extract the root of the boil. And to extract the root, *Saṃyukta Āgama* 895 insists, one must seek a great teacher.

### 1.2 Central Thesis — The Ten Māras of the Perception Aggregate as the Mirror Inversion of "Seeking the Great Teacher"

What, then, of a practitioner who has already crossed the form aggregate (S6-P08), broken through the receptive aggregate (S6-P09), and entered the territory of the perception aggregate? Does he still need to seek a great teacher?

From the standpoint of the Śūraṅgama, the answer is yes. But it is precisely at this position that an extraordinarily subtle and dangerous reversal occurs: **the practitioner here both most needs the spiritual friend and most easily encounters the spiritual friend's mirror inversion.**

Fascicle 9's description of the perception-aggregate territory establishes the danger of the situation from its very first sentence:[^5]

> "Ānanda! That good son's receptive aggregate has become empty and subtle. He encounters no errant cogitation. His perfect *samādhi* unfolds in luminous clarity. Within the *samāpatti*, the mind cherishes perfect luminosity, sharpens its keen contemplation, and *craves expedient skill* (*tān qiú shànqiǎo* 貪求善巧). At this moment, the celestial māra, watching for his chance, dispatches an essence to possess a person, who then expounds dharmas through that person's mouth."

Notice the sequence: receptive aggregate empty and subtle → encountering no errant cogitation → perfect *samādhi* unfolding in luminous clarity → mind cherishing perfect luminosity → sharpening keen contemplation → *craving expedient skill* → celestial māra watching for his chance → dispatching essence to possess a person → expounding dharmas through that person's mouth. The first four steps are unambiguously positive — these are the marks of a genuinely advanced state. The fifth step introduces the verb "to sharpen" (*ruì* 銳); the sixth introduces the two characters "to crave" (*tān qiú* 貪求). These two characters mark the pivot from forward progress into reversal. The seventh step then follows immediately: the celestial māra "watches for his chance" (*hòu dé qí biàn* 候得其便). The māra does not appear at random; he has been waiting. Waiting for what? For those two characters: *tān qiú*.

The phrase that follows — "dispatches essence to possess a person, who expounds dharmas through that person's mouth" — is the Śūraṅgama's central description of how perception-aggregate māric events make themselves visible. **Dispatched essence**: the celestial māra sends a subordinate essence-spirit. **Possessing a person**: this essence enters another person — *not* the practitioner himself, but a third party. **Expounding dharmas through the mouth**: through that possessed person's voice, content that resembles the Dharma is spoken aloud.

This three-step description encodes the central thesis of P10: **the ten māras of the perception aggregate are not ten distinct demonic-essence problems but ten objectifications of one structure, the structure of craving.** Each craving conjures a mirror-other — a person who appears to already possess what the practitioner craves — and through that other, the māra completes its disturbance of the practitioner.

"Dispatching essence to possess a person" is not a random tactic deployed by the celestial māra. It is the necessary phenomenological unfolding of the craving structure itself: **because the perception aggregate processes craving for external capacities, and external capacities require a "possessor" as their object.** If you crave expedient skill, you need someone "able to expound dharmas with skillful means" as your target; if you crave divine power, you need someone "able to manifest miraculous powers" as your target; if you crave knowledge of past lives, you need someone "able to speak of former existences" as your target. Without that target object, the craving has nowhere to land. The celestial māra needs invent nothing new — he need only, at the moment each craving arises, supply a person who *looks like* the object of that craving. The rest the practitioner accomplishes himself.

This is why the structure of the ten perception-aggregate māras is a *mirror* structure. It is the literal inverse of the seeking-the-great-teacher structure — the same three-fold *practitioner → teacher → instruction* schema, but running in reverse. *Saṃyukta Āgama* 895's forward version: the practitioner craves the severance of craving → seeks a great teacher → the great teacher gives instruction → severance is realized. Fascicle 9's reverse version: the practitioner craves capacity X → a mirror-other appears → the mirror-other "ascends the seat and expounds the dharma" → the practitioner falls into errant view. Same structure, opposite direction. The "possessed person" (the *guài* ghost, the *bá* ghost, the *mèi* ghost…) is *the māra's great teacher* — these figures fill the structural seat of "great teacher," but what fills it is not a true teacher but the mirror image of one.

### 1.3 Why the Perception-Aggregate Māra Must Manifest Externally — Answering the Open Question of P09

At the close of P09 I left an open question: why can the receptive-aggregate māra "turn the practitioner upon himself" (Jiāoguāng's *zì diān* 自顛) while the perception-aggregate māra must "possess a person"? Why does *craving* require an external māra to cooperate, while *misjudged sensation* can be completed alone?[^6]

The time has come to answer it directly.

The core of the answer is this: **the receptive aggregate processes "the misrecognition of one's own state" (an inward-facing object), while the perception aggregate processes "craving for an external capacity" (an outward-facing object). The former requires only the practitioner himself; the latter requires an external target object.**

Each of the ten receptive-aggregate māras — sorrow, exhilaration, recollection, easy contentment, constant grief, excessive joy, great self-conceit, fondness for ease, emptiness, sensual desire — concerns the practitioner's *reading* of his own present sensation. The object is an internal state; the practitioner alone can complete the entire process of "construing limitless tranquility as sagely attainment." No second person needs to exist; no external target object needs to exist. The māric event takes place entirely within the heart. This is why Jiāoguāng describes the receptive-aggregate stage as "the māra darkly enters the practitioner's own heart, causing him without awareness to turn upon himself." The two characters *zì diān* — "turn upon oneself" — are the key: the practitioner alone is enough.

The ten perception-aggregate māras are wholly different. Each māra's object is some kind of **external capacity** — the eloquence of expedient skill, the spiritual mobility of pervasive experience, fusion with some target, sharp analytic discrimination, resonance with the obscure, deep stillness, knowledge of past lives, miraculous powers, the realization of deep emptiness, eternal lifespan. Notice the common feature: **these are all capacities that must be *displayed*, not states that can be *felt*.** You cannot "feel expedient skill" by yourself — expedient skill must be exhibited in the act of teaching; you cannot "feel knowledge of past lives" by yourself — such knowledge must be exhibited in recall and narration; you cannot "feel divine power" by yourself — divine power must be exhibited in action. A capacity, by its very nature, requires a venue of display, an object of display, and a witness to display.

When the practitioner *craves* a capacity, he does not yet possess it (if he did, he would not crave it). So he requires **a person who already possesses that capacity** as the object of his pursuit. His craving is directional, object-bearing, target-laden. The target cannot be empty — it must be a particular person, someone who utters certain words, performs certain gestures, displays certain abilities.

What the celestial māra exploits is precisely this structural requirement. The māra cannot directly give the practitioner "the capacity" (the māra himself does not possess any genuine capacity), but he can give the practitioner "a person who appears to possess that capacity." He dispatches an essence-spirit to take possession of someone — usually a fellow practitioner near at hand, or a wandering "spiritual friend" who happens by — and through that possessed person displays the form of the craved object. From the practitioner's vantage point, the possessed person *is* the realization of the capacity he has been seeking — *is* the spiritual friend he has been looking for.

This is the structural necessity of "dispatching essence to possess a person." It is not a superstitious detail of the Śūraṅgama, nor a piece of irrelevant ghost-lore. **It is the necessary unfolding of the phenomenological structure of "craving for an external capacity":** external capacity requires display, display requires a performer, the performer is the possessed person. Without this performer, craving has nowhere to land; with this performer, the practitioner has bound himself in his own craving.

Zǐxuán and Jiāoguāng's judgments are highly congruent on this point. Zǐxuán: "While the perception aggregate is not yet exhausted, external māras are still drawn in. Now that the perception aggregate is exhausted and the formations aggregate is laid bare, [the practitioner] generates errant views regarding his perceived objects, grasping these as right — and so we speak of mind-māras."[^7] Note the precision of the periodization: **the existence-period of the external māra terminates the moment the perception aggregate is exhausted.** Up to that moment, external māras are still drawn in; beyond that moment, they no longer reach.

Jiāoguāng states it even more plainly: "Because once the receptive aggregate is exhausted [the māra] cannot enter the practitioner's heart-organs, it borrows a bystander to confuse him, turning him to disturb himself."[^8] Two key points are encoded here. First, **at the level of the heart-organs the perception-aggregate practitioner is already protected** — the māra cannot directly enter his heart. Second, **at the level of perceptual-relational structure he is vulnerable** — the māra can disturb him from outside, through a bystander. The mirror-other is the embodiment of this relational pathway; "dispatching essence to possess a person" is its concrete mechanism.

The whole of P10 unfolds this phenomenological structure to its conclusion — to the ten cravings, the ten mirror-others, and the precise correspondences between them.

## 2. Textual Basis

This section follows the *sūtra-first, treatise-second* sequence. **Part A** cites two groups of canonical sources: the Āgamas — the three cravings, the boil simile, the eleven-link chain of cravings-for-existence, the leashed-dog simile, and the Dharma-wheel discourse on the truth of origination — as the early-text doctrinal basis; and the Śūraṅgama's fascicle 9 passages on the perception-aggregate territory and the ten māras as the deep-meditative diagnostic system. **Part B** cites Zǐxuán's *Yìshū* (T1799) and Jiāoguāng Zhēnjiàn's *Zhèngmài shū* (X275) as the treatise-layer support.

### Part A: Sūtras — Āgama and Śūraṅgama

#### 2.1 Āgama: The Three Cravings, the Boil Simile, and the Causal Chain of Cravings-for-Existence

**2.1.1 The Three Cravings (*Saṃyukta Āgama* 895).** As cited in §1, *Saṃyukta Āgama* 895 supplies the standard tripartite classification: *kāma-tṛṣṇā* (craving for sensual pleasure), *rūpa-tṛṣṇā* (craving for form), *arūpa-tṛṣṇā* (craving for the formless). "Craving" (*tṛṣṇā* / 愛) is the core content of the truth of origination (*samudaya-satya*) in the Four Noble Truths. The three-fold division corresponds to the three realms — desire, form, formless — which means that craving runs through every level of *saṃsāra* without exception; no realm is immune to its bondage.

The sūtra's immediately following sentence is critical:[^9]

> "For the sake of severing these three cravings, one should seek a great teacher."

The structure "for the sake of severing the three cravings, seek a great teacher" links *severing craving* directly to *seeking the teacher*. Its logic is straightforward: the three cravings are the bondage, the great teacher is the one who unties the bondage, therefore severing requires seeking. At the level of the ordinary practitioner this logic is unobjectionable — when you want to learn something, find someone who can. But in the depths of meditation this logic conceals an extraordinarily subtle danger: **the action of "seeking the great teacher" can itself become a craving** — a craving for "the one who can teach me to sever craving." When this subtle craving arises, the practitioner slides from "seeking the teacher in order to sever craving" into "seeking the teacher for the sake of seeking the teacher" — means and end have inverted. The ten perception-aggregate māras occur precisely when this inversion has matured.

**2.1.2 The Boil Simile (*Madhyama Āgama* fascicle 28).** The "boil" simile of *Madhyama Āgama* fascicle 28 is the early canon's most direct image for the three cravings:[^10]

> "The *bhikṣu* knows the cessation of the cravings-for-existence, extracts their root, so that they will never arise again… The boil — this means the body. Coarse form composed of the four great elements, born of father and mother… subject to impermanence, decay, dissolution — this is called the boil. The root of the boil — this means the three cravings. *Kāma-tṛṣṇā*, *rūpa-tṛṣṇā*, *arūpa-tṛṣṇā* — this is called the root of the boil. The discharges of the boil — these are the six contact-bases."

The simile is three-layered: **boil = body** (the visible problem), **root of boil = three cravings** (the cause of the problem), **discharges = the six sense bases** (where the problem leaks out). To treat the boil one must extract the root — squeezing the pus is futile, since pus regenerates. To extract the root, one must address the mechanism of craving directly.

For P10's argument, the boil simile supplies a crucial perspective: **the ten cravings of the perception-aggregate are not "boils on the body" but "the refinement of the root of the boil itself."** They are not coarse three-cravings but the highly refined version of the three cravings in deep meditation — cravings for capacity, miraculous power, eternal lifespan are all extensions of *kāma-tṛṣṇā*, *rūpa-tṛṣṇā*, *arūpa-tṛṣṇā*, only with new objects. Craving for divine power is the high-end form of "having more force for myself," a refined extension of *kāma-tṛṣṇā*; craving for deep emptiness is the refinement of *arūpa-tṛṣṇā*; craving for eternal lifespan is "permanently possessing this body" at the very ceiling of the desire realm. The ten cravings are not a new category — they are the three cravings as they appear in the depths of meditative practice.

**2.1.3 Cravings-for-Existence Proceed in Successive Completion (*Madhyama Āgama* fascicle 10).** The "successive-completion of cravings-for-existence" (*yǒu'ài zhǎnzhuǎn jù chéng* 有愛展轉具成) passage in *Madhyama Āgama* fascicle 10 supplies an eleven-link causal chain for *bhava-tṛṣṇā*:[^11]

> "Having associated with evil persons, one then has association with bad spiritual friends. Having associated with bad spiritual friends, one then hears bad dharmas. Having heard bad dharmas, one then generates lack of faith. Having generated lack of faith, one then has wrong reflection. Having wrong reflection, one then has wrong mindfulness and wrong knowing. Having wrong mindfulness and wrong knowing, one then fails to guard the sense faculties. Failing to guard the sense faculties, one then has the three evil conducts. Having the three evil conducts, one then has the five hindrances. Having the five hindrances, one then has ignorance. Having ignorance, one then has cravings-for-existence. In this way, cravings-for-existence are progressively completed."

The reverse (therapeutic) chain is also eleven-linked: associating with true persons → spiritual friends → hearing the true Dharma → faith → right reflection → right mindfulness and right knowing → guarding the faculties → the three wholesome conducts → the seven factors of awakening → knowledge and liberation. **Both chains begin with *people*** — evil persons or true persons — meaning the people you associate with determine the direction of the entire chain. Each link is the fruit of the previous and the cause of the next; the chain is rigorously concatenated.

For P10, this chain supplies the structural ground for the importance of "seeking the great teacher": **the great teacher (the true person, the spiritual friend) is the *starting point* of the entire therapeutic chain.** If the starting point is correct, the chain unfolds automatically toward knowledge and liberation; if the starting point is wrong, it runs in reverse, eventually depositing the practitioner into "the completion of cravings-for-existence." The mirror structure of the ten perception-aggregate māras is precisely what occurs at the moment when **the starting point has been substituted** — when the "great teacher" the practitioner has located is in fact a person possessed by an essence-spirit dispatched by the celestial māra. The chain then runs in reverse: from bad spiritual friends, into ever deeper bondage.

**2.1.4 The Leashed Dog (*Saṃyukta Āgama* 266).** *Saṃyukta Āgama* 266 employs a singularly evocative simile for the binding power of the craving-knot:[^12]

> "In beginningless *saṃsāra*, covered by ignorance, bound by the knot of craving, [beings] revolve through the long night and do not know the original boundary of suffering… *Bhikṣus*, it is like a dog leashed to a post. The leash unbroken, through the long night it circles the post, revolving in *saṃsāra*."

The leashed-dog simile renders the nature of the craving-knot with surgical precision: **the dog believes itself to be running (pursuing some goal), but it is in fact only circling the post.** The post is the craving-knot; the rope is the practitioner's grasping; the dog imagines itself to have traveled great distances while in fact remaining in place.

For P10 the simile maps directly onto the inner dynamic of the ten perception-aggregate māras: the practitioner believes he is "craving expedient skill," "craving divine power," "craving deep emptiness" — believes he is advancing toward some higher state. But in fact he is bound at the same structural point and circling. Each time he "finds" a mirror-other (a possessed person), he believes he is closer to the goal; in fact each time only deepens the circuit around the same craving-knot. The more mirror-others appear, the more circuits; the more circuits, the more he believes he is making progress. **From the practitioner's internal vantage point he is practicing diligently and seeking the Path; from the structural vantage point he is running circles around the post of craving.** This is the deepest tragedy of the ten perception-aggregate māras.

**2.1.5 The Dharma-Wheel Discourse and the Truth of Origination (*Saṃyukta Āgama* 379).** *Saṃyukta Āgama* 379, the most complete Chinese version of the *Dharmacakra-pravartana Sūtra*, treats the truth of origination thus:[^13]

> "This noble truth of the origination of suffering: it is to be known and to be severed (*yǐ zhī dāng duàn* 已知當斷) — a teaching never heard before. One should rightly reflect upon it. Then arose vision, knowledge, illumination, awakening."

Although in the Āgamas the content of *samudaya-satya* is not always defined explicitly as "craving," the structure of the three turnings and twelve aspects — "to be known and severed" — is unambiguous: the truth of origination is *that which is to be eliminated*. In later treatises and the corresponding Pāli sūtras, *samudaya* is identified with *tṛṣṇā*. This means: **craving is the core object of the path's countering activity, not a tool of practice.** Any practice that mistakes "craving" for "diligence" violates the structure of the Four Noble Truths at the root.

This is precisely the problem with the ten perception-aggregate māras: **they disguise craving as diligence.** "Craving expedient skill" sounds like striving for eloquence; "craving knowledge of past lives" sounds like striving for wisdom; "craving divine power" sounds like striving for the capacity to liberate others. But all these "cravings" violate the *yǐ zhī dāng duàn* principle — they do not sever craving but exchange it (from coarse worldly craving to subtle meditative, supernormal, and capacity-craving).

#### 2.2 Śūraṅgama: The Perception-Aggregate Territory and the Ten Māras

The fascicle-9 perception-aggregate section is the third of the great māra-territory expositions, following the form-aggregate (P08) and receptive-aggregate (P09) sections. The text first defines the criteria for "perception-aggregate territory" (*xiǎngyīn qūyǔ* 想陰區宇) and "exhaustion of perception aggregate" (*xiǎngyīn jìn* 想陰盡), then presents the ten māric states in sequence. Each follows a near-formulaic structure: **receptive aggregate empty and subtle, encountering no errant cogitation → perfect *samādhi* unfolding → the mind cherishes X → keenly contemplates and craves Y → the celestial māra watches for his chance, dispatches an essence to possess a person, expounds dharmas through the mouth → the practitioner perceives this one as a spiritual friend → various deviant conduct and views ensue → loss of right reception**.

**2.2.1 The Perception-Aggregate Territory and Its Exhaustion.**[^14]

> "Ānanda! That good son cultivating *samādhi* who has exhausted the receptive aggregate — although not yet exhausted of *āsravas*, his mind has departed from his form, like a bird leaving its cage. He has already accomplished the capacity, from this ordinary body, to traverse the bodhisattva's sixty sage stations, attaining a mind-made body that goes wherever it wills without obstruction. It is as a sleeping man speaks in his dream: although the man himself knows nothing apart, his speech has rhythm and order, so that those not asleep may understand his words. This is called the territory of the perception aggregate. If the moving thoughts are exhausted and floating notions dissolved, then upon the awareness-luminous mind, as dust and stains are removed, the entire generation-and-cessation of one cycle of birth-and-death is illumined from beginning to end. This is called the exhaustion of the perception aggregate, and such a person can pass beyond the turbidity of afflictions. Examining its origin, the false thought of pervasive transformation (*róngtōng wàngxiǎng* 融通妄想) is its root."

Several key points stand out. First, the mark of the receptive aggregate's exhaustion is "the mind departing from the form, like a bird leaving its cage" — one stage deeper than the receptive aggregate, the practitioner's mind is now able to leave the body's boundary. Second, such a practitioner "traverses the sixty sage stations of the bodhisattva and attains a mind-made body" — an extraordinarily high level of attainment. Third, even so, the perception aggregate remains unexhausted — and the text uses the simile of "speaking in deep sleep" with striking precision: **the perception-aggregate practitioner is like a sleep-talker — his speech has rhythm and meaning, others can understand him, but he himself does not know what he is saying.** Fourth, the mark of perception-aggregate exhaustion is "moving thoughts exhausted, floating notions dissolved, the awareness-luminous mind cleansed of dust, the entire cycle of birth-and-death of one class of beings illumined from beginning to end." Fifth, the root of the perception aggregate is "the false thought of pervasive transformation" — Zǐxuán glosses this as "perception is able to fuse and transform; the body follows the mind; the mind imagines a sour plum and saliva flows in the mouth — fusing and pervading material obstruction, hence called *róngtōng*."[^15]

*Róngtōng wàngxiǎng* — "the false thought of pervasive transformation" — is the substantial determinant of the perception aggregate. The form aggregate's root is *jiāngù wàngxiǎng* (solidified false thought, P08); the receptive aggregate's root is *xūmíng wàngxiǎng* (false thought of empty luminosity, P09); the perception aggregate's root is *róngtōng wàngxiǎng* — perception's capacity to transform the real (saliva flowing at the imagination of a plum), to make the body follow the mind, to pervade and dissolve material obstruction. From solidified to empty-luminous to pervasively-transforming is a three-step transition from *form* to *formlessness* to *formlessness-able-to-take-form*. **The perception aggregate is the stage of *what can transform*** — and precisely because it can transform, it can convert psychological craving into perceptual reality, into a relational other, into a "spiritual friend" met along the path. The mirror-other can exist at all only because *róngtōng wàngxiǎng* can convert craving into form.

**2.2.2 The Ten Māras in Sequence.** The fascicle-9 text gives all ten māras with a near-uniform structure. Each entry has four parts: (a) the arising condition (*the mind cherishes X, craves Y*), (b) the celestial māra mechanism (*watches for his chance, dispatches essence to possess a person, expounds dharmas through the mouth*), (c) the manifestation of the māric nature, (d) the consequences. To economize space I compress the formulaic adjudication clauses but preserve the core terms.

***First state: craving expedient skill → 怪鬼 (the Strange Ghost).***[^16]

> "Ānanda! That good son's receptive aggregate has become empty and subtle. He encounters no errant cogitation. His perfect *samādhi* unfolds in luminous clarity. Within the *samāpatti*, the mind cherishes perfect luminosity, sharpens his keen contemplation, and *craves expedient skill*. At this moment, the celestial māra, watching for his chance, dispatches an essence to possess a person. That person, unaware that he is possessed by the māra, claims himself to have attained unsurpassed *nirvāṇa*. He goes to the place of the man who craves skill, takes a seat, and expounds dharmas. In an instant, his form may appear as a *bhikṣu* to the practitioner, or as Indra, or as a woman, or as a *bhikṣuṇī*… This is named the Strange Ghost (*guài guǐ*), aged into demonhood (*nián lǎo chéng mó* 年老成魔), disturbing this man. When weariness and satiety arise, it leaves the body of the host. The disciple and the teacher are then both caught up in the calamities of the law of the land."

Arising condition: the mind cherishes perfect luminosity, craves expedient skill (eloquence, the skillful capacity for exposition). Celestial-māra mechanism: dispatched essence possesses a Strange Ghost host, takes a seat, expounds dharmas. Mirror-other: the Strange Ghost — able to transform shape, suddenly appearing as *bhikṣu*, Indra, woman, or *bhikṣuṇī*. *Guài* means "shape-changing"; this exactly corresponds to the capacity of "expedient skill" (skillful adaptation of speech).

***Second state: craving wandering experience → 魃鬼 (the Bá Ghost).***

> "Again, the good son's receptive aggregate is empty and subtle… within the *samāpatti*, the mind cherishes wandering, scatters his keen contemplation, and *craves to traverse all places*. At this moment, the celestial māra watches for his chance, dispatches an essence to possess a person, who expounds dharmas… This is named the *Bá* Ghost, aged into demonhood, disturbing this man."

Arising condition: the mind cherishes wandering; craving to traverse all places. Mirror-other: the *Bá* Ghost — the drought-ghost, traveling through waterless places, exactly corresponding to the capacity of "unobstructed travel."

***Third state: craving fusion → 魅鬼 (the Mèi Ghost).***

> "…the mind cherishes intimate clinging, distills his keen contemplation, and *craves fusion*. At this moment, the celestial māra watches for his chance, dispatches an essence to possess a person, who expounds dharmas… This is named the *Mèi* Ghost, aged into demonhood, disturbing this man."

Arising condition: the mind cherishes intimate clinging; craving fusion (mystical merging with some target). Mirror-other: the *Mèi* Ghost — *mèi* means "to seduce, to charm," causing one to enter a trance and merge — exactly corresponding to the capacity of "fusion."

***Fourth state: craving sharp analysis → 蠱毒鬼 (the Gu-Poison Ghost).***

> "…the mind cherishes the root, exhausts the contemplation of the beginnings and endings of all transformations, refines his mind, and *craves sharp discrimination*. At this moment, the celestial māra watches for his chance, dispatches an essence to possess a person, who expounds dharmas… This is named the Gu-Poison and Suppressing-Vow Evil Ghost, aged into demonhood, disturbing this man."

Arising condition: the mind cherishes the root; craving sharp analysis (penetrating discernment of the beginnings and ends of material transformations). Mirror-other: the *Gu*-Poison Ghost — *gu*-poison can penetrate finely into the bones, exactly corresponding to the capacity of "sharp analytic discernment that penetrates to the marrow."

***Fifth state: craving obscure resonance → 癘鬼 (the Pestilence Ghost).***

> "…the mind cherishes distant response, circulates his keen contemplation, and *craves obscure resonance*. At this moment, the celestial māra watches for his chance, dispatches an essence to possess a person, who expounds dharmas… This is named the Pestilence Ghost, aged into demonhood, disturbing this man."

Arising condition: the mind cherishes distant response; craving obscure resonance (sensitivity to remote and hidden things). Mirror-other: the Pestilence Ghost — pestilential vapor is invisible but transmitted, exactly corresponding to the capacity of "obscure resonance" (formless co-vibration).

***Sixth state: craving silent stillness → 大力鬼 (the Powerful Ghost).***

> "…the mind cherishes deep penetration, grinds himself down with hardship, delights in dark seclusion, and *craves silent stillness*. At this moment, the celestial māra watches for his chance, dispatches an essence to possess a person, who expounds dharmas… This is named the Powerful Ghost, aged into demonhood, disturbing this man."

Arising condition: the mind cherishes deep penetration; craving silent stillness (deep meditative quiescence). Mirror-other: the Powerful Ghost — great force capable of suppressing all disturbance, exactly corresponding to the capacity of "silent stillness as embodied force."

***Seventh state: craving knowledge of past lives → 山林鬼神 (Mountain-and-Forest Spirits).***

> "…the mind cherishes knowing-and-seeing, diligently labors in research, and *craves knowledge of past lives*. At this moment, the celestial māra watches for his chance, dispatches an essence to possess a person, who expounds dharmas… These are named the Mountain, Forest, Earth, City-God, and River-Mountain Spirits, aged into demonhood…"

Arising condition: the mind cherishes knowing-and-seeing; craving knowledge of past lives. Mirror-other: mountain-forest spirits — long-resident in the mountains, the soil, the city walls, the rivers and peaks, with extraordinarily long sedimentation in time — exactly corresponding to the capacity of "knowledge of past lives" (the time-sedimentation of one who knows the past).

***Eighth state: craving divine power → 精魅 (Powerful Essences of mountain, sea, wind, river, earth).***

> "…the mind cherishes divine penetrations and various transformations, researches the source of transformation, and *craves divine power*. At this moment, the celestial māra watches for his chance, dispatches an essence to possess a person, who expounds dharmas… These are named the Great Powerful Mountain Essences, Sea Essences, Wind Essences, River Essences, Earth Essences of heaven and earth, all the eon-accumulated essences of grasses and trees; or dragon spirits; or those reincarnated immortals whose lifespans are exhausted but whose bodies have not transformed; or those upon whom another anomaly has attached itself, aged into demonhood, disturbing this man."

Arising condition: the mind cherishes divine penetrations and transformations; craving divine power. Mirror-other: the powerful essences — the great essences of mountain, sea, wind, river, earth — themselves the concrete embodiment of "divine power."

***Ninth state: craving deep emptiness → 日月薄蝕精氣 (Essence-Vapors of Solar and Lunar Eclipse).***

> "…the mind cherishes entry into cessation, researches the nature of transformation, and *craves deep emptiness*. At this moment, the celestial māra watches for his chance, dispatches an essence to possess a person, who expounds dharmas… Although attaining empty quiescence, secretly engaging in lust, and those who receive his lust likewise attain empty mind, denying cause and effect. These are named the Essence-Vapors of Solar and Lunar Eclipse, the Gold-Jade Numinous Grasses, the Unicorn, Phoenix, Tortoise, and Crane that have lived a thousand or ten thousand years without dying and become numinous, born within the realms, aged into demonhood."

Arising condition: the mind cherishes entry into cessation; craving deep emptiness (deep entry into the emptiness of cessation). Mirror-other: the essence-vapors of solar and lunar eclipse — *bó shí* (薄蝕) is the eclipse, the moment when the light of sun or moon is *swallowed* and obscured. Light swallowed by emptiness exactly corresponds to the capacity of "deep emptiness" (emptiness swallowing all things).

***Tenth state: craving eternal lifespan → 自在天魔 (Sovereign Heavenly Māra).***

> "…the mind cherishes long life, painfully researches the subtle, *craves eternal lifespan*, abandoning segmental birth-and-death, abruptly aspiring to transformative birth-and-death, [seeking] the subtle mark to abide permanently. At this moment, the celestial māra watches for his chance, dispatches an essence to possess a person, who expounds dharmas… This is named the World-Abiding Sovereign Heavenly Māra (*zhù shì zìzài tiānmó* 住世自在天魔), employing his retinue such as the *Camuṇḍā* and the Vaiśāli children of the Four Heavenly Kings who have not yet generated *bodhicitta* — exploiting his hollow luminosity, devouring his essence-vapor. Or, *not necessarily through a teacher, the practitioner himself directly perceives [the māra]*, claiming to be Vajrapāṇi giving him long life, manifesting as a beautiful female form, vigorously engaging in lust, and within the year or so his liver and brains are dried up."

Arising condition: the mind cherishes long life; craving eternal lifespan (transcending segmental birth-and-death, aspiring to transformative permanent abiding). Mirror-other: **here the mirror-other has been *upgraded***. It is no longer some lower-grade ghost-essence but the **World-Abiding Sovereign Heavenly Māra** — the king of *paranirmita-vaśavartin*, the highest desire-realm heaven — who *personally* intervenes, employing as auxiliaries his retinue of essence-devouring ghosts (*Camuṇḍā*, Vaiśāli children, etc.). Note the special features of the tenth state. It is the only state in the ten where **the celestial māra himself directly intervenes** (rather than merely dispatching subordinate ghosts as messengers). It is also the only state where the practitioner can perceive the māra **without a possessed-person intermediary** ("not necessarily through a teacher"); even without a third-party host, the practitioner may directly hallucinate the māra appearing as Vajrapāṇi or as a beautiful woman. Its corresponding craving is "eternal lifespan" — that is, the highest form of desire-realm grasping. The tenth state is the terminus of the perception-aggregate stage and the bridge to the formations aggregate; S6-P11 will pick up from this point.

**2.2.3 The Closing Adjudication.**[^17]

> "Ānanda! These ten kinds of *dhyāna*-manifesting realms are all states caused by the perception aggregate's interaction in the use of mind. Beings, dull and confused, do not weigh themselves; encountering this circumstance, they fail to recognize themselves and claim to have ascended to sagehood. Great false speech is committed; they fall into the Avīci hell. After my *parinirvāṇa*, in the Latter Dharma age, you must transmit and reveal the words of the Tathāgata to all beings, that they may awaken to this meaning. Do not allow the celestial māra to gain his opportunity. Preserve and protect them, that they may accomplish the unsurpassed Way."

The closing adjudication of the perception-aggregate section is almost identical to that of the receptive aggregate — the same designation of the ten māras as "*dhyāna*-manifesting realms," the same attribution to "interaction in the use of mind" (imbalance of *samādhi* and wisdom), the same warning against "claiming to have ascended to sagehood, committing great false speech," the same charge to the disciples for the Latter Dharma age. But there is one subtle difference: the perception-aggregate adjudication adds the word "must" (*bìxū* 必須), making the imperative more emphatic than the receptive-aggregate adjudication (*yì dāng* 亦當, "should also"). Why? Because **the māric events of the perception aggregate are publicly visible** — they manifest through another person's mouth, another person's form, another person's "spiritual friend" identity. The Latter Dharma danger therefore exceeds that of the receptive-aggregate stage. **Receptive-aggregate māric events are invisible (taking place only within the practitioner's mind); perception-aggregate māric events are visible (externally manifested through the mirror-other).** The former harms only the practitioner; the latter can confuse a community. The "must" is a structural choice.

### Part B: Treatises — Zǐxuán and Jiāoguāng

#### 2.3 Zǐxuán's *Yìshū* (T1799) — "While the Perception Aggregate Is Not Yet Exhausted, External Māras Are Still Drawn In"

Zǐxuán's treatment of the perception-aggregate section has two structural pivots.

**2.3.1 The Unified Annotation Format for the Ten Māras.** Zǐxuán comments on each of the ten māras using "the receptive aggregate empty and subtle, encountering no errant cogitation" as the starting point. In his annotation on the first māra (craving expedient skill), he gives the foundational adjudication:[^18]

> "*The mind cherishes* — this means craving arises. *Empty and subtle* — this is empty quiescence. *Errant cogitation* — these are the previous ten mental events, namely the arising of sorrow, exhilaration, etc. *Cherishing perfect luminosity* — this is attachment to the *samādhi*-object. *Sharpens* — sharpens to a point. **He tempers and sharpens his loving-mind, making it subtle and refined; therefore he craves expedient skill.**"

The key phrase is *cuì lì àixīn* (淬利愛心) — "tempering and sharpening the loving-mind." *Cuì* originally means to plunge red-hot iron into water to harden it; *cuì lì àixīn* is to temper the loving-mind into something sharper. Zǐxuán's diagnosis is exact: **the source of the ten perception-aggregate māras is not some external demon, but the result of the practitioner's own loving-mind being sharpened by deep meditation.** The previous "errant cogitations" (the receptive-aggregate māras of sorrow, exhilaration, and so on) have already been overcome — but what arises after their overcoming is not love-lessness but *tempered* love: love processed by meditation into a sharper, subtler, more target-precise form. The ten cravings are this tempered love taking aim at ten targets.

**2.3.2 "While the Perception Aggregate Is Not Yet Exhausted, External Māras Are Still Drawn In; Now That the Perception Aggregate Is Exhausted and the Formations Aggregate Is Laid Bare, [the Practitioner] Generates Errant Views Regarding His Perceived Objects, Hence Mind-Māras."** In his annotation at the opening of fascicle 10 (the formations-aggregate section), Zǐxuán gives an adjudication that is structurally critical for both P10 and P11:[^19]

> "*Calamity* — this means disaster. While the perception aggregate is not yet exhausted, external māras are still drawn in. Now that the perception aggregate is exhausted and the formations aggregate is laid bare, [the practitioner] generates errant views regarding his perceived objects, grasping these as right — and so we speak of mind-māras. Therefore, by careful examination one should well discern, not losing right view, and arrive at the unsurpassed [awakening]. The Buddha thus deeply entrusts [Ānanda] to transmit and display this, that practitioners may not enter the net of error or lose the path of right awakening."

The structural significance of this passage is enormous. Zǐxuán explicitly distinguishes two existence-periods:

- **The existence-period of *external māras*** = before the perception aggregate is exhausted (the form, receptive, and perception-aggregate stages).
- **The existence-period of *mind-māras*** = after the perception aggregate is exhausted (the formations and consciousness-aggregate stages).

That is: **at the moment the perception aggregate is exhausted, the external māras exit the stage.** From that point onward, the practitioner no longer faces external celestial māras, ghosts, or demons, but only "errant views generated by his own mind regarding its perceived objects" — that is, the grasping at errant views generated by his own mind. This adjudication cleaves the fifty skandha-māras into two halves: **the first thirty (form, receptive, perception) belong to "the world of external māras," and the latter twenty (formations, consciousness) belong to "the world of mind-māras."** The perception aggregate is the final stage on which the external māra performs.

For P10, this confirms one thing: **the ten perception-aggregate māras are the most complete, most complex, and most refined unfolding of the "external-māra structure" — they are the high point of the external-māra world.** In the form-aggregate stage, the māra is still relatively concealed (only a manifestation of the practitioner's own mind); in the receptive-aggregate stage, the māra has been summoned but does not yet manifest its body ("darkly entering the heart, causing the practitioner to turn upon himself"); in the perception-aggregate stage, the māra finally manifests in full (dispatching essence to possess a person, taking a seat to expound the dharma). From form to receptive to perception, the external māra grows more and more obvious, more and more concrete, more and more recognizable — but also more and more refined, more and more capable of imitating a spiritual friend, more and more difficult to detect.

#### 2.4 Jiāoguāng Zhēnjiàn's *Zhèngmài shū* (X275) — "Because Once the Receptive Aggregate Is Exhausted [the Māra] Cannot Enter the Heart-Organs, It Borrows a Bystander to Confuse Him"

Jiāoguāng's three-layered adjudication has been partially cited in P08 and P09; for P10 the third layer (the perception-aggregate stage) must be unfolded in full.

**2.4.1 The Structural Annotation on "Dispatching Essence to Possess a Person."** Jiāoguāng's annotation on the phrase "the celestial māra watches for his chance, dispatches an essence to possess a person, who expounds dharmas through the mouth" supplies the single most important sentence for the entire P10 argument:[^20]

> "Although the celestial māra was previously summarized [in the form and receptive sections], it was not specifically named. Here, section by section, the text speaks of how, once the receptive aggregate is exhausted and *samādhi* deepens, the celestial māra acts according to the meaning of the sūtra. **He does not himself come; he merely dispatches the māra's party.** Hence *fēi* (dispatch) means rapid sending forth, like the urgent dispatch of a military command or the urgent issuance of an official warrant. *Jīng* (essence) means the various essence-spirits of the māra's party — and **each comes according to its own kind**. Below, the Buddha himself names each of the possessed types: these are *other people who have always been susceptible to errant influence*. **Because once the receptive aggregate is exhausted [the māra] cannot enter the practitioner's heart-organs, it borrows a bystander to confuse him, turning him to disturb himself.** The same applies hereafter."

This passage compresses the entire central thesis of P10. It deserves sentence-by-sentence reading.

"Although the celestial māra was previously summarized, it was not specifically named" — in the earlier form and receptive sections, the celestial māra was mentioned but not concretely unfolded; only in the perception-aggregate section does the text begin to specify.

"He does not himself come; he merely dispatches the māra's party" — the celestial māra does not arrive in person but only sends his subordinates (essence-spirits).

"*Fēi* means rapid sending forth, like the urgent dispatch of a military command" — the original sense of *fēi* is rapid dispatch, like the *fēi xí* (military express) or *fēi piào* (official express).

"*Jīng* means the various essence-spirits of the māra's party, and **each comes according to its own kind**" — *jīng* refers to the māra's essence-spirits, and each kind of craving summons its corresponding kind of essence-spirit.

"Below, the Buddha names each of the possessed types: these are other people who have always been susceptible to errant influence" — the "possessed person" referred to below is, explicitly, *another person who has always been susceptible to errant influence*.

"**Because once the receptive aggregate is exhausted [the māra] cannot enter the practitioner's heart-organs, it borrows a bystander to confuse him, turning him to disturb himself**" — because the practitioner who has exhausted the receptive aggregate (i.e., entered the perception-aggregate stage) is no longer accessible to direct entry into his heart-organs, the māra can only borrow a bystander to confuse him, leading him to disturb himself. This is the most authoritative annotation on the structural necessity argued in P10 §1.3.

"The same applies hereafter" — the remaining nine states unfold by the same structure.

Three crucial implications follow from this annotation. **First, "each comes according to its own kind"** — the kinds of essence-spirits and the kinds of cravings stand in one-to-one correspondence, which directly supports the original "mirror-other structure" of §3.3 below. **Second, "another person who has always been susceptible"** — the possessed person is not the practitioner himself, and not just any innocent bystander, but specifically a person whose own susceptibility makes them suitable as a host. **Third, "because once the receptive aggregate is exhausted [the māra] cannot enter the heart-organs"** — this is the most authoritative structural support for the entire P10 argument.

**2.4.2 "These Ghosts Are the Essence-Spirits Dispatched by the Celestial Māra."** In his annotation on the first māra (the Strange Ghost), Jiāoguāng adds a critical clarification of the relation between ghost-essences and the celestial māra:[^21]

> "The Strange Ghost is one that takes form by encountering things. Only after many years does it become an emissary of the māra; hence we say *aged into demonhood*. **However, this ghost is itself the essence-spirit dispatched by the celestial māra**; the Buddha here gives its name only so people may distinguish and recognize it. It is not the case that earlier the agent was the celestial māra and now it has changed into a ghost-spirit. The reason the older annotators failed to grasp that *dispatching essence* is itself *sending the ghost* and so treated these as two separate matters, was that their analysis ran into incoherence. The same applies hereafter."

Here Jiāoguāng corrects a misreading found in earlier commentators: some old annotations had treated "the celestial māra watches for his chance" and "this is named the Strange Ghost aged into demonhood" as two distinct events (first the celestial māra, then a ghost). Jiāoguāng points out that this is wrong — **"this ghost is itself the essence-spirit dispatched by the celestial māra."** The "Strange Ghost," "*Bá* Ghost," "*Mèi* Ghost," and so on are themselves the essence-spirits the celestial māra dispatches; they are not an independent existence. The Buddha "names each" only so practitioners can recognize the specific shape of each kind of māric event, not because there is a ghost-spirit world independent of the celestial māra.

This correction matters greatly for P10's argument: it allows us to read all ten perception-aggregate māras as a single unified set — "the ten manifestations of the celestial māra" — rather than as ten independent ghost-spirit events. **The ten ghost-essences are the ten faces of the celestial māra**, each face corresponding to one kind of craving. This is exactly the foundation for the mirror-other structure of §3.3.

The shared judgment of Zǐxuán and Jiāoguāng on the perception-aggregate stage may be summed up thus: **the external māra reaches its peak of manifestation in the perception aggregate, but its source is always the practitioner's own "tempered loving-mind" (Zǐxuán) or his "fissure in the heart-organs" (the inverse formulation of Jiāoguāng's phrase).** Without craving, there is no entry-point for the external māra; with craving, the external māra has its form of manifestation. We now turn to the core arguments of §3, where the structure is unfolded to its conclusion.

## 3. Core Arguments

This section unfolds seven layers of argument, paralleling the seven-layer architecture of P09. §3.1 answers the open question left by P09 (why the perception aggregate must manifest externally). §3.2 reframes the ten perception-aggregate māras as the mirror inversion of the "seeking the great teacher" structure. §3.3 advances the paper's original "ten cravings → ten mirror-others" phenomenological pairing analysis. §3.4 deepens the phenomenology of Jiāoguāng's "because once the receptive aggregate is exhausted [the māra] cannot enter the heart-organs." §3.5 maps the Āgama "seek the teacher" therapeutic chain onto the perception-aggregate mirror chain in full. §3.6 uses the three cravings as a differential diagnostic tool. §3.7 reads Zǐxuán's "while the perception aggregate is not yet exhausted, external māras are still drawn in" as the bridge to P11 (the formations aggregate).

### 3.1 Why the Perception-Aggregate Māra Must Manifest Externally — Unfolding the Logic of Object-Structure

The open question left at P09 §6.3 was: why can the receptive-aggregate māra "turn upon itself," while the perception-aggregate māra must "possess a person"? P10 §1.3 already gave the core answer: the receptive aggregate processes "misrecognition of one's own state" (inward); the perception aggregate processes "craving for an external capacity" (outward). This section unfolds that answer in finer phenomenological detail.

The key concept is *object-structure* (the intentionality of the object). Every psychological dynamic has its own object-structure — what it points at, what it aims at, what kind of target object it requires in order to complete itself. The object of the ten receptive-aggregate māras is "the practitioner's own present sensation." This object requires nothing from the external world; it lies entirely within the practitioner's present awareness, and the practitioner alone can complete the entire process of misrecognition. The object of the ten perception-aggregate māras is "the possession-state of some external capacity." This object intrinsically requires the existence of an external world, because *possession* requires a *possessor*, *capacity* requires a *display of capacity*, and neither *possessor* nor *display* can be completed within pure inward awareness.

A simple example. The receptive-aggregate experience of "limitless tranquility" is an internal state — the practitioner alone can feel it; it is unaffected by the absence of others, the absence of any external situation, the absence of any external feedback. But the perception-aggregate "craving for expedient skill (eloquence)" is wholly different — "expedient skill" exists only within the situation of "expounding to someone." Without an audience, there is no display of expedient skill; without display, there is no validation of expedient skill itself. If the practitioner sits alone in his cell and tells himself "I have great expedient skill," the self-statement is meaningless — because the very nature of expedient skill is *the capacity to teach others*, and without others there is no expedient skill.

This is why the practitioner who craves expedient skill must locate, in the external world, "a person who can expound the dharma with skillful means" as his target. He needs an exemplar — someone who has already realized expedient skill — as the object of his pursuit. Without such an exemplar, his craving has nowhere to land. What the celestial māra exploits is precisely this structural requirement: he cannot give the practitioner real expedient skill (the māra has none), but he can give him "a person who appears to possess expedient skill" — a person possessed by a Strange Ghost, capable of shape-shifting, capable of expounding. From the practitioner's standpoint, this person *is* the embodiment of expedient skill he was seeking; from the structural standpoint, this person is the mirror provided by the celestial māra to exploit the practitioner's craving.

The same logic holds for all ten cravings: craving for wandering experience requires "one who can display traveling through all places"; craving for fusion requires "one who can display merging with the target"; craving for knowledge of past lives requires "one who can speak of former existences"; craving for divine power requires "one who can display supernormal capacity"… Each craving requires a corresponding *possessor* as its object, and each possessor is a mirror dispatched by the celestial māra.

This is P10's complete answer to P09's open question: **the ten perception-aggregate māras must manifest externally because the phenomenological structure of "craving for an external capacity" intrinsically requires an external target object; and the celestial māra, faced with this structural requirement, supplies a mirrored target object.** "Dispatching essence to possess a person" is not a random tactic but the necessary unfolding of the craving structure.

### 3.2 The Ten Perception-Aggregate Māras as the Mirror Inversion of "Seeking the Great Teacher"

The structure of *Saṃyukta Āgama* 895's "for the sake of severing the three cravings, seek a great teacher" is unfolded in *Madhyama Āgama*'s "successive completion of cravings-for-existence" therapeutic chain into eleven links: true person → spiritual friend → hearing the true Dharma → faith → right reflection → right mindfulness and right knowing → guarding the faculties → the three wholesome conducts → the seven factors of awakening → knowledge and liberation. This therapeutic chain is the Āgama's detailed unfolding of "seeking the great teacher" — it tells us that "seeking the great teacher" is not a one-time action but a successively unfolding process, each link's fruit being the next link's cause, the whole chain leading to "knowledge and liberation."

The mirror structure of the ten perception-aggregate māras is the literal inversion of this therapeutic chain. Its mirror chain has a structurally similar shape, but each link runs in reverse:

| Spiritual-friend chain (forward) | Mirror-other chain (reverse) |
|---|---|
| true person | mirror-other (the possessed person) |
| spiritual friend | the one "ascending the seat to expound the dharma" |
| hearing the true Dharma | hearing the mirror-dharma |
| faith | great faith arising in the mirror-other |
| right reflection | reflective compliance with the mirror-dharma |
| right mindfulness and right knowing | ever-deepening "mindfulness of" this mirror-other |
| guarding the faculties | shielding one's own doubts on behalf of the mirror-other |
| the three wholesome conducts | various offerings and services to the mirror-other |
| the seven factors of awakening | the seven factors mirrored as "discernment, energy, joy" with respect to the māra-dharma |
| knowledge and liberation | great false speech committed; falling into the Avīci hell |

Note the precision of this mirror chain. **Each link is the literal correspondent of a true spiritual-friend link — only the direction is reversed.** At the moment the practitioner's inner motive flips from "severing craving" to "obtaining capacity," he activates this mirror chain. Its operation looks externally identical to that of the forward chain — he is indeed "associating with" a person, indeed "hearing the dharma," indeed "generating faith," indeed "reflecting," indeed "practicing." From the level of external behavior, you cannot distinguish whether he is on the forward chain or the mirror chain; only at the level of **inner motive** (whether he wants to sever craving or to obtain capacity) can the distinction be made.

This is the deepest concealment of the ten perception-aggregate māras: **the entire external form of the mirror chain is identical to the form of a real spiritual-friend relationship.** This is why the Śūraṅgama, in treating the perception-aggregate stage, repeatedly emphasizes that "the person himself is unaware that he is possessed by the māra" — the *possessed* person himself does not feel that he is possessed; he feels himself to be a Buddha, a bodhisattva, a great spiritual friend. Likewise, the practitioner does not feel that he is on the mirror chain; he feels himself finally to have met a true spiritual friend. Both parties are sincere, and both parties are inside the mirror.

Zǐxuán's four characters *cuì lì àixīn* (tempering and sharpening the loving-mind) here display their precision again. The entire operating force of the mirror chain comes from the practitioner's "tempered love" — a love refined by deep meditation into something extraordinarily subtle, subtle enough to take aim at "expedient skill," "fusion," "divine power," "eternal lifespan." Coarse love (the love for the five sense-pleasures) has been overcome, but subtle love (the love for the capacities of deep meditation) has taken its place. This subtle love is genuine love; only its object has changed — from worldly five-pleasures to the "capacities of the sage stations." Structurally, however, it is still *tṛṣṇā*, still *samudaya-satya*, still "to be known and severed."

### 3.3 ⭐ P10's Original Contribution: The Phenomenological Pairing of Ten Cravings with Ten Mirror-Others

This section presents the paper's most important original contribution. Traditionally, commentators on the Śūraṅgama's ten perception-aggregate māras (including Zǐxuán and Jiāoguāng) have noted that "the various ghost-essences each come according to their own kind" (Jiāoguāng's phrase), but none has systematically unfolded the specific pairing principle behind this "according to their own kind." The proposal here is: **the forms of the ten ghost-essences are not randomly assigned but precisely paired with the ten cravings — each ghost-essence's nature is exactly the *objectified form* of the craving it answers.**

The complete pairing:

| Nº | Craving | Mirror-Other | Pairing Principle |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | expedient skill (eloquence) | Strange Ghost (怪鬼) | *guài* = shape-changing ↔ verbal adaptability |
| 2 | wandering experience | *Bá* Ghost (魃鬼) | drought-ghost roaming waterless places ↔ unobstructed traversal |
| 3 | fusion | *Mèi* Ghost (魅鬼) | *mèi* = enchantment-into-merger ↔ fusion with target |
| 4 | sharp analysis | *Gu*-Poison Ghost (蠱毒鬼) | *gu*-poison penetrating the marrow ↔ marrow-sharp discrimination |
| 5 | obscure resonance | Pestilence Ghost (癘鬼) | invisibly transmitted miasma ↔ formless co-vibration |
| 6 | silent stillness | Powerful Ghost (大力鬼) | overwhelming force suppressing all ↔ stillness as embodied force |
| 7 | knowledge of past lives | Mountain-Forest Spirits (山林鬼神) | long-resident in mountain and river ↔ time-sedimentation of past-knowing |
| 8 | divine power | Powerful Essences (天地大力精魅) | the essences of natural forces ↔ the embodiment of divine power |
| 9 | deep emptiness | Eclipse Essence-Vapors (日月薄蝕精氣) | *bó shí* = light swallowed ↔ emptiness swallowing all things |
| 10 | eternal lifespan | Sovereign Heavenly Māra (自在天魔) | desire-realm peak ↔ the highest form of the long-life desire |

**The pairing law: the capacity you crave determines the form in which the māra appears.** This is not a superstitious detail of the Śūraṅgama but the precise unfolding of the craving structure — the form of the māra is determined by the content of the craving, because the māra must impersonate the craved object, and impersonation requires matching the impersonated.

#### 3.3.1 Why the Pairing Is Not Arbitrary

Let us now read each pairing in subtler detail.

**First māra: expedient skill ↔ Strange Ghost.** The original sense of *guài* in classical Chinese is "shape that deviates from the ordinary," extended to "that which can change shape." The Strange Ghost can change shape — "in an instant his form may appear as a *bhikṣu*, or as Indra, or as a woman, or as a *bhikṣuṇī*." Why does the practitioner who craves expedient skill summon a shape-changing Strange Ghost? Because **the very core of "expedient skill" (skill in expedient teaching) is the verbal and formal adaptability that responds to the listener's capacities** — the one with skillful means is the one able to vary his form of teaching to suit each audience. The Strange Ghost's "ability to change shape" is exactly the materialized form of the capacity called "expedient skill."

**Second māra: wandering experience ↔ *Bá* Ghost.** The *Bá* Ghost is a drought-ghost, traveling through waterless places. Why does the practitioner who craves wandering experience (traveling all places) summon a *Bá* Ghost? Because **the original sense of "wandering" is "the ability to pass through every kind of environment,"** and the *Bá* Ghost is precisely the being able to travel through the harshest of environments (drought-land, waterless places). The *Bá* Ghost's "ability to traverse all places" is exactly the materialized form of "craving wandering."

**Third māra: fusion ↔ *Mèi* Ghost.** The original sense of *mèi* is "to seduce one into merging" — the *Mèi* Ghost causes the deceived to abandon themselves and merge with it. Why does the practitioner who craves fusion summon a *Mèi* Ghost? Because **the very core of "fusion" is "the merger of subject with target,"** and the *Mèi* Ghost is precisely the embodiment of such merger — its mode of existence consists in causing the bewitched to surrender their selfhood and merge with the *Mèi* Ghost. The *Mèi* Ghost's "ability to fuse" is exactly the materialized form of "craving fusion."

**Fourth māra: sharp analysis ↔ *Gu*-Poison Ghost.** *Gu*-poison was the classical Chinese name for an extraordinarily fine poison able to penetrate to the marrow. Why does the practitioner who craves sharp analysis summon a *Gu*-Poison Ghost? Because **the very core of "sharp analysis" is "the capacity for discrimination so fine it penetrates to the marrow,"** and *gu*-poison is precisely the substance able to penetrate to the marrow — to reach where ordinary poisons cannot. *Gu*-poison's "marrow-penetration" is exactly the materialized form of "craving sharp analysis."

**Fifth māra: obscure resonance ↔ Pestilence Ghost.** The Pestilence Ghost is a plague-ghost; pestilential vapor (癘氣) is an invisible medium of transmission. Why does the practitioner who craves obscure resonance (formless responsiveness) summon a Pestilence Ghost? Because **the very core of "obscure resonance" is "the formless transmission of co-vibration,"** and pestilential vapor is precisely the formless transmitter — bodiless yet able to pass between people. The pestilential vapor's "formless transmission" is exactly the materialized form of "craving obscure resonance."

**Sixth māra: silent stillness ↔ Powerful Ghost.** Why does the practitioner who craves silent stillness (deep quiescence) summon a *Powerful* Ghost? This is the pairing most likely to puzzle a reader — stillness and power seem opposites. But this is exactly where the pairing becomes most subtle: **true "silent stillness" is not "absence of sound" but "force strong enough to suppress all sound."** Both Zǐxuán and Jiāoguāng note this aspect of the force-quality of stillness — "grinding himself down with hardship, delighting in dark seclusion" is an active, exertional, intensive stillness, not a passive quiet. The Powerful Ghost's "suppressing-all" is exactly the materialized form of "stillness as force."

**Seventh māra: knowledge of past lives ↔ Mountain-Forest Spirits.** Why does the practitioner who craves knowledge of past lives summon mountain-forest spirits? Because **the very core of "knowledge of past lives" is "the knowledge of the ancient past,"** and the mountain, earth, city-god, and river-mountain spirits are beings "long-resident in one place, with extraordinarily deep sedimentation in time" — they have lived in a place for centuries or millennia, witnessing innumerable generations, knowing innumerable past events. Their "time-sedimentation" is exactly the materialized form of "craving knowledge of past lives."

**Eighth māra: divine power ↔ Powerful Essences.** This pairing is the most direct of all — the powerful essences are themselves the embodiment of supernormal force. The Great Powerful Mountain Essence, Sea Essence, Wind Essence, River Essence, Earth Essence each represent the crystallized essence of some natural force. Their "essence of natural force" is exactly the materialized form of "craving divine power."

**Ninth māra: deep emptiness ↔ Eclipse Essence-Vapors.** *Bó shí* (薄蝕) means "the sun and moon swallowed and their light obscured" — the classical Chinese description of solar and lunar eclipses. Why does the practitioner who craves deep emptiness summon eclipse essence-vapors? Because **the very core of "deep emptiness" is the image of "luminosity swallowed by emptiness"** — the limit of emptiness is "all things returning to emptiness," and the eclipse is the most direct astronomical image of that returning to emptiness. The eclipse essence-vapors' "swallowing of light" is exactly the materialized form of "craving deep emptiness."

**Tenth māra: eternal lifespan ↔ Sovereign Heavenly Māra.** Why does the practitioner who craves eternal lifespan (deathlessness) summon the Sovereign Heavenly Māra (the king of the desire-realm peak)? Because **the very core of "eternal lifespan" is the grasping at "permanently possessing this body within the desire realm" — that is, the highest form of desire-realm grasping.** *Paranirmita-vaśavartin* is the highest of the desire-realm heavens; the Sovereign Heavenly Māra is its sovereign — he himself represents "the longest-lived, most sovereign, most unwilling-to-let-go being of the desire realm." His "supreme desire-realm long-life sovereignty" is exactly the materialized form of "craving eternal lifespan" — and because this is the highest form of desire-realm grasping, the corresponding mirror-other is no longer a low-grade ghost-essence but the celestial māra-king himself, intervening in person.

#### 3.3.2 The Overall Pattern: From Lower to Higher Refinement

Setting all ten pairings side by side reveals an extraordinarily fine overall pattern: **the *spiritual rank* of the mirror-other escalates with the refinement of the craving.** The first five māras (expedient skill, wandering, fusion, sharp analysis, obscure resonance) correspond to relatively low-rank ghost-types (Strange, *Bá*, *Mèi*, *Gu*-Poison, Pestilence) — concrete-form ghosts. The middle three māras (silent stillness, knowledge of past lives, divine power) correspond to subtler beings (Powerful Ghost, mountain-forest spirits, powerful natural essences) — verging on natural deities. The ninth māra (deep emptiness) corresponds to a cosmic-scale essence-vapor (the eclipse). The tenth (eternal lifespan) corresponds to the celestial māra-king himself, sovereign of the desire-realm peak.

**From the Strange Ghost to the celestial māra-king, the mirror-other's rank rises continuously, exactly tracking the refinement of the ten cravings — from relatively external (rhetorical capacity) to absolutely internal (the deepest grasping at this body).** This overall pattern is the inner architecture of the Śūraṅgama, not a forced reading. The text arranges the ten māras in this order to tell us: **the deeper the craving, the stronger the mirror-other; the stronger the mirror-other, the harder the practitioner finds it to detect.** The first māra's Strange Ghost is relatively easy to detect (its "now a *bhikṣu*, now a woman" shape-shifting is too obvious); the tenth māra's Sovereign Heavenly Māra is nearly impossible to detect (he manifests directly as Vajrapāṇi or as a beautiful woman, and can manifest "without a teacher, the practitioner perceiving him directly" — that is, with no possessed-person intermediary, in the form of pure hallucination). From the first māra to the tenth is a sequence of escalating danger.

#### 3.3.3 The Four Laws of the Mirror-Other

Synthesizing the foregoing observations, we may distill four basic laws of the mirror-other:

1. **The Law of Form-Correspondence.** The nature of the mirror-other must match the craved capacity — the "shape-changing ghost" answers to "craving expedient skill," the "traveling ghost" to "craving wandering experience," and so on.
2. **The Law of Spiritual-Rank Correspondence.** The spiritual rank of the mirror-other escalates with the refinement of the craving — from low-grade ghost-essence to natural deity to the desire-realm-peak heavenly māra-king.
3. **The Law of Inverse Detectability.** The higher the rank of the mirror-other, the more closely it approaches the appearance of a "true spiritual friend," and therefore the harder it is to detect. The first māra is easiest; the tenth nearly impossible.
4. **The Law of Decreasing Intermediation.** The higher the rank of the mirror-other, the less it requires a third-party intermediary (the possessed person). The first nine māras all require "dispatching essence to possess a person." The tenth can occur "without a teacher, the practitioner perceiving directly" — manifesting as pure hallucination without intermediary.

These four laws together draw an exceptionally precise phenomenological map: **the ten perception-aggregate māras are not ten randomly arranged events but ten stages in the refinement of craving, each stage answered by a structurally necessary mirror-other.**

### 3.4 "Cannot Enter the Heart-Organs" — The Māra's Relational Pathway and the Practitioner's Protective Structure

Jiāoguāng's critical annotation — "because once the receptive aggregate is exhausted [the māra] cannot enter the heart-organs, it borrows a bystander to confuse him, turning him to disturb himself" — deserves still deeper unfolding. The sentence carries a paradoxical structural insight: **at the level of the heart-organs the perception-aggregate practitioner is protected, but precisely this protection forces the māra to turn to a more externally manifest, more difficult-to-detect, relational pathway.**

Let us articulate the paradox. The receptive-aggregate practitioner has not yet completed "exhaustion of the receptive aggregate"; his heart-organs still have a fissure — the māra can "darkly enter the heart, causing him without awareness to turn upon himself" (Jiāoguāng). From one angle, this is the receptive-aggregate stage's weakness; from another angle, it is also its "alarm system" — when the māra darkly enters the heart, the practitioner at least retains some possibility of perceiving subtle inner signals that something is wrong (although the words "without awareness" mark how difficult that perception is).

The perception-aggregate practitioner has completed "exhaustion of the receptive aggregate"; his heart-organs no longer have a fissure — the māra cannot enter from within. From one angle, this is the perception-aggregate stage's progress; from another angle, it brings a new vulnerability: **because the māra cannot enter from within, he must enter from without — and entry from without is precisely where the practitioner is least defended.**

Why? Because all the practitioner's meditative training is directed *inward* — he trains himself not to be disturbed by external objects, not to be carried away by inner emotions, not to chase after thoughts. His entire defense system faces inward. But when the māra appears from outside, and not in the form of "disturbance" but in the form of "spiritual friend," the practitioner's defensive system fails entirely — because "spiritual friend" is not an object to be defended against; "spiritual friend" is an object to be approached, trusted, served. The practitioner's entire training has taught him to *approach* spiritual friends; no part of his training has taught him to *defend against* spiritual friends.

This is the structural trap of the perception-aggregate stage: **the practitioner's interior is now protected (the receptive aggregate is exhausted), so the māra can come only from outside; but the practitioner's exterior defense is not designed for "spiritual-friend forms," so a māra coming from outside passes his defenses without obstruction.** Internal protection becomes external vulnerability. This is not a failure of meditative training but the natural limit of meditative structure — any training directed only inward cannot handle the relational problem of facing outward.

The direction of the corrective response must therefore include two aspects. First, **internal "balance of *samādhi* and wisdom"** (Zǐxuán) remains necessary — but this cannot solve the external problem. Second, **external "discernment of spiritual friends"** — a capacity that meditation itself does not provide and that requires verbal *prajñā* (knowledge of canonical teachings), the protection of *vinaya* (traditional knowledge of "dispatched essence possessing a person"), and a long-standing relationship with a real, authentic spiritual friend as its support. **What the perception-aggregate practitioner most needs is not deeper meditation but a wider knowledge of māric events.** This is the fundamental reason the Śūraṅgama devotes such enormous space in fascicle 9 to unfolding the fifty skandha-māras — it is the *recognition manual* prepared for this stage of practitioner, not a guide to deeper meditation.

### 3.5 From the Āgamas' "Seek the Teacher" to the Śūraṅgama's "Dispatched Essence Possessing a Person" — The Mirror Inversion of the Spiritual-Friend Structure

§3.2 has already tabulated the contrast between the forward spiritual-friend chain and the reverse mirror-other chain. This section deepens the contrast through historical and structural analysis, connecting the early Āgama teaching and the later Śūraṅgama teaching into a single complete genealogical line.

The core presupposition of the Āgama's "seek the teacher" structure is this: **a true spiritual friend is rare, requires active seeking, and the criterion of the seeking is "able to sever the three cravings," not "able to manifest capacity."** This presupposition is most clearly displayed in the *Madhyama Āgama*'s "successive completion of cravings-for-existence" therapeutic chain — its starting point is "associating with true persons," its endpoint is "knowledge and liberation." Each link concerns not the *display of capacity* but the *severance of defilement*. True person → spiritual friend → hearing the true Dharma → faith → right reflection → right mindfulness and right knowing → guarding the faculties → the three wholesome conducts → the seven factors of awakening → knowledge and liberation — there is no "supernormal power," no "eloquence," no "knowledge of past lives," no "eternal life" anywhere in the chain. Not a single link is about *capacity*.

In other words, **the Āgama's "seek the teacher" structure is *severance-oriented*, not *capacity-oriented*.** A person able to sever the three cravings is the true teacher, and the mark of being-able-to-sever is not the display of some capacity but the cessation of one's own afflictions.

The mirror inversion of the ten perception-aggregate māras occurs precisely at the moment the *orientation* is substituted. At the moment the practitioner's inner motive flips from "severance" to "obtaining capacity," the criterion he applies in seeking a teacher flips with it — he no longer seeks "one able to sever the three cravings" but "one able to display some capacity." Once the criterion changes, the object changes; once the object changes, the mirror-other has its standing-ground.

This is why each of the ten māras takes "craving capacity X" as its starting point, not "craving the severance of defilement X." The text's phrasing is exact — it never says "craving the severance of greed," "craving the severance of hatred," "craving the severance of delusion" (which would be *severance-oriented* phrasing); it always says "craving expedient skill," "craving divine power," "craving knowledge of past lives" (which is *capacity-oriented* phrasing). **The single-character difference between "sever" and "obtain" is the entire boundary between the forward spiritual-friend chain and the reverse mirror chain.**

For the reader this provides an extraordinarily practical diagnostic tool: **when you find yourself seeking a "teacher able to display some capacity," the alarm should already be ringing. When you find yourself seeking a "teacher able to help you sever some attachment," you are most probably still inside the Āgama's forward structure.** The criterion is "capacity-display" or "cessation-of-affliction" — this distinction is the diagnostic line of the ten perception-aggregate māras at its root.

### 3.6 The Distribution of the Three Cravings within the Ten Cravings (Borrowed for Differential Diagnosis)

As P09 used the three sensations and three latent tendencies as a differential-diagnostic tool for the ten receptive-aggregate māras, P10 uses the Āgama's three cravings (*kāma*, *rūpa*, *arūpa*) as a differential-diagnostic tool for the ten perception-aggregate māras. The same methodological caveat must be stressed: **the correspondence is *inclinational*, not *exclusive*.** Each of the ten cravings internally contains traces of all three cravings; the table marks only the dominant component, as a diagnostic starting point rather than a classificatory dogma.

| Three-craving system | Corresponding ten cravings | Structural feature |
|---|---|---|
| *Kāma-tṛṣṇā* (sensual) | 1 expedient skill, 2 wandering, 5 obscure resonance, 10 eternal lifespan | craving for "performative experience" or "grasping of this body" |
| *Rūpa-tṛṣṇā* (form-realm) | 3 fusion, 4 sharp analysis, 6 silent stillness, 7 knowledge of past lives, 8 divine power | craving for "form-realm *dhyāna*-factors and supernormal capacities" |
| *Arūpa-tṛṣṇā* (formless) | 9 deep emptiness | craving for "the ultimate mark of the formless realm" |

**The four sensual-craving cravings** (expedient skill, wandering, obscure resonance, eternal lifespan) share the structure of "performative experience or grasping of this body": expedient skill is the performance of eloquence; wandering is the performance of mobility; obscure resonance is the performance of responsiveness to external situations; eternal lifespan is the ultimate grasping of this body. They all answer to *kāma-tṛṣṇā* — the form of craving closest to the desire-realm root motive. Note the placement of the tenth māra (eternal lifespan) here: although "eternal lifespan" sounds like a transcendence of the desire realm, it is in fact the highest form of desire-realm grasping — "permanently possessing this body in the desire realm" is the final crystallization of *kāma-tṛṣṇā*, which is why its corresponding mirror-other is the king of the desire-realm peak heaven himself.

**The five form-realm cravings** (fusion, sharp analysis, silent stillness, knowledge of past lives, divine power) share the structure of "craving form-realm *dhyāna*-factors and supernormal capacities": fusion answers to form-realm meditative merger; sharp analysis to form-realm wisdom-discrimination; silent stillness to form-realm deep concentration; knowledge of past lives to form-realm temporal-spatial supernormal capacity; divine power to form-realm transformative supernormal capacity. The form realm is the realm of "form without desire," and these five cravings all revolve around the "form-without-desire" *dhyāna* capacities.

**The single formless-realm craving** (deep emptiness) is the craving for the ultimate mark of the formless realm — "craving deep emptiness" is exactly the desire for the formless realm's ultimate quiescence. Note that only one craving falls in this category, not more — this is because the formless realm is by nature *formless*, and most of the perception-aggregate cravings concern "displayable capacities" (which require *form*), so *arūpa-tṛṣṇā* receives only one slot in the ten perception-aggregate māras.

#### 3.6.1 Why the Tenth Māra Is *Kāma-tṛṣṇā* and Not *Arūpa-tṛṣṇā*

A reader may object: the tenth māra "eternal lifespan" pursues "abandoning segmental birth-and-death, abruptly aspiring to transformative birth-and-death, [seeking] the subtle mark to abide permanently" — this sounds like an aspiration to transcend the desire realm and enter a more refined mode of existence. Why classify it under *kāma-tṛṣṇā*?

The answer is: **"aspiring to transformative permanence" sounds refined, but its essence is still "unwilling to relinquish this body."** The core of *kāma-tṛṣṇā* is not "the specific grasping at the five sense-pleasures" but "the grasping at *possession itself*" — the grasping at the structure of "what I possess." When the practitioner "craves eternal lifespan," what he pursues is not "entry into the formless realm's neither-existence-nor-nonexistence" but "permanently possessing this body in some more refined form." The four characters *xì xiàng cháng zhù* (subtle mark to abide permanently) give it all away — what he wants is the *permanent abiding* (永遠擁有) of a *mark* (a form of existence). This is still the structure of "possession," only the object of possession has shifted from "a coarse body" to "a subtle mark." Essentially, it is the most refined version of *kāma-tṛṣṇā*, not a form of *arūpa-tṛṣṇā*.

This is why the mirror-other of the tenth māra is the desire-realm-peak Sovereign Heavenly Māra — the most refined form of *kāma-tṛṣṇā* requires the highest desire-realm being to play the mirror. The text's arrangement here once again shows its precision.

### 3.7 From External Māra to Mind-Māra — The Axis of Transition at Perception-Aggregate Exhaustion

Zǐxuán's annotation at the opening of fascicle 10 (the formations-aggregate section) — "while the perception aggregate is not yet exhausted, external māras are still drawn in; now that the perception aggregate is exhausted and the formations aggregate is laid bare, [the practitioner] generates errant views regarding his perceived objects, grasping these as right — and so we speak of mind-māras" — is the bridge from P10 to P11.

It announces a major structural transition: **at the moment the perception aggregate is exhausted, the external māra exits the stage and the mind-māra enters.** From that point on, the practitioner faces no longer external celestial māras, ghosts, or *piśācas* but his own "errant views generated regarding perceived objects" — the grasping at errant views generated by his own mind regarding the objects of awareness. The mirror-teacher disappears, but the *mirror-student* remains.

What is the structural significance of this transition? Let us approach it from several angles.

**First, from the angle of object-structure.** The object of the external māra is "the possession of an external capacity" (which requires a mirror-other as object). The object of the mind-māra is "the practitioner's interpretation of his own perceived objects" (which requires no mirror-other, only the practitioner's own awareness and interpretation). The external māra exits because its object-structure has lost its purchase — once the practitioner has crossed the perception aggregate and entered the formations aggregate, the motive of "craving for external capacity" has been resolved (or has become so refined that it no longer requires external display for verification), so the external māra has lost its job.

**Second, from the angle of protective mechanism.** Before the perception aggregate is exhausted, the practitioner's interior still has the breach of "craving for the external," and the external māra can enter through that breach. After perception-aggregate exhaustion, the breach is sealed — the practitioner no longer craves any external capacity. But this sealing does not mean the practitioner is fully safe; it means, on the contrary, that the danger has shifted to the interior: all the practitioner's problems now unfold within his own mind, with no external object to share the burden of the inward problem. The danger of the mind-māra is that it has no external form to be exposed — you cannot detect a mind-māra by "observing the one ascending the seat to expound," because the mind-māra has no "that one," only "your own mind."

**Third, from the angle of detection difficulty.** The external māra, although manifest, can still be detected — if the practitioner has sufficient canonical knowledge (knows the structure of "dispatching essence to possess a person"), sufficient *vinaya* protection, and the contrast of a real spiritual friend, he can at some moment notice "this person isn't right." The mind-māra is wholly different — it has no "other party" to observe, only "one's own mind" to observe, and the very mind that observes one's own mind may itself already be contaminated by the mind-māra. **The external māra can be detected; the mind-māra can only be traversed.** This is why the formations-aggregate māras of P11 are deeper than the receptive- and perception-aggregate māras of P09 and P10 — there is no external enemy, only a deeper self.

P11 will begin from this transition point and unfold a complete answer to the question "what does the practitioner face after the external māra exits."

## 4. Interdisciplinary Resonance

### 4.1 ⚠️ A Minimal Contemporary Analogy: Parasocial Relationships and the Guru Effect

Contemporary social psychology has long studied a phenomenon called the *parasocial relationship* — the one-directional emotional bond an audience forms toward a media figure (television presenter, online personality, fictional character). The viewer believes they "know" the figure, believes they share a real relationship with the figure, when in fact the relationship is wholly one-directional: the figure does not know the viewer exists, while the viewer projects substantial emotion, trust, and identification onto the figure.[^22]

A more recent line of research on the *guru effect* extends this further: when a person craves some capacity or some spiritual state, they tend to follow a figure who appears to have already realized that capacity or state — and the following is often based not on objective evaluation of the figure but on projection of "I want to become like that."[^23]

These two contemporary phenomena, considered as **heuristic analogies**, share structural features with the mirror-other framework of the ten perception-aggregate māras:
- **Parasocial relationship** roughly corresponds to "the practitioner believes himself to have a real teacher-student relationship with the possessed person" (when in fact it is one-way projection).
- **Guru effect** roughly corresponds to "the practitioner follows a figure based on his craving for some capacity that figure appears to display" (when in fact this is the objectification of craving).

But the analogy is *only* heuristic. Four points of non-equivalence must be marked explicitly. ⚠️

1. **Difference of level.** Contemporary parasocial / guru-effect research studies the coarse psychological phenomena of the ordinary person (infatuation with celebrities, following of cult leaders); the ten perception-aggregate māras concern the refined structure at the depths of meditation (craving for meditative capacities).
2. **Difference of motive.** In contemporary research the follower's motive is typically secular (desire for respect, belonging, recognition); in the ten perception-aggregate māras the practitioner's motive is religious (desire for awakening, liberation, Buddhahood).
3. **Difference of object.** In contemporary research the "followed figure" is a conscious person (though potentially playing a role); in the ten perception-aggregate māras the "possessed person" is genuinely possessed by an essence-spirit and *himself* unaware ("the person himself is unaware that he is possessed by the māra").
4. **Difference of corrective direction.** Contemporary psychology's corrective direction is "reduce projection, build a real relationship"; the Śūraṅgama's corrective direction is "discern the mirror, see through the structure of craving itself."

P08 has already established the methodology of *clinical differential diagnosis*, and this section merely extends that methodology to a finer contemporary analogy without further elaboration. The core corrective for the ten perception-aggregate māras remains, throughout, the Āgama's "seek the great teacher" structure and the Śūraṅgama's "discernment of the mirror-other" — not contemporary social psychology.

## 5. Middle-Way Correction

This section advances six middle-way corrections.

### 5.1 ❌ The Ten Perception-Aggregate Māras Are Not a Folk Demonology

The fascicle-9 list of ten ghost-essences (Strange Ghost, *Bá* Ghost, *Mèi* Ghost, *Gu*-Poison Ghost, Pestilence Ghost, Powerful Ghost, mountain-forest spirits, powerful essences, eclipse essence-vapors, Sovereign Heavenly Māra) is often read literally as "the Śūraṅgama asserts the existence of ten ghost-spirit entities." This is a serious misreading. The naming of the ten ghost-essences is *phenomenological classification* — they are the traditional names for ten "objectified forms of craving," not an ontological assertion of ten independent ghost-spirit entities.

Both Zǐxuán and Jiāoguāng are clear on this. Zǐxuán uses "according to their own kind" and "tempered loving-mind"; Jiāoguāng uses "essence-spirits dispatched by the celestial māra" and "borrowing a bystander to confuse him, turning him to disturb himself." Both locate the source of the ghost-essences in the practitioner's own inner structure and in the celestial māra's tactical maneuvering — not in some independent ghost-spirit world.

Reading the ten perception-aggregate māras as folk demonology is to read a medical textbook as a horror anthology — the categorial error compounds into interpretive error.

### 5.2 ❌ "Dispatching Essence to Possess a Person" Does Not Mean All Meditators Will Encounter Possessed Hosts

The "dispatching essence to possess a person" structure occurs in the specific context of the perception-aggregate stage — that is, when the practitioner has crossed the form aggregate, broken through the receptive aggregate, and entered the perception aggregate, and has begun to generate refined craving for an external capacity. It is not a general meditative experience but an extraordinarily specific structural trap. To generalize "dispatching essence to possess a person" into "any meditation will summon a possessed person" is a serious misreading that turns the entire fifty-skandha-māras framework into an argument against meditation — exactly the misuse already warned against in P09 §5.6.

More importantly, even within the perception-aggregate stage, "dispatching essence to possess a person" is only the concrete mechanism that follows once the celestial māra has "gained his opportunity"; it is not an automatically occurring event. If the practitioner has no breach of craving, the celestial māra "does not gain his opportunity" and the dispatching does not happen. The fundamental corrective is not "avoid meditation" but "avoid craving."

### 5.3 ❌ The Mirror-Other Does Not Mean Every Spiritual Friend Is Counterfeit

This paper has repeatedly emphasized that the "mirror-other" is the celestial māra's exploitation of the craving structure, but this does *not* mean that every spiritual friend is counterfeit, every teacher a mirror. **Real spiritual friends exist, and they are the fundamental refuge for the severance of the three cravings** — exactly the foundational position of *Saṃyukta Āgama* 895's "for the sake of severing the three cravings, seek a great teacher."

The difference between a real spiritual friend and a mirror-māra-teacher does not lie in external form (the two may be externally indistinguishable) but in inner dynamic: **a real spiritual friend leads toward the severance of craving; a mirror-māra-teacher feeds craving.** If the longer you follow a teacher the *more* refined and intense your craving (for capacity, for fame, for the feeling of "I have arrived") becomes, you are most probably inside a mirror chain. If the longer you follow a teacher the *fainter* your grasping becomes, the *weaker* your clinging to "I," the *sharper* your detection of defilement, you are most probably inside a forward chain. The criterion is "augmentation of craving versus cessation of affliction," not "displayed capacity versus ordinary inconspicuousness."

Generalizing the mirror-other into "do not trust any spiritual friend" is another extreme misreading — it pushes the practitioner into the solitary practice of having no spiritual friend at all, which is itself another trap (the loss of the very starting point of the Āgama's "associate with the true person" therapeutic chain).

### 5.4 ❌ The Manifest Quality of the Perception-Aggregate Māra Does Not Mean Greater Danger

In phenomenological visibility, the perception-aggregate māra (manifesting externally) is more visible than the receptive-aggregate māra (concealed within); it shows itself through another person's mouth, another person's form, another person's "spiritual friend" identity. But "more visible" does not mean "more dangerous." In fact, **because the form of the perception-aggregate māra is external, it is in principle detectable** — provided the practitioner has sufficient canonical knowledge, *vinaya* protection, and the contrast of real spiritual friends, at some moment he can notice "this person isn't right."

The receptive-aggregate māra is, on the contrary, harder to detect, because it has no external object to observe (P09 §3.4 has already argued this). And the formations-aggregate māra (which P11 will treat) is harder still, because it has not even an "object" — only the practitioner's own mind. **The progression from receptive to perception aggregate is in the direction of "more visible"; the progression from perception to formations aggregate is in the direction of "more profound."** Visibility and danger are not the same dimension.

### 5.5 ❌ "Craving" Does Not Equal All Path-Seeking Aspiration

The Śūraṅgama's repeated criticism of "craving" (craving expedient skill, craving wandering experience, craving fusion…) is often read as "a critique of all path-seeking aspiration." This is wrong. The Buddhist tradition distinguishes *tṛṣṇā* (craving) from *dharmacchanda* (the wholesome desire for the Dharma) — the former is binding grasping, the latter is the wholesome desire that drives practice. Without *dharmacchanda* there is no motive for practice; with *tṛṣṇā* there is bondage.

What separates them? The core distinction is **object and motive**. *Dharmacchanda*'s object is "severing afflictions, realizing the real," its motive is "for the welfare of beings"; craving's object is "obtaining some capacity or state," its motive is "for the welfare of self." **Wholesome desire points toward severing; craving points toward obtaining.** From this angle, "craving expedient skill" is craving (because it points toward "obtaining expedient skill"); "studying expedient skill in order to benefit beings" is *dharmacchanda* (because it points toward "service to others"). The same external behavior can be either craving or wholesome desire; the difference lies only in the inner orientation.

The corrective direction is not to extinguish all path-seeking aspiration but to shift the inner orientation of the path-seeking from "for self-acquisition" to "for the severance of affliction in service of others" — and this shift is precisely the structural core of the Mahāyāna bodhisattva path.

### 5.6 ❌ "Exhaustion of the Perception Aggregate" Is Not the Emptying of Consciousness

Zǐxuán and Jiāoguāng describe "exhaustion of the perception aggregate" with phrases such as "moving thoughts exhausted, floating notions dissolved, the awareness-luminous mind cleansed of dust like the removal of stains." This is often read literally as "consciousness is wholly emptied." This is a serious misreading. **Exhaustion of the perception aggregate is "the cessation of floating false thought," not "the cessation of all conscious activity."**

*Saṃjñā* (perception/想) in Buddhist psychology has a specific meaning — it is "the capacity for grasping the image of, recognizing, and conceptualizing the object of awareness." Exhaustion of the perception aggregate is the cessation of this image-grasping–recognition–conceptualization process; this is not equivalent to consciousness itself stopping. In fact, the practitioner whose perception aggregate is exhausted "upon the awareness-luminous mind, as dust and stains are removed, illuminates the entire generation-and-cessation of one cycle of birth-and-death from beginning to end" — his awareness becomes more luminous, capable of round illumination of an entire class of beings' birth-and-death. This is plainly not "emptying of consciousness" but "the cessation of one specific function (image-grasping) of consciousness."

Misreading "exhaustion of the perception aggregate" as "emptying of consciousness" leads practitioners to set the goal of meditation as "no thought, blankness, nothing at all" — a direction that contradicts the entire Śūraṅgama exposition and that is itself a subtle form of grasping (grasping at "nothingness").

## 6. Conclusion

### 6.1 Summary of P10 — The Structural Necessity of the Mirror-Other

The central thesis of this paper is: **the ten māras of the perception aggregate are not ten distinct demonic-essence problems but ten objectifications of one structure — craving. Each craving conjures a "mirror-other" — a person who appears to already possess what the practitioner craves — and through that other's form, voice, and teaching the māra completes its disturbance of the practitioner.**

"Dispatching essence to possess a person" is not a random tactic deployed by the celestial māra but the necessary phenomenological unfolding of the craving structure itself. The receptive-aggregate māra can "turn upon itself" (Jiāoguāng) because its object is an internal state; the perception-aggregate māra must "possess a person" because its object is an external capacity. Capacity demands display, display demands a performer, and the performer is the possessed person. Zǐxuán and Jiāoguāng arrive at a single judgment on this point: "while the perception aggregate is not yet exhausted, external māras are still drawn in" (Zǐxuán); "because once the receptive aggregate is exhausted [the māra] cannot enter the heart-organs, it borrows a bystander to confuse him" (Jiāoguāng) — once the receptive aggregate has been broken through, the māra can no longer enter from within and must work from without through a third party.

The paper unfolds this thesis through seven layers of argument: (1) why the perception-aggregate māra must manifest externally — because craving for an external capacity requires an external target object; (2) the ten perception-aggregate māras as the mirror inversion of the "seek the great teacher" structure — same structure, opposite direction; (3) the phenomenological pairing of ten cravings with ten mirror-others — the four basic laws; (4) the paradoxical structure of Jiāoguāng's "cannot enter the heart-organs" — internal protection becoming external vulnerability; (5) the genealogical line from the Āgama's "seek the teacher" to the Śūraṅgama's "dispatching essence to possess a person" — the criterion shifting from "able to sever the three cravings" to "able to display capacity"; (6) the inclination distribution of the three cravings within the ten cravings — four sensual, five form-realm, one formless; (7) the transition from external māra to mind-māra — the moment the perception aggregate is exhausted, the external māra exits and the mind-māra enters (the bridge to P11).

The phenomenological pairing of "ten cravings → ten mirror-others" is the paper's most important original contribution. It discloses that the inner structure of the ten perception-aggregate māras is not ten randomly arranged events but ten stages in the refinement of craving, each stage answered by a structurally necessary mirror-other form. The Strange Ghost as strange, the *Bá* Ghost as drought, the *Mèi* Ghost as enchantment, the *gu*-poison as marrow-penetration, the pestilence as formless transmission, the powerful ghost as suppressing force, mountain-forest spirits as time-sedimentation, powerful essences as embodied force, eclipse-essence as light-swallowing, Sovereign Heavenly Māra as desire-realm-peak — each ghost-essence's nature precisely answers the craving-object it is to impersonate. This is not a superstitious detail of the Śūraṅgama but the precise unfolding of the craving structure.

Zǐxuán cuts to the bone in eight characters: ***cuì lì àixīn, lìng qí jīngmiào, gù tān shànqiǎo*** (淬利愛心，令其精妙，故貪善巧) — *he tempers and sharpens his loving-mind, making it subtle and refined; therefore he craves expedient skill*. The source of the ten perception-aggregate māras is not some external demon but the result of the practitioner's own loving-mind being sharpened by deep meditation. The previous "errant cogitations" (the receptive-aggregate māras) have been overcome — but what arises after their overcoming is not love-lessness but *tempered love*: love processed by meditation into a sharper, subtler, more target-precise form.

### 6.2 Foreshadowing P11 — From Mirror-Teacher to Mirror-Student

Zǐxuán's critical adjudication — "while the perception aggregate is not yet exhausted, external māras are still drawn in; now that the perception aggregate is exhausted and the formations aggregate is laid bare, [the practitioner] generates errant views regarding his perceived objects, grasping these as right — and so we speak of mind-māras" — announces the structural transition that closes the perception-aggregate stage. **At the moment the perception aggregate is exhausted, the external māra exits the stage and the mind-māra enters.** The mirror-teacher disappears, but the mirror-student remains.

The central question of P11 is: when the external mirror-other disappears, what does the practitioner face? The answer: **the practitioner faces his own mind's interpretation of "perceived objects" — and the danger of this interpretation is that it has no "other party" to be detected, only "one's own mind" to observe, and the very mind that observes one's own mind may itself already be contaminated by the mind-māra.** The external māra can be detected (because it has external form); the mind-māra can only be traversed (because it has no external form).

The specific contents of the ten formations-aggregate māras (the four notions of universal permanence, the four notions of partial permanence, the four notions of finiteness, the four sophistries, the four notions of post-mortem existence-with-form, the five notions of post-mortem nonexistence, the eight notions of post-mortem neither-this-nor-that, the seven notions of annihilation, the five notions of *nirvāṇa* in the present life) are all systematic catalogues of the practitioner's "errant views generated regarding perceived objects" — they are pure speculative misjudgments, with no external "expounder of dharma" as intermediary. From perception to formations, the form of māric events shifts from *relational* (requiring an other) to *speculative* (requiring only one's own mind).

### 6.3 Open Question for P11 — Why Is the Formations-Aggregate Māra Deeper Than the Perception-Aggregate Māra?

The core question left for P11: **Why, when the māra no longer comes from outside, is the danger in fact greater? Why is the "mirror-student" harder to address than the "mirror-teacher"?**

A preliminary direction (P11 will argue this in full): because the "mirror-teacher" has form, and that which has form is in principle detectable — provided you have canonical teachings, *vinaya*, and the contrast of authentic spiritual friends, at some moment you can notice "this person isn't right." But the "mirror-student" *is the practitioner himself* — how do you detect yourself? With what do you observe the very mind that is observing? Observer and observed are the same. This structural reflexive difficulty is the core difficulty of the ten formations-aggregate māras.

Put differently: **the danger of the perception-aggregate stage is "having the wrong teacher"; the danger of the formations-aggregate stage is "having become the wrong teacher oneself."** The former can be solved by "changing teachers"; the latter has no recourse to "change," because you are yourself, you cannot change yourself out. The corrective direction must be wholly different — it must shift from "seeking the right spiritual friend" to "preserving right view within the reflexive structure." The full unfolding of this shift will be the central topic of P11.

### 6.4 A Final Word to the Reader

If you have read both P09 and P10, you may worry: ten cravings sound so subtle — how can I tell whether I have fallen into the ten perception-aggregate māras?

The answer is again two-layered, continuing the structure of P09.

The first layer is reassurance: **if you have not yet entered the depths of meditation that come after exhaustion of the receptive aggregate, you are not yet at risk of falling into the ten perception-aggregate māras.** The prerequisites of the ten perception-aggregate māras are extraordinarily high meditative foundations — "the receptive aggregate empty and subtle, encountering no errant cogitation, perfect *samādhi* unfolding in luminous clarity, the mind departed from the form like a bird leaving its cage" is the entry ticket; without that ticket, you cannot even enter the stage on which the ten perception-aggregate māras are performed. The everyday-life pursuits of eloquence, the longing for travel, curiosity about supernormal powers, the desire for longevity all belong to the coarse afflictions of the ordinary level; their corrective is the foundational *satipaṭṭhāna* on feeling and basic mindfulness training, not the "fifty skandha-māras" framework.

The second layer is preparation: **if you are practicing seriously and aspire to go deep, then etching the four laws of the mirror-other into memory is a preventive diagnostic tool**:

1. **The Law of Form-Correspondence**: the mirror-other's nature matches the craved capacity.
2. **The Law of Spiritual-Rank Correspondence**: the mirror-other's rank rises with the refinement of the craving.
3. **The Law of Inverse Detectability**: the higher the rank, the harder to detect.
4. **The Law of Decreasing Intermediation**: the higher the rank, the less it requires a third-party host.

When you find yourself following a figure "able to display some capacity," the alarm should already be ringing. When you find yourself following a figure "able to help you sever some attachment," you are most probably still inside the Āgama's forward structure. **The criterion is "capacity-display" or "cessation-of-affliction"** — this distinction is the diagnostic line of the ten perception-aggregate māras at its root.

The ten characters of *Saṃyukta Āgama* 895 remain the axis of the entire structure: *wèi duàn sān ài gù, dāng qiú dàshī* — *for the sake of severing the three cravings, seek a great teacher*. The purpose of seeking the teacher is, always, the severance of the three cravings, not the obtaining of some capacity. Once the purpose has reversed, the teacher has become a mirror.

From "severing" to "obtaining" — this is the entire substance of the ten perception-aggregate māras.
