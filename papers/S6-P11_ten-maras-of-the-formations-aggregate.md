## 摘要 Abstract

本文承續 S6-P10《想陰十魔——貪求的鏡像他者》,進入照妖鏡的第四篇——行陰十魔的鑑別診斷。本文核心命題:**行陰十魔不是十種新的錯誤,而是一張早已織好的網——《長阿含·梵動經》六十二見的禪那重演。每一種計度,都是行者觀察自己行陰流動之後對這流動所作的某種「徹底的判定」。觀察者就是被觀察者。鏡像他者退場,鏡像學生登場——行者自己就是那個被自己騙的人**。子璿在《首楞嚴義疏注經》卷十指出:「想陰未盡猶引外魔,今想陰盡行陰明露,但於所覺境界別生異見,執此為是,故云心魔。」這十六個字一刀切開五十陰魔的全部結構——從外魔三十切換為心魔二十。子璿同時對行陰十計的根源作出關鍵判詞:「皆修行至此,邪慧忽生,名為見發,非是本來別有外道。」此句徹底顛覆把行陰十魔看作「印度外道在禪定中出現」的常見誤讀,並為楞嚴與《梵動經》的結構同構打開了空間。本文最重要的獨創貢獻是「**行陰十魔四維拓樸學**」——時間軸、量詞軸、邊界軸、因果軸的窮舉性組合解釋了為什麼數目正好是六十二,以及為什麼這個數目不是歷史偶然而是結構必然。

This paper continues S6-P10 (*The Ten Māras of the Perception Aggregate: Craving's Mirror-Other*) and constitutes the fourth installment in the demon-revealing mirror series — the differential diagnosis of the ten māras of the formations aggregate (*saṃskāra-skandha*, 行陰). The central thesis: **the ten formations-aggregate māras are not ten new errors but a single net long since woven — the dhyāna repetition of the sixty-two views of the *Brahmajāla Sūtra* (長阿含·梵動經). Each calculative reckoning is some form of "exhaustive adjudication" the practitioner pronounces upon his own formations-flow after observing it. The observer is the observed. The mirror-other has exited the stage; the mirror-student has entered — the practitioner himself is now the one who deceives himself.**

Zǐxuán, in fascicle 10 of his *Yìshū zhùjīng*, supplies the pivotal adjudication: "While the perception aggregate is not yet exhausted, external māras are still drawn in. Now that the perception aggregate is exhausted and the formations aggregate is laid bare, [the practitioner] generates errant views regarding his perceived objects, grasping them as right — and so we speak of *mind-māra*" (*xiǎngyīn wèijìn yóu yǐn wàimó, jīn xiǎngyīn jìn xíngyīn mínglù, dàn yú suǒ jué jìngjiè bié shēng yìjiàn, zhí cǐ wéi shì, gù yún xīnmó* 想陰未盡猶引外魔，今想陰盡行陰明露，但於所覺境界別生異見，執此為是，故云心魔). These sixteen characters cleave the entire fifty-skandha-māra structure in half: from the *thirty external-māra states* (form, receptive, perception aggregates — ten each, all featuring the celestial māra "watching for his chance") to the *twenty mind-māra states* (formations and consciousness aggregates — ten each, with no external māric event whatsoever, only the practitioner's own errant views).

Zǐxuán supplies a second key adjudication on the source of the formations-aggregate calculations: "All of these arise when, the practitioner having reached this point, **errant wisdom suddenly arises — this is named the bursting forth of view; it is not that there are originally separate non-Buddhist schools** [coming in from outside]" (*jiē xiūxíng zhì cǐ, xiéhuì hūshēng, míng wéi jiànfā, fēi shì běnlái bié yǒu wàidào* 皆修行至此，邪慧忽生，名為見發，非是本來別有外道). This sentence overturns at the root the common misreading of the formations-aggregate māras as "the appearance of Indian non-Buddhist views during meditation." The errant views are not external schools entering the practitioner from without; they are the internal product of "errant wisdom suddenly arising" at a particular meditative juncture. The reason the ten formations-aggregate māras correspond one-to-one to the historical Indian non-Buddhist positions (*ucchedavāda*, *śāśvatavāda*, the *amarāvikkhepika* equivocators, and so on) is not contagion but **structural isomorphism**: "errant adjudication of the formations aggregate" is itself an *exhaustive structural enumeration*, and any practitioner who enters deep meditation but turns wrong at the formations-aggregate stage will, of necessity, fall into this net in some form. This is the cross-tradition isomorphic resonance, across more than seven centuries of teaching history, between the Śūraṅgama and the *Brahmajāla Sūtra*.

The paper proceeds along four axes. **First**, taking as point of entry the *Long Āgama Brahmajāla*'s simile of the fisherman — "as a skilled fisherman covers a small pond with a fine-meshed net, all the water-creatures of the pond enter the net with no place to escape" — it establishes the sixty-two views as an *exhaustive structural enumeration of errant adjudications upon the nature of self and world*, then exhibits its line-by-line correspondence with the Śūraṅgama's ten formations-aggregate māras. **Second**, it grounds the analysis in the early canonical sources (the *Brahmajāla Sūtra* itself; *Saṃyukta Āgama* 568 on the three layers of *saṃskāra*; *Saṃyukta Āgama* 298 and 576 on the formations and impermanence; *Madhyama Āgama* 189, the *Sacred Path Sūtra*, on right view as the head of the eightfold path) and in the Śūraṅgama's fascicle-10 passages on the formations-aggregate territory and the ten māras. **Third**, it advances the paper's most important original contribution: the **four-axis topology of the ten formations-aggregate māras** — a temporal axis (past / present / future), a quantifier axis (universal / particular / both / neither, the *catuṣkoṭi*), a boundary axis (finite / infinite), and a causal axis (caused / uncaused). Any "exhaustive adjudication of the flow of formations" must occupy some position in this four-dimensional space; the number of positions is not arbitrary but the structurally necessary product of the dimensional combinatorics. **Fourth**, drawing on Zǐxuán and Jiāoguāng's joint adjudication of *mind-māra*, it gives the direct answer to the open question left at the close of P10: *why is the reflexive structure harder to overcome than the mirror-other?* — through three structural arguments (the failure of the comparison-tool, the reflexive entanglement of the observation-act, and the irreversibility of the error).

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 The Fisherman Simile — The *Brahmajāla Sūtra* and a Net of Sixty-Two Meshes

On some evening in the fifth century BCE, in the bamboo grove at Magadha, the Buddha addressed the *bhikṣu*-saṅgha. The discourse was preserved as the twenty-first sūtra of the *Long Āgama* (T1), and the title eventually fixed for it was *Brahmajāla* — meaning, by the Buddha's own gloss in the closing of the discourse, that this teaching "moves the Brahmā" (*fàn dòng* 梵動): it shakes the entire metaphysical apparatus of the Indian schools of the Buddha's day.[^1]

In the body of the discourse, the Buddha enumerated, in detail, sixty-two positions on "the nature of self and world" then in circulation among *śramaṇas* and brahmins. These divide into two great groups: the **eighteen views concerning the past** (*běnjié běnjiàn* 本劫本見 — inferences regarding the prior aeon: four pervasive-permanence doctrines, four partial-permanence doctrines, four finite-and-infinite doctrines, four equivocation doctrines, two no-cause doctrines) and the **forty-four views concerning the future** (*mòjié mòjiàn* 末劫末見 — inferences regarding the latter aeon: sixteen having-perception doctrines, eight no-perception doctrines, eight neither-perception-nor-no-perception doctrines, seven annihilationist doctrines, five present-life nirvāṇa doctrines). For each of the sixty-two, the *Brahmajāla* describes the meditative background out of which it arises, the form of the inference made, and the conclusion finally reached.

After enumerating all sixty-two, the Buddha gave his closing simile:[^2]

> "Whatever views any *śramaṇas* or brahmins hold concerning the prior aeon and concerning the latter aeon — innumerable, of every kind, asserted as they please — all enter into these sixty-two views… **Just as a skilled fisherman covers a small pond with a fine-meshed net, you should know that all the water-creatures of the pond enter into the net, with no place to escape — and not one passes beyond.**"

The sharpness of the simile lies in its claim of *exhaustiveness*. The Buddha does not say "beyond the sixty-two views, more views remain possible." He says **"all enter into the net, with no place to escape."** That is: any effort to make an *exhaustive adjudication* of the nature of self and world will, in the end, fall into one of these sixty-two types. This is not because human imagination is impoverished. **It is because the task itself — "exhaustive adjudication of the nature of [a flowing object]" — has only sixty-two structural possibilities.**

At the close of the discourse, Ānanda asked: "This Dharma is profound; by what name should we call it? How should we receive and uphold it?" The Buddha replied: "Call this discourse by these names — **the Moving of Meaning, the Moving of Dharma, the Moving of Views, the Moving of Māra, the Moving of Brahmā**" (*yìdòng, fǎdòng, jiàndòng, módòng, fàndòng* 義動、法動、見動、魔動、梵動). These five names came eventually to be summarized in the single title "the *Brahmajāla*." "Moving of meaning" — it shakes every doctrinal meaning. "Moving of dharma" — it shakes every dharma-mark. "Moving of views" — it shakes every standpoint. "Moving of māra" — it shakes every māric event. "Moving of Brahmā" — it shakes every doctrine of a creator-Brahmā. **The true import of the five "movings" is this: any position from which one tries to make an exhaustive adjudication concerning self and world will be moved by this discourse.**

### 1.2 The Central Thesis — The Ten Formations-Aggregate Māras as the Dhyāna Repetition of the Sixty-Two Views

Seven centuries later,[^3] in a sūtra of an entirely different provenance, language, and doctrinal system — fascicle 10 of the *Mahā-Buddhoṣṇīṣa Śūraṅgama Sūtra* — the Buddha addressed Ānanda concerning something whose structure is astonishingly similar: **the ten *dhyāna* mad-interpretations of the formations aggregate**.

The Śūraṅgama opens this section as follows:[^4]

> "Ānanda, you should know that, in this *śamatha* of right knowing, the noble men, with mind concentrated, luminous, and upright, are not given their opportunity by the ten classes of celestial māra. They proceed to the depths of investigation, exhausting the original source of all classes of beings. Within this original source of their own class, when the source-marker is laid bare, they observe that obscure-pure perfectly-disturbing source. **From within this perfect source they begin to make calculative reckonings**, and such a person falls into the two no-cause doctrines…"

Note the opening sequence: **right knowing → *śamatha* → mind concentrated, luminous, upright → the ten classes of celestial māra are *not* given their opportunity → deepening investigation → exhausting the source of all classes of beings → observing the obscure-pure perfectly-disturbing source → beginning to make calculative reckonings within the perfect source → falling into [some particular view].** The first seven steps are extraordinarily exalted meditative attainments — and the line "*the ten classes of celestial māra are not given their opportunity*" is especially significant. It explicitly marks the fact that, **upon entering the formations-aggregate stage, the entire previous apparatus (the form-aggregate ten, the receptive-aggregate ten, the perception-aggregate ten, and the whole machinery of "the celestial māra watches for his chance") has exited the stage.** The ten classes of celestial māra (corresponding respectively to the ghost-essence emissaries of the form, receptive, and perception aggregates) "are not given their opportunity" — the practitioner has entered a position the external māra cannot any longer invade.

But it is *precisely at this position* that the three characters "*qǐ jìduó*" (起計度, "begins to make calculative reckonings") appear. And immediately following them: "falls into the two no-cause doctrines."

Between "the ten classes of celestial māra are not given their opportunity" and "falls into the two no-cause doctrines," there is no intermediary. **No dispatched essence. No possessed person. No mirror-other ascending the seat to expound the dharma. There is only the practitioner's own *calculative reckoning*. This is the structural pivot between P10 and P11: the māra no longer comes from outside. From where does it come, then? From the practitioner's own observation-act.**

The ten māras then unfold in sequence: (1) the two no-cause doctrines, (2) the four pervasive-permanence doctrines, (3) the four partial-permanence doctrines, (4) the four finite-and-infinite doctrines, (5) the four perverted, immortality-equivocating, totally-fabricated doctrines, (6) the sixteen having-form doctrines, (7) the eight no-form doctrines, (8) the eight neither-having-nor-lacking-form doctrines, (9) the seven annihilationist doctrines, and (10) the five present-life nirvāṇa doctrines.[^5] If we juxtapose this list against the sixty-two views of the *Brahmajāla Sūtra*, the correspondence is remarkably tight:

| # | Śūraṅgama: ten formations māras | *Brahmajāla*: parallel | Sub-count |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Two no-cause doctrines | Two no-cause doctrines | 2 |
| 2 | Four pervasive-permanence doctrines | Four pervasive-permanence doctrines | 4 |
| 3 | Four partial-permanence doctrines | Four partial-permanence doctrines | 4 |
| 4 | Four finite/infinite doctrines | Four finite-and-infinite doctrines | 4 |
| 5 | Four equivocation doctrines | Four immortality-equivocating doctrines | 4 |
| 6 | Sixteen having-form doctrines | Sixteen having-perception doctrines | 16 |
| 7 | Eight no-form doctrines | Eight no-perception doctrines | 8 |
| 8 | Eight neither doctrines | Eight neither-perception-nor-no-perception doctrines | 8 |
| 9 | Seven annihilationist doctrines | Seven annihilationist doctrines | 7 |
| 10 | Five present-nirvāṇa doctrines | Five present-life nirvāṇa doctrines | 5 |
| **Total** | | | **62** |

**Total: sixty-two.**

The internal sub-counts of the ten formations-aggregate māras of the Śūraṅgama, summed, are exactly sixty-two. The order, the structure, and the names run almost completely in parallel. This correspondence cannot be accidental, and it admits only one explanation: **the two sūtras are not describing two different things but a single thing. When a meditator attempts an *exhaustive adjudication of the nature of the formations aggregate*, the positions structurally available to him are exactly sixty-two.** The Buddha in the *Brahmajāla* described these sixty-two positions in early canonical idiom; the Śūraṅgama in fascicle 10 unfolded the same sixty-two positions in the idiom of Mahāyāna meditative cultivation, and adjudicated them as "ten dhyāna mad-interpretations of the formations aggregate."

This is the central thesis of P11: **the ten formations-aggregate māras are the *dhyāna* repetition of the sixty-two views. They are not ten independent errors but a single net long since woven — and the reason this net is a *net* is that it exhausts the structural possibilities of errant adjudication upon the nature of the formations aggregate.**

### 1.3 Why This Net Is "Māra" — Zǐxuán and Jiāoguāng on the Adjudication of *Mind-Māra*

If the sixty-two views were merely an "epistemological misclassification," they would constitute a philosophical problem, not a problem of practice. But the Śūraṅgama calls them **māra** — "ten māras of the formations aggregate," "*dhyāna* mad-interpretations," "falls into the Avīci hell" — and the gravity of this language demands attention. Why is a cognitive map called *māra*?

Zǐxuán, in fascicle 10 of his *Shǒulèngyán yìshū zhùjīng*, supplies the most pivotal adjudication of the entire S6 series:[^6]

> "*Niè* (孽) means 'calamity.' **While the perception aggregate is not yet exhausted, external māras are still drawn in. Now that the perception aggregate is exhausted and the formations aggregate is laid bare, [the practitioner] generates errant views regarding his perceived objects, grasping these as right — and so we speak of mind-māra.** Therefore [the Buddha] makes [the practitioner] vigilant in awareness, capable of discerning the messages, not losing right view — that he may reach the unsurpassed. So this is most carefully entrusted, transmitted and shown, that the practitioner may not enter the errant net or lose the path of right awakening."

These sixteen characters cleave the entire fifty-skandha-māra structure with one stroke. **The thirty external-māra states** (form-aggregate ten, receptive-aggregate ten, perception-aggregate ten) share a common feature: in each of them "the celestial māra watches for his chance" — the māra appears under some external form, whether as luminous visions (form aggregate), as misjudgments of internal sensation (receptive aggregate), or as the dispatched essence-spirit possessing a person to play the part of mirror-other (perception aggregate). All have *some* exterior component. **The twenty mind-māra states** (formations-aggregate ten, consciousness-aggregate ten) share a different common feature: they have no exterior form whatsoever. They *are* the practitioner's own "generating errant views regarding perceived objects, grasping them as right." Zǐxuán's two characters *xīnmó* 心魔 — "mind-māra" — name this transition with surgical precision. It is not that the external māra has grown stronger; it is that the *referent* of the word *māra* has shifted — from "disturber of external objects" to "the practitioner's mind's own *reading* of its perceived objects."

Zǐxuán's second adjudication, on the source of the formations-aggregate calculations, follows immediately:[^7]

> "[As to the way] the doctrines of the non-Buddhist schools come into being on this account: **all of these arise when, the practitioner having reached this point, errant wisdom suddenly arises — this is named the bursting forth of view; it is not that there are originally separate non-Buddhist schools [coming in from outside]**."

This sentence settles, once and for all, an issue that is easily misread. The reason the ten formations-aggregate māras correspond one-to-one to the named positions of the Indian non-Buddhist schools (*Sāṃkhya*'s primal *prakṛti*, *Naturalism*, eternalism, annihilationism…) is *not* that these Indian schools entered the meditator's mind from outside. It is that **errant adjudication of the nature of the formations aggregate is itself a structural trap, and that trap naturally generates these views.** Put differently: the Indian non-Buddhist schools are not "first existing, then contagiously transmitted to meditators." Rather: "errant adjudication of the formations aggregate has sixty-two structural possibilities; the Indian schools happen to be the historical concretions of those sixty-two structures; a meditator who falls into one of them is *birthing errant wisdom* (Zǐxuán), not *being infected*."

Jiāoguāng Zhēnjiàn, in fascicle 10 of his *Zhèngmài shū*, frames the same transition from a different angle:[^8]

> "Earlier the text spoke of *dhyāna manifesting objects* (*chánnà xiànjìng* 禪那現境) — these are cases where the celestial māra watches for his chance. Here it speaks of *dhyāna mad-interpretations* (*chánnà kuángjiě* 禪那狂解) — these are cases where **the mind-māra arises of itself, generating its own deep calamity**."

The contrast between *xiànjìng* ("manifesting objects") and *kuángjiě* ("mad interpretations") is precise. In the form, receptive, and perception aggregates, māric events take the form of "objects" — even the receptive-aggregate māras (which are purely the misjudgment of internal sensation) are still some form of *object* surfacing (sorrow, exhilaration, recollection, joy). The formations-aggregate māras are wholly different: they are not "objects" at all but *the reading of objects* — the practitioner pinning the present state down with some exhaustive conceptual framework. The two characters *kuángjiě* are especially exact: not *kuángjìng* ("mad object"), but *kuángjiě* ("mad interpretation"). What is mad is the act of interpretation, not the object interpreted.

Jiāoguāng also offers an exquisite condensation of the ten calculations:[^9]

> "Discussed in general, the ten errant interpretations **do not exit the four characters: annihilation, permanence, empty, existent** (*duàn, cháng, kōng, yǒu* 斷常空有). The first five belong to the annihilation-permanence pair; the latter five belong to the empty-existent pair."

Concretely: the first (two no-cause doctrines) is *annihilationism* (*duàn*); the second (four pervasive-permanence) is *permanence* (*cháng*); the third (four partial-permanence) is *both* (亦斷亦常); the fourth (four finite/infinite) and fifth (four equivocation) are extensions of the *both* position. The sixth (sixteen having-form) is *grasping at existent* (執有); the seventh (eight no-form) is *grasping at empty* (執空); the eighth (eight neither) is *neither existent nor empty* (雙非); the ninth (seven annihilation) is *ultimate empty annihilation*; the tenth (five present-nirvāṇa) is *ultimate stagnation in the existent*. **The ten formations-aggregate māras, in their doctrinal structure, are the exhaustive combinations of the four basic conceptual positions: annihilation, permanence, empty, existent.** Any attempt to capture the nature of the *flow of formations* using a fixed set of exhaustive concepts will, in the end, fall into one of these ten types.

Zǐxuán's and Jiāoguāng's adjudications, taken together, supply P11 with a clear working framework. **The reason the ten formations-aggregate māras are *māra* is not that there is some external disturber, but that they exhaust the structural possibilities of "the mind's exhaustive adjudication of its own perceived objects" — and each such structural adjudication is one step from the real Middle Way and is therefore a *mad interpretation*.** From this framework, all of P11's subsequent argument unfolds a single question: *when the māra no longer comes from outside, why is the danger nevertheless greater?*

## 2. Textual Basis

This section follows the *sūtra-first, treatise-second* sequence. **Part A** cites two groups of canonical sources: the Śūraṅgama's fascicle-10 passages on the formations-aggregate territory and the ten māras as the deep-meditative diagnostic system; and the Āgama corpus — the *Brahmajāla*'s sixty-two views, *Saṃyukta Āgama* 568 on the three layers of *saṃskāra*, and *Madhyama Āgama* 189's *Sacred Path Sūtra* on right view as the head of the eightfold path — as the early-text doctrinal anchor. **Part B** cites Zǐxuán's *Yìshū* (T1799) and Jiāoguāng's *Zhèngmài shū* (X275) as treatise-layer support.

### Part A: Sūtras — Śūraṅgama and Āgama

#### 2.1 Śūraṅgama Fascicle 10: The Formations-Aggregate Territory and Its Exhaustion

The Śūraṅgama opens its account of the formations-aggregate stage as follows:[^10]

> "Ānanda! Those noble men cultivating *samādhi* whose perception aggregate is exhausted — for these persons, ordinary dreams have ceased; waking and sleep are constantly one. Their awareness-luminous mind is empty and still, like a clear sky, no longer with the gross, heavy, prior-dust shadow-events… The root-source of birth-and-death is from this point laid bare. They see all twelve classes of beings throughout the ten directions, exhausting their kinds. Although they do not yet penetrate the particular causes of each life, they see the common ground of generation, like wild horses [a mirage], **shimmering and pure-disturbing, the ultimate hinge-and-socket of the floating sense-faculties and sense-objects** — **this is named the *territory of the formations aggregate*** (*xíngyīn qūyǔ* 行陰區宇). When this pure-disturbing shimmering source-nature enters the source-clarity, when one clarification clears the source-habit, like waves stilling and changing into pure water — this is named the *exhaustion of the formations aggregate* (*xíngyīn jìn* 行陰盡); such a person can transcend the turbidity of beings. Examining the cause: **the false thought of obscure concealment** (*yōuyǐn wàngxiǎng* 幽隱妄想) **is its root**."

Four key phrases reward close attention. **First**, "those whose perception aggregate is exhausted — for these persons, ordinary dreams have ceased; waking and sleep are constantly one" — the mark of the perception aggregate's exhaustion is the radical cessation of dreaming and the unification of the waking and sleeping states. **Second**, "the root-source of birth-and-death is from this point laid bare" — the mark of entry into the formations-aggregate stage is that "the root-source of birth-and-death" (*shēngmiè gēnyuán* 生滅根元) is for the first time directly present, no longer occluded by the prior three aggregates. **Third**, "like wild horses, shimmering and pure-disturbing" — the term *yěmǎ* ("wild horses") is the classical Chinese rendering of the Indic *marīci*, the *mirage of heated air*: in the meditator's observation, this is a subtle, ceaselessly shimmering disturbance — the phenomenological signature of the formations aggregate. **Fourth**, "the false thought of obscure concealment is its root" — the fundamental nature of the formations aggregate is here defined as *yōuyǐn wàngxiǎng* (corresponding to *jiāngù wàngxiǎng* 堅固妄想 for the form aggregate, *xūmíng wàngxiǎng* 虛明妄想 for the receptive, *róngtōng wàngxiǎng* 融通妄想 for the perception, and *wǎngxiàng xūwú diāndǎo wàngxiǎng* 罔象虛無顛倒妄想 for the consciousness aggregate).

The two characters *yōuyǐn* 幽隱 — "obscure concealment" — are the key to all of P11's argument. Why is the formations aggregate so named? Zǐxuán's gloss: "The arising-and-ceasing of the formations aggregate is subtle and difficult to be aware of; hence we say *obscure concealment*." Jiāoguāng goes one step further: "The formations aggregate moves stealthily, never noticed; hence we say *obscure concealment*." **"Obscure concealment" does not mean "invisible." It means: it cannot be identified, in the very moment of being seen, as the thing being seen.** It is right there — but its mode of motion is such that the ordinary act of observation cannot make it into an object. The form aggregate is *solid* (it can be taken as object); the receptive aggregate is *vacuously bright* (sensations can be pointed at); the perception aggregate is *fluid-and-fusing* (thoughts can be named); but the formations aggregate is *thought-after-thought-in-flow* — and the very act by which you would make it an object is itself a manifestation of the formations aggregate.

This is the structural difficulty of the formations-aggregate stage: **the act of observing the formations aggregate is itself within the formations aggregate.** When the practitioner "observes that obscure-pure perfectly-disturbing source" (in the Śūraṅgama's phrase), his observation-act is itself one component of the formations-flow. There is no position outside the formations aggregate from which to observe the formations aggregate. This *reflexive structure* is the deepest reason the ten formations-aggregate māras form a structural enumeration — and §3 below will unfold this directly.

#### 2.2 Śūraṅgama Fascicle 10: The Exhaustive Sequence of the Ten Formations-Aggregate Māras

Because the full text of the ten māras runs to more than ninety lines, this paper does not undertake a verbatim citation of each.[^11] But the core structure of each must be marked, because the order and inner architecture of the ten are exactly the basis for the topological analysis of §3.4 below. The ten core structures, in compressed form:

**(1) Two no-cause doctrines.** "Within the perfect source he begins to make calculative reckonings." On the basis of his eight-hundred eye-faculty merits (the capacity to see eighty thousand kalpas), he infers that *what cannot be observed beyond eighty thousand kalpas* equals *no cause*. Internally divided into "seeing the past as no-cause" and "seeing the future as no-cause."

**(2) Four pervasive-permanence doctrines.** "Within the perfect permanence he begins to make calculative reckonings." On the basis of the past kalpas he can recall in *samādhi* (twenty thousand → forty thousand → eighty thousand), he infers that "beings neither increase nor decrease, but remain perpetually gathered, never dispersing" — and so he posits "self and world are permanent." Internally divided into four: exhausting the mind-and-object-nature, exhausting the four great elements, exhausting the six sense-faculties and the *manas*, exhausting the source of perception.

**(3) Four partial-permanence doctrines.** "Within self-and-other he begins to make calculative reckonings." On the basis of the framework of "self versus other," he renders the verdict "one part permanent, one part impermanent." Internally divided into four: "I am permanent, others are impermanent" (taking the wondrous luminous mind as the divine *ātman*), "world is impermanent, my mind is permanent," "I am subtle and permanent, body is impermanent," and "the formations are permanent, the perceptions etc. are impermanent."

**(4) Four finite-and-infinite doctrines.** "Within positions of arrangement he generates calculative reckonings." On the limits of space and time he renders verdicts of finite or infinite. Internally divided into four: "past and future are finite, present is infinite," "before eighty thousand kalpas is infinite, where there are beings is finite," "I omnipresently know the infinite, others are finite," "beings are half-arising and half-perishing — half is finite, half is infinite."

**(5) Four perverted, immortality-equivocating, totally-fabricated doctrines.** "Within his cognitive views he generates calculative reckonings." This is the most peculiar of the ten. When asked metaphysical questions, he deliberately gives answers that are vague, self-contradictory, and immune to refutation. Internally divided into four: "muddled answers (*also arising, also perishing; also existent, also non-existent; also increasing, also decreasing*)," "answering only with 'no'," "answering only with 'is'," and "saying 'also existent is also non-existent; within also non-existent is no also existent.'" **This is the only one of the sixty-two views classified by *speech strategy* rather than by content of position.**

**(6) Sixteen having-form doctrines.** "Within the inexhaustible flow he generates calculative reckonings." On the question of *the post-mortem mark*, he takes the four possible relations of self and form (form is self; self has form; form belongs to self; self resides in form) and multiplies them by the four aggregates (form, receptive, perception, formations) — yielding sixteen calculations of "has-form after death."

**(7) Eight no-form doctrines.** "Within the prior cessation of form, receptive, and perception he generates calculative reckonings." Reasoning further that "form having ceased, body has nothing on which to depend; observing perception having ceased, mind has nothing to which to attach," he reaches the eight calculations of "no-form after death." Terminus: "the cause and effect of nirvāṇa are altogether empty."

**(8) Eight neither doctrines.** "While the formations still subsist, with receptive and perception simultaneously ceased, he doubly calculates having and not-having." On post-mortem *has-form / no-form*, he renders a double negation. The Śūraṅgama's adjudication of this is "the latter limit is dim and confused, with nothing that can be said." The exactness of the phrase *dim and confused* (*hūnméng* 昏瞢) is critical: this is not the lucid double-negation of a Madhyamaka *neither*, but a confused, fogged double-negation.

**(9) Seven annihilationist doctrines.** "Within the post-post non-arising he generates calculative reckonings." On the basis of his observation of "the place where the formations aggregate moment-by-moment perishes," he infers that all beings will reach annihilation at seven possible levels (cessation of body, cessation of desire-exhaustion, cessation of suffering-exhaustion, cessation of utmost bliss, cessation of utmost equanimity, and so on).

**(10) Five present-nirvāṇa doctrines.** "Within the post-post arising he generates calculative reckonings." He directly identifies some present meditative state (full luminosity of the desire realm, the joy of the first dhyāna born of separation, the joy of the second dhyāna born of *samādhi*, the marvelous bliss of the third dhyāna apart from joy, the equanimous-thought purity of the fourth dhyāna) as nirvāṇa itself. **The tenth māra is the bridge from P11 to P12**, because "present-life nirvāṇa" is precisely the structure of "misidentifying some current position as the ultimate position" — a structure that recurs, in a still more refined form, in the consciousness-aggregate stage of P12.

The closing adjudication of the entire formations-aggregate section reads:[^12]

> "Ānanda! **These ten kinds of dhyāna mad-interpretations all arise from the mind-use intertwining of the formations aggregate; therefore such enlightenments appear** (*xíngyīn yòngxīn jiāohù, gù xiàn sī wù* 行陰用心交互，故現斯悟). Beings, dim and deluded, do not measure themselves; encountering these in the present, they take delusion for understanding, declare themselves to have ascended to sagehood, complete the great false speech, and fall into the Avīci hell."

"Mind-use intertwining of the formations aggregate" is the Śūraṅgama's standard name for the mechanism by which the ten māras arise. *Mind-use* (*yòngxīn*) refers to the practitioner's act of observation; *intertwining* (*jiāohù*) refers to the reflexive entanglement between this act and the formations-flow. **It is precisely this intertwining — the entanglement between the observer's act and the observed flow — that produces the "mad interpretation."** §3 below will unfold this structure in detail.

#### 2.3 The *Long Āgama Brahmajāla*: Structure of the Sixty-Two Views and the Fisherman Simile

As described in §1.1, the *Brahmajāla* divides the sixty-two views into two great classes: eighteen views concerning the prior aeon and forty-four views concerning the latter aeon. Each class has its full subclassification (four pervasive-permanence, four partial-permanence, four finite-and-infinite, four equivocation, two no-cause; sixteen having-perception, eight no-perception, eight neither-perception-nor-no-perception, seven annihilationist, five present-life nirvāṇa). For nearly every individual view, the discourse describes the conditions of its arising — and the great majority of the views are introduced by the formula "by various means he enters *samādhi* of mental concentration; with that *samādhi*-mind…" That is, **these are not views inferred by philosophers in their studies. They are views the meditator infers from what he observes in deep concentration.** This is the deepest commonality between the *Brahmajāla* and the Śūraṅgama's formations-aggregate section: both describe not the abstract metaphysical positions of philosophers but the structural traps that befall a meditator when he attempts to make exhaustive adjudication upon what he sees in deep meditation.

Take the first view of the second class (the four partial-permanence doctrines) as illustration.[^13] Its conditions of arising are described as follows: certain beings, after passing away from the *Ābhāsvara* (luminous-sound) heaven, are reborn in the empty Brahma-heaven. The first to be reborn there sees those who are reborn after, and conceives the thought: "I in this place am Brahmā, the great Brahmā; I am self-existent, none made me… I am the father of all beings; I alone existed first, and the other beings came after — these later beings I have transformed and produced." Those reborn after, in turn, believe him. Later, when these beings pass away from the heaven and are reborn in the human realm, they leave home and enter *samādhi*; with the *samādhi*-mind they recall their previous birth, and recall: "That great Brahmā was self-creating, and none created him… he was the father of all beings, abiding eternally without change." From this they conclude: **"I and the world are half-permanent, half-impermanent — this is true and the rest is false."** Brahmā is permanent; we are impermanent.

The exquisiteness of this narrative is that it reduces a metaphysical position (the doctrine of Brahmā the Creator) to a specific sequence of recollection and inference performed in *samādhi*. **It is not "the doctrine of Brahmā the Creator first exists, and then the meditator goes to study it." Rather: "a meditator in *samādhi* undergoes a specific recollective phenomenon, and the structure of this phenomenon necessarily leads to a particular metaphysical conclusion."** The other forty-some views are unfolded in similar fashion — each with its conditions of arising, its meditative basis, and the specific form of its inference.

The fisherman simile appears at the close of the discourse, after all sixty-two views have been laid out:[^14]

> "Whatever views any *śramaṇas* or brahmins hold concerning the prior aeon and the latter aeon, each according to what they see — all enter into the sixty-two views, each according to what they see, all dependent and resting upon them, and not one passes beyond. **Just as a skilled fisherman covers a small pond with a fine-meshed net, all the water-creatures of the pond enter the net with no place to escape, and not one passes beyond.** So it is with all *śramaṇas* and brahmins."

The fisherman simile has two key terms: *fine-meshed* (*xìmù* 細目) and *covers* (*fù* 覆). *Fine-meshed* refers to the precision of the net's openings — sufficient to catch every size of view. *Covers* refers to the net's encompassing the pond completely from above — leaving no position from which to escape. **The simile is making a precise claim: the sixty-two views are not "the sixty-two most common errors" but the *complete structural enumeration of the possible exhaustive adjudications upon the nature of self and world*.** Any attempt to make a statement of the form "self and world are X" will land in one of these sixty-two positions.

How, then, does one exit the net? The discourse offers a single sentence in answer:[^15]

> "If a *bhikṣu* with respect to the six contacts knows, as it actually is, their arising, ceasing, savor, peril, and emancipation — this is supreme, and goes beyond all those views."

"With respect to the six contacts knows, as it actually is, their arising, ceasing, savor, peril, and emancipation" — concerning the contact of the six sense-faculties with their objects, knowing as it actually is the manner of arising, the manner of ceasing, the present quality, the dangers entailed, and the way of emancipation. Note the form of the answer: **it is *not* "give the sixty-third correct view." It is a *transfer of the object and method of observation*** — from "rendering verdicts on self and world" to "knowing the six contacts as they actually are." The full structural significance of this transfer will be unfolded in §3.7 and §6 — it is the Āgama prototype of the Śūraṅgama's "non-grasping is essential" (*bù qǔ wéi yào* 不取為要) principle.

#### 2.4 *Saṃyukta Āgama*: The Three Layers of *Saṃskāra*

The Śūraṅgama's term *xíngyīn* — "formations aggregate" — inherits directly from the Āgamas' early definition of *saṃskāra*. *Saṃskāra* in the Āgamas has at least three layers of meaning.[^16]

**First layer: the formations aggregate among the five aggregates.** The fourth of the five — alongside form, receptive, perception, consciousness. *Saṃyukta Āgama* 59 describes the five aggregates of clinging thus: "The form-aggregate-of-clinging, the receptive, perception, formations, and consciousness aggregates of clinging… the arising of contact is the arising of the receptive, perception, and formations; the ceasing of contact is the ceasing of the receptive, perception, and formations." Here the formations aggregate is an independent psychological category alongside sensation and cognition.

**Second layer: the formations of the twelve-link dependent origination.** The "ignorance conditions formations, formations condition consciousness" link. *Saṃyukta Āgama* 298 supplies the standard analytic definition: "Formations are of three kinds — bodily formations, verbal formations, mental formations." This three-fold definition is further refined in *Saṃyukta Āgama* 568, in the dialogue between the householder Citta and the *bhikṣu* Kāmabhū: "**Out-breath and in-breath are called the bodily formation; thought-conception and sustained examination are called the verbal formation; perception and intention are called the mental formation**" (*chū-rù xī wéi shēnxíng; yǒu jué yǒu guān wéi kǒuxíng; xiǎng sī wéi yìxíng* 出息入息名為身行；有覺有觀名為口行；想思名為意行). This very subtle definition will be reactivated in §3.1 — it directly explains why the Śūraṅgama describes the formations aggregate as "shimmering and pure-disturbing like wild horses [a mirage]."

**Third layer: a synonym for all conditioned phenomena.** "All formations" (*zhū xíng* 諸行) is "all conditioned things." *Saṃyukta Āgama* 576 records the verse spoken by the Buddha himself: "**All formations are impermanent — this is the law of arising and ceasing; what arises will also cease — when both are still, this is bliss**" (*yīqiè xíng wúcháng, shì zé shēngmiè fǎ; shēng zhě jì fù miè, jù jìmiè wéi lè* 一切行無常，是則生滅法，生者既復滅，俱寂滅為樂). At the Buddha's parinirvāṇa, this verse is uttered again by Śakra (*Saṃyukta Āgama* 1197), and it becomes one of the most celebrated summary verses of the Āgama corpus.

The Śūraṅgama's formations-aggregate stage inherits all three layers but makes one important modification: **it contracts "formations" from a *conceptual category* to a *phenomenological position*.** The formations aggregate is no longer an abstract synonym for "all conditioned things" but the specific phenomenon a meditator experiences at a particular stage — "the subtle, ceaselessly flowing, difficult-to-objectify shimmering current." When the Śūraṅgama says "shimmering and pure-disturbing like wild horses [a mirage], the ultimate hinge-and-socket of the floating sense-faculties and sense-objects," it is describing a precise meditative phenomenon, and identifying that phenomenon as "the root-source of birth-and-death" (the ultimate root of segmented birth-and-death). This is the Śūraṅgama's *meditative-phenomenological transposition* of the Āgama concept of *saṃskāra*.

#### 2.5 *Madhyama Āgama Sacred Path Sūtra*: Right View as the Head of the Eightfold Path

If the ten formations-aggregate māras are "the exhaustive structural trap of errant adjudications upon the nature of the formations aggregate," then in what direction does the cure lie? *Madhyama Āgama* 189 (*Sacred Path Sūtra*, *Mahācattārīsaka Sutta* in the Pali parallel) gives a critical hint — **right view must take "the awareness of wrong view" as its starting point**:[^17]

> "**If one sees a wrong view as a wrong view, this is called right view; and if one sees a right view as a right view, this also is called right view**… Having known thus, one then seeks to learn — desiring to sever wrong view and to establish right view — this is called right effort. The *bhikṣu* with mindfulness severs wrong view and establishes right view — this is called right mindfulness. These three branches follow upon right view from the side of view-effort — **therefore right view is most foremost**."

The internal structure of this passage rewards close reading. It does *not* define right view as "holding a particular content as correct." It defines right view as a *double act of awareness* — "seeing a wrong view as a wrong view" and "seeing a right view as a right view." That is, the core of right view is not "believing some correct proposition" but "the very capacity, in the present, to discern whether the view one is currently holding is wrong or right."

This definition is critical for P11, because **the entire danger of the ten formations-aggregate māras is that the practitioner cannot discern whether the view he currently holds is wrong or right** — having just had "errant wisdom suddenly arise" (Zǐxuán's phrase), he immediately takes that errant wisdom for true realization, with no intermediate act of awareness to interrupt the process. The *Sacred Path Sūtra*'s reminder is that even at the highest depths of *samādhi*, right view is not "to believe something" but "to be able to *see the very nature* of the present believing itself." This reminder constitutes the Āgama foundation for the cure-principle of §3.7 below.

The *Sacred Path Sūtra* goes further, unfolding "right view foremost" into the entire eightfold path: right view begets right thought, right thought begets right speech, right speech begets right action, right action begets right livelihood, right livelihood begets right effort, right effort begets right mindfulness, right mindfulness begets right concentration.[^18] Note the directionality of this generative chain: **right view is not a fruit of some later stage of practice; it is the *starting point* of practice.** If the starting point is wrong — if the practitioner takes some present "mad interpretation" as "right view" — the whole chain runs in reverse, from the direction of liberation back into the direction of bondage. This structure is exactly the Āgama lineage behind the Śūraṅgama's severe warning at the close of the formations-aggregate section: "completing the great false speech, falling into the Avīci hell."

### Part B: Treatises — Zǐxuán and Jiāoguāng

#### 2.6 Zǐxuán's *Yìshū zhùjīng* (T1799), Fascicle 10: External-Māra Thirty, Mind-Māra Twenty

Zǐxuán's commentary on fascicle 10 of the Śūraṅgama is the authoritative scaffold for the entire P11 argument. In his annotation on the closing of the formations-aggregate section ("…lest the mind-māra arise of itself, generating its own deep calamity…"), Zǐxuán supplies the most pivotal adjudication of the entire S6 series:[^19]

> "*Niè* (孽) means 'calamity.' **While the perception aggregate is not yet exhausted, external māras are still drawn in. Now that the perception aggregate is exhausted and the formations aggregate is laid bare, [the practitioner] generates errant views regarding his perceived objects, grasping these as right — and so we speak of mind-māra.** Therefore [the Buddha] makes [the practitioner] vigilant in awareness, capable of discerning the messages, not losing right view, that he may reach the unsurpassed. So this is most carefully entrusted, transmitted and shown, that the practitioner may not enter the errant net or lose the path of right awakening."

This passage cleaves the entire fifty-skandha-māra structure with one stroke. "While the perception aggregate is not yet exhausted, external māras are still drawn in" — this marks where P10 ends. So long as the practitioner is still within the perception-aggregate stage (even at its very end), the māra remains in its *external* form (luminous visions in the form aggregate; misjudged sensations in the receptive; the dispatched essence-spirit possessing a person in the perception). "Now that the perception aggregate is exhausted and the formations aggregate is laid bare" — this marks where P11 begins. Once the perception aggregate has been crossed, and the formations aggregate is directly present, the mode of the māra's existence is utterly transformed. "[The practitioner] generates errant views regarding his perceived objects, grasping these as right" — this is the precise definition of the new form of māric event: not "the disturbance of an external object" but "the very *reading* of the perceived object." "And so we speak of *mind-māra*" — from this point on, it is called *mind-māra*.

Zǐxuán's second crucial adjudication concerns the source of the formations-aggregate calculations:[^20]

> "[As to the way] the doctrines of the non-Buddhist schools come into being on this account: **all of these arise when, the practitioner having reached this point, errant wisdom suddenly arises — this is named the bursting forth of view; it is not that there are originally separate non-Buddhist schools [coming in from outside]**."

"Errant wisdom suddenly arises — this is named the bursting forth of view" — these six characters are the seed of all of P11 §3.3's argument. They state explicitly: the correspondence between the formations-aggregate calculations and the Indian non-Buddhist schools is *not* a relation of cause and effect (it is not the case that the non-Buddhist schools cause the meditator's fall, nor that the meditator has studied them); it is **structural isomorphism** — the structure of "errant wisdom suddenly arising" at the formations-aggregate stage necessarily issues in one of these sixty-two forms, and the Indian schools are precisely the historical concretions of those sixty-two structures. §3.3 below will unfold this argument directly. It is P11's structural upgrade to P10's "mirror-other" framework.

Zǐxuán's annotation on the "territory of the formations aggregate" is equally noteworthy:[^21]

> "The formations aggregate is the source of arising and ceasing, because of its flowing-and-fabricating motion. The perception aggregate having been exhausted, the formations aggregate manifests; hence we say *laid bare*… 'Like wild horses' — heated air gathered with dust-particles, drummed up into a shimmering form. *Shimmering* — the flashing of light arising, similizing arising-and-ceasing. *Pure-disturbing* — the formations aggregate's disturbance, subtle and never-ceasing… **'The ultimate hinge-and-socket of the floating sense-faculties and sense-objects' — the upper bracket of a door is called *hinge*; the lower socket is called *socket*; both are the critical points of motion. The sense-faculties, the sense-objects, and arising-and-ceasing all take the formations aggregate as their pivot-knot.**"

The annotation on "*shū-xué*" (hinge-and-socket) is exceptionally precise. The formations aggregate is not some independent "thing"; it is the *pivot-knot* (the critical point of articulation) of all the operations of the sense-faculties, sense-objects, and arising-and-ceasing. This annotation lays the groundwork for §3's reflexivity argument: **because the formations aggregate is the pivot-knot of all observation-acts, "observation of the formations aggregate" is a structural impasse — the observation-act itself operates within the formations aggregate.**

#### 2.7 Jiāoguāng's *Zhèngmài shū* (X275), Fascicle 10: The Four Characters and the Five-Aggregate Water Simile

Jiāoguāng's commentary on the formations-aggregate section supplies P11 with two critical tools.

**First, the condensation of the ten calculations into four characters: annihilation, permanence, empty, existent.**[^22]

> "Discussed in general, the ten errant interpretations do not exit the four characters: annihilation, permanence, empty, existent. **The first five belong to annihilation-permanence; the latter five belong to empty-existent.** The first is annihilation-view; the second is permanence-view; the third is *both* (also annihilation, also permanence); the fourth and fifth are extensions of *both*… The sixth is grasping at the existent; the seventh is grasping at the empty; the eighth is *neither*… The ninth is the extension — ultimate empty annihilation. The tenth is the extension — ultimate stagnation in the existent."

The importance of this condensation is that it exposes the dual structure inside the ten māras. The first five process the *temporal* problem (the essence of annihilation-permanence is "the verdict on whether a flowing thing persists in time"); the latter five process the *real-existence* problem (the essence of empty-existent is "the verdict on whether the flowing thing still exists once time has run out"). The two groups are arranged symmetrically, each with *both* / *neither* as its central axis, and each closing with an extreme — *ultimate empty annihilation* and *ultimate stagnation in the existent*. This internal structure is not random; it is the exhaustion of the conceptual possibilities for "rendering exhaustive verdict upon a flowing thing." §3.4 below uses this condensation as the basis for the full four-axis topological framework.

**Second, the water simile of the five aggregates:**[^23]

> "[The perception aggregate] is like a great wave; the formations aggregate is like a fine ripple; the consciousness aggregate is like flowing water without waves. The body of true awareness should be like still and motionless water. Therefore the formations aggregate is the root of segmented [birth-and-death]; the consciousness aggregate is the root of variation [birth-and-death]."

The exquisiteness of this water simile lies in its use of different levels of waves to depict the refinement of false thought: form-aggregate = the foam at the shore; receptive aggregate = the swelling of the wave; perception aggregate = the great wave; formations aggregate = the fine ripple; consciousness aggregate = flowing water without waves; true awareness = still and motionless water. Note the expression "*flowing water without waves*" (*wú làng liúshuǐ* 無浪流水) — it depicts a state in which "the water still flows but the surface no longer shows any wave." That is, a position with no visible disturbance but still in motion. This is precisely the subtlety of the consciousness aggregate: it has been refined to the point where it "looks like it is not moving," but in fact it is still moving. This water simile supplies the core metaphorical framework for P12.

For P11, the placement "formations aggregate = fine ripple" is especially important. *Fine ripple* is not *no ripple* — it is still a ripple, only with amplitude and frequency reduced to near the threshold of observation. **The signature of the formations aggregate is that it operates at the threshold of observability** — it lies precisely on the boundary between the visible and the invisible. Any attempt to pin it down with an act of verdict-rendering will founder, because of its ceaseless flow — but because the foundering happens at the threshold of observation, the practitioner readily *misreads the foundering as seeing*. This is the phenomenological mechanism of the "mad interpretation."

Jiāoguāng's annotation on the exhaustion of the formations aggregate is equally subtle:[^24]

> "First raising the formations aggregate and calling it *source-nature*, [the text] makes clear that it is the root of arising and ceasing. *Source-nature* is body, substance… It is the [root of the] turbidity of beings; therefore the exhaustion of the formations aggregate transcends [this turbidity]. **The formations aggregate moves stealthily, never noticed; hence we say obscure concealment.**"

"*Moves stealthily*" (*mì yí* 密移) and "*never noticed*" (*céng wú jué wù* 曾無覺悟) are the precise unfolding of the two characters *yōuyǐn* (obscure concealment). The formations aggregate is "obscurely concealed" not because it cannot be seen but because it "moves stealthily" (an extremely subtle displacement) and because that displacement is not captured by any ordinary act of awareness. **The obscurely-concealed quality of the formations aggregate is precisely the structural basis for its becoming the "mirror-student" — a thing that cannot be brought to awareness obviously cannot be detected as "one's own mirror image."** §3.6 below will unfold this directly.

## 3. Core Arguments

This section is the center of P11. Sections 1 and 2 above have established three things: the ten formations-aggregate māras correspond, in number and order, almost completely to the sixty-two views of the *Brahmajāla Sūtra*; Zǐxuán and Jiāoguāng identify the source of this correspondence as "errant wisdom suddenly arising — named the bursting forth of view; not that there are originally separate non-Buddhist schools"; and the fundamental quality of the formations-aggregate stage is *obscure concealment* and *stealthy motion* — it sits exactly at the threshold of observation. This section unfolds the structural arguments behind these three: why this correspondence is necessary, why this position is reflexive, and why the "mirror-student" is more difficult to overcome than the "mirror-master."

### 3.1 Why the Formations Aggregate Is "Obscurely Concealed" — A Phenomenology of the Observation Threshold

Let us begin from a very specific question. When the practitioner "exhausts the original source of all classes of beings, observing the obscure-pure perfectly-disturbing source" (Śūraṅgama fascicle 10), *what is he observing*?

Return to the dialogue between the householder Citta and the *bhikṣu* Kāmabhū in *Saṃyukta Āgama* 568. Kāmabhū's definition of "formations" is: **bodily formation = out-breath and in-breath; verbal formation = thought-conception and sustained examination; mental formation = perception and intention.** This definition is further refined in the later *abhidharma* treatises, but its core structure is already complete in the Āgamas. **A *formation* is "a subtle motion that takes place automatically at the edge of awareness."** The going-out and coming-in of breath (the level of bodily formation), the rising and falling of conception and examination (the level of verbal formation), the surfacing of perception and intention (the level of mental formation) — these three layers of motion share a common feature: **they require no act of voluntary will; they happen of themselves.** You do not have to make an effort to breathe; breath happens. You do not have to actively initiate a thought; thoughts surface.

In the meditative stages of the prior three aggregates (form, receptive, perception), the practitioner is mainly handling things that "have already surfaced" — forms, sensations, thoughts. He can mark them with definite labels ("this is sorrow"; "this is joy"; "this is a particular thought"). But when he crosses the perception aggregate and enters the formations-aggregate stage, he discovers that what he is now facing is no longer "the already-surfaced object" but **"the very process by which the object is in the act of surfacing."** Every thought, every sensation, every form, *before it becomes the definite "this,"* has a subtle, fluid, not-yet-objectified surfacing-process. *That surfacing-process is the formations aggregate.*

The Śūraṅgama uses "shimmering and pure-disturbing like wild horses [a mirage]" to describe this phenomenon. *Wild horses* (*marīci*, the heated-air mirage) have two features: first, they are *there* — you can see the shimmering at a distance; second, when you walk over to grasp it, *it disappears*. The formations aggregate has exactly the same dual character: **it is there (the practitioner can sense a subtle disturbing-flow), but the moment you try to objectify it, your very act of objectification has already turned it into something else** (turned it into a "thought," a "sensation," a "form" — that is, into one of the objects of the prior three aggregates, no longer the formations aggregate itself).

This is the precise sense of the two characters *yōuyǐn* (obscure concealment). *Yōu* (obscure) does not mean "in the dark"; it means "at the threshold of awareness." *Yǐn* (concealed) does not mean "non-existent"; it means "becomes something else in the moment of being identified." **The very nature of the formations aggregate is that it cannot be presented as an object** — it can be sensed only as the *background* of all object-presenting acts, but the moment it becomes the object of an act of sensing, it is no longer what it was.

This phenomenological characteristic determines the entire form of the māric events at the formations-aggregate stage. In the prior three aggregates, the māra can appear as an *object* (luminous visions, misjudged sensations, the possessed mirror-other) — because the prior three aggregates are themselves the level of objectification. At the formations-aggregate stage, the māra cannot appear as an object, because the formations aggregate itself cannot be objectified. **What does the māra become, then? It becomes "the *manner of reading* the observation-act applies to the formations aggregate."** Each "mad interpretation" is one specific manner of reading — pinning this non-objectifiable flow with some exhaustive conceptual framework, then taking the result of the pinning for "having seen."

### 3.2 From Mirror-Master to Mirror-Student — The Reflexive Structure Enters

The central thesis of P10 was: the māric events of the perception-aggregate stage *must* be completed through an external mirror-other, because craving for an external capacity *requires* an external target object. "Dispatching essence to possess a person" is the necessary unfolding of the craving-structure — the celestial māra dispatches an essence-spirit to possess a person, and through that possessed person displays the form of the craved object; the practitioner, drawn in by this mirror-other, walks into errant view.

P11 will argue the opposite structure: the māric events of the formations-aggregate stage *cannot* be completed through an external mirror-other, because the object of the formations-aggregate stage is no longer "an external capacity" but "the *reading* of one's own formations-flow." No external person can perform this reading on the practitioner's behalf — only the practitioner himself can perform it. So the mirror-other exits the stage.

But this does not mean the *mirror structure* disappears. The mirror structure persists in an entirely different form: **the practitioner himself becomes the mirror.**

Concretely: when the practitioner observes his own formations-flow and then renders some verdict on that flow ("this is permanent," "this is annihilated," "this is finite," "this is infinite"…), the very process of rendering the verdict has an extraordinarily subtle structure — **the "I" who renders the verdict and the "formations aggregate" being verdicted-upon are, phenomenologically, the same.** Why? Because the "I" who renders the verdict is constituted by a thought-after-thought-flowing observation-act, and that thought-after-thought-flowing observation-act is exactly the *content* of the formations aggregate. **The subject of the verdict *is* the object of the verdict — only, in the moment of verdict-rendering, the practitioner is unaware of this identity.**

This is the precise structure of the *mirror-student*. The practitioner believes he is "observing the formations aggregate" (just as the perception-aggregate practitioner believed he was "observing an external spiritual friend"), but in fact what he is observing is *himself*. He has objectified his own observation-act *as* "the formations aggregate"; he has then rendered some verdict upon that objectification; he has then taken that verdict for "having seen." The whole process is completed entirely within his own interior; no external person needs to participate. **He is at once the teacher and the student; at once the observer and the observed; at once the speaker of the dharma and the hearer.** All "mad interpretations" are things he says to himself — but because he cannot recognize this identity, he believes what he hears is "the truth."

The structural difference between P10's "mirror-other" and P11's "mirror-student" can be summarized thus:

| Dimension | P10 Perception: Mirror-Other | P11 Formations: Mirror-Student |
|---|---|---|
| Mirror object | External spiritual friend (Strange Ghost, *Bá* Ghost, Sovereign Heavenly Māra…) | The practitioner's own observation-act |
| Mirror form | Dispatched essence possessing a person; concrete form | The mind's reading of perceived objects; no form |
| Mediation requirement | Must have a "possessed person" as intermediary | No intermediary; the practitioner is the entirety |
| Mode of detection | By comparison with sūtra-teaching, *vinaya*, true spiritual friends | The comparison-tool no longer functions — comparison is itself within the formations aggregate |
| Source of danger | Being deceived by another person | Being deceived by oneself |
| Direction of cure | Find the true spiritual friend (seek the great teacher) | Render no exhaustive verdict on perceived objects (non-grasping is essential) |

The last row is the most crucial. P10's cure remained within the forward Āgama structure of "for the sake of severing the three cravings, seek a great teacher" — only one had to detect the *mirror-master* as counterfeit. But P11's cure cannot be "find a better teacher," because **the root of the problem is not "having found the wrong teacher" but the very act of "rendering exhaustive verdict on the perceived object."** Even if the practitioner were to find the very best spiritual friend, if he continued to render exhaustive verdicts on his own meditative state, he would still fall into the ten māras — only the specific content of the māras would shift form. The direction of the cure must change altogether — from "correction of the object" to "cessation of the act." §3.7 below will unfold the full structure of this turn.

### 3.3 ⭐ P11 Original: "Errant Wisdom Suddenly Arises" and Structural Isomorphism — Why the Śūraṅgama and the *Brahmajāla* Necessarily Correspond

Zǐxuán's adjudication — "[As to the way] the doctrines of the non-Buddhist schools come into being on this account: all of these arise when, the practitioner having reached this point, errant wisdom suddenly arises — this is named the bursting forth of view; it is not that there are originally separate non-Buddhist schools" — is the seed of the entire argument of P11 §3.3. This section unfolds that seed into a positive structural argument.

**The question:** why do the ten formations-aggregate māras of Śūraṅgama fascicle 10 (totaling sixty-two specific calculations) correspond, in number, order, and naming, almost exactly to the sixty-two views of the *Long Āgama Brahmajāla*?

**The first possible explanation: literary borrowing.** The compilers of the Śūraṅgama read the *Brahmajāla* (or some pre-Chinese version of it), and reworked it in fascicle 10 into a meditative map. This explanation is not impossible, but it cannot explain why it was the Śūraṅgama and not some other Mahāyāna sūtra that performed the systematic meditative transposition of the sixty-two views; nor can it explain why the transposition corresponds so precisely to the formations-aggregate stage (rather than to some other aggregate).

**The second possible explanation: structural isomorphism.** The *Brahmajāla* and the Śūraṅgama formations-aggregate section describe not two independent things but the same structural trap, in two different doctrinal idioms. **The act of "rendering exhaustive verdict upon the nature of the formations aggregate" has exactly sixty-two structural possibilities, so whether one refers to the early canonical "*śramaṇas* and brahmins with their *samādhi*-mind" or to the Mahāyāna sūtra's "noble men of *śamatha*," so long as they are committing the same kind of error at the same position, the form of their error must land in those same sixty-two structural positions.**

The second explanation is the direction Zǐxuán points to. "Errant wisdom suddenly arises — named the bursting forth of view; it is not that there are originally separate non-Buddhist schools" — the key phrase is *not that there are originally separate non-Buddhist schools*. Zǐxuán explicitly denies that "there are non-Buddhist schools out there, and then meditators go to learn them"; he affirms instead that "meditators, at a particular stage, have errant wisdom suddenly arise, and the form of this errant wisdom happens to be the form of the non-Buddhist schools." The term *non-Buddhist school* (*wàidào* 外道) here does not designate a group of historical figures; it designates **a set of structural view-positions** — and the meditator at the formations-aggregate stage, when "errant wisdom suddenly arises," falls naturally into one of those structural positions.

Why, then, is the number of structural positions exactly sixty-two? This is the question §3.4 ("the four-axis topology") will unfold positively. But even before entering the topology, we can already state a weaker version: **because the task of "rendering exhaustive verdict upon the nature of the formations aggregate" has only finitely many internal dimensions** (temporality, quantification, boundary, causality), and each dimension has only finitely many positions (permanence/impermanence; universal/particular/both/neither; finite/infinite; caused/uncaused), the exhaustive combinations of these dimensions are also finite. The number sixty-two is not a historical accident but the specific computational result of these combinatorics.

The argument of structural isomorphism has two layers of significance for P11. **First**, it pulls the Śūraṅgama formations-aggregate section out of the category of "exotic Eastern meditative experience" and into the more universal category of *the structural enumeration of errant adjudications upon the formations aggregate*. Anyone who enters meditation deeply enough to reach the formations-aggregate position and there attempts an exhaustive adjudication of what he sees — regardless of his tradition, language, or historical era — will fall into one of these sixty-two types. This is not the special discovery of the Śūraṅgama; it is the intrinsic nature of the act of "rendering exhaustive verdict at the formations-aggregate position." **Second**, it explains why the names of the "Indian non-Buddhist schools" reappear in the Śūraṅgama in the form of a meditative map — not because the Śūraṅgama is borrowing from Indian philosophical history, but because those non-Buddhist names are themselves the historical labels of these sixty-two structural positions. When the Śūraṅgama uses the names *primal-prakṛti school*, *naturalism school*, *eternalist school*, *annihilationist school*, and so on, to label the specific types of the ten formations-aggregate māras, it is not citing Indian philosophical history; it is using a fixed structural-pointing vocabulary that has come to be settled.

### 3.4 ⭐ P11 Original: The Four-Axis Topology of the Ten Formations-Aggregate Māras

This section is the most important structural contribution of P11. The thesis to be unfolded directly is: **the sixty-two specific positions of the ten formations-aggregate māras are not an arbitrary historical accident; they are the exhaustive structural unfolding of a four-dimensional conceptual space.** The four dimensions are: *temporal axis*, *quantifier axis*, *boundary axis*, and *causal axis*.

**Dimension 1: The Temporal Axis (past / present / future)**

The essence of the formations aggregate is *thought-after-thought-flowing* — *temporality* is its most basic phenomenological feature. Any exhaustive adjudication upon the formations aggregate must necessarily involve a positional choice along the temporal dimension. The *Brahmajāla* offers a clear bipartition on this point: **the views concerning the prior aeon** (inferences about the past) and **the views concerning the latter aeon** (inferences about the future). The eighteen views concerning the prior aeon correspond to the first five formations-aggregate māras of the Śūraṅgama (two no-cause, four pervasive-permanence, four partial-permanence, four finite-infinite, four equivocation); the forty-four views concerning the latter aeon correspond to the latter five formations-aggregate māras (sixteen having-form, eight no-form, eight neither, seven annihilation, five present-nirvāṇa). **The first five māras process "from where do things come?" (the past axis); the latter five process "where do things go?" (the future axis).**

What about *the present*? The present dimension does not form its own group, because **the present dimension is the special position of the reflexive structure** — a verdict on "the present" is a verdict on "the very act of verdict-rendering," and this position appears only in the tenth māra (five present-nirvāṇa doctrines), in a specific form (taking some current meditative state for nirvāṇa). §3.5 below will unfold the singular character of the tenth māra.

**Dimension 2: The Quantifier Axis (universal / particular / both / neither — the *catuṣkoṭi*)**

For any conceptual position (permanent, impermanent, existent, non-existent), there are logically four ways of quantification: all is A, all is non-A, partly A and partly non-A, neither A nor non-A. These four quantifications constitute the *catuṣkoṭi* of Indian *hetuvidyā*. The internal structure of the ten formations-aggregate māras displays this four-corner structure in several places:

- The second māra (four pervasive-permanence) = *universal permanence* ("self and world are permanent").
- The third māra (four partial-permanence) = *both* ("partly permanent, partly impermanent").
- The seventh māra (eight no-form) = *universal non-existence* (no form after death).
- The sixth māra (sixteen having-form) = *universal existence* (has form after death).
- The eighth māra (eight neither) = *neither* (neither has-form nor no-form after death).

Note that the four-corner structure within the ten māras is unfolded not in pure logical fashion but as "each quantifier position multiplied by multiple content-variables" (form, receptive, perception, formations; form, no-form, perception, no-perception; finite, infinite; bliss, suffering, immeasurable perception, etc.) — yielding the specific counts of sixteen having-form, eight no-form, eight neither, and so on. **This is why the internal counts of the ten formations-aggregate māras are 2, 4, 4, 4, 4, 16, 8, 8, 7, 5 — these counts are not arbitrary; they are the products of "conceptual position × content-variable."**

**Dimension 3: The Boundary Axis (finite / infinite)**

A central question concerning the formations-flow is: *does it have a boundary?* — is it finite or infinite? The fourth māra (four finite-and-infinite doctrines) handles this dimension directly. But the boundary axis appears not only in the fourth māra; it recurs as a *secondary dimension* in the inner unfoldings of the sixth, seventh, and eighth māras (sixteen having-form, eight no-form, eight neither), whose content-variables include "finite-having-perception / infinite-having-perception," "finite-no-perception / infinite-no-perception," and so on. **The boundary axis is a *helper dimension*; it can combine with other dimensions to expand the count of specific calculations.**

**Dimension 4: The Causal Axis (caused / uncaused)**

The first māra (two no-cause doctrines) handles the causal dimension directly — it takes "no cause" as an exhaustive position. The tenth māra (five present-nirvāṇa doctrines) is, in a sense, the opposite extreme of the causal axis — not "no cause," but identifying the present *fruit-position* with the ultimate, thereby eliminating the need for any "causal continuation." **The causal axis is the *boundary condition* of the ten māras** — the first sits at the extreme of "no cause," the tenth sits at the extreme of "present-fruit-as-ultimate," and the eight intervening māras handle various specific verdicts within the causal chain.

Combining these four dimensions, we obtain the basic topological schema: **the ten formations-aggregate māras = the exhaustive combinations of exhaustive verdicts along the temporal axis, the quantifier axis, the boundary axis, and the causal axis.** Concretely:

- Pure temporal-axis verdicts: permanence (māra 2), annihilation (māras 7, 9), half-permanent-half-annihilated (māra 3).
- Pure quantifier-axis verdicts: has-form (māra 6), no-form (māra 7), neither (māra 8).
- Boundary axis: appears alone in māra 4; appears as a secondary dimension throughout māras 6, 7, 8.
- Causal axis: no-cause (māra 1); present-nirvāṇa (māra 10).
- Equivocation axis: māra 5 (the *speech-strategy* axis — a meta-language version of the quantifier axis: an *evasion of the act of verdict-rendering itself*).

**This is the structural explanation of the number sixty-two.** It is not "sixty-two historical discoveries" but the exhaustive combinatorial result of these four dimensions (plus an "evasion dimension") — the specific number of positions in this combinatorial space.

Jiāoguāng's condensation into "the four characters annihilation-permanence-empty-existent" is the abstract version of this four-axis structure. *Annihilation-permanence* corresponds to the temporal axis; *empty-existent* corresponds to the synthesis of the quantifier and causal axes. The first five māras belong to *annihilation-permanence* (the temporal axis primary); the latter five belong to *empty-existent* (the quantifier and causal axes primary). The fifth māra (equivocation) is the closing of the temporal-axis group — the *evasion* of temporal-axis verdict-rendering. The eighth māra (*neither*) is the central axis of the quantifier-axis group — the *neither* position. The tenth māra (five present-nirvāṇa) is the radicalization of the causal axis — the collapse of the causal chain into the present.

The cure-significance of this topology is: **any attempt to find a "correct position" within this four-dimensional space will fail.** Because the problem is not finding the correct position; the problem is that *the very act of "rendering exhaustive verdict upon the formations aggregate" is itself the error*. The Buddha says, at the close of the *Brahmajāla*: "**If a *bhikṣu* with respect to the six contacts knows, as it actually is, their arising, ceasing, savor, peril, and emancipation — this is supreme, and goes beyond all those views.**" This answer is not "give the sixty-third correct view"; it is *to transfer the object of observation* from "the formations aggregate itself" to "the arising-ceasing-savor-peril-emancipation of the six contacts." §3.7 below will unfold the structural significance of this transfer.

### 3.5 The Tenth Māra "Five Present-Nirvāṇa Doctrines" — The Specific Danger of Taking the Present for Nirvāṇa

The tenth māra occupies a special position within the ten-māra structure. Unlike the first nine, which handle "verdict upon something not yet attained" (the past, the future, the finite, annihilation, and so on), it handles "verdict upon the present state" — concretely, identifying some present meditative state (full luminosity of the desire realm, the first dhyāna, the second, third, or fourth dhyāna) directly with nirvāṇa.

The Śūraṅgama's description:[^25]

> "Within the post-post arising he generates calculative reckonings: such a person falls into the five nirvāṇa doctrines. Some take the desire realm as the right transference of basis, observing its luminous fullness and giving rise to attached longing; some take the first dhyāna, because its nature is without sorrow; some take the second dhyāna, because its mind is without suffering; some take the third dhyāna, because of its supreme delight; some take the fourth dhyāna, because suffering and pleasure both depart and one no longer receives the nature of *saṃsāric* arising-and-ceasing. **He confuses a contaminated heaven with an uncontaminated state, taking these five places of safety as the supreme pure refuge.**"

The eight characters "confuses a contaminated heaven with an uncontaminated state" (*mí yǒulòu tiān, zuò wúwéi jiě* 迷有漏天，作無為解) point precisely to the structure of the tenth māra. *Contaminated heaven* (*yǒulòu tiān* 有漏天) refers to the desire realm and the four dhyānas of the form realm — all still contaminated states within *saṃsāra*. *Uncontaminated* (*wúwéi* 無為) refers to true nirvāṇa, the unconditioned beyond *saṃsāra*. The error of the tenth māra is to misidentify the "contaminated heaven" as the "unconditioned" — to mistake a present meditative state still within *saṃsāra* for ultimate liberation.

Why is the tenth māra especially dangerous? Because **it is the closest to genuine realization**. The errors of the first nine all consist of "verdicts rendered outside the present state" (about the past, the future, fundamental nature) — these errors are relatively easy to detect, because they involve inferences about "what is not present," and they can be checked by other means. But the error of the tenth is "exhaustive verdict on the present state" — and the present state is itself real (the practitioner is genuinely in some highly profound meditative condition); only this meditative condition is not yet ultimate. To misidentify a *real, profound, but not-yet-ultimate state as ultimate* — **this is the deepest "mirror-student" structure within the ten formations-aggregate māras.**

A second structural significance of the tenth māra is that **it is the bridge from P11 to P12.** The ten consciousness-aggregate māras of P12 (grasping the cause as a non-cause; grasping the agent as non-agent; … fixed-nature *śrāvaka*; fixed-nature *pratyekabuddha*) share a deep isomorphism with the tenth formations-aggregate māra: each is, in some sense, "generating a verdict of full attainment at a not-yet-complete position" (Jiāoguāng's phrase). The tenth handles "taking the meditative state as nirvāṇa"; the ten consciousness-aggregate māras of P12 handle "taking a subtle consciousness-object as the ultimate." From the tenth māra to the consciousness-aggregate māras, the "mirror-student" structure refines from "misidentification of the meditative state" to "the final grasping of the subtle object."

### 3.6 Why Reflexivity Is Harder to Overcome Than the Mirror-Other — The Direct Answer to P10's Open Question

P10 closed with an open question: when the māra no longer comes from outside, why is the danger nevertheless greater? Why is the "mirror-student" harder to overcome than the "mirror-master"? This section is the direct answer to that question.

**First reason: the failure of the comparison-tool.**

To detect the *mirror-master* one necessarily relies upon some comparison — comparison with sūtra-teaching (does what this person says match the Buddha's word?), comparison with the *vinaya* (does this person's behavior conform to the precepts?), comparison with true spiritual friends (do other true friends accept this person?). Comparison is possible because "the object to be detected" and "the tool with which to detect it" are two different things. The comparison-tool functions as an external standard in the detection of the mirror-master.

But for the detection of the *mirror-student*, no such comparison is available. Why? Because **anything that one might attempt to deploy as a comparison-tool is itself still operating within the formations aggregate.** When the practitioner tries to "compare against the sūtra-teaching," his memory of the sūtra-teaching, his understanding, his interpretation are all part of the formations-flow; when he tries to "compare against the *vinaya*," his judgment in applying the *vinaya* is part of the formations-flow; when he tries to "compare against the true spiritual friend," his recognition of "who is a true spiritual friend" is part of the formations-flow. **No comparison-tool can step outside the formations aggregate** — every tool is inside it. Comparison fails.

**Second reason: the reflexive entanglement of the observation-act.**

To detect anything, one must first observe it. But the special character of the formations-aggregate stage is this: **the act of observing the formations aggregate is itself the formations aggregate.** When the practitioner tries to "see" his own awareness-flow, the "eye" with which he sees is itself part of the awareness-flow. This is a structural impasse — the observer cannot observe the totality of his own observation-act, because the observation-act cannot be objectified by itself.

The Śūraṅgama uses the phrase "mind-use intertwining of the formations aggregate" (*xíngyīn yòngxīn jiāohù* 行陰用心交互) to describe this impasse. *Mind-use* is the observation-act; *intertwining* is the reflexive entanglement between the observation-act and the observed object. **It is precisely this entanglement that produces the "mad interpretation"** — the practitioner believes he has "seen the formations aggregate clearly," but in fact what he has seen is the specific shaping his observation-act has imposed upon the formations aggregate. Each "mad interpretation" is a specific shape of the observation-act — permanence, annihilation, having-form, no-form, neither, annihilation, present-nirvāṇa — each is a particular way the observation-act tries to pin the formations aggregate down, and the result of each pinning is not the formations aggregate itself but the *shape pinned*.

**Third reason: the irreversibility of the error.**

The error of the *mirror-master* is correctable — you discover that you have followed the wrong teacher, you can leave, you can find another teacher, you can begin again. The error is *directional*; it can be reversed.

But the error of the *mirror-student* is *structural* — **once you have rendered some exhaustive verdict on your own meditative state and taken it for true, that verdict has become the implicit framework of your subsequent observation.** All subsequent observation will be conducted under this implicit framework, and the implicit framework will not be questioned by those observations (because no observation questions the framework on which it itself depends). The annihilationist view is not overturned by further observation, because once the practitioner holds the annihilationist view, all his subsequent observation will automatically confirm it; the same holds for the eternalist view, the present-nirvāṇa view, and so on. **Once the error has occurred, it has become the formal condition of observation itself; there is no room for "beginning again."**

This is why the Śūraṅgama's warning concerning the ten formations-aggregate māras takes the severe form "completing the great false speech, falling into the Avīci hell." Not because it is threatening the practitioner (the Śūraṅgama never uses threats as instruction), but because it is describing a structural fact — **once the "mad interpretation" of the formations-aggregate stage has been taken for true, the practitioner has entered a closed loop that admits no self-correction.** *Avīci hell* is not an external punishment but the structural name for that closed loop itself — a structure with no gap, no exit, no margin within which correction might be possible.

### 3.7 The Cure-Principle: Non-Grasping Is Essential — From the *Brahmajāla*'s "Knowing the Six Contacts as They Actually Are" to the Śūraṅgama's "Forming No Saintly Interpretation"

If the structural cause of the ten formations-aggregate māras is "the very act of rendering exhaustive verdict upon the nature of the formations aggregate," then the direction of the cure can only be one: **stop this act.** But the word *stop* requires extreme care in unfolding, because it is easily misread as "do not think" or "enter a state of non-discrimination."

The positive unfolding must begin from the cure-formula of the *Brahmajāla*. The Buddha says: "**If a *bhikṣu* with respect to the six contacts knows, as it actually is, their arising, ceasing, savor, peril, and emancipation — this is supreme, and goes beyond all those views.**" This formula has two key parts.

**The first part is "with respect to the six contacts."** The cure does not occur at the level of "observation of the formations aggregate itself"; it occurs at the level of "the as-it-actually-is awareness of the contact of the six sense-faculties with their objects." **Note that the object of the cure has been replaced** — no longer "the formations aggregate" (that non-objectifiable flow), but "the six contacts" (the specific events of sense-faculty-meeting-sense-object). The six contacts can be the object of awareness, because they have specific boundaries (this faculty, this object, this contact); the formations aggregate cannot be the object of awareness, because it has no boundaries. The cure-strategy is **to transfer observation from a non-objectifiable position to an objectifiable position.**

**The second part is "arising, ceasing, savor, peril, and emancipation."** The awareness of the six contacts is to include five aspects: how they arise (*samudaya*), how they cease (*nirodha*), the present quality (*assāda*), the dangers entailed (*ādīnava*), and the way of emancipation (*nissaraṇa*). All five are *verbal* in form — they pronounce no exhaustive verdict on "the nature of the six contacts"; they only describe "how the six contacts flow." **The method of cure is not to render verdict on the essence of the flowing thing but to see clearly the flow itself.**

The Śūraṅgama supplies a finely matched name for this principle — "**form no saintly interpretation**" (*bù zuò shèng jiě* 不作聖解, also written *bù zuò shèng xīn* 不作聖心). In every section of warning throughout the fifty skandha-māras, the Śūraṅgama repeats the same line: "**If you form a saintly interpretation, you receive the host of errancies; if you form no saintly interpretation, this is named a wholesome state**" (fascicle 9). This line is structurally identical with the *Brahmajāla*'s cure-formula — "form no saintly interpretation" is "render no exhaustive verdict on perceived objects," and "this is named a wholesome state" is letting the state remain in its flowing quality without being objectified by any shaping.

Concretely at the formations-aggregate stage, "form no saintly interpretation" means: **when the practitioner sees the thought "self and world are permanent" surfacing, he does not adjudicate it as true or false — he only sees it surfacing; when he sees the thought "annihilation" surfacing, he does not adjudicate it as right or wrong — he only sees it surfacing; when he sees the feeling "five present-nirvāṇa" surfacing, he does not adjudicate whether this is ultimate — he only sees it surfacing.** The seed of every "mad interpretation" is a surfacing thought or feeling, and the key to the cure is not "supplying the correct verdict" but "*not adjudicating, in the moment of surfacing*."

The deepest aspect of this cure-principle is that **it dissolves the very position of "the correct verdict."** It does not say "you should hold the *neither* of Madhyamaka," or "you should hold the *paratantra* of Yogācāra," or "you should hold the *true permanence* of *Tathāgatagarbha*" — it says "render no exhaustive verdict whatever." **Right view is not some correct content; it is the very capacity of *not rendering exhaustive verdict on perceived objects*.** This is exactly the deepest meaning of the *Madhyama Āgama Sacred Path Sūtra*'s "if one sees a wrong view as a wrong view, this is called right view; and if one sees a right view as a right view, this also is called right view" — the core of right view is the clarity of the act of awareness itself, not the specific content of what is being aware-of.

From this cure-principle, the entire structure of the ten formations-aggregate māras is fundamentally traversed. Not by passing through to a "sixty-third correct view" but by passing through to a position in which "no view needs to be established" — and that position is not emptiness, but the present flow of the six contacts itself. **This is the entire purport of the Śūraṅgama's cure for the formations-aggregate stage, and P11's fundamental upgrade to P10's "mirror-other" framework.** P10 teaches the practitioner to detect the mirror-master and find the true spiritual friend; P11 teaches the practitioner that he no longer needs any mirror — because he has discovered that the very position of "the correct verdict on perceived objects," which he has always been seeking, is itself the mistake.

## 4. Interdisciplinary Resonance

### 4.1 ⚠️ A Minimal Contemporary Analogy: The Observer's Paradox and Second-Order Awareness

The reflexive structure of the formations-aggregate stage has, in contemporary epistemology and cognitive science, a far-from-perfect but structurally comparable parallel — **the *observer's paradox***. This paradox was first named in linguistic field research and has subsequently been encountered repeatedly in quantum measurement, ethnographic sociology, and neurophenomenology: **any act of observing a system alters the system, such that "the original state prior to observation" is in principle unobservable.**

The standard response to the observer's paradox is *second-order awareness* — not denying the observation-act's effect on the observed object, but bringing the observation-act itself into the scope of observation, so as to render a more integral description of the composite system *observation-plus-observed*.

⚠️ This analogy has a structural resonance with the reflexive structure of the formations-aggregate stage on the following points:

**First, the observation-act alters the observed object.** At the formations-aggregate stage, the observation-act not merely *alters* the formations aggregate but *is* part of the formations aggregate — this strengthening of the reflexivity makes the problem more thoroughgoing than the observer's paradox.

**Second, the limits of second-order awareness.** Even with the introduction of second-order awareness, the reflexive structure of observation persists — the act of observing the composite "observation-plus-observed" is itself another observation, and it introduces a fresh reflexive entanglement. At the formations-aggregate stage, this limitation is not relative but absolute — awareness of *any order* is still within the formations aggregate.

**Third, the direction of cure does not lie in "finding the correct method of observation."** Researchers of the observer's paradox have eventually conceded that no method of observation can wholly eliminate reflexive influence; one can only describe and manage it as best as possible. The Śūraṅgama's direction of cure is more thoroughgoing — not "describing and managing" reflexivity, but "stopping the exhaustive verdict-rendering on the observed object," so that reflexive entanglement has no conceptual framework within which to break out.

**The limit of this analogy must be marked explicitly:** the observer's paradox is an epistemological tool whose direction of cure is more refined methods of description; the reflexivity of the formations-aggregate stage is a phenomenological position whose direction of cure is the cultivation of the act of awareness itself. The two have a structural resonance on the single point of "the reflexive entanglement of observer and observed," but the formations-aggregate position cannot be reduced to a special case of the observer's paradox. **No epistemological tool can substitute for the concrete experience of meditative phenomenology — and §5 will reiterate this limit again and again.**

## 5. Middle-Way Correction

This section advances six middle-way corrections.

### 5.1 ❌ The Ten Formations-Aggregate Māras Are Not a Philosophical Taxonomy

The first misreading to be avoided: treating the ten formations-aggregate māras as "a catalog of Indian philosophical positions," then attempting to evaluate the merits of these positions with the tools of modern logic or metaphysics. The problem with this misreading is that it transforms Śūraṅgama fascicle 10 into an academic text and so misses entirely its meditative-phenomenological character. **The ten formations-aggregate māras are not "ten philosophical positions worth evaluating" but "ten structural traps that befall the meditator's exhaustive adjudication upon the nature of the formations aggregate."** Their object is not the disputation of philosophers in the study but the concrete experience of meditators in the depths of *samādhi*. Any effort to extract them from this meditative context will turn them into something they are not.

### 5.2 ❌ "The External Māra Has Exited" Does Not Equal "Lower Danger"

The second misreading to be avoided: since the formations-aggregate stage has no external māra ("the celestial māra is not given his opportunity"), this stage is therefore *safer* than the perception-aggregate stage. The fact is exactly the opposite — the entire discourse of Zǐxuán and Jiāoguāng emphasizes that the danger at this stage is *substantially upgraded*, not lowered. "The celestial māra is not given his opportunity" does not mean "no danger"; it means "the form of the danger has changed" — from the external disturber to the internal reading itself. **Precisely because the māra no longer has any external form available to be detected, the practitioner has thereby lost all his comparison-tools.** The greater the sense of safety, the deeper the danger — this is the basic paradox of the formations-aggregate reflexive structure.

### 5.3 ❌ Reflexivity Does Not Equal "Self-Awareness Is Sufficient"

The third misreading to be avoided: since the core of the problem is reflexivity (the observer is the observed), the solution is therefore "deeper self-awareness" — to deepen layer after layer until the identity of observer and observed is utterly seen through. The problem with this misreading is that it misreads the Śūraṅgama's direction of cure as "still higher meta-awareness," whereas in fact the Śūraṅgama's direction of cure is "stopping the exhaustive verdict-rendering on perceived objects" — not a higher level of verdict but the cessation of the very act of verdict-rendering. **Reflexivity cannot be solved by deeper observation; it can only be traversed by the awareness-posture of "forming no saintly interpretation."** Any effort to use higher-level awareness as a solution will fall into the impasse of "treating reflexivity with reflexivity."

### 5.4 ❌ "Non-Grasping Is Essential" Does Not Equal "Do Not Think"

The fourth misreading to be avoided: since the core of the cure is "render no exhaustive verdict on perceived objects," the practitioner should therefore stop thinking and enter a state of non-discrimination. This misreading conflates two different things — "exhaustive verdict-rendering" and "ordinary thought." **"Exhaustive verdict-rendering" is the act of trying to pin a flowing thing with a set of conceptual frameworks; "ordinary thought" is the act of making specific judgments within the flow.** The former is the root of the ten formations-aggregate māras; the latter is what daily practice requires (including discriminating good and evil, choosing remedies, responding to conditions). The *Madhyama Āgama Sacred Path Sūtra* explicitly says: "if one sees a wrong view as a wrong view, this is called right view" — that act of discrimination is itself thinking. The cure is not the cessation of thinking but the cessation of *exhaustive declarations about the nature of perceived objects*.

### 5.5 ❌ Structural Isomorphism Between the Śūraṅgama and the *Brahmajāla* Does Not Equal Plagiarism

The fifth misreading to be avoided: since the Śūraṅgama's ten formations-aggregate māras correspond, structurally, almost completely to the sixty-two views of the *Brahmajāla*, the Śūraṅgama must therefore have plagiarized the *Brahmajāla* (or some version of it). This misreading commits a common error of textual studies — reading "structural isomorphism" directly as "literary borrowing." **Structural isomorphism can occur along two entirely different paths:** first, literary borrowing (the later text has read the earlier and rewritten it); second, structural necessity (both texts describe the same structural trap, so wherever and in whatever language they appear, they will appear in the same form). Zǐxuán's "errant wisdom suddenly arises — named the bursting forth of view; not that there are originally separate non-Buddhist schools" explicitly supports the second path — Zǐxuán does not read the Śūraṅgama formations-aggregate section as "a meditative transposition of the Indian non-Buddhist schools" but as "the structural error generated internally in the practitioner at a particular stage." The path taken in §3.3 of this paper is also the second — the isomorphism between the Śūraṅgama and the *Brahmajāla* is not a literary relation but structural isomorphism.

### 5.6 ❌ The Subtle Grasping of the Consciousness Aggregate and the Reflexive Misjudgment of the Formations Aggregate Are Not to Be Confused

The sixth misreading to be avoided: since both P11 and P12 handle "mind-māra" (Zǐxuán's term), the danger at these two stages must therefore be essentially the same. In fact, they are two structurally different positions. **The danger at the formations-aggregate stage is "rendering exhaustive verdict on a flowing thing"** — the object is "the awareness-in-flow," and the error is to pin the flow with a conceptual framework; **the danger at the consciousness-aggregate stage is "the final grasping of a subtle object"** — the object is "something so subtle as to be barely distinguishable from true mind," and the error is to identify that subtle thing as the ultimate. Both are "mind-māra," but the specific internal structures of "mind-māra" differ. P12 will unfold the special structure of the consciousness-aggregate stage and distinguish in detail its positional difference from the formations aggregate. The present paper does not undertake to handle the content of P12 in advance — because these two structures must be handled separately if the clarity of each is to be preserved.

## 6. Conclusion

### 6.1 Summary of P11 — The Net of the *Brahmajāla* and the Mirror-Student

The central thesis of this paper is: **the ten formations-aggregate māras are not ten new errors but a single net long since woven — the *dhyāna* repetition of the sixty-two views of the *Long Āgama Brahmajāla*. Each calculative reckoning is some form of "exhaustive adjudication" the practitioner pronounces upon his own formations-flow after observing it. The observer is the observed. The mirror-other has exited the stage; the mirror-student has entered — the practitioner himself is now the one who deceives himself.**

Zǐxuán and Jiāoguāng arrive at a single judgment on this point. Zǐxuán: "while the perception aggregate is not yet exhausted, external māras are still drawn in; now that the perception aggregate is exhausted and the formations aggregate is laid bare, [the practitioner] generates errant views regarding his perceived objects, grasping these as right — and so we speak of *mind-māra*." Jiāoguāng: "earlier the text spoke of *dhyāna manifesting objects* — these are cases where the celestial māra watches for his chance. Here it speaks of *dhyāna mad-interpretations* — these are cases where the *mind-māra* arises of itself, generating its own deep calamity." Both commentators state explicitly: **the māric events of the formations-aggregate stage are structurally different from those of the prior three aggregates — the māra no longer has any external form; it is the very reading the practitioner's own mind applies to its perceived objects.**

The paper has unfolded seven layers of argument to clarify this thesis: (1) the formations aggregate is "the false thought of obscure concealment" — it sits exactly at the threshold of observation, and any attempt to objectify it turns it into something else; (2) the structural transition from mirror-master to mirror-student — the identity of observer and observed renders the comparison-tool inoperative; (3) ⭐ "errant wisdom suddenly arises" and structural isomorphism — the correspondence between the Śūraṅgama and the *Brahmajāla* is not literary borrowing but structural necessity; (4) ⭐ the four-axis topology of the ten formations-aggregate māras — the exhaustive combinations along temporal, quantifier, boundary, and causal axes explain the non-arbitrariness of the number sixty-two; (5) the singular character of the tenth māra "five present-nirvāṇa" — taking the present state for nirvāṇa is the ten formations-aggregate māras' deepest "mirror-student," the position closest to genuine realization; (6) three reasons why reflexivity is harder to overcome than the mirror-other — the failure of the comparison-tool, the reflexive entanglement of the observation-act, the irreversibility of the error; (7) the cure-principle of "non-grasping is essential" — from the *Brahmajāla*'s "knowing the six contacts as they actually are" to the Śūraṅgama's "forming no saintly interpretation," right view is not some correct content but the very clarity of the act of awareness itself.

**The four-axis topology is the most important original contribution of this paper.** It exposes the inner architecture of the ten formations-aggregate māras as not an arbitrary array of sixty-two events but the structural enumeration of "the act of rendering exhaustive verdict upon the nature of the formations aggregate." Jiāoguāng's condensation "do not exit the four characters: annihilation, permanence, empty, existent" is the abstract version of this topological structure — the first five māras belong to the temporal axis (annihilation-permanence); the latter five belong to the quantifier and causal axes (empty-existent). To understand this topology is to understand why the correspondence between the Śūraṅgama and the *Brahmajāla* is structural necessity rather than historical accident — anyone who enters meditation deeply enough to reach the formations-aggregate position and there attempts an exhaustive adjudication of what he sees, in any tradition whatsoever, will fall into the exhaustive combinations of these four dimensions.

Zǐxuán cuts through to the root: "**errant wisdom suddenly arises — named the bursting forth of view; it is not that there are originally separate non-Buddhist schools.**" The source of the ten formations-aggregate māras is not some external non-Buddhist school but the structural product of the practitioner's own mind, at a particular stage, having "errant wisdom suddenly arise." The "tempered loving-mind" of the perception aggregate (P10 §6.1) is here further refined into "errant wisdom suddenly arising" — craving has been processed away, but what replaces craving is "rendering exhaustive verdict on perceived objects," a still finer form of grasping. From "the tempered loving" to "the errantly born wisdom," the inner refinement of the fifty skandha-māras is wholly displayed.

### 6.2 Forward to P12 — From Reflexive Misjudgment to the Final Grasping of the Subtle

The last of the ten formations-aggregate māras — the five present-nirvāṇa doctrines — pushes the "mirror-student" structure to its extreme, in the form of "confusing a contaminated heaven with an uncontaminated state": taking the present meditative state, still within *saṃsāra*, for the ultimate. This extreme is precisely the entry point of the consciousness-aggregate stage of P12.

The central question of the consciousness-aggregate stage of P12 is: when the practitioner has set down even "the exhaustive verdict on the nature of the formations aggregate" and entered the position "exhausting all formations to emptiness, returning to the source within consciousness" (Jiāoguāng's "between the two columns" — the front column of the formations aggregate having been broken but the back column of the consciousness aggregate not yet rounded off), what does he face? The answer is: **he faces an extraordinarily subtle object — the "diffuse-form void-non-being" of the consciousness aggregate.** The signature of the consciousness aggregate is "seemingly substantial yet vacuous, seemingly existent yet not" (Jiāoguāng's phrase) — refined to the point of "seemingly singular, seemingly permanent," looking like the original nature of true awareness, but in fact still the false awareness of phenomenal luminosity.

The ten māras of the consciousness-aggregate stage (grasping the cause as a non-cause; grasping the agent as non-agent; grasping the permanent as non-permanent; grasping the knowing as non-knowing; grasping the arising as non-arising; grasping the returning as non-returning; grasping the craving as non-craving; grasping the true as non-true; fixed-nature *śrāvaka*; fixed-nature *pratyekabuddha*) share a common structure: **at this extraordinarily subtle position they "establish supreme interpretation" — misidentifying this not-yet-completed transitional state as the ultimate.** Note that each māra is named with the character *zhí* 執 — *grasping*: "grasping" is "the final grasping of a subtle object." From the formations-aggregate stage's "exhaustive verdict on a flowing thing" to the consciousness-aggregate stage's "final grasping of a subtle object," the last layer of refinement of the "mirror-student" structure has occurred.

The final two māras of the consciousness aggregate (fixed-nature *śrāvaka*, fixed-nature *pratyekabuddha*) carry a special structural significance — they are not "non-Buddhist" but "the two vehicles capped too early." Jiāoguāng's adjudication is exquisitely precise: "**that *māra* is the term for what harms the right** … and the two vehicles, in clearly turning their backs and falling, are stalled at a branching path; their harming of the right is on a level. What are they if not māra? Yet by the qualifier *fixed-nature* one means only those who do not turn back the heart." Why are the two vehicles listed among the māras? Because from the standpoint of the Śūraṅgama's One Buddha Vehicle, **"taking the not-yet-complete as complete" is itself māra — and "taking the *arhat* fruit as complete" is the deepest form of "taking the not-yet-complete as complete."** This adjudication directly connects to S7 *Lotus Sūtra* "opening the expedient and revealing the real, the One Buddha Vehicle" — the entire narrative of the *Lotus* is devoted to disclosing the "One Buddha Vehicle" structure to "two-vehicle followers capped too early," and the ninth and tenth consciousness-aggregate māras are the Śūraṅgama's meditative-phenomenological version of the same problem.

P12 will unfold the special structure of the consciousness-aggregate stage and, through the position of "fixed-nature two vehicles," complete the bridge from the S6 Śūraṅgama series to the S7 *Lotus* series. The present paper does not undertake to handle the content of P12 in advance; it only marks the transitional axis P11 → P12 → S7.

### 6.3 A Final Word to the Reader

If you have read P09 (receptive aggregate), P10 (perception aggregate), and P11 (formations aggregate), you may worry: the ten formations-aggregate māras sound so structural, so hard to avoid — *how do I know whether I have fallen into them*?

The answer, again, has two layers, in continuity with P09 and P10.

**The first layer is reassurance: if you have not yet entered the deep meditative position of "perception aggregate exhausted," you cannot fall into the ten formations-aggregate māras.** The precondition of the ten formations-aggregate māras is an extraordinarily profound meditative basis — "those whose perception aggregate is exhausted, for whom ordinary dreams have ceased and waking and sleep are constantly one, whose awareness-luminous mind is empty and still, like a clear sky" is the entrance ticket. Without that ticket, you cannot even reach the stage on which the ten formations-aggregate māras are performed. The ordinary metaphysical confusion of daily life — "what is the ultimate nature of the world?", "do I still exist after I die?", "is the present moment everything?" — all belong to the conceptual activity of the ordinary practitioner, and their cure is basic right-view training and study of the sūtras, not the "ten formations-aggregate māras" framework.

**The second layer is preparation: if you are practicing seriously, and you intend to go deep, then engraving the cure-principles of the ten formations-aggregate māras in memory is a preventive diagnostic tool** —

1. **"Obscure concealment" means "non-objectifiable."** When you discover that you are facing some "shimmering current too subtle to be pointed at," you are already approaching the formations-aggregate stage.
2. **Any exhaustive verdict is a mad interpretation.** When you discover that you are trying to pin the present state with such concepts as "permanent," "impermanent," "existent," "non-existent," "annihilation," "present-nirvāṇa," the alarm should already be ringing — this is the structural entry-point of the ten formations-aggregate māras.
3. **The comparison-tools will fail.** The deepest danger of the formations-aggregate stage is that you will discover none of your comparison-tools (sūtra-teaching, *vinaya*, the memory of true spiritual friends) can tell you "am I now in a mad interpretation?" The failure of the comparison itself is the signature of the formations-aggregate stage.
4. **Form no saintly interpretation.** The core of the cure is not "supplying the correct verdict" but "*not adjudicating, in the moment the verdict-act surfaces*." Let thoughts surface, let thoughts cease, render no declaration upon their nature. This is the essence of all of the Śūraṅgama's cure for the formations-aggregate stage.

From P10's "for the sake of severing the three cravings, seek a great teacher" to P11's "knowing the six contacts as they actually are, go beyond all those views" — the juxtaposition of these two Āgama cure-formulas is the entire purport of S6's transition from "external-māra cure" to "mind-māra cure." The cure of "seeking the great teacher" is *finding the correct direction* (finding a spiritual friend who can sever the three cravings); the cure of "knowing the six contacts as they actually are" is *stopping the act of verdict-rendering* (rendering no exhaustive declaration on perceived objects). The former is the core of P10; the latter is the core of P11.

**From "severing" to "non-grasping" is the entire purport of the ten formations-aggregate māras.**

If a single sentence could summarize P11, it would be the one the Śūraṅgama already gave in fascicle 9: "**If you form a saintly interpretation, you receive the host of errancies; if you form no saintly interpretation, this is named a wholesome state.**" This line appears in every section of the fifty skandha-māra warnings — but its deepest meaning is unfolded fully only at the formations-aggregate stage. In the prior three aggregates, "form no saintly interpretation" is "do not misidentify a meditative state as realization"; at the formations-aggregate stage, "form no saintly interpretation" is "render no exhaustive verdict whatsoever upon the nature of the perceived object." Form no saintly interpretation, and the state is a wholesome state; form a saintly interpretation, and even the deepest meditative state becomes a "māric realm" — because at the formations-aggregate stage the word *māra* points to nothing external; it points to the very act of "rendering exhaustive verdict on perceived objects."

The entire cultivation of the formations-aggregate stage is contained in the four characters *bù zuò shèng jiě* — "form no saintly interpretation."

From the early canonical core of the *Brahmajāla* to the Mahāyāna meditative transposition of the Śūraṅgama, more than seven centuries had elapsed.
