## Abstract

「照見五蘊皆空，度一切苦厄」— these fourteen characters at the heart of the *Heart Sūtra* answer two fundamental questions: What exactly does prajñā "see"? And once it sees, how is suffering transcended?

Drawing on the Āgama sūtras as its foundation and Yogācāra as its interpretive framework, this paper argues three propositions. First, "illuminating insight" (zhàojiàn 照見) is not a new capacity unlocked through practice, but rather a case of *innate cause, cultivated fruition* (yīn běnyǒu, guǒ xiūdé 因本有、果修得): the innate uncontaminated seeds (anāsrava-bīja) represent the latent potential, while study, reflection, and meditative cultivation (śruta-cintā-bhāvanā) are the effort required to bring them into actuality. Second, "emptiness" (śūnyatā) here refers specifically to the emptiness of the thoroughly imagined nature (parikalpita): the other-dependent (paratantra) and the perfectly accomplished (pariniṣpanna) are not empty — the three natures are neither wholly empty nor wholly non-empty. Third, "transcending all suffering" (dù yīqiè kǔ'è 度一切苦厄) is the natural output of illuminating insight: suffering arises from the sense of "self" (ātman); illuminating insight dissolves self-grasping; suffering is thereby transcended — this is āśraya-parāvṛtti (the transformation of the basis).

This paper also completes the closure of the awakening circuit: Avalokiteśvara (S2-P02) → deep practice (S2-P03) → illuminating the emptiness of the five aggregates (first half of this paper) → transcending suffering (second half), with the endpoint returning to the starting point: self-mastery (zìzài 自在) is the default state after the transformation of the basis.

**Keywords:** illuminating insight (zhàojiàn), knowing-as-it-is (yathābhūta-jñāna), emptiness of the five aggregates, transcending all suffering, self-island self-refuge, awakening circuit, āśraya-parāvṛtti, prajñāpāramitā

## 1. The Problem

When most people hear "illuminating the emptiness of the five aggregates," their first response is to *think* about it — thinking about what form is, what feeling is, what emptiness is. But illuminating insight is not thinking.

Form, feeling, perception, volition, consciousness — the third of the five aggregates (pañca-skandha) is "perception" (saṃjñā), whose function is to apprehend characteristics, discriminate, and analyze[^1]. When you use perception to understand the emptiness of the five aggregates, you are using one of the five aggregates to negate all five — a paradox on its face. It is like a mother recognizing her child in a crowd: she is not analyzing "his hair looks like this, his height is this, he's wearing that shirt." She sees and knows. That recognition is not *thought out*; it is directly recognized.

Illuminating insight is precisely this kind of direct recognition.

Yet while illuminating insight may sound easy, it is in fact extraordinarily difficult. Form-aggregate is still relatively easy to observe — the material world you can see and touch. But what about feeling? Can you clearly discern at every moment whether you are experiencing suffering, pleasure, or neither-suffering-nor-pleasure? Perception? Can you catch a thought at the very instant it arises and see it as "perception"? Volitional formations? Those subtle impulses of will (cetanā) that drive your behavior — can you detect them? Consciousness? Many people cannot even articulate the difference between the eye-faculty and eye-consciousness.

Remove one measure of affliction, gain one measure of wisdom. Dust on a mirror must be wiped away bit by bit — wipe a little clean, and you see the five aggregates a little more clearly. This is not accomplished in a single leap.

S2-P02 established the four phases of the awakening circuit: Avalokiteśvara (subject) → deep practice (method) → illuminating the emptiness of the five aggregates (insight) → transcending all suffering (fruition). S2-P03 explored the second phase in depth — how prajñā sinks into the saṃskāra-skandha, transforming conceptual understanding into instinctive response. The present paper addresses the latter half of the circuit: after prajñā has sunk in, what does it "see"? And after seeing, how is suffering transcended?

Core thesis: Illuminating insight is the actualization of innate uncontaminated seeds — innate in cause, cultivated in fruition; it is not a newly manufactured capacity. What is "seen" as empty refers specifically to parikalpita (the thoroughly imagined nature) — not that nothing exists at all. Transcending suffering is the natural output of illuminating insight: suffering arises from "self"; illuminating insight dissolves self-grasping; āśraya-parāvṛtti (the transformation of the basis) is accomplished here, and the awakening circuit closes.

## 2. Scriptural Foundations

### 2.1 The Buddha's Teaching on "Knowing-as-It-Is" — The Āgama Source of Illuminating Insight

The *Heart Sūtra*'s "illuminating insight" did not emerge from nowhere. In the Buddha's earliest teachings, this capacity was called "knowing-as-it-is" (yathābhūta-jñāna, rúshí zhī 如實知).

In a classic exchange between Śāriputra and Mahākoṣṭhila, Śāriputra defines what constitutes "clear knowing" (vidyā):

> 「拘絺羅！於此五受陰如實知、見、明、覺、慧、無間等，是名為明；成就此法者，是名有明。」[^2]
>
> "Koṣṭhila! To know the five appropriated aggregates as they truly are — to know, see, understand, awaken to, and penetrate them without interval — this is called 'clear knowing.' One who has accomplished this is called 'one with clear knowing.'"

"Clear knowing" (vidyā) is knowing the five aggregates as they truly are — knowing their impermanence, their destruction, their arising and ceasing. Its opposite, "ignorance" (avidyā), is precisely not knowing the five aggregates as they truly are. The opposite of illuminating insight is not "failing to see," but "knowing without truth" — you think you see, but what you actually see is your own projection.

Knowing-as-it-is is no empty talk. The Buddha's direct instruction to Rādha reveals its practical effect:

> 「若多聞聖弟子於此五受陰如實觀察非我、非我所者，於諸世間都無所取，無所取者無所著，無所著故自覺涅槃：『我生已盡，梵行已立，所作已作，自知不受後有。』」[^3]
>
> "If a learned noble disciple observes the five appropriated aggregates as they truly are — as not-self, as not belonging to self — then in regard to all worldly things there is no grasping. Without grasping, there is no attachment. Without attachment, one realizes nirvāṇa for oneself: 'Birth is ended, the holy life is fulfilled, what was to be done has been done, I know there will be no further becoming.'"

Truly observing the five appropriated aggregates as not-self leads to non-grasping, non-attachment, and self-realization of nirvāṇa. This is the most refined prototype of "illuminating the emptiness of the five aggregates" found in the Āgama corpus.

Even more critically, the Buddha reveals the direct causal relationship between knowing-as-it-is and the cessation of suffering:

> 「多聞聖弟子於色集、色滅、色味、色患、色離如實知；如實知故，於彼色不起愛樂、讚歎、攝受、染著；不愛樂故，色愛則滅，愛滅則取滅，取滅則有滅，有滅則生滅，生滅則老、病、死、憂、悲、苦、惱滅，如是純大苦聚滅。」[^4]
>
> "The learned noble disciple knows as it truly is the arising, cessation, gratification, danger, and escape with respect to form. Because of this true knowing, no delight, praise, appropriation, or attachment arises toward form. Without delight, craving for form ceases; with craving's cessation, grasping ceases; with grasping's cessation, becoming ceases; with becoming's cessation, birth ceases; with birth's cessation, aging, illness, death, grief, sorrow, suffering, and distress cease — thus the entire mass of suffering ceases."

Knowing-as-it-is → no delight → no grasping → cessation of becoming → cessation of birth and death → cessation of suffering. The reversal sequence of the twelve links of dependent origination has "knowing-as-it-is" at its core — complete clarity regarding the gratification, danger, and escape of the five aggregates. This is the causal chain linking illuminating insight to the transcendence of suffering.

The Buddha's own account of ānāpānasati practice demonstrates the most fundamental operation of knowing-as-it-is:

> 「入息時念入息如實知，出息時念出息如實知。若長若短，一切身覺入息念如實知。」[^5]
>
> "Breathing in, one is mindfully aware — one knows it as it is. Breathing out, one is mindfully aware — one knows it as it is. Whether long or short, whatever bodily sensation accompanies the in-breath, one knows it as it is."

Starting from the breath, one "knowing-as-it-is" at a time. First knowing the coarse characteristics of the breath, then entering ever subtler observation. Illuminating insight does not arrive in a single bound; it begins with the most basic body-mindfulness (kāya-smṛty-upasthāna) and deepens layer by layer.

### 2.2 The Noble Truth of Suffering — The Buddha's Foundational Teaching at the First Turning of the Dharma Wheel

The ultimate aim of illuminating insight is to transcend suffering. What, then, is suffering?

In his first teaching to the five ascetics at Deer Park, the Buddha directly defines the Noble Truth of Suffering:

> 「此苦聖諦，本所未曾聞法，當正思惟。時，生眼、智、明、覺。」[^6]
>
> "This Noble Truth of Suffering — a teaching never heard before — when rightly contemplated, gives rise to vision, knowledge, clear knowing, and awakening."

"Gives rise to vision, knowledge, clear knowing, and awakening" — the Noble Truth of Suffering is not a pessimistic conclusion but a mode of illuminating insight. The very moment you see suffering clearly is the moment wisdom arises.

The *Madhyama Āgama*'s *Discourse on the Analysis of the Noble Truths* unfolds the definition of the eight kinds of suffering in detail. The four sufferings of birth, aging, illness, and death deserve particular attention:

> 「眾生生法，不離生法，欲得令我而不生者，此實不可以欲而得。老法、死法、愁憂慼法，不離憂慼法，欲得令我不憂慼者，此亦不可以欲而得。」[^7]
>
> "Beings are subject to the nature of birth and are inseparable from it; wishing 'May I not be born' — this truly cannot be achieved by wishing. Subject to aging, death, and grief — wishing 'May I not grieve' — this likewise cannot be achieved by wishing."

"This truly cannot be achieved by wishing" — you may wish to never age or die, but this is beyond your control. The sufferings people ordinarily experience — hunger, being disliked, not getting what one wants — are relatively superficial; a measure of good fortune can alleviate them. But the four sufferings of birth, aging, illness, and death are entirely beyond control. No matter how wealthy, powerful, or respected you are, you cannot control aging, you cannot control illness, you cannot control death. That "all suffering" can be transcended — consider the magnitude of this wisdom.

### 2.3 Suffering Arises from "Self" — Imputing a Self Is the Cause of Suffering

Where is the root of suffering? The Buddha's answer is clear: it lies in "self."

> 「諸沙門、婆羅門計有我，一切皆於此五受陰計有我。……如是愚癡無聞凡夫，計我、無明、分別如是觀，不離我所；不離我所者，入於諸根；入於諸根已，而生於觸；六觸入所觸，愚癡無聞凡夫生苦樂，從是生此等及餘。」[^8]
>
> "All recluses and brahmins who impute a self, all impute it upon these five appropriated aggregates. ... Thus the ignorant unlearned worldling, imputing a self through ignorance and discrimination, does not separate from 'mine'; not separating from 'mine,' one enters into the sense faculties; having entered, contact arises; touched by the six sense bases, the ignorant worldling generates suffering and pleasure — from this arise all these and the rest."

The causal chain is perfectly clear: imputing a self → not separating from "mine" → entering the sense faculties → contact → suffering and pleasure. Where there is "self," there is birth, aging, illness, and death. The Buddha continues:

> 「法無有吾我，亦復無我所；我既非當有，我所何由生？比丘解脫此，則斷下分結。」[^9]
>
> "In phenomena there is no self, nor is there anything belonging to a self. Since self will not exist, how could 'mine' arise? A bhikṣu who is liberated from this severs the lower fetters."

No self, no possessions of self — a bhikṣu who can free themselves from this view severs the three fetters and attains the fruit of stream-entry. The "emptiness" in "illuminating the emptiness of the five aggregates" ultimately comes down to the dissolution of self-grasping.

The twelve links of dependent origination further unfold the mechanism by which suffering accumulates. The Buddha uses the analogy of planting a tree:

> 「如人種樹，初小軟弱，愛護令安，壅以糞土，隨時溉灌，冷暖調適，以是因緣，然後彼樹得增長大。如是，比丘！結所繫法味著將養，則生恩愛，愛緣取，取緣有，有緣生，生緣老、病、死、憂、悲、惱、苦，如是如是純大苦聚集。」[^10]
>
> "Just as when someone plants a tree — initially small and tender — they protect and nurture it, heap it with soil, water it at proper times, and adjust for cold and warmth, and through these conditions the tree grows large. Just so, bhikṣus! When one relishes and nurtures what the fetters bind, affection arises. Conditioned by craving, grasping arises; conditioned by grasping, becoming; conditioned by becoming, birth; conditioned by birth, aging, illness, death, grief, sorrow, distress, and suffering — thus the entire great mass of suffering accumulates."

Your relishing of the five aggregates is like watering the tree of suffering. Craving → grasping → becoming → birth → aging-illness-death-suffering: this is not abstract philosophy but something each person does every day.

The Buddha also uses the analogy of lamp oil:

> 「譬如緣膏油及炷，燈明得燒，數增油、炷，彼燈明得久住不？……如是，諸比丘！於色取味著、顧念、愛縛，增長愛緣故取，取緣有，有緣生，生緣老、病、死、憂、悲、苦、惱。」[^11]
>
> "Suppose a lamp burns depending on oil and a wick. If one continually adds oil and wick, will that lamp not continue to burn? ... Just so, bhikṣus! Relishing, cherishing, and being bound by craving toward form — because craving increases, grasping arises; conditioned by grasping, becoming; conditioned by becoming, birth; conditioned by birth, aging, illness, death, grief, sorrow, suffering, and distress."

A lamp to which oil is continually added will not go out; a person who continually relishes the five aggregates will not be free from suffering.

### 2.4 The Original Teaching on Transcendence — Self-Island Self-Refuge, and Even the Dharma Should Be Relinquished

How, then, is suffering transcended?

In the scriptures, the Buddha repeatedly employs the metaphor of crossing a river. The *Saṃyukta Āgama* compares saṃsāra to a river in violent flood:

> 「譬如河水從山㵎出，彼水深駛，其流激注……眾人涉渡，多為水所漂，隨流沒溺，遇浪近岸，手援草木，草木復斷，還隨水漂。如是，比丘！若凡愚眾生不如實知色、色集、色滅，不如實知故，樂著於色，言色是我，彼色隨斷。……若多聞聖弟子如實知色，如實知故，不樂著於色。」[^12]
>
> "Suppose river water rushes from a mountain gorge — deep, swift, its current torrential ... Many people try to ford it, but most are swept away by the water, carried along and drowned. Encountering waves near the bank, they grab at grasses and branches, but the grasses break and they are swept away again. Just so, bhikṣus! When ignorant worldlings do not know form as it truly is, they delight in and attach to form, claiming 'form is my self' — and that form breaks apart. ... But the learned noble disciple knows form as it truly is, and therefore does not delight in or attach to form."

Those who cling to the five aggregates (the grasses and branches) are swept away by the flood; those who know the five aggregates as they truly are can cross safely. The core of transcending suffering is not to annihilate suffering, but to know-as-it-is.

But more importantly — the first step of transcendence is to transcend oneself. After Śāriputra's parinirvāṇa, the Buddha said to Ānanda:

> 「阿難！當作自洲而自依，當作法洲而法依，當作不異洲不異依。」[^13]
>
> "Ānanda! You should make yourself an island, make yourself your own refuge. Make the Dharma an island, make the Dharma your refuge. Do not seek any other island, any other refuge."

Self-island, self-refuge — take yourself as your island, take yourself as your refuge. How? The Buddha continues: the four foundations of mindfulness. Body contemplation, feeling contemplation, mind contemplation, dharma contemplation. The Buddha's final instruction to Ānanda before his own parinirvāṇa was likewise:

> 「阿難！當自熾燃，熾燃於法，勿他熾燃；當自歸依，歸依於法，勿他歸依。」[^14]
>
> "Ānanda! You should be your own lamp, blaze with the Dharma, do not depend on another for light. Take yourself as refuge, take the Dharma as refuge, do not take refuge in another."

Here we encounter a common inversion in modern thinking: the belief that "if I help others across, I myself will be saved." The Buddha's teaching is precisely the opposite — first save yourself. The *Dīrgha Āgama* states it even more explicitly:

> 「自能調伏，能調伏人；自得止息，能止息人；自度彼岸，能使人度；自得解脫，能解脫人；自得滅度，能滅度人。」[^15]
>
> "One who can discipline oneself can discipline others. One who has attained tranquility can bring others to tranquility. One who has crossed to the far shore can enable others to cross. One who has attained liberation can liberate others. One who has attained nirvāṇa can bring others to nirvāṇa."

First cross to the far shore yourself, then you can help others cross. If you yourself cannot swim, how can you teach others to swim? The first thing to be illuminated is this: the one who needs to be saved is yourself. You yourself are caught in birth, aging, illness, and death, and time is running short.

And after reaching the far shore? The Buddha teaches through the classic raft simile:

> 「今我此栰多有所益，乘此栰已，令我安隱，從彼岸來，度至此岸。我今寧可更以此栰還著水中，或著岸邊而捨去耶？……如是。我為汝等長夜說栰喻法，欲令棄捨，不欲令受。若汝等知我長夜說栰喻法者，當以捨是法，況非法耶？」[^16]
>
> "'This raft has been of great benefit to me. Riding it, I came safely from that shore to this shore. Should I now carry this raft upon my head or set it aside on the bank and go on my way?' ... Just so. For a long time I have taught you the Dharma using the simile of the raft — it is for crossing over, not for holding on to. If you understand the Dharma taught through the raft simile, then even the Dharma should be relinquished — how much more so what is not-Dharma?"

The true Dharma is like a raft for crossing the river: once you reach the far shore, it should be set down. If even the Dharma should be relinquished, how much more so what is not-Dharma. The spirit of transcendence is not clinging to a tool or an identity, but naturally letting go once the crossing is complete.

The *Diamond Sūtra* pushes the paradox of transcendence to its ultimate expression:

> 「所有一切眾生之類……我皆令入無餘涅槃而滅度之。如是滅度無量、無數、無邊眾生，實無眾生得滅度者。何以故？須菩提！若菩薩有我相、人相、眾生相、壽者相，即非菩薩。」[^17]
>
> "All the various types of sentient beings ... I shall lead them all to nirvāṇa without remainder, thereby liberating them. Yet having thus liberated immeasurable, countless, boundless sentient beings, in truth no sentient being has been liberated. Why? Subhūti! If a bodhisattva has the notion of a self, the notion of a person, the notion of a sentient being, or the notion of a life span, then they are not a bodhisattva."

Liberating all sentient beings while in truth no sentient being is liberated — if the thought "I am liberating" arises, one falls into the notion of a self, and is not a bodhisattva. This is the ultimate meaning of transcendence.

## 3. Core Arguments

### 3.1 Where Does the Capacity for Illuminating Insight Come From? — Innate in Cause, Cultivated in Fruition

If illuminating insight is simply knowing-as-it-is, why are most people unable to achieve it?

The Āgama sūtras provide half the answer: because of the obstruction of ignorance. "Not knowing form as it truly is, one delights in form" — it is not that you lack the capacity to see, but that the dust is too thick. Yogācāra provides the other half: the capacity for illuminating insight has always been present — as innate uncontaminated seeds — but it requires study, reflection, and cultivation to bring it into actuality.

Kuījī defines illuminating insight through the five kinds of vision:

> 「今在因位，慧眼達空，明了矚觀，故名照見。然此空性，資糧位中聽聞、思惟，多唯信解；在加行位方純修觀，雖皆名照，猶帶相故，而未證真；住十地中起無漏觀，通達真理，方實照空；至如來位照見圓滿。」[^18]
>
> "At present, in the causal stage, the wisdom-eye penetrates emptiness — clearly gazing and observing — hence the name 'illuminating insight.' Yet this emptiness: at the stage of accumulation (sambhāra), through hearing and reflection, is mostly only accepted on faith; at the stage of preparation (prayoga), one first engages in pure contemplation, and though all of this can be called 'illuminating,' because it still involves characteristics, it has not yet realized truth; dwelling in the ten bhūmis, one arouses uncontaminated contemplation and penetrates true reality — only then is emptiness truly illuminated; at the stage of a Tathāgata, illuminating insight reaches perfection."

Four stages, each deeper than the last: accumulation (faith-based understanding, not yet true illumination) → preparation (contemplation with characteristics, not yet realized) → the ten bhūmis (true illumination of emptiness) → Tathāgata stage (illuminating insight perfected). This is not instantaneous mystical power but a long process of wiping the mirror clean.

✅ **Doctrinally sound** (義理可立): Illuminating insight is a function of the prajñā wisdom-eye, not a sensory experience of the physical eye or divine eye. The four stages of gradual deepening are consistent with the Āgama teaching on the progressive strengthening of the five spiritual faculties.

Here an analogy may help.

When an animal looks in a mirror, it does not recognize itself — it becomes frightened, even attacks the reflection. This is the ordinary person's relationship to the five aggregates: not knowing that the aggregates are manifestations of one's own mind, one takes them for external entities, and so craves, fears, and fights them.

A great ape can recognize that the figure in the mirror is itself. But think back to your own first experience of looking in a mirror — were you not startled? That moment of recognition is analogous to the shock of first encountering the Dharma: "So *this* is how it all works."

A mother recognizes her child in a crowd at a glance — no analysis, no comparison, she sees and knows. This is what illuminating insight is like once prajñā has been activated: without the mediation of "perception" (saṃjñā), directly recognizing the true nature of the five aggregates.

The mirror itself has always been there. When the dust is thick, it reflects nothing clearly; when a little dust is removed, it reflects a little more clearly. Remove one measure of affliction, gain one measure of wisdom; wipe away one measure of dust, see the five aggregates one measure more clearly. The capacity for illuminating insight is not installed from outside — it is polished from within.

⚠️ **Provisional analogy** (假說比喻): A mirror passively reflects; illuminating insight is the active contemplation of non-discriminative wisdom (nirvikalpa-jñāna). This analogy is used only for its sense of "an innate capacity that becomes manifest through the removal of obstructions," not for the sense of "passive reflection."

### 3.2 The Two Levels of Illuminating Insight — Capacity and Object

Illuminating insight operates on two levels, both indispensable.

**Level one: Do you have the capacity for illuminating insight?**

The form-aggregate — the material world — is still relatively easy to observe. But feeling? Can you discern at every moment whether you are experiencing suffering, pleasure, or neither-suffering-nor-pleasure? Perception? Can you catch a thought at the instant it arises? Volitional formations? Those subtle impulses of will driving your reactions — can you detect them? Consciousness? Many people cannot even articulate the difference between the eye-faculty and eye-consciousness.

The faculty of wisdom is defined as knowing the Four Noble Truths as they truly are[^19]. The capacity for illuminating insight is the power of this wisdom-faculty.

**Level two: What are you illuminating?**

Some people, having gained a degree of insight, see "the five aggregates all exist" — "Right, I have form, I have feeling, I have perception, I have volitional formations, I have consciousness." They do not realize that what is to be seen is "the five aggregates are empty."

This distinction is critical. Illuminating insight is not merely "seeing"; it is seeing the right thing. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* points out that even the five kinds of vision find "mind to be unapprehensible":

> 「佛以五眼觀此心不可得。……眾生心，見者，如實見；不如凡夫人憶想分別見。」[^20]
>
> "The Buddha, with the five kinds of vision, observes this mind and finds it unapprehensible. ... The minds of sentient beings — to truly see them is to see as they really are; not like the recollective-discriminative seeing of an ordinary worldling."

True illuminating insight is "seeing as it really is" (yathābhūta-darśana), not "recollective-discriminative seeing." Using "perception" — one of the five aggregates — to analyze the emptiness of the five aggregates is precisely "recollective-discriminative seeing": inference (anumāna) rather than direct perception (pratyakṣa). Illuminating insight is the direct intuition of prajñā, the direct perception of non-discriminative wisdom, which transcends the domain of "perception" altogether.

### 3.3 The Emptiness of the Five Aggregates — Eliminating the False, Preserving the Real

What exactly does "empty" mean?

Kuījī's definition is precise:

> 「據實三性非空非不空，對破有執總密說空，非後二性都無名空，說一切空是佛密意，於有及無總說空故。」[^21]
>
> "In reality, the three natures are neither empty nor non-empty. To counter attachment to existence, the Buddha spoke of 'emptiness' in a comprehensive and implicit way. It is not that the latter two natures are called 'empty' in the sense of total nonexistence. 'All is empty' is the Buddha's implicit intention — speaking of 'emptiness' with respect to both existence and non-existence."

The three natures (tri-svabhāva) are neither empty nor non-empty: parikalpita (the thoroughly imagined) is empty — the "self" and "mine" you project onto things are empty; but paratantra (the other-dependent) is not empty — the functioning of the five aggregates as produced by conditions is real; and pariniṣpanna (the perfectly accomplished) is not empty — the essence of true suchness (tathatā) is genuinely real. "All is empty" is the Buddha's implicit teaching (abhiprāya), not a declaration that nothing whatsoever exists.

Yuáncè further harmonizes the emptiness and existence schools:

> 「護法依《深密》等……立一切法通有及無，遍計所執情有理無，依他起性因緣故有，圓成實性理有非無。」[^22]
>
> "Dharmapāla, relying on the *Saṃdhinirmocana* and related texts, establishes that all dharmas encompass both existence and non-existence: the thoroughly imagined nature exists emotionally but not in principle; the other-dependent nature exists through conditions; the perfectly accomplished nature exists in principle and is not non-existent."

The five-aggregate similes — form is like foam, feeling like a bubble, perception like a mirage, formations like a plantain trunk, consciousness like a magical illusion[^23] — describe not "the five aggregates do not exist" but the other-dependent character of the five aggregates: lacking inherent nature, lacking solidity, produced by conditions, arising and ceasing moment to moment. The solid, unchanging "self" you imagine (parikalpita) is empty, but the dynamic flow of the five aggregates themselves is real.

✅ **Doctrinally sound** (義理可立): Emptiness here refers specifically to the emptiness of parikalpita. "Illuminating the emptiness of the five aggregates" = illuminating the fact that the parikalpita projections onto the five aggregates are empty, not that the five aggregates themselves do not exist.

### 3.4 Transcending All Suffering — First Save Yourself; Suffering Arises from "Self"

After illuminating the emptiness of the five aggregates, transcending all suffering is the natural output. Why?

Because suffering arises from "self." As already cited from the Buddha's teaching: imputing a self → not separating from "mine" → entering the sense faculties → contact → suffering and pleasure. Where there is "self," there is birth, aging, illness, and death. When one illuminates the emptiness of parikalpita (self-grasping) projected onto the five aggregates, the cause of suffering is undercut at its root.

But here lies a common blind spot of the modern mind: it is easy to see that others are suffering — seeing disasters in the news and feeling pity, seeing a friend heartbroken and feeling sympathy — yet completely lacking the wisdom to illuminate that one is oneself the sufferer. If a person does not perceive their own suffering, they will see no need for transcendence at all.

The first thing to be illuminated is this: the one who needs to be saved is yourself.

The Buddha says "make yourself an island, make yourself your own refuge." Before his parinirvāṇa, the Buddha says "be your own lamp, take the Dharma as refuge." The *Dīrgha Āgama* says "first cross to the far shore yourself, then you can help others cross." The consensus of all the scriptures is: save yourself first.

A view that has gained currency today is problematic: "If I save others, I myself will be saved." This sounds noble, but it is logically incoherent — if you cannot swim yourself, how can you teach others to swim? The bodhisattva's union of compassion and wisdom is built upon one's own realization.

The three characters "all suffering" (yīqiè kǔ 一切苦) merit deep reflection. The suffering sentient beings ordinarily experience tends to be relatively superficial: hunger, being disliked, not getting what one wants — these are the suffering of suffering (duḥkha-duḥkhatā), alleviated somewhat by good fortune. But among the eight kinds of suffering, the four of birth, aging, illness, and death are entirely beyond control. No matter how wealthy, how powerful, how respected you are, you cannot control aging, cannot control illness, cannot control death. That "all suffering" can be transcended — consider the sheer magnitude of this prajñā wisdom.

An ant needs to cross a small stream — for the ant, it is an abyss. But a human steps across effortlessly. The same stream, but from a different vantage point the difficulty changes completely. In the same way, what is unbearable suffering for some — someone says one word and it lodges in their mind — others can move past without difficulty. Prajñā wisdom elevates your vantage point from ant to human. It is not that suffering ceases to exist; it is that you have acquired the capacity to cross over.

⚠️ **Provisional analogy** (假說比喻): The difference in perspective between ant and human is a metaphor for how levels of wisdom produce different experiences of suffering. This analogy is used for its sense of "an elevated vantage point renders obstacles no longer obstacles," not for the sense of "suffering does not exist." Transcending suffering is not anesthesia — it is a fundamental elevation of capacity.

Kuījī uses the analogy of "a moth emerging from its cocoon" for transcending suffering:

> 「既照性空，離諸分別，如蛾出繭，永離纏裹，便度苦厄。」[^24]
>
> "Having illuminated the emptiness of nature and departed from all discrimination — like a moth emerging from its cocoon, forever leaving its wrapping behind — one thereby transcends suffering."

Illuminating the emptiness of nature → departing from all discrimination → like a moth from its cocoon → transcending suffering. The cocoon is transcended, not annihilated. Suffering still exists in the world, but one is no longer bound by it.

More precisely, transcending suffering is āśraya-parāvṛtti — the transformation of the basis. The cultivation of non-discriminative wisdom (nirvikalpa-jñāna) brings about this transformation. The *Chéng Wéishí Lùn* defines it:

> 「轉捨依他起上遍計所執，及能轉得依他起中圓成實性。由轉煩惱得大涅槃，轉所知障證無上覺。」[^25]
>
> "To relinquish the thoroughly imagined nature superimposed upon the other-dependent, and to obtain the perfectly accomplished nature within the other-dependent. By transforming afflictions, one attains great nirvāṇa; by transforming the cognitive obscurations, one realizes supreme awakening."

Āśraya-parāvṛtti does not annihilate the five aggregates. Rather, upon the same other-dependent basis (the conditioned flow of the five aggregates), it relinquishes parikalpita (the projections of self-grasping and dharma-grasping) and reveals pariniṣpanna (tathatā, true suchness). Transforming afflictions yields nirvāṇa; transforming cognitive obscurations yields bodhi — this is the Yogācāra formula for "transcending all suffering."

At this point, the awakening circuit completes its closure: Avalokiteśvara (the initial aspiration toward freedom) → deep prajñāpāramitā (transformation at the level of the saṃskāra-skandha) → illuminating the emptiness of the five aggregates (knowing-as-it-is that the parikalpita upon the aggregates is empty) → transcending all suffering (āśraya-parāvṛtti). And the default state after this transformation is precisely "Avalokiteśvara" — self-mastery, not because one has controlled something, but because the cause of suffering has been uprooted.

The endpoint returns to the starting point. This is the complete structure of the *Heart Sūtra*'s opening fourteen characters.

## 4. Interdisciplinary Dialogue

⚠️ **The following are heuristic analogies, not theoretical equivalences.**

The contemporary cognitive science theory of "Predictive Processing" provides an interesting point of comparison. According to this theory, the brain does not passively receive external information but actively constructs perception — what we "see" is largely the brain's prediction, not the actual signal[^26].

This bears structural similarity to the mechanism of parikalpita: sentient beings do not directly experience the conditioned flow of the five aggregates (paratantra) but rather project patterns of "self" and "mine" onto them (parikalpita), then mistake the projection for reality. You think you are seeing the world, but in fact you are seeing your own projection.

Illuminating insight, in this analogy, is like pausing the brain's automatic prediction — no longer interpreting experience through habitual patterns, but facing experience itself directly. "Eliminating the false, preserving the real" (qiǎnxū cúnshí 遣虛存實) is like stripping away the predictive overlay to reveal the underlying actual signal.

However, this analogy has three strict boundaries. First, Predictive Processing always operates within a subject-object framework — there is a "brain" predicting an "external world"; illuminating insight, through non-discriminative wisdom, transcends subject-object duality. Second, the phenomenological "epoché" is a methodological suspension; āśraya-parāvṛtti is an ontological revolution — not a temporary suspension of projection, but a transformation of the very seeds of projection. Third, the "updating of priors" in cognitive science is a computational adjustment — replacing an old model with a better one; "eliminating the false, preserving the real" is the cessation of the parikalpita mechanism itself, not the substitution of a better set of imagined constructs.

## 5. Middle Way Corrections

### ❌ 5.1 "Illuminating Insight" Does Not Mean "No Practice Is Needed" (邏輯有誤)

Innate in cause does not mean the fruition arises by itself. Kuījī explicitly identifies four stages: at the stage of accumulation, there is only faith-based understanding; at the stage of preparation, contemplation still involves characteristics and has not yet realized truth; only at the ten bhūmis is emptiness truly illuminated; only at the Tathāgata stage is illumination perfected. A mirror has the capacity to reflect from the moment it is manufactured, but if the dust is not wiped away, it reflects nothing. "Originally endowed" refers to the potential, not to a ready-made accomplishment.

### ❌ 5.2 "The Five Aggregates Are Empty" Does Not Mean "Nothing Exists At All" (邏輯有誤)

Kuījī's warning cannot be ignored: "'All is empty' is the Buddha's implicit teaching." When the Buddha speaks of "emptiness," it is an expedient means to counter sentient beings' attachment to existence — not a description of nihilistic nothingness. The actual position is that the three natures are neither empty nor non-empty: only parikalpita is empty; paratantra functions are genuinely existent; pariniṣpanna is truly real in principle. To interpret "emptiness" as "nothing exists at all" is, ironically, just another form of parikalpita.

### ❌ 5.3 Śrāvaka Contemplation of Emptiness Is Not the Same as the Bodhisattva's Illuminating Insight (邏輯有誤)

The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* draws a clear distinction: the five-aggregate similes (form like foam, etc.) belong to the Śrāvaka Dharma — dissolving self-grasping while acknowledging the real existence of conditions; the gandharva-city simile belongs to the Mahāyāna Dharma — even the conditions themselves are empty, dissolving dharma-grasping as well[^27]. The same act of "contemplating emptiness," but with different ranges of application. The *Heart Sūtra*'s "illuminating the emptiness of the five aggregates" is the bodhisattva's contemplation, surpassing the Śrāvaka level.

### ❌ 5.4 "Transcending Suffering" Does Not Mean "No Longer Feeling Suffering" (邏輯有誤)

Transcending suffering is not anesthesia. Kuījī's "moth emerging from the cocoon" makes this perfectly clear — the cocoon is transcended, not annihilated. After transcending suffering, the bodhisattva does not dwell in a vacuum free from suffering; quite the opposite — the bodhisattva continues to save sentient beings in the suffering world. What changes is not the existence of suffering, but one's relationship to suffering. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* says that after the bodhisattva has contemplated the true character of all dharmas, great compassion causes them to "return and perform meritorious deeds" to save sentient beings[^28] — the union of compassion and wisdom has as its prerequisite that you have already saved yourself.

### ❌ 5.5 "Saving Others" Does Not Mean "Saving Yourself" (邏輯有誤)

A common misunderstanding: "If I save others, I myself will be saved." The Buddha's teaching is precisely the opposite — "make yourself an island, yourself your refuge"; "be your own lamp, take the Dharma as refuge"; "first cross to the far shore yourself, then you can help others cross." The consensus of all the scriptures is: save yourself first. If you cannot swim, how can you teach others to swim? The bodhisattva's "saving both self and others" does not mean skipping self-liberation to go straight to liberating others; it is built upon one's own level of realization. The *Diamond Sūtra*'s "in truth no sentient being has been liberated" goes even further — if in the process of liberating beings you harbor the thought "I am liberating," that is precisely attachment to the notion of a self, and that is precisely not being a bodhisattva.

## 6. Conclusion

「照見五蘊皆空，度一切苦厄」— fourteen characters, a complete path of awakening:

First, **illuminating insight is recognition, not acquisition.** Innate uncontaminated seeds are the potential; study, reflection, and cultivation are the labor of wiping the mirror. You are not installing a new function — you are removing obstructions so that an innate capacity can manifest.

Second, **illuminating insight is not thinking.** Using "perception" to analyze the emptiness of the five aggregates is like using one of the five aggregates to negate all five. Illuminating insight is the direct intuition of prajñā — direct perception (pratyakṣa), not inference (anumāna); a mother recognizing her child, not analyzing her way to her child.

Third, **"emptiness" has a precise scope.** Emptiness here refers specifically to the emptiness of parikalpita — the "self" and "mine" you project onto the five aggregates are empty. The conditioned flow of paratantra is not empty; the tathatā of pariniṣpanna is not empty. The three natures are neither empty nor non-empty.

Fourth, **the first thing to illuminate is that you yourself are the sufferer.** People readily see others' suffering but do not know they are themselves mired in it. Self-island, self-refuge — save yourself first.

Fifth, **transcending suffering is the natural output of illuminating insight.** Suffering arises from "self"; illuminating insight dissolves "self"; the cause of suffering is undercut at its root. The moth leaves the cocoon; the basis is transformed — not annihilating the five aggregates, but relinquishing parikalpita upon paratantra to reveal pariniṣpanna.

Sixth, **the awakening circuit closes here.** Avalokiteśvara → deep practice → illuminating insight → transcending suffering → back to Avalokiteśvara. Self-mastery is the default state after the transformation of the basis. The endpoint is the starting point.

This paper provides the "general account." Next, S2-P05 will proceed to the "detailed analysis" — *form is not different from emptiness, emptiness is not different from form* — examining aggregate by aggregate how prajñā illuminates the emptiness of each.
