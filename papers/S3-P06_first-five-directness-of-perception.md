## Abstract

The first five consciousnesses — eye, ear, nose, tongue, and body consciousness — constitute the mode of cognition closest to pure direct perception (pratyakṣa) within the eight-consciousness architecture. They present the intrinsic characteristics of their objects directly, without verbal-conceptual overlay, without comparative judgment, without conceptual discrimination. Yet precisely because of this directness, the five sense-consciousnesses also serve as the entry point of the entire assembly line of suffering: "Conditioned by the eye and form, eye consciousness arises; the conjunction of these three produces contact; conditioned by contact, feeling; conditioned by feeling, craving" — from pure seeing to "I like this" lies merely a single thought-moment apart. The space for practice resides precisely in that interval.

This paper takes the three verses on the first five consciousnesses from Xuanzang's *Verses on the Characteristics of the Eight Consciousnesses* (八識規矩頌) as its structural framework. Drawing sūtra-level authority from the *Saṃyukta Āgama*, *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra*, *Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra*, *Śūraṅgama Sūtra*, *Avataṃsaka Sūtra*, *Heart Sūtra*, and *Vimalakīrti Sūtra*, and treatise-level argument from the *Cheng Weishi Lun* and *Bāshí guījǔ bǔzhù*, it systematically unfolds the complete doctrine of the five sense-consciousnesses.

§II establishes the scriptural foundations in two tiers: sūtras first (Āgama six sense-bases and sense-faculty restraint, *Saṃdhinirmocana* torrent-and-wave metaphor, *Laṅkāvatāra* four conditions for eye consciousness and ocean-wave metaphor, *Śūraṅgama* six sense-base deconstruction and the perfect penetration methods, *Avataṃsaka* verses on entering samādhi through the six faculties, *Heart Sūtra* negation of the twelve bases), then treatises (*Cheng Weishi Lun* fascicle 7 on the five consciousnesses' essential nature, *Verses on Eight Consciousnesses* with complete annotations on all three verses). §III unfolds the core argument in eight layers: real objects and direct perception; the nine conditions for consciousness-arising; contact versus distance sense-apprehension; the critical distinction between faculty and consciousness; thirty-four associated mental factors; realm-and-ground distribution; epistemological limitations even at the fruit stage; and the wisdom of accomplished activities with ultimate transformation. §IV offers the primary visual cortex and association cortex division as a heuristic cross-disciplinary analogy. §V provides five middle-way corrections.

**Keywords:** first five consciousnesses (pañca-vijñāna-kāya), Verses on Eight Consciousnesses, real objects and direct perception (svabhāva-viṣaya, pratyakṣa), nine conditions for consciousness-arising, contact and distance sense-apprehension, pure form faculty (prasāda-rūpa), thirty-four mental factors, wisdom of accomplished activities (kṛtyānuṣṭhāna-jñāna)

## I. Introduction

### 1.1 Textual Position

S3-P03 unfolded the ālaya-vijñāna — the warehouse keeper, the silent holder of all seeds. S3-P04 unfolded manas — the middle manager wearing tinted glasses, ceaselessly grasping at self without knowing it. S3-P05 unfolded the sixth consciousness — the only mind that can look in the mirror, the primary battlefield of practice, the initiator of all weighty karma. Beginning with this paper, we arrive at the windows of the eight-consciousness architecture that open most directly onto the external world: the first five consciousnesses.

Among the eight consciousnesses in the *Śatadharmaprakāśamukha-śāstra* (百法明門論), the five sense-consciousnesses — eye, ear, nose, tongue, and body — are listed first through fifth.[^1] "First" does not mean primitive. Quite the contrary: the first five consciousnesses are the mode of cognition closest to pure direct perception (pratyakṣa) within the entire eight-consciousness system. They apprehend only real objects (svabhāva-viṣaya), employ only direct perception (pratyakṣa), and do not engage in conceptual discrimination (abhinirūpaṇā-vikalpa). Among all eight consciousnesses, the first five embody the "purest act of seeing."

The three verses on the first five consciousnesses from the *Verses on the Characteristics of the Eight Consciousnesses* encompass their complete doctrine:

> Real objects, direct perception, pervading three moral natures; / Eye, ear, and body — these three reside in two grounds; / Universals, object-specifics, eleven wholesome, / Two medium, eight major, plus greed, hatred, and delusion.[^2]

> All five alike depend on pure form faculties; / Nine conditions, seven and eight — close neighbors in their arising; / Three by contact, two at distance — thus they observe the dusty world; / The foolish find it hard to distinguish consciousness from faculty.[^3]

> Apprehending emptiness through transformed images — only post-attainment [wisdom]; / Even at the fruit stage, they still cannot directly engage the real; / When perfect luminosity first arises, they become non-outflow; / Three classes of emanation-bodies bring the wheel of suffering to rest.[^4]

Three verses, three layers: the first describes their essential nature — what objects they apprehend, what cognition they employ, what mental factors they associate with; the second describes their structural conditions — what faculties they depend on, what conditions they require, how they apprehend their objects; the third describes their transformation — what epistemological limits they face, where their ultimate destiny lies. This paper unfolds each layer in sequence.

### 1.2 The Problem

Are the first five consciousnesses merely passive couriers?

At first glance, they appear to be nothing more than deliverers of sensory signals — the eye sees form, the ear hears sound, the nose smells scent, the tongue tastes flavor, the body feels touch — and then they hand the signals over to the sixth consciousness for discrimination and judgment. If the primary battlefield of practice lies in consciousness (as S3-P05 established), the first five seem to be minor players.

But this understanding overlooks something critical: the entire assembly line of suffering begins at the first five consciousnesses. The Buddha's repeated Āgama teaching — "Conditioned by the eye and form, eye consciousness arises; the conjunction of these three produces contact; conditioned by contact, feeling; conditioned by feeling, craving; conditioned by craving, clinging ... thus arises the entire mass of suffering"[^5] — places the first link of the causal chain at the "conjunction of three" (根境識三和合) of the sense-consciousnesses. Without this starting point, the sixth consciousness's conceptual discrimination has nothing to work with.

More critically, the Buddha's teaching on sense-faculty restraint (indriya-saṃvara) — "When the eye sees form, do not grasp at the sign, do not grasp at the secondary characteristics"[^6] — operates precisely at the level of the first five consciousnesses. "Not grasping at the sign" does not mean closing one's eyes; it means not allowing the habitual pattern of sign-grasping to activate at the very moment of seeing. The space for practice lies between "pure seeing" and "that's beautiful, I like it" — between the five consciousnesses' direct presentation and the sixth consciousness's conceptual discrimination. That gap is where everything happens.

This is the true position of the first five consciousnesses in spiritual practice: they are not passive couriers but the place where the entrance to suffering and the entrance to liberation coexist. Through the same window, one can either trigger the entire twelve-link chain of dependent origination on its path of suffering-accumulation, or — if awareness is maintained in that gap — open the path of suffering-cessation.

### 1.3 Hundred Dharmas Coverage and Structure

This paper centers on the five sense-consciousnesses (mind-dharmas one through five), encompassing the complete argument for their thirty-four associated mental factors. The individual analysis of each mental factor will be unfolded in S3-P07 (universals and object-specifics) through S3-P10 (subsidiary afflictions); this paper focuses on the structural reasoning for why the five consciousnesses associate with these thirty-four and not the remaining seventeen.

The paper follows a sūtra-then-treatise two-tier architecture. §II establishes scriptural foundations in two layers: sūtras (Āgama, *Saṃdhinirmocana*, *Laṅkāvatāra*, *Śūraṅgama*, *Avataṃsaka*, *Heart Sūtra*, *Vimalakīrti*) and treatises (*Cheng Weishi Lun*, *Verses on Eight Consciousnesses*). §III unfolds the core argument in eight layers following the three-verse structure. §IV offers cross-disciplinary analogies. §V provides middle-way corrections. §VI concludes.

## II. Scriptural Foundations

### A. Sūtras — The Authority of the Buddha's Word

#### 2.1 The Āgamas: Six Sense-Bases and the Conjunction of Three

The doctrinal roots of the five sense-consciousnesses are fully established in the Buddha's earliest teachings. In the Āgamas, the Buddha uses the frameworks of the six sense-bases (āyatana) and the conjunction of faculty-object-consciousness to provide the foundational teaching on the conditioned arising of the five consciousnesses.

*Saṃyukta Āgama* Sūtra 321: the Buddha defines "all dharmas" for the brahmin Jāṇussoṇi:

> "Eye and form, eye consciousness, eye-contact, and feeling born of eye-contact — whether painful, pleasant, or neither-painful-nor-pleasant. Ear, nose, tongue, body, mental faculty and dharma-objects, mental consciousness, mental contact, and feeling born of mental contact — whether painful, pleasant, or neither-painful-nor-pleasant. This is called 'all dharmas.'"[^7]

"All dharmas" comprises nothing more than the six faculties, six objects, six consciousnesses, plus contact and feeling. Each of the five sense-consciousnesses occupies its own position within this framework.

*Saṃyukta Āgama* Sūtra 306 (the Hand-Clap Simile Sūtra) illustrates the mechanism of conjunction through a vivid metaphor:

> "Just as two hands brought together produce a sound — so, conditioned by the eye and form, eye consciousness arises. The conjunction of these three produces contact, and together with contact arise feeling, perception, and volition. All these dharmas are not-self, impermanent — they are an impermanent self, not enduring, not stable, subject to change."[^8]

The sound of a handclap belongs neither to the left hand nor to the right hand but arises from their conjunction — eye consciousness likewise belongs neither to the eye faculty nor to the form-object but arises from their conditioned conjunction. "All these dharmas are not-self, impermanent" — contact, feeling, perception, volition are all impermanent, all without self.

Sūtra 214 provides the most concise statement: "There are two conditions for the arising of consciousness: namely, eye-and-form, ear-and-sound, nose-and-scent, tongue-and-flavor, body-and-tangible, mental-faculty-and-dharma-objects."[^9] Consciousness does not arise from the faculty alone or the object alone, but from the conjunction of two conditions — this "conjunction" is the original seed from which Yogācāra later developed its precise system of "nine conditions for consciousness-arising."

#### 2.2 The Āgamas: Sense-Faculty Restraint — Practice at the Level of the Five Consciousnesses

The Buddha not only analyzed the structure of the five sense-consciousnesses but directly taught practice methods operating at their level — sense-faculty restraint (indriya-saṃvara).

*Saṃyukta Āgama* Sūtra 275 (Nanda's Closing of the Sense-Gates) is the paradigmatic teaching:

> "When the eye sees form, he does not grasp at the sign, does not grasp at the secondary characteristics. Whatever would cause the eye faculty to become unrestrained — the darkness of ignorance, worldly craving, unwholesome dharmas — these do not flood his mind. He generates restraint, guarding the eye. Likewise for the ear, nose, tongue, body, and mental faculties."[^10]

"Not grasping at the sign, not grasping at the secondary characteristics" — when the eye sees form, one does not seize the overall appearance and does not pursue attractive details. This is not closing one's eyes; it is maintaining awareness at the very junction of seeing so that the habitual momentum of sign-grasping does not carry one away.

Sūtra 241 (the Red-Hot Copper Needle Simile) underscores the gravity of this with extreme imagery:

> "Bhikkhus, it would be better for a foolish, unlearned ordinary person to have their eyes pierced with a red-hot copper needle, blazing and burning, than to grasp at the sign of forms with eye consciousness, than to grasp at the secondary characteristics. Why? Because grasping at the sign of forms and at the secondary characteristics leads to falling into evil destinies, sinking like an iron ball."[^11]

Better to be blinded by a hot needle than to let eye consciousness grasp at signs — not out of hatred for the senses, but from a profound recognition that once sign-grasping occurs, the entire causal chain of suffering activates with unstoppable momentum. Each of the five faculties receives its own parallel simile: copper needle for the eyes, iron awl for the ears, sharp knife for the nose, sharp knife for the tongue, iron spear for the body.

Sūtra 1167 (the Turtle Simile) offers a gentler expression of the same principle:

> "In the past there was a turtle dwelling in river grass. A hungry jackal came seeking prey. The turtle saw it coming and withdrew its six [limbs]. The jackal watched and waited ... the turtle never emerged; the jackal departed in frustration."[^12]

The turtle withdraws its six limbs into its shell; the bhikkhu restrains the six faculties. The six faculties are like the turtle's limbs — not severed, but skillfully withdrawn when needed.

#### 2.3 The Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra: Five Consciousnesses Arising in Dependence on the Ādāna — The Torrent-and-Wave Metaphor

If the Āgamas provide the original teaching on the five sense-consciousnesses, the *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra*'s chapter on mind-mentation-consciousness provides their structural positioning within the eight-consciousness architecture — the five consciousnesses arise in dependence on the ādāna consciousness.

> "Viśālamati, the ādāna consciousness serves as the support and foundation for the arising of the six consciousness-bodies — namely eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, and mental consciousness. Among these: conditioned by the eye and form, eye consciousness arises, and along with eye consciousness, simultaneously accompanying it, in the same temporal moment and the same cognitive field, a discriminating mental consciousness arises."[^13]

Three critical points: first, the five sense-consciousnesses take the ādāna consciousness (i.e., the ālaya-vijñāna) as their fundamental support; second, eye consciousness arises from the two conditions of eye faculty and form-object — continuous with the Āgama teaching of "two conditions for consciousness-arising"; third, accompanying every sense-consciousness there necessarily arises a "discriminating mental consciousness" — this is what Yogācāra calls the "five-concomitant consciousness" (五俱意識). The five sense-consciousnesses never operate alone.

The sūtra then deploys two masterly metaphors:

> "Viśālamati, it is like a great torrent of water: when the conditions for one wave are present, only one wave arises; when the conditions for two or many waves are present, many waves arise. Yet the torrent itself flows on, of its own kind, without interruption or exhaustion. Again, it is like a well-polished mirror surface: when the conditions for one reflection are present, only one reflection appears; when the conditions for many reflections are present, many reflections appear. The mirror surface does not transform into the reflections, nor can any exhaustion of their use be found."[^14]

The torrent metaphor: the ālaya-vijñāna is like the ever-flowing torrent; the five consciousnesses are like waves that arise and subside according to conditions. The mirror metaphor: the ālaya-vijñāna is like a clean mirror; the five consciousnesses are like reflections that appear and disappear. "The mirror surface does not transform into the reflections" — this perfectly illustrates the direct-perception (pratyakṣa) character of the five consciousnesses: the mirror simply reflects as things are, without processing.

The closing verse crystallizes the teaching:

> "The ādāna consciousness is profoundly subtle; / all seeds are like a torrent; / I do not teach this to the foolish, / lest they discriminate and grasp it as a self."[^15]

#### 2.4 The Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra: Four Conditions for Eye Consciousness and the Ocean-Wave Metaphor

The *Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra* deepens the analysis through a different lens. The Śikṣānanda translation identifies four conditions for eye consciousness:

> "There are four kinds of conditions for the arising of eye consciousness. What are the four? Not being aware of one's own mind's manifestation and grasping externally; beginningless habitual tendencies of false attachment to form; the intrinsic nature of consciousness being as it is; and the delight in seeing various forms."[^16]

Four conditions: (1) ignorance of one's own mind-projections (avidyā); (2) beginningless habit-patterns of attachment to form (seeds); (3) the intrinsic nature of consciousness (dharmatā); (4) the inclination to see forms (desire). Every act of the five sense-consciousnesses is not a simple stimulus-response but the convergence of these four layers — among which ignorance and habit-patterns are what practice addresses, while the intrinsic nature and dharmatā point toward the possibility of transformation.

The ocean-wave metaphor expands the dynamic:

> "The ālaya-vijñāna is like a torrent of water; it gives rise to the waves of the operational consciousnesses. Like the eye consciousness, so too with all the rest — in all the faculties, pores, and openings, the operational consciousnesses arise either simultaneously, like images appearing in a bright mirror."[^16]

And the celebrated verse:

> "The ocean of the store consciousness abides constantly; / stirred by the winds of the object-realm, / the waves of the various consciousnesses / surge and leap forth in their arising."[^17]

The ālaya-vijñāna is the ever-present ocean, objects are the winds, the five sense-consciousnesses are the surging waves. Ocean, wind, and wave — store consciousness, objects, and operational consciousnesses — form a single dynamic system, inseparable.

The same sūtra further subsumes the position of the first five consciousnesses within the framework of the five dharmas, three self-natures, and eight consciousnesses:

> "The mind's own manifestation, discriminated eightfold — namely the store consciousness, mentation, mental consciousness, and the five consciousness-bodies — their marks are unreal, being products of false discrimination."[^18]

The "marks" of the five consciousness-bodies — the object-appearances they present — are also "unreal," also subsumed under "false discrimination." Here "false discrimination" (妄想, vikalpa) does not mean subjective fantasy but the nature of dependent-arising dharmas as falsely discriminated — although the first five consciousnesses operate through direct perception, what they present are still image-components of the dependent nature (paratantra), not the suchness of the perfected nature (pariniṣpanna).

#### 2.5 The Śūraṅgama Sūtra: The Unreality of the Six Sense-Bases and the Perfect Penetration Methods

The *Śūraṅgama Sūtra*, with its distinctive method of "dismantling the false to reveal the true," deconstructs the faculty, object, and consciousness of each of the five senses, while simultaneously demonstrating their contemplative potential through the perfect penetration (圓通) methods.

In fascicle 3, the Buddha systematically reveals the unreality of each sense-base. Taking the eye-base as exemplar:

> "Because of two kinds of false dust — brightness and darkness — the seeing arises between them, absorbing these dust-images, and is named 'seeing-nature.' This seeing, apart from brightness and darkness, is ultimately without substance. ... Therefore you should know: the eye sense-base is unreal, fundamentally neither caused by conditions nor arising of its own nature."[^19]

Each of the five sense-bases has its pair of dust-objects — the eye depends on brightness/darkness, the ear on motion/stillness, the nose on open/blocked, the tongue on sweet/bitter, the body on contact/separation — and each is declared "unreal, fundamentally neither caused by conditions nor arising of its own nature."[^20]

The eighteen-element (dhātu) analysis goes further. For the eye-consciousness element:

> "If it arises from the eye, then without form or space there is nothing to discriminate ... If it arises from form, when form is absent your consciousness should be extinguished — how then could it know the nature of empty space? ... Therefore you should know: the eye-consciousness element, arising from the conditions of eye and form, is found nowhere in these three locations."[^21]

"Found nowhere in three locations" — this does not deny the existence of eye consciousness but denies any independent, self-existing substance. This aligns perfectly with the Yogācāra determination of "dependent arising" (paratantra).

In fascicle 5, Aniruddha's perfect penetration through the eye-faculty stands out:

> "When I first entered the renunciant life, I was always fond of sleeping. The Tathāgata reproved me as being of the animal class. Hearing the Buddha's reproof, I wept and reproached myself, and for seven days did not sleep, losing my eyesight. The World-Honored One showed me the Vajra Samādhi of Delightful Seeing and Luminous Illumination. Without relying on the eye, I perceived the ten directions; the essential truth shone through clearly, like observing a fruit in my palm."[^22]

Aniruddha lost his physical eyes yet gained vision surpassing physical sight — "without relying on the eye, I perceived the ten directions." This demonstrates that the goal of practice with the five sense-consciousnesses is not to enhance sensory capacity but to transcend dependence on the senses.

Avalokiteśvara's perfect penetration through the ear-faculty is the supreme method selected by Mañjuśrī:

> "At first, within hearing I entered the stream and relinquished the object. The object of entry becoming quiescent, the two marks of motion and stillness clearly ceased to arise."[^23]

And Mañjuśrī's selection verse:

> "In this land, the true essence of the teaching / resides purely in hearing. / To attain samādhi, / one truly enters through hearing. ... The eye cannot perceive beyond barriers; / mouth and nose are likewise limited. / The body knows only through contact; / the mind's thoughts are tangled and orderless. / Yet sound can be heard through walls; / far and near alike can be perceived. / The five faculties cannot match this — / this, then, is the truly penetrating one."[^24]

#### 2.6 The Avataṃsaka Sūtra: Verses on Entering Samādhi through the Six Faculties

The *Avataṃsaka Sūtra* (80-fascicle), Ten Abidings chapter, contains a remarkable set of verses describing the bodhisattva's capacity to enter and exit samādhi freely through each of the six faculties:

> "Entering right samādhi within the eye-faculty, / emerging from samādhi within the form-realm, / revealing the inconceivable nature of form — / all devas and humans cannot fathom this. / Entering right samādhi within the form-realm, / arising from samādhi at the eye, mind undisturbed, / declaring that the eye is unborn and unarisen, / its nature empty, quiescent, without function."[^25]

Entering samādhi within the eye-faculty and emerging within the form-realm — faculty and object are no longer obstacles but become the very ground of meditative absorption. "Declaring that the eye is unborn and unarisen, its nature empty, quiescent" — the faculty's self-nature is originally empty. Twelve parallel verses cover all six faculties and their objects, demonstrating the bodhisattva's perfect freedom within all sensory activity. This is the ultimate face of the five sense-consciousnesses in the Avataṃsaka worldview: not departing from the senses, yet transcending the senses.

#### 2.7 The Heart Sūtra and the Vimalakīrti Sūtra: Emptiness of the Five Faculties

The *Heart Sūtra* negates the self-nature of the six faculties, six objects, and six consciousnesses in its most compressed form:

> "No eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, or mind; no form, sound, scent, taste, tangible, or dharma; no eye-element ... up to no mental-consciousness-element."[^26]

Fazang's *Brief Commentary on the Heart Sūtra* provides a precise gloss: "This says 'no' from the perspective of mutual negation; in truth, it does not destroy form and the rest — since they are empty of self-nature, one need not wait for their destruction [to know they are empty]."[^27] Saying "no" does not annihilate the six faculties; it reveals that their self-nature is originally empty.

Nāgārjuna's *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* expands this through the concept of "inner emptiness": "The eye — the eye is empty; neither permanent nor annihilated. Why? Its nature is simply thus."[^28] "Neither permanent nor annihilated" is the middle way — falling into neither eternalism nor nihilism.

The *Vimalakīrti Sūtra* synthesizes: "The six sense-bases are without accumulation; eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind have been transcended."[^29] "Without accumulation" — the six faculties are not substantial entities built up through aggregation. To observe the true nature of one's own body is to observe the true nature of the Buddha.

### B. Treatises — Yogācāra's Systematic Argument

#### 2.8 Cheng Weishi Lun, Fascicle 7: The Essential Nature of the Five Sense-Consciousnesses

The *Cheng Weishi Lun* positions the five sense-consciousnesses with the foundational verse "Relying on the root consciousness, the five consciousnesses appear according to conditions":

> "'Root consciousness' means the ādāna consciousness, because it is the root from which all defiled and pure consciousnesses arise. 'Relying on' means that the first six operational consciousnesses take the root consciousness as their shared intimate support. 'Five consciousnesses' means the first five operational consciousnesses; because their types are similar, they are discussed together. 'Appearing according to conditions' indicates they do not arise constantly — 'conditions' means the conditions of attention, faculty, object, and so forth. The five consciousness-bodies internally depend on the root consciousness, and externally follow attention, the five faculties, objects, and other conditions; only when the full assembly of conditions converges do they appear. Hence they may arise simultaneously or not — because the external conditions may converge suddenly or gradually. Like water's waves following conditions, more or fewer."[^30]

Three critical concepts: first, "appearing according to conditions" — the five sense-consciousnesses do not arise constantly; they arise infrequently and cease frequently, following whether external conditions are complete. Second, "types are similar" — though each depends on a different physical faculty, their cognitive structure is homologous. Third, the "water's waves" simile — continuous with the *Saṃdhinirmocana*'s torrent-and-wave and the *Laṅkāvatāra*'s ocean-wave metaphors.

The treatise further contrasts the five consciousnesses with the other three groups:

> "Because the five operational consciousnesses have coarse and active cognitive modes and their required conditions are frequently incomplete, they arise infrequently and cease frequently. The sixth consciousness, though also coarse and active, has conditions that are never incomplete; only adverse conditions cause it sometimes not to arise. The seventh and eighth consciousnesses have subtle cognitive modes, and their required conditions are present at all times; hence no conditions obstruct their operation."[^31]

And on the five consciousnesses' inability to deliberate:

> "The five consciousness-bodies cannot deliberate; they only operate through the external gate, require many conditions, and hence are interrupted frequently, manifesting infrequently."[^32]

"Cannot deliberate" — the five sense-consciousnesses lack the capacity for deliberation, decision, and initiation. They can only "operate through the external gate" — directly presenting external objects without internal reflection. This is precisely why they possess only direct perception without inference or non-veridical cognition — and it is precisely their locus of purity.

#### 2.9 The Three Verses with Annotations

The *Bāshí guījǔ bǔzhù*'s detailed annotations on the three verses provide the systematic framework for the core argument.

On the first verse, "real objects, direct perception" — the annotation clarifies:

> "The first five consciousnesses, among the two kinds of transformation, engage in condition-based transformation (因緣變). Therefore they apprehend only the intrinsic characteristic of the object apart from verbal designations."[^33]

"Condition-based transformation" — the image-component (nimitta-bhāga) produced by the five consciousnesses arises through condition-based transformation (not discrimination-based transformation), hence it apprehends only "the intrinsic characteristic apart from verbal designations." This is the epistemological ground of "real objects and direct perception."

On the second verse, "nine conditions, seven, and eight" — the annotation provides the mnemonic formula "eye consciousness arises from nine conditions, ear from only eight, nose-tongue-body from seven"[^34], systematically listing all nine conditions and their assignments. This will be fully developed in §III.2.

On the third verse, the annotation identifies the fundamental epistemological limitation:

> "Even the non-outflow five consciousnesses at the Buddha-fruit stage still cannot directly engage suchness (tathatā). Because fundamental wisdom depends on the mind-faculty, it directly engages suchness; post-attainment wisdom depends on the form-faculties and involves discrimination, and therefore cannot directly engage suchness."[^35]

Even at Buddhahood, the five sense-consciousnesses cannot directly apprehend suchness, because they depend on physical form-faculties rather than the non-physical mind-faculty that supports fundamental non-discriminating wisdom.

## III. Core Argument

### 3.1 Real Objects and Direct Perception — The Essential Nature of the Five Sense-Consciousnesses

#### 3.1.1 Eight-Consciousness Cognitive Comparison

The cognitive position of the first five consciousnesses within the eight-consciousness system can be mapped across three dimensions:

| Consciousness | Three Objects | Three Modes | Three Natures |
|---|---|---|---|
| Eighth (ālaya) | Real only | Direct only | Neutral only |
| Seventh (manas) | Composite only | Non-veridical only | Obscured-neutral only (causal stage) |
| First five | **Real only** | **Direct only** | **Wholesome, unwholesome, neutral** |
| Sixth (mano) | All three | All three | All three |

The five sense-consciousnesses share with the ālaya its exclusive apprehension of real objects and use of direct perception — but they add a moral dimension (pervading three natures) that the ālaya lacks, while avoiding the cognitive distortion risk of the sixth consciousness (no composite objects, no shadow-only objects, no inference, no non-veridical cognition).

"Real object" (svabhāva-viṣaya) is defined: "Without verbal designations and without deliberative mentation — this is called a real object."[^36] "Without verbal designations" — not accompanied by linguistic concepts; "without deliberative mentation" — not processed through comparison or inference. When the five sense-consciousnesses see red, they present the intrinsic characteristic of redness itself — without the overlay of "what is this?" or "is it good?"

"Direct perception" (pratyakṣa): "When mind and mental factors apprehend objects, free from obstruction and veiling, clearly and distinctly, grasping the object's own nature — this is called direct perception."[^33] Direct perception has four embodiments: the five sense-consciousnesses, the simultaneous mental consciousness, the self-witnessing component of all mental events, and all minds in meditative concentration. The five consciousnesses' direct perception is the most fundamental kind: directly presenting the intrinsic characteristic of the object, "free from verbal categories and erroneous discrimination."

"Pervading three natures" — unlike the ālaya, which is fixed as neutral, the five sense-consciousnesses follow the guidance of the accompanying consciousness through wholesome, unwholesome, and neutral. This "pervading" means: the five consciousnesses have no fixed moral orientation; their moral quality depends on the consciousness and mental factors accompanying them. The plasticity of practice resides precisely here.

#### 3.1.2 The Practice Significance of Direct Perception

The "direct perception" of the five sense-consciousnesses is not merely an epistemological concept but the starting point of practice. Every first moment of seeing form or hearing sound — that instant before verbal labels intervene, before preference-judgments arise — is direct perception. The *Saṃyukta Āgama* Sūtra 218 describes both the path of suffering-accumulation and the path of suffering-cessation as beginning at precisely this point:

> "Conditioned by the eye and form, eye consciousness arises; the conjunction of these three produces contact; conditioned by contact, feeling; conditioned by feeling, craving; conditioned by craving, clinging ... thus arises the entire mass of suffering."[^37]

> "Conditioned by the eye and form, eye consciousness arises; the conjunction of these three produces contact; when contact ceases, feeling ceases; when feeling ceases, craving ceases ... thus the entire mass of suffering ceases."[^37]

Both suffering-accumulation and suffering-cessation branch at the contact → feeling → craving link. The contact of the five sense-consciousnesses is morally neutral — the conjunction of three itself is neither wholesome nor unwholesome. The problem lies in the automatic chaining of contact → feeling → craving, a sequence so rapid that we ordinarily take "seeing something beautiful and liking it" as a single, undifferentiated event. The practice of sense-faculty restraint inserts awareness into this automatic chain — allowing the direct perception of the five consciousnesses to linger a moment longer, allowing the conceptual discrimination of the sixth consciousness to arrive a moment later.

A daily-life illustration: you are walking down a street and catch the scent of freshly baked bread. The nose consciousness presents its direct perception: fragrance. Just that — a pure olfactory characteristic. But almost in the same instant, the sixth consciousness intervenes: "smells good" → "I want some" → "where's the bakery?" → your feet are already turning. The entire process is so fast that you take "smelling the aroma and wanting bread" as one event. Practice means decomposing this single event into two: "nose consciousness apprehends fragrance" is one event; "mental consciousness generates craving" is another. Between them lies a gap — extremely brief, but real.

### 3.2 Nine Conditions, Seven and Eight — The Precise Architecture of Conditioned Arising

Another defining characteristic of the first five consciousnesses is that they require the most conditions of any consciousness — eye consciousness requires nine, ear eight, and nose-tongue-body each require seven. By contrast, mental consciousness requires only five, manas three, and the ālaya four.

The complete list of nine conditions:

| Condition | Nature | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| Space (ākāśa) | Spatial interval | A gap is needed between faculty and object — the eye cannot see by pressing against the object |
| Illumination (āloka) | Light | Sight is impossible in darkness — sunlight, moonlight, or lamplight is required |
| Faculty (indriya) | Sense support | The pure form faculty (prasāda-rūpa) upon which each consciousness depends |
| Object (viṣaya) | Cognitive target | Form, sound, scent, taste, tangible — each consciousness's cognitive object |
| Attention (manaskāra) | Alerting function | The universal mental factor of attention, which alerts the mind and directs it toward the object |
| Discriminating support | Sixth consciousness | The five consciousnesses take mental consciousness as their discriminating support |
| Defiled/pure support | Seventh (manas) | Manas serves as the pivot of defilement and purity |
| Root support | Eighth (ālaya) | The ālaya-vijñāna as the fundamental support of all consciousnesses |
| Seed condition | Proximate seed | Each consciousness's own generative seed — the direct cause (hetu-pratyaya) |

The mnemonic: "Eye consciousness arises from nine conditions; ear from only eight (excluding illumination); nose, tongue, and body from seven (excluding space and illumination). The latter three [sixth, seventh, eighth] from five, three, and four respectively. Adding the immediately antecedent condition, add one to each."[^34]

Eye consciousness requires all nine because the eye faculty apprehends objects at a distance, requiring spatial interval and light. Ear consciousness requires eight — no illumination needed (one hears in darkness) but space is still required (sound reaches the ear across distance). Nose, tongue, and body require seven — neither space nor illumination needed, since these faculties must make contact with their objects.

This differential carries practical significance. The more conditions required, the more easily a consciousness is interrupted — "when conditions are many, interruption is frequent; when few, continuity prevails."[^34] The five sense-consciousnesses require the most conditions and are therefore the most easily interrupted: "they arise infrequently and cease frequently."[^31] This means the five sense-consciousnesses have natural gaps — and in meditation, one exploits precisely these gaps to establish calm abiding. Closing the eyes removes a condition for eye consciousness; sitting in silence reduces the conditions for ear consciousness; holding the body still reduces the contacts that trigger body consciousness. The practice of restraining the six faculties is, at its structural core, a systematic reduction of the conditions required for the five consciousnesses' arising, creating space for the mind to settle.

### 3.3 Three by Contact, Two at Distance — Two Modes of Object-Apprehension

The five sense-consciousnesses apprehend objects in two ways: at a distance and through contact. "Three by contact, two at distance — thus they observe the dusty world"[^3] — the eye and ear consciousnesses apprehend at a distance, while nose, tongue, and body consciousnesses apprehend through contact.

"At a distance" means the faculty and object are spatially separated — the eye can see a distant mountain; the ear can hear sound through a wall. "Through contact" means the faculty and object must make physical contact — the nose must be near enough to smell; the tongue must taste; the body must touch.

The *Bāshí guījǔ bǔzhù* addresses a critical objection:

> "Some say: since the eye and ear consciousnesses apprehend objects at a distance, their objects must be outside the mind — how then can this be 'consciousness-only'? ... But what the verse calls 'distance' refers to the faculty and its object. The eighth consciousness's self-witnessing component transforms into the seeing and image components; seeing is mind and mental factors, image is the faculty-body and vessel-world. When we say the eye and ear consciousnesses apprehend objects distant from their faculties, they have never departed from the transforming self-witnessing component. 'Distance and contact describe the faculty illuminating the object; consciousness-only describes the mind apprehending the object.'"[^38]

"Distance" is not distance from mind (from consciousness-only) but distance from the physical faculty. The spatial separation between faculty and object is a phenomenon-level description within the image-component; the entire seeing-and-image structure never departs from the transforming self-witnessing component. The two levels — phenomenal distance/contact and consciousness-only — operate at different registers and do not contradict each other.

Mañjuśrī's selection verse in the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* evaluates the perfect penetration methods precisely through this distance/contact distinction:

> "The eye cannot perceive beyond barriers; mouth and nose are likewise limited. The body knows only through contact; the mind's thoughts are tangled and orderless. Yet sound can be heard through walls; far and near alike can be perceived. The five faculties cannot match this — this, then, is the truly penetrating one."[^24]

The ear faculty is judged supreme among the five precisely because its distance-apprehension is the most unobstructed — far and near, with or without barriers.

### 3.4 The Foolish Find It Hard to Distinguish Consciousness from Faculty

"The foolish find it hard to distinguish consciousness from faculty"[^3] — the *Bāshí guījǔ bǔzhù* identifies the "foolish" as Hīnayāna practitioners who "do not know that faculty and consciousness each have their own seeds and manifest activity, and assume that faculty and consciousness generate each other."[^39]

The distinction is fundamental:

> "The faculty is form-dharma — the image-component of the eighth consciousness. Consciousness is mind-dharma — the seeing-component of the eighth consciousness. These are the difference between form and mind."[^39]

Faculty is form (the ālaya's image-component); consciousness is mind (the ālaya's seeing-component) — heterogeneous in nature. Yet the faculty is not ordinary insentient matter — as the ālaya's "intimate image-component," it possesses the quality of "appropriation" (upādāna): the eighth consciousness holds it as its own body, maintains it without disintegration, takes it as an object, and enables it to produce sensation.[^39]

> "The faculty illuminates objects; consciousness apprehends objects. Their functions differ."[^39]

The faculty provides the sensory condition (like a lamp illuminating a room); consciousness performs the cognitive act (like the eye that sees what the lamp reveals). The Buddha's question to Ānanda in the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* — "Can the door see?" — makes this distinction through analogy: "The mind sees through the faculty, just as a person sees things by means of a lamp."[^39]

The annotation on "pure form faculty" (prasāda-rūpa):

> "The five consciousnesses are named 'pure form' in the ultimate sense — their substance is not defiled dharma, being exclusively of the white-pure neutral nature."[^40]

"Pure form faculty" does not refer to the visible gross organ (the "floating dust" faculty) but to the ultimate-sense faculty — its nature is white-pure neutral form. The five consciousnesses are named after their respective faculties, possessing five relationships: dependence, generation, belonging, assistance.[^40]

### 3.5 Thirty-Four Mental Factors — Why These Associate and Those Do Not

The five sense-consciousnesses associate with thirty-four mental factors and do not associate with the remaining seventeen. This is not an arbitrary assignment but follows rigorous doctrinal logic.

The thirty-four associated factors:

| Category | Count | Specific Factors |
|---|---|---|
| Universals | 5 | Attention, contact, feeling, perception, volition |
| Object-specifics | 5 | Desire, resolve, mindfulness, concentration, wisdom |
| Wholesome | 11 | Faith, conscience, shame, non-greed, non-hatred, non-delusion, diligence, pliancy, non-negligence, equanimity, non-harmfulness |
| Root afflictions | 3 | Greed, hatred, delusion |
| Medium subsidiaries | 2 | Shamelessness, brazenness |
| Major subsidiaries | 8 | Restlessness, torpor, faithlessness, laziness, negligence, lost mindfulness, distraction, incorrect knowing |

The verse summarizes: "Universals, object-specifics, eleven wholesome, / two medium, eight major, plus greed, hatred, and delusion."[^2] Among the six categories of mental factors, "only the indeterminates are absent."

Why are the remaining seventeen excluded? Each exclusion has a precise rationale.

**Root afflictions: pride, doubt, and five views excluded.** The *Cheng Weishi Lun*, fascicle 6, provides the definitive statement:

> "Which consciousnesses do these ten afflictions associate with? The store consciousness has none; manas has four; mental consciousness has all ten; the five consciousnesses have only three — namely greed, hatred, and delusion. Because they lack discrimination. Pride and such arise through comparative assessment."[^41]

"Because they lack discrimination" — the five sense-consciousnesses lack conceptual discrimination. Pride requires comparative assessment ("I am superior"); doubt requires deliberative examination ("is it or isn't it?"); the five views require erroneous reasoning and attachment — all of these require conceptual discrimination that the five consciousnesses cannot perform.

Greed, hatred, and delusion, by contrast, can arise spontaneously (俱生) without deliberation: seeing an attractive form directly triggers greed; touching something painful directly triggers aversion; being confused about an object directly manifests as delusion.

**All ten minor subsidiary afflictions excluded.** The *Cheng Weishi Lun* rules:

> "The ten minor [subsidiary afflictions] are coarse and fierce; they are absent in the five consciousnesses. The medium and major [subsidiary afflictions] are shared; they can be present in the five consciousnesses."[^42]

Why are the ten minor subsidiaries (anger, resentment, concealment, affliction, jealousy, miserliness, deceit, flattery, harmfulness, arrogance) "absent in the five consciousnesses"? Because they "arise individually" with "coarse and fierce cognitive modes" — each is a specific, intense psychological reaction requiring the sixth consciousness's discriminative judgment. One does not "feel jealous with eye consciousness" — jealousy requires knowing what the other person has and comparing it to what one lacks, which is entirely the work of mental consciousness.

**All four indeterminate factors excluded.** The *Cheng Weishi Lun*, fascicle 7:

Regret and sleep: "Regret and sleep associate only with the sixth consciousness, because they are not among the five consciousnesses' functions."[^43]

Investigation (vitarka) and analysis (vicāra): Dharmapāla's authoritative position determines that "the five consciousnesses definitely lack investigation and analysis." Against the objection that the *Yogācāra* texts say "the five consciousnesses have investigation and analysis," Dharmapāla responds:

> "When it is said that the five consciousnesses 'have' investigation and analysis, this means they mostly arise because of these [i.e., investigation and analysis trigger the arising of the five consciousnesses], not that these factors associate with them."[^44]

Investigation and analysis "take name-bodies and their meanings as their objects; the five consciousness-bodies do not take name-bodies and their meanings as objects"[^44] — investigation and analysis deal with verbal-conceptual content, which the five sense-consciousnesses do not apprehend.

Regarding pliancy among the eleven wholesome factors, the *Cheng Weishi Lun* records two positions: one holds that the five consciousnesses have only ten wholesome factors (being inherently restless, they lack pliancy); the other holds that they also have pliancy (when wholesome consciousness arises through meditative power, pliancy can be present; the wisdom of accomplished activities must include pliancy).[^45] The second view is more comprehensive.

### 3.6 Realm-and-Ground Distribution — Eye, Ear, and Body Reside in Two Grounds

"Eye, ear, and body — these three reside in two grounds"[^2] — the five sense-consciousnesses are distributed unevenly across the three realms and nine grounds.

The *Bāshí guījǔ bǔzhù* explains:

> "The desire realm with its five destinies [constitutes the] miscellaneous-dwelling ground; the form realm's first dhyāna [constitutes the] ground of joy born from separation. Above the first dhyāna, since there is no investigation or analysis, the consciousnesses do not arise. ... The verse implicitly omits nose and tongue consciousness — these exist in only one realm and one ground: the desire realm alone. Above it, there is no coarse food; coarse food has scent, taste, and tangible as its substance. Where there is no coarse food, these two consciousnesses do not arise."[^46]

Eye, ear, and body consciousness exist in two grounds: the desire realm's miscellaneous-dwelling ground and the form realm's first dhyāna (ground of joy born from separation). Above the first dhyāna, without investigation or analysis (which the five sense-consciousnesses require as a discriminating support through the sixth consciousness), the sense-consciousnesses do not manifest.

Nose and tongue consciousness exist in only the desire realm. The form realm and above sustain beings through meditative joy and dharma-bliss, not coarse food composed of scent, taste, and tangible — without this food, nose and tongue consciousness lack the conditions for arising.

This distribution reveals a practice insight: as meditative absorption deepens, the activity range of the five sense-consciousnesses progressively contracts. In the desire realm, all five are fully active; in the first dhyāna, only eye, ear, and body remain; from the second dhyāna upward, none of the five manifest. Meditation itself is a systematic narrowing of the five consciousnesses' operating conditions — not through suppression but because the coarse conditions required for their arising are naturally absent in deeper states.

### 3.7 Transformed Images and Post-Attainment Wisdom — The Epistemological Limit

The first two phrases of the third verse expose the fundamental epistemological limitation of the five sense-consciousnesses — even at the Buddha-fruit stage, they cannot directly apprehend suchness (tathatā).

> "Apprehending emptiness through transformed images — only post-attainment [wisdom]" — the five consciousnesses can contemplate emptiness only by "transforming and bearing an image-component," and this belongs to post-attainment wisdom, not fundamental wisdom.[^35]

> "Even at the fruit stage, they still cannot directly engage the real" — even the non-outflow five consciousnesses at the stage of Buddhahood still cannot directly apprehend suchness.[^35]

Why this limitation? First, fundamental non-discriminating wisdom "depends on the mind-faculty" — the non-physical faculty of the sixth consciousness — and thereby directly apprehends the inexpressible dharma-nature of suchness. Post-attainment wisdom "depends on form-faculties and involves discrimination"; the five sense-consciousnesses, depending on physical form-faculties, cannot directly access what transcends physicality.

Second, the five consciousnesses' mode of cognition is "directly taking the intrinsic characteristic of the object" — they can only cognize the concrete appearance of things, not the dharma-nature of things. Suchness is not something that can be seen, heard, smelled, tasted, or touched.

This limitation is not a deficiency. It means: the direct realization of suchness must come through the sixth consciousness's non-discriminating wisdom, not through sensory refinement. One cannot awaken by seeing more acutely or hearing more finely — one must transform at the level of consciousness. Practice with the five sense-consciousnesses (faculty restraint, perfect penetration methods) serves as a critical support and entry point, but ultimate transformation occurs at the level of consciousness.

### 3.8 Perfect Luminosity First Arising — The Wisdom of Accomplished Activities and Three Classes of Emanation-Bodies

The final two phrases of the third verse unfold the five consciousnesses' ultimate transformation:

> "When perfect luminosity first arises, they become non-outflow; / three classes of emanation-bodies bring the wheel of suffering to rest."[^4]

"Perfect luminosity first arising" — when the great mirror wisdom (corresponding to the eighth consciousness's transformation) suddenly manifests, this is called "first arising." The *Bāshí guījǔ bǔzhù* traces the causal chain:

> "At the Buddha-stage, after the eighth consciousness transforms into the non-outflow, pure white consciousness, its corresponding mental factors become the great mirror wisdom, suddenly appearing — hence 'first arising.' Then the five consciousnesses immediately become non-outflow — hence 'become non-outflow.'"[^47]

The causal sequence: eighth consciousness transforms → great mirror wisdom manifests → five faculties become non-outflow (since they are the eighth consciousness's image-component) → five consciousnesses become non-outflow → wisdom of accomplished activities manifests. Because the five faculties are the ālaya's image-component, when the transforming root-consciousness becomes non-outflow, the faculties it projects follow suit; when the faculties become non-outflow, the consciousnesses they support follow suit — link by link.

The wisdom of accomplished activities (kṛtyānuṣṭhāna-jñāna), as defined by the *Cheng Weishi Lun*:

> "The wisdom of accomplished activities, corresponding mental factor assemblage: this assemblage, desiring to benefit and bring joy to all sentient beings, universally throughout the ten directions manifests various transformation-activities of the three kinds of karma, accomplishing what should be accomplished through the power of original vows."[^48]

This wisdom is not a truth-cognizing wisdom (that belongs to the contemplation wisdom and great mirror wisdom) but a wisdom of manifesting transformation-activities through the three karmas to benefit beings — "accomplishing what should be accomplished through the power of original vows." The five consciousnesses transform from passive sensory reception at the ordinary stage to active beneficent manifestation at the Buddha stage — from couriers to fully empowered agents of liberation.

"Three classes of emanation-bodies" are three modes of the transformation-body (nirmāṇa-kāya): the great transformation-body (for bodhisattvas in the stages of intensified practice), the lesser sixteen-foot transformation-body (for bodhisattvas in the resource-accumulation stages and for the two vehicles), and the body of class-specific emanation (universally benefiting the three vehicles and all six destinies).[^47]

When does the wisdom of accomplished activities first arise? The *Cheng Weishi Lun* records two views; Dharmapāla's authoritative position holds: "Only upon attaining Buddhahood can it first arise — because during the ten grounds, the five sense-faculties transformed by the maturation consciousness are still outflow, and outflow faculties cannot generate non-outflow consciousness."[^49]

This connects to the five consciousnesses' "instantaneous complete transformation at the vajra-like samādhi." The *Cheng Weishi Lun* states:

> "When the vajra-like samādhi manifests, all original coarse obstructions are permanently severed, and the complete and perfect transformation of the Buddha-fruit is instantly realized."[^50]

The five consciousnesses' transformation is not gradual (unlike the sixth consciousness, which transforms stage by stage from the Joyful Ground to the Far-Reaching Ground). The five consciousnesses leap in a single instant from outflow to non-outflow at the vajra-like samādhi — the most dramatic transformation in the entire Yogācāra path.

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Dialogue

### 4.1 Primary Sensory Cortex and Association Cortex — The Division of Labor between the Five Consciousnesses and Consciousness ⚠️

Cognitive neuroscience research on perceptual systems provides a framework for structural analogy with the "five consciousnesses → sixth consciousness" division of labor. ⚠️ The following is a heuristic analogy, not a reductive equivalence. Neuroscience describes signal-processing mechanisms at the physical level; Yogācāra analyzes the structure of cognition at the epistemological level. The two operate at fundamentally different explanatory registers.

The primary visual cortex (V1) receives raw visual signals from the retina, presenting basic features such as edges, contrast, and orientation — structurally analogous to eye consciousness's direct apprehension of the intrinsic characteristic of form-objects. The association cortex integrates, classifies, and names these raw signals — structurally analogous to the sixth consciousness's conceptual discrimination, which processes the intrinsic characteristic into conceptualized cognition.

In this analogy, bottom-up processing — from raw sensory signals to higher-order cognition — structurally corresponds to the five consciousnesses' direct presentation being passed to the sixth consciousness for discrimination. But neuroscience has simultaneously discovered extensive top-down processing — higher-order cognition retroactively shaping sensory perception. What we "see" is substantially influenced by what we "expect to see."

This has structural resonance with the *Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra*'s four conditions for eye consciousness: "not being aware of one's own mind's manifestation and grasping externally; beginningless habitual tendencies of false attachment to form"[^16] — ignorance and habitual tendencies (top-down) shape what eye consciousness presents. The five consciousnesses' "direct perception" does not operate in a vacuum; it is always conditioned by ignorance and habit-patterns. This is why an ordinary person's direct perception and a sage's direct perception, though structurally identical, differ in quality.

### 4.2 Mindful Sensory Exercises — Practice Experiments in Remaining at Direct Perception ⚠️

Modern mindfulness (sati) training exercises such as "mindful eating" and "mindful walking" structurally resonate with the principle of practicing at the level of the five consciousnesses' direct perception. Mindful eating asks the practitioner to chew extremely slowly, noticing each taste-consciousness characteristic — sweet, salty, sour, bitter — without rushing to judge "delicious" or "unpleasant." The essence of this practice is extending the duration of tongue-consciousness's direct perception and delaying the intervention of the sixth consciousness's conceptual discrimination.

The well-known "raisin exercise" in Jon Kabat-Zinn's Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR) directs participants to attend with extreme focus to a single raisin's color (eye consciousness), texture (body consciousness), scent (nose consciousness), and taste (tongue consciousness) — each step remaining at direct perception without rushing to concatenate them into the conceptual cognition "I am eating a raisin."

The Buddha's Āgama teaching on sense-faculty restraint is more thoroughgoing than modern mindfulness — it aims not merely to extend the pause in direct perception but to sever, at the root, the causal chain from sign-grasping through craving to karma-creation. But the structural insight is shared: the entry point of suffering lies at the moment of sensory contact, and the space for practice lies in the gap between direct perception and conceptual discrimination.

## V. Middle-Way Corrections

### ❌ Correction 1: The Five Sense-Consciousnesses Are Purely Passive Receptors

A common misconception pictures the five sense-consciousnesses as purely passive sensors — like cameras mechanically recording the external world. But the Yogācāra five sense-consciousnesses are not passive: first, they arise from the ālaya-vijñāna's seeds through condition-based transformation, not through external "stimulation"; second, they are conditioned by ignorance and habitual tendencies (the *Laṅkāvatāra*'s four conditions), so an ordinary person's eye consciousness and a sage's eye consciousness, seeing the same flower, do not present the same thing; third, they pervade three moral natures — they are morally qualified cognition, not mechanical signal transduction.

### ❌ Correction 2: Practice Means Suppressing the Five Sense-Consciousnesses

There is a tendency to understand practice as "shutting down the senses" — not seeing, not hearing, not smelling, not tasting, not touching. The Turtle Simile's "withdrawing the six" is misread as "permanently closing the six faculties." But the Buddha taught "skillfully guarding the body, protecting the sense-gates"[^12] — skillful protection, not rigid closure. The *Śūraṅgama Sūtra*'s perfect penetration methods make this clear: Aniruddha lost his physical eyes yet "without relying on the eye, perceived the ten directions"[^22] — transcending the senses, not destroying them. The *Avataṃsaka*'s verses on entering samādhi through the sense-faculties: "entering right samādhi within the eye-faculty, emerging from samādhi within the form-realm"[^25] — faculty and object are not obstacles but grounds for meditative freedom. Practice transforms the five consciousnesses' mode of object-apprehension; it does not abolish their function.

### ❌ Correction 3: Direct Perception by the Five Consciousnesses Equals "Correct Cognition"

Direct perception (pratyakṣa) does not equal "true" — it means only "direct." An ordinary person's five sense-consciousnesses are indeed direct perception, but they are conditioned by ignorance and habitual tendencies. What they present is still the image-component of dependent arising, not the suchness of the perfected nature. More critically, the five consciousnesses' direct perception is almost instantly overwritten by the sixth consciousness's discrimination — what you believe to be "pure seeing" already has consciousness's overlay. Genuinely pure direct perception is realized only in a sage's meditative concentration. Do not equate the ordinary person's "I directly saw it" with "I know it as it truly is."

### ❌ Correction 4: The Five Sense-Consciousnesses Can Practice and Transform Independently

The five sense-consciousnesses never operate independently — they must always co-arise with the five-concomitant consciousness (discriminating support), depend on manas (defiled/pure support), and rest on the ālaya (root support). Therefore, there is no such thing as "practicing the five sense-consciousnesses alone." Sense-faculty restraint appears to operate at the five-consciousness level, but it is the sixth consciousness making the decision: "do not grasp the sign" is a decision made by consciousness; the five sense-consciousnesses themselves have no capacity to "decide not to grasp." The five consciousnesses' transformation is also not independently achieved — it is the chain reaction following the eighth consciousness's transformation into the non-outflow white-pure consciousness. The five consciousnesses do not "practice their way" to the wisdom of accomplished activities; they are carried there by the entire system's instantaneous reversal at the vajra-like samādhi.

### ❌ Correction 5: Limits of the Cross-Disciplinary Analogy

The V1 → association cortex signal-processing model cannot be straightforwardly equated with the five consciousnesses → sixth consciousness structure. Three reasons: first, Yogācāra "consciousness" is not physical neural activity but an epistemological category of mind-dharma; second, the five consciousnesses' seed → manifest-activity → perfumation cycle has no counterpart in neuroscience; third, the five consciousnesses' ultimate transformation (the wisdom of accomplished activities) exceeds what can be described in terms of the physical brain. The analogy has heuristic value only at the level of "structural division of labor"; extending it beyond that register constitutes improper overreach.

## VI. Conclusion

The first five consciousnesses are the mode of cognition closest to pure direct perception within the eight-consciousness system — real objects, direct perception, without verbal-conceptual overlay, without conceptual discrimination. They are the direct windows onto the sensory world, presenting the intrinsic characteristics of form, sound, scent, taste, and tangible with the shortest cognitive distance. But this directness cuts both ways: through the same window flows both "the conjunction of three produces contact, contact conditions feeling, feeling conditions craving" — the entry to suffering-accumulation — and "when the eye sees form, do not grasp at the sign" — the entry to practice.

The Buddha established a complete practice method for the five sense-consciousnesses in the Āgamas — sense-faculty restraint, maintaining awareness at the very moment of sensory contact. The *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra* used the torrent-and-wave and mirror metaphors to elucidate the conditioned-arising structure by which the five consciousnesses arise in dependence on the ālaya. The *Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra* deepened the dynamic analysis through four conditions and the ocean-wave metaphor. The *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* dismantled the substantialist view of the senses through its "six sense-bases are unreal" analysis and demonstrated practice pathways through the perfect penetration methods. The *Avataṃsaka Sūtra* presented the bodhisattva's freedom of entering and exiting samādhi through the sense-faculties. The *Heart Sūtra*, with "no eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, or mind," negated the self-nature of the five faculties; the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* established the middle-way emptiness of "neither permanent nor annihilated — its nature is simply thus."

The *Cheng Weishi Lun* and the *Verses on Eight Consciousnesses* provided the precise Yogācāra analysis: the nine-condition architecture of conditioned arising, the contact-versus-distance modes of object-apprehension, the epistemological distinction between faculty and consciousness, the complete argument for thirty-four associated mental factors, and the realm-and-ground boundaries of existence. These analyses serve not for rote memorization of terminology but for the precise identification of the operational point of practice: the space between the five consciousnesses' direct presentation and the sixth consciousness's conceptual discrimination.

The five consciousnesses' ultimate destination is the wisdom of accomplished activities — from passive sensory reception to active beneficent emanation. "Three classes of emanation-bodies bring the wheel of suffering to rest" — with transformation-bodies pervading the ten directions and the miraculous freedom of the five faculties, all suffering's wheel is brought to rest. But this transformation is not achieved by the five consciousnesses on their own; it is the result of the entire eight-consciousness system's instantaneous reversal at the vajra-like samādhi — the eighth consciousness transforms, the five faculties transform, the five consciousnesses transform — in a single thought-moment, all outflows are relinquished and all non-outflow [qualities] suddenly manifest.

S3-P05 unfolded the translator — how the sixth consciousness receives signals from the five, discriminates, judges, and initiates bodily and verbal karma. This paper unfolded the couriers — how the five consciousnesses gather signals from the external world, through what modes they present them, and under what conditions they are limited. The story of the translator and the couriers concludes here. Next, we will enter the psychological tools that both the five consciousnesses and the sixth consciousness jointly employ — the universal mental factors and the object-specific mental factors (S3-P07) — opening the "psychological engine" section of the Yogācāra toolkit.
