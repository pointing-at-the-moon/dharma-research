# Four Categories and Seven Elements: All Dharmas Are Originally Tathāgatagarbha

**The Return-to-Nature Structure of the Four Categories and Seven Elements from the Perspectives of the Empty and Non-Empty Tathāgatagarbha**

## Abstract

The Śūraṅgama Sūtra (T0945), from the end of fascicle two through fascicle three, exhaustively classifies all dharmas into four categories — the five aggregates (*pañca-skandha*), six entrances (*ṣaḍ-āyatana*), twelve sense-bases (*dvādaśa-āyatana*), and eighteen realms (*aṣṭādaśa-dhātu*) — and seven elements (*sapta-mahābhūta*: earth, water, fire, wind, space, seeing, consciousness). Within each classification, the Buddha demonstrates that these dharmas are "originally neither caused by conditions nor arising from a self-nature" — their nature is "the marvelous true suchness of the tathāgatagarbha" (如來藏妙真如性, *tathāgatagarbha-māhā-tattva-svabhāva*). This paper argues that this teaching does not mean "the aggregates are empty, therefore they are tathāgatagarbha," but rather "the aggregates, sense-bases, realms, and elements have always been tathāgatagarbha in their very nature." Drawing on Jiāoguāng Zhēnjiàn's (交光真鑑) exegetical framework in X0275, the paper demonstrates a structural progression: the four categories belong to the empty tathāgatagarbha (*śūnya-tathāgatagarbha*) — subsuming phenomena into principle — while the seven elements belong to the non-empty tathāgatagarbha (*aśūnya-tathāgatagarbha*) — fully manifesting the true. This represents an upgrade from "each dharma is true in itself" to "each dharma pervades all."

**Keywords:** Four categories and seven elements, tathāgatagarbha, empty tathāgatagarbha, non-empty tathāgatagarbha, originally pure by nature, neither caused by conditions nor arising from self-nature

## 一 Introduction: Who Is the One Who Just Realized You Were Seeing?

The previous paper (S6-P03) opened the world of the ten demonstrations of seeing with a single experience: close your eyes — what do you see? You answer "darkness" or "nothing" — but you forgot that you were *seeing*. The ten demonstrations spent nine rounds extracting the seeing-nature (*jiàn-xìng* 見性) from the objects of sight — that capacity to see which does not arise and perish with light and dark.

But the story did not end there.

The moment you finally realize "I am seeing" — notice this — **who is the one who just realized that you were seeing?**

You might say: "That's my mind." Very well — where is your mind? P02 has already shown you: the seven locations have been searched, and the mind is not in any of them. You might say: "That's some deeper awareness." Very well — where did that deeper awareness come from? Did your eyes produce it? Did light bring it? Was it already there in empty space?

From the end of fascicle two through fascicle three, the Śūraṅgama pushes this question to its limit. The Buddha no longer discusses only the seeing-nature. He lays out *all dharmas* — your body, your feelings, your thoughts, your eyes, the colors you see, your consciousness, even earth, water, fire, wind, and space — and within each one points out the same thing:

> These dharmas are originally neither caused by conditions nor arising from a self-nature — they are the marvelous true suchness of the tathāgatagarbha.[^1]

If this sounds like the Heart Sūtra's "illuminated the emptiness of all five aggregates" (*zhào jiàn wǔ yùn jiē kōng* 照見五蘊皆空), your intuition is correct — but the direction differs. The Heart Sūtra offers the Prajñā reading: emptying your attachment to the aggregates. The Śūraṅgama offers the tathāgatagarbha reading: the nature of the aggregates has *always been* tathāgatagarbha. Not "empty first, then tathāgatagarbha" — but "originally so."

This is the teaching of the four categories and seven elements. The Buddha employs four classification systems plus seven fundamental elements to exhaustively enumerate all dharmas — not for the sake of classification, but to demonstrate within each category: **nowhere can you find an independently existing "thing," yet nowhere are you apart from the marvelous true suchness of the tathāgatagarbha.**

P03 led you to recognize the seeing-nature. P04 asks you to recognize: the seeing-nature was only the entrance — all dharmas are the same.

## 二 Scriptural Foundations

### A. Sūtra Sources

#### 2.1 The Five Aggregates as Originally Tathāgatagarbha (T0945, Fascicle 2)

The Buddha uses five vividly concrete analogies to expose, one by one, the illusory character and tathāgatagarbha nature of each aggregate.

**Form aggregate — "solid" delusion** (*jiāngù wàngxiǎng* 堅固妄想). The analogy is staring until the eyes produce fatigue-illusions:

> "Ānanda! Suppose a person with clear eyes gazes at a bright, clear sky — nothing but pure, open clarity, utterly void of anything. If for no reason he does not blink but stares fixedly until fatigue arises, he then sees in that empty sky wild flowers dancing… Therefore you should know that the form aggregate is illusory; it is originally neither caused by conditions nor arising from a self-nature."[^2]

You stare at a clear sky long enough and you begin to see floating patterns. Those patterns are not in the sky, nor do they come from your eyes. The form aggregate works the same way: what you take to be a solid material world is a "fatigue-of-staring" (*dèng yǐ fā láo* 瞪以發勞) — an appearance on the originally pure tathāgatagarbha produced by fixation.

**Sensation aggregate — "insubstantial luminous" delusion** (*xūmíng wàngxiǎng* 虛明妄想). The analogy is rubbing the palms together in empty air:

> "Ānanda! Suppose a person whose limbs are at ease and body well adjusted, suddenly as if forgetting he is alive, with a nature free from pleasure or pain. For no reason he rubs his two palms together in empty space, and between his two hands there falsely arise sensations of roughness, smoothness, cold, and heat… Therefore you should know that the sensation aggregate is illusory; it is originally neither caused by conditions nor arising from a self-nature."[^3]

**Perception aggregate — "interpenetrating" delusion** (*róngtōng wàngxiǎng* 融通妄想). The analogy is hearing about sour plums:

> "Ānanda! Suppose someone discusses sour plums and saliva flows in his mouth; suppose someone thinks of stepping to the edge of a precipice and the soles of his feet tingle with apprehension… Therefore you should know that the perception aggregate is illusory; it is originally neither caused by conditions nor arising from a self-nature."[^4]

**Formation aggregate — "hidden" delusion** (*yōuyǐn wàngxiǎng* 幽隱妄想). The analogy is a rushing torrent:

> "Ānanda! As when a violent current's waves follow one upon another, the former not crossing over to the latter's place. The formation aggregate should be known to be likewise… Therefore you should know that the formation aggregate is illusory; it is originally neither caused by conditions nor arising from a self-nature."[^5]

**Consciousness aggregate — "inverted" delusion** (*diāndǎo wàngxiǎng* 顛倒妄想). The analogy is a bottle carried full of "space":

> "Ānanda! Suppose a person takes a *piṅgala* vase, stops both its openings, fills it with 'space,' and carries it a thousand miles to deliver to another land… Therefore you should know that the consciousness aggregate is illusory; it is originally neither caused by conditions nor arising from a self-nature."[^6]

Five analogies, five modes of delusion, but one identical conclusion: **originally neither caused by conditions nor arising from a self-nature** (*běn fēi yīnyuán, fēi zìrán xìng* 本非因緣、非自然性). This does not mean the five aggregates do not exist — it means their mode of existence is neither externally manufactured (not by conditions) nor independently self-generated (not from a self-nature). What are they? Manifestations upon the tathāgatagarbha's marvelous true suchness.

#### 2.2 The Six Entrances as Originally Tathāgatagarbha (T0945, Fascicle 3)

The second layer of the four categories is the six entrances (the six sense-faculties). Taking the eye-entrance as example:

> "That very eye-essence that stares until fatigue arises — the eye together with its fatigue are equally a fatigued appearance of bodhi's staring… This seeing, apart from the two dusts of light and dark, is ultimately without substance… Therefore you should know that the eye-entrance is illusory; it is originally neither caused by conditions nor arising from a self-nature."[^7]

Note the logical progression. The five aggregates address "what you think you experience is not what you think it is." The six entrances go one step further — **the very tools you use to experience are themselves fatigue-appearances upon the tathāgatagarbha.**

Particularly significant is the sixth entrance — the mind-entrance (*yì rù* 意入):

> "Suppose a person, when weary, sleeps; when sleep matures, he wakes; surveying sense-data, he remembers; losing memory, he forgets. This cycle of arising, abiding, changing, and ceasing… is called the 'knowing faculty of mind.' … This knowing nature, apart from the two dusts of waking-sleeping and arising-ceasing, is ultimately without substance… Therefore you should know that the mind-entrance is illusory; it is originally neither caused by conditions nor arising from a self-nature."[^8]

This is the sūtra's deepest engagement with the mind-faculty (*manas-indriya*) within the four categories. What S3-P05 demonstrated as "the sixth consciousness is the only one that can look in the mirror" — its instrument, the mind-faculty, is here revealed by the Śūraṅgama as itself a fatigue-appearance upon the tathāgatagarbha.

#### 2.3 Twelve Sense-Bases and Eighteen Realms (T0945, Fascicle 3)

Expanding outward from the six entrances, adding the six sense-objects yields the twelve sense-bases; adding the six consciousnesses yields the eighteen realms. The Buddha applies the same logic to dismantle each.

On the twelve sense-bases (color-object and eye-object as representative):

> "Observe this Jeta grove and its springs and pools. What do you think — does color produce eye-seeing, or does the eye produce color-appearance? … Therefore you should know that seeing and color, together with space, are all without fixed location; the two bases of color and seeing are illusory; they are originally neither caused by conditions nor arising from a self-nature."[^9]

On the eighteen realms (eye-consciousness realm as representative):

> "If it arises from the eye, then without color and space there is nothing to discriminate — even if you have consciousness, what will you use it for? … Therefore you should know that the eye, color as condition, and the eye-consciousness realm — all three locations are without substance; the eye, color, and eye-consciousness realm are originally neither caused by conditions nor arising from a self-nature."[^10]

The logical structure of the four categories is now complete. From five aggregates to six entrances to twelve sense-bases to eighteen realms, this is not a random stack of classification systems but a progressive increase in analytical resolution: the aggregates address gross experience, the entrances isolate the instruments, the sense-bases pair instruments with objects, and the realms add the cognitive function. At each level of increased resolution, the Buddha points out the same thing: **originally neither caused by conditions nor arising from a self-nature.**

The finer the analysis, the fewer "independently existing entities" remain.

#### 2.4 The Seven Elements as Originally Tathāgatagarbha (T0945, Fascicle 3)

After the four categories, the Buddha introduces an entirely different framework — the seven elements: earth, water, fire, wind, space, seeing, and consciousness.

Here appears the most important formula in this section of the Śūraṅgama. Taking the earth-element as example:

> "You do not know that within the tathāgatagarbha, **the nature of form is true emptiness, the nature of emptiness is true form** — **originally pure by nature, pervading the dharma-realm; following sentient beings' minds, corresponding to what they know; manifesting according to their karma.** Worldly beings in their ignorance mistake it for conditions or a self-nature — all this is the discriminating calculation of the consciousness-mind; there are merely words, utterly without real meaning."[^11]

This formula — **"originally pure by nature, pervading the dharma-realm; following sentient beings' minds, corresponding to what they know; manifesting according to their karma"** (*qīngjìng běnrán, zhōubiàn fǎjiè, suí zhòngshēng xīn, yìng suǒ zhī liàng, xún yè fāxiàn* 清淨本然，周遍法界，隨眾生心，應所知量，循業發現) — appears in full at the conclusion of every single element, seven times total.[^12] For earth it is "the nature of form is true emptiness, the nature of emptiness is true form"; for fire, "the nature of fire is true emptiness"; for water, wind, space, seeing, and consciousness, each substitutes its own name into the identical formula. This is not redundancy — it is pedagogical design: seven repetitions so that the practitioner personally confirms the same structure within each element.

Even more significant is the incremental subsumption of the seven elements' conclusions:

> Space-element conclusion: "Together named the **five elements**, their nature is truly perfect and interpenetrating; all are tathāgatagarbha, originally without arising or ceasing."
> Seeing-element conclusion: "Together named the **six elements**…"
> Consciousness-element conclusion: "Together named the **seven elements**, their nature is truly perfect and interpenetrating; all are tathāgatagarbha, originally without arising or ceasing."[^13]

Step by step the Buddha gathers — five, six, seven — subsuming the material, the perceptual, and the cognitive entirely into "their nature is truly perfect and interpenetrating; all are tathāgatagarbha." Nothing is left outside.

#### 2.5 Ānanda's Verse-Praise — The Listener's Response (T0945, End of Fascicle 3)

After the four categories and seven elements, Ānanda and the assembly's response is itself part of the teaching:

> "Each member of the great assembly knew for himself that his mind pervaded the ten directions and saw the ten directions of space as if viewing a leaf held in the palm of his hand. All things in all worlds were nothing but the wondrous, luminous original mind of bodhi; the essence of mind, perfectly complete, encompassed the ten directions. Looking back upon the body born of father and mother, it was like a speck of dust floating in the vast ten directions of empty space, now present, now gone; like a single bubble in a vast, still ocean, arising and ceasing with nowhere to be found."[^14]

"Mind pervades the ten directions" — after hearing the four categories and seven elements, the assembly's perspective has inverted: not "I am inside the world" but "the world is inside the mind." The body born of parents, from this perspective, is a mote of dust in boundless space, a bubble in the ocean.

Ānanda then made a great vow: "If a single sentient being has not yet attained Buddhahood, I will never enter nirvāṇa."[^15] Yet immediately after, he added: "Great Hero, great Power, great Compassion! I pray you further examine and remove the subtlest delusions." He knew he had understood — but he also knew that "understanding" is not "realization." Subtle delusions remain. This is yet another manifestation of the "knowing vs. realizing" (*zhī/zhèng* 知/證) distinction that runs through the entire S6 series.

### B. Commentary Sources

#### 2.6 Zǐxuán, *Shǒuléngyán Yìshū Zhùjīng* (T1799)

Zǐxuán's summary of the four categories and seven elements distills a core formula:

> "The three categories and seven elements are 'within-appearance-precisely-the-nature' (*jí xiàng jí xìng* 即相即性) — originally unborn, and now without ceasing; arising, ceasing, coming, and going are all tathāgatagarbha, perfectly pervading, unmoving, originally pure by nature."[^16]

**"Within-appearance-precisely-the-nature"** — these four characters are the key to understanding the four categories and seven elements. It is not that one departs from appearances to seek the nature (that would be treating the tathāgatagarbha as something hidden behind appearances); rather, it is precisely *within* appearances that one directly sees the nature. You do not need to "empty out" the five aggregates before you can see the tathāgatagarbha — the very body of the five aggregates *is* the tathāgatagarbha.

On the seven elements, Zǐxuán provides a further precision:

> "The seven categories subsume all dharmas — space and existence, faculties and objects, form and mind, nature and appearance — dust upon dust, dharma upon dharma, none that does not pervade, none that does not contain… **merging appearance into nature, and since nature pervades, appearance pervades as well** — only this deserves the name of 'great' in the supreme and ultimate sense."[^17]

"Merging appearance into nature, and since nature pervades, appearance pervades" (*huì xiàng jí xìng, xìng biàn xiàng biàn* 會相即性，性遍相遍) — because the nature pervades all, so too do the appearances. The seven elements do not merely say "each element is true in itself"; they say "each element pervades all." This is the upgrade from the four categories to the seven elements.

#### 2.7 Jiāoguāng Zhēnjiàn, *Zhèngmài Shū* (X0275)

Jiāoguāng's exegesis provides the clearest structural framework for the four categories and seven elements. He first confirms the tathāgatagarbha classification of the four categories:

> "It is precisely the subsuming of phenomena into principle, the merging of appearances into nature, nothing more. It has not yet reached the arising of appearances from the nature, the forming of phenomena from principle. Among the three storehouses, **it belongs precisely to the empty tathāgatagarbha**."[^18]

He then identifies the crucial difference between the four categories and the seven elements:

> "The four categories merely speak of each dharma as being truly the nature, but have not yet said that each dharma pervades the dharma-realm… It is only with the seven elements that each dharma pervades the dharma-realm, hence they are collectively called 'great'… **The former reveals that each dharma is true and constant in its own body; the latter reveals that each dharma is interpenetrating and all-pervading.**"[^19]

This passage warrants repeated reading. The four categories say "each dharma is true and constant in itself" (*fǎ fǎ dāngtǐ zhēncháng* 法法當體真常). The seven elements say "each dharma interpenetrates and pervades all" (*fǎ fǎ yuánróng zhōubiàn* 法法圓融周遍). The former belongs to the empty tathāgatagarbha (subsuming phenomena into principle); the latter enters the non-empty tathāgatagarbha (fully manifesting the true).

Jiāoguāng's position within his overall exegetical framework:

> "**The seven elements fully manifest the true.** For only the destruction of [attachment to] consciousness is an ultimate destruction; the rest are all not ultimate destructions."[^20]

P02's seven locations of mind = "completely destroying the false." P03's ten demonstrations of seeing = "mostly manifesting the true, with some destruction of the false." P04's four categories and seven elements reach "fully manifesting the true" — no more destroying anything, but directly pointing out the true face of all dharmas.[^21]

### C. Supporting Sūtras

#### 2.8 Heart Sūtra — "Illuminated the Emptiness of All Five Aggregates" (T0251)

> "The Bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara, coursing in the deep practice of Prajñāpāramitā, illuminated the emptiness of all five aggregates and crossed beyond all suffering and difficulty."[^22]

The Heart Sūtra's "illuminated the emptiness of all five aggregates" represents the Prajñāpāramitā perspective, emphasizing *emptiness* — emptying the *parikalpita* (universally constructed) superimpositions upon the aggregates. The Śūraṅgama's "the five aggregates are originally the tathāgatagarbha's marvelous true suchness" represents the tathāgatagarbha perspective, emphasizing *original suchness* — the *pariniṣpanna* (perfectly accomplished nature) has always been there. These two readings are not contradictory — one removes what is false (emptying the *parikalpita*), the other points out what is true (revealing the *pariniṣpanna*). The path walked in S2 here converges with S6.

#### 2.9 Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra — Tathāgatagarbha and Store-Consciousness (T0670/T0671)

The Laṅkāvatāra provides the most direct cross-sūtra corroboration for the tathāgatagarbha concept:

> "The ālaya-vijñāna is called the tathāgatagarbha, and it exists together with ignorance and the seven consciousnesses, **like the ocean's waves, never ceasing**, because it is born with the body."[^23]

> "The tathāgatagarbha-consciousness is not within the ālaya-vijñāna; therefore the seven consciousnesses arise and cease, but **the tathāgatagarbha-consciousness neither arises nor ceases**."[^24]

The Laṅkāvatāra's "ocean-wave" metaphor corresponds structurally to the four categories and seven elements: the ocean (tathāgatagarbha) neither arises nor ceases; the waves (the seven consciousnesses, all dharmas) arise and cease — yet the waves have never left the ocean. The four categories and seven elements say precisely this: you have been watching waves; the Śūraṅgama shows you the ocean.

Crucially, the Laṅkāvatāra includes a protective declaration:

> "When I speak of the tathāgatagarbha, it is not the same as the ātman spoken of by non-Buddhists."[^25]

This anticipates the Middle Way correction in §五.2 of the present paper.

#### 2.10 Avataṃsaka Sūtra — "The Mind Is Like a Master Painter" (T0279)

> "**The mind is like a master painter, able to paint all worlds; the five aggregates all arise from it, and there is no dharma it does not create.**"
> "**If a person wishes to know all buddhas of the three times, they should contemplate the nature of the dharma-realm: all is made by mind alone.**"[^26]

The Avataṃsaka's "all is made by mind alone" (*yīqiè wéi xīn zào* 一切唯心造) resonates structurally with the four categories and seven elements: "the mind is like a master painter" does not mean there is a mind somewhere externally creating the world — rather, the unfolding of all dharmas *is* the unfolding of mind, just as the painter's paintings are the painter's mental intentions made manifest. Each dharma in the four categories and seven elements is a "painting" of the tathāgatagarbha — the painting has never left the painter.

## 三 Core Argument

### 3.1 The Progressive Structure of the Four Categories — Approaching from Coarse to Fine

The four categories are not four parallel classification systems but a progressively deepening analytical structure:

The **five aggregates** are the coarsest layer — form, sensation, perception, formation, consciousness: the totality of a person's experience. At this layer, the Buddha's message is: what you take to be a solid, experienced world is in fact a "fatigue-of-staring appearance" upon the tathāgatagarbha.

The **six entrances** go one step inward — no longer examining what you experience, but the instruments you use to experience. The five aggregates dismantle the "what" (the experienced); the six entrances dismantle the "what-with" (the tools of experience).

The **twelve sense-bases** pair the faculties with their objects — your eye and the color you see, your ear and the sound you hear. The Buddha's argument: there is no "location" where color and seeing truly meet.

The **eighteen realms** add consciousness to faculty and object — the interplay of faculty, object, and cognition. The Buddha asks: is eye-consciousness produced by the eye, by color, or by both? No matter how you answer, you cannot locate an independent "realm."

Four layers of increasing analytical resolution — yet the more finely one analyzes, the fewer "independently existing entities" one finds. This is the pedagogical design: **you assume analysis will discover building blocks, but analysis carried to completion discovers the tathāgatagarbha.**

### 3.2 The Double Negation — "Originally Neither Caused by Conditions Nor Arising from a Self-Nature" as Middle Way Structure

The formula that concludes every single item in the four categories is "originally neither caused by conditions nor arising from a self-nature" (*běn fēi yīnyuán, fēi zìrán xìng* 本非因緣、非自然性). This double negation is not casual rhetoric — it precisely targets the two most common erroneous ontologies:

**Condition-ism** (*yīnyuán lùn* 因緣論) holds that all dharmas are products of assembled conditions — with no independent essence, only combinations of conditions. This is a position held even by some Buddhist schools. The Śūraṅgama's "originally not by conditions" does not deny dependent origination — it says that if you treat conditionality as the *final* explanation, you reduce the tathāgatagarbha to a product of conditions. But the tathāgatagarbha is *asaṃskṛta* (unconditioned) — it is not manufactured.

**Naturalism** (*zìrán lùn* 自然論) holds that all dharmas emerge naturally from their own nature — without requiring conditions. This approaches certain non-Buddhist positions of inherent-nature doctrine (*svabhāva-vāda*). The Śūraṅgama's "not from a self-nature" denies this: the tathāgatagarbha is not a self-existing entity that automatically generates all dharmas.

"Originally neither caused by conditions nor arising from a self-nature" is a precise Middle Way positioning: it transcends the binary opposition between "externally determined" and "internally self-generated."[^27]

### 3.3 The Core Formula — "Originally Pure by Nature, Pervading the Dharma-Realm; Following Sentient Beings' Minds, Corresponding to What They Know; Manifesting According to Their Karma"

This formula appears complete at the conclusion of each of the seven elements, seven times in total.[^28] A phrase-by-phrase analysis:

**"Originally pure by nature"** (*qīngjìng běnrán* 清淨本然) — the intrinsic state of the tathāgatagarbha. Not "purified through practice" but "originally pure." Not "becoming pure at some later point" but "having been so from the outset." This echoes the Heart Sūtra's "neither defiled nor pure" — never having been contaminated, it does not need to be "purified."

**"Pervading the dharma-realm"** (*zhōubiàn fǎjiè* 周遍法界) — there is nowhere the tathāgatagarbha does not reach. Not present in some places and absent from others.

**"Following sentient beings' minds"** (*suí zhòngshēng xīn* 隨眾生心) — the critical turn. Although the tathāgatagarbha is pure by nature and pervades the dharma-realm, it does not "statically sit there." It *follows* sentient beings' minds — given different minds, it manifests different appearances.

**"Corresponding to what they know"** (*yìng suǒ zhī liàng* 應所知量) — the mode of "following." Not random manifestation, but *corresponding* to your "scope of knowing" — your cognitive capacity and range determine what you see. An ant and a human experience different worlds, not because there are two worlds, but because the "scope of knowing" differs.

**"Manifesting according to their karma"** (*xún yè fāxiàn* 循業發現) — the specific mechanism. Along the tracks of karma — your past actions, thoughts, habitual patterns — the tathāgatagarbha "manifests" whatever world appears before you.

Five phrases forming a complete theory of tathāgatagarbha manifestation: essence (originally pure) → scope (pervading all) → motive force (following minds) → mode (corresponding to knowing) → mechanism (manifesting through karma).

### 3.4 From Four Categories to Seven Elements — "Each Dharma Is True in Itself" Upgrades to "Each Dharma Pervades All"

Jiāoguāng Zhēnjiàn's insight warrants full development at the argumentative level.

The four categories demonstrate within each category that the dharmas it subsumes are "originally neither caused by conditions nor arising from a self-nature" — their nature is the tathāgatagarbha. This is **subsuming phenomena into principle** (*shè shì guī lǐ* 攝事歸理). The result: "each dharma is true and constant in its own body" (*fǎ fǎ dāngtǐ zhēncháng* 法法當體真常). But the four categories do not further address the relationships among these dharmas.

The seven elements break through this limitation. They do not merely say "the earth-element is tathāgatagarbha" — they say the earth-element is "originally pure by nature, **pervading the dharma-realm**." Each element pervades the dharma-realm — earth pervades all, water pervades all, fire pervades all. They are not seven separate things occupying seven separate territories but seven aspects of the single tathāgatagarbha.

This is the upgrade from "each dharma is true in itself" to "each dharma pervades all." The four categories tell you each dharma is true; the seven elements tell you each dharma not only is true but pervades and contains all.[^29]

In Jiāoguāng's three-storehouse exegesis, this corresponds to the progression from the empty tathāgatagarbha to the non-empty tathāgatagarbha:

| Textual Scope | Tathāgatagarbha Classification | Method | Result |
|---|---|---|---|
| Four categories (end of fasc. 2 — fasc. 3) | Empty tathāgatagarbha | Subsuming phenomena into principle | Each dharma is true in itself |
| Seven elements (fasc. 3) | Non-empty tathāgatagarbha | Fully manifesting the true | Each dharma pervades all |

The "empty" in empty tathāgatagarbha does not mean "nothing there" but "emptied of delusion" — the illusory aspects of phenomenal appearances are subsumed away, revealing the principle-body of the tathāgatagarbha. The "non-empty" in non-empty tathāgatagarbha does not mean "filled with stuff" but "replete with merit-qualities" — the tathāgatagarbha itself is endowed with all virtuous qualities and can manifest all dharmas according to conditions.[^30]

### 3.5 Heart Sūtra and Śūraṅgama — Two Sides of the Same Coin

The S2 series' core teaching is "illuminated the emptiness of all five aggregates" — the Prajñāpāramitā perspective. S6-P04's core teaching is "the five aggregates are originally the tathāgatagarbha's marvelous true suchness" — the tathāgatagarbha perspective.

These two perspectives do not contradict each other, but their directions differ:

**Prajñā's direction is "emptying the parikalpita."** The aggregates are not nonexistent — it is your *attachment* to the aggregates that fails to hold. The Heart Sūtra's work is to strip away the "extra layer" you have superimposed upon the aggregates — your reification, solidification, and eternalization of them.

**The Śūraṅgama's direction is "revealing the pariniṣpanna."** The aggregates do not *become* tathāgatagarbha only after being emptied — the nature of the aggregates has *always been* tathāgatagarbha. The Śūraṅgama does not require you first to "empty" the aggregates before you can see the tathāgatagarbha. It points out directly within the aggregates: **this is it.**

In S5 language: Prajñā is the *jífēi* (即非) — "X is precisely not-X." The Śūraṅgama is the *shì míng* (是名) — "is called X," and X has always been tathāgatagarbha. S5's Diamond Sūtra used *jífēi* to dismantle attachment to the hundred-dharma toolkit; S6's Śūraṅgama uses *shì míng* to tell you: what remains after the dismantling is the original face.[^31]

### 3.6 Recursive Closure — "There Is No Outside"

The question left at the end of P03 was: "Who is the one who just realized you were seeing?"

The four categories and seven elements answer: **that realizer, too, is within the tathāgatagarbha.**

This is a recursive closure. You want to find an observer standing "outside" looking at the tathāgatagarbha — but the four categories and seven elements have already exhaustively subsumed all dharmas into the tathāgatagarbha. The five aggregates are within it (your bodily-mental experience), the six entrances are within it (your perceptual tools), the twelve sense-bases are within it (tools plus objects), the eighteen realms are within it (tools plus objects plus cognition), the seven elements are within it (earth, water, fire, wind, space, seeing, consciousness). With what could you "stand outside"?

Ānanda's response after hearing the four categories and seven elements confirms this: "Mind pervades the ten directions; seeing the ten directions of space is like viewing a leaf in the palm."[^32] His perspective has inverted — not "I am inside the tathāgatagarbha" but "the tathāgatagarbha is my mind." There is no inside and outside.

A significant preview must be noted here. The five aggregates are tagged in the four categories and seven elements with five types of delusion: form is "solid" delusion, sensation is "insubstantial luminous" delusion, perception is "interpenetrating" delusion, formation is "hidden" delusion, consciousness is "inverted" delusion.[^33] In this paper these classifications are incidental markers — but in S6-P08 through P10, they will become the structural framework for the fifty skandha-māras: each aggregate harbors ten meditation states that may arise (demonic states), fifty in total. The four categories and seven elements tell you all dharmas are tathāgatagarbha; the fifty skandha-māras tell you what can distort this "knowing" during the course of practice.

## 四 Interdisciplinary Resonance: The Earth Analogy

⚠️ *The following analogy is heuristic, not a theoretical equivalence.*

Imagine you are an astronaut, viewing Earth from space.[^34]

From outside, Earth is a complete sphere. Originally pure by nature. Everything is happening on it — typhoons, earthquakes, tsunamis, the rise and fall of civilizations, wars and peace — yet Earth as a whole neither gains nor loses, is neither defiled nor purified. A volcanic eruption reshapes the surface, but Earth has not "lost a piece." An ice age covers the land, but Earth has not "gained a layer."

Now shift your perspective: you are standing on Earth's surface. You are a person in an earthquake. You feel the world shaking, life and death hanging in the balance. The four great elements (*mahābhūta*) churn beneath your feet; civilizations are overturned overnight. From your vantage point, everything is in violent flux — arising, abiding, changing, ceasing; waves following one upon another.

People on Earth believe they are in control of something — building skyscrapers, constructing dams, redirecting rivers. But from the perspective of Earth as a whole, these activities make scarcely any difference. The four elements overturn — earthquakes, volcanoes, tsunamis — rewriting civilizational history, yet Earth as a whole still neither gains nor loses.

The teaching of the four categories and seven elements performs exactly this perspective-shift. You have been standing on "Earth's surface" all along — the five aggregates, six entrances, twelve sense-bases, and eighteen realms are everything you experience as "the world shaking." What the Buddha wants you to see is the "view from space": the totality of all dharmas has always been the tathāgatagarbha's marvelous true suchness — originally pure by nature, pervading the dharma-realm. All the arising and ceasing you experience on the surface is, from that perspective, like weather on Earth — intense, but it does not change Earth itself.

**Similarity:** The perspective-shift is the core pedagogical move of the four categories and seven elements. From "seeing dharmas from within dharmas" to "seeing dharmas from the tathāgatagarbha" — just as from standing on the surface to viewing from space. "Originally pure by nature" corresponds to Earth's holistic neither-gaining-nor-losing; "following sentient beings' minds, corresponding to what they know, manifesting according to their karma" corresponds to experiencing the same Earth from different positions and in different ways.

**Fundamental difference:** Earth has a boundary — it is a finite physical object with a time of formation and will eventually be consumed by its sun. The tathāgatagarbha has no boundary — "pervading the dharma-realm" is not a large sphere but an absence of inside and outside. Earth's "neither gaining nor losing" is relative (approximately stable on geological timescales); the tathāgatagarbha's "neither arising nor ceasing" is absolute (originally without arising or ceasing). The observer on Earth and Earth are two different things — you can leave Earth; but the "observer" within the tathāgatagarbha has never been outside it — the recursive closure of §3.6 has no correspondence in the Earth analogy.

**Irreducibility statement:** This analogy aids understanding of the "perspective-shift" pedagogical structure, but it cannot be used to *prove* the existence of the tathāgatagarbha. Earth is a physical entity; the tathāgatagarbha is not. If you directly equate "Earth" with "tathāgatagarbha," you commit the error of spatialization — the tathāgatagarbha does not occupy space. The analogy should be set down here. ⚠️

## 五 Middle Way Corrections

### ❌ 5.1 Tathāgatagarbha Eternalism: "Originally a Buddha, So No Need to Practice"

"The five aggregates are originally the tathāgatagarbha's marvelous true suchness" — the most common misreading of this statement is: since everything is already the tathāgatagarbha, I am already a Buddha, so why practice?

This conflates "endowed by nature" (*xìng jù* 性具) with "manifested by nature" (*xìng xiǎn* 性顯). A seed buried in the ground is not the same as a flower in bloom. The tathāgatagarbha's "originally pure by nature" pertains to the level of nature, but sentient beings' "manifesting according to karma" means your present experience is a version obscured by karmic forces. You are indeed originally the tathāgatagarbha, but you are presently in the state of "staring until fatigue arises" — the clear sky has always been there, but what you see is wild flowers dancing. Practice is not "creating" the tathāgatagarbha; it is "ceasing to stare."

### ❌ 5.2 Tathāgatagarbha ≠ Ātman

"Originally pure by nature, pervading the dharma-realm" — this sounds remarkably like the Brahmanical *brahman* or the Upaniṣadic *ātman*: a pervasive, unarising-unceasing, pure entity.

The Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra already anticipated this problem; the Buddha clarified directly: "When I speak of the tathāgatagarbha, it is not the same as the ātman spoken of by non-Buddhists."[^35] The critical difference lies in the formula's second half: "following sentient beings' minds, corresponding to what they know, manifesting according to karma." The *ātman* is a self-existing, unconditional, permanently fixed entity; the tathāgatagarbha is not a "thing" — its manner of manifestation is "according to conditions." It has no fixed "appearance of its own" — what it manifests before you depends on your karma and cognition. The *ātman* is an object that can be "found"; the tathāgatagarbha is not — the very act of searching is itself within the tathāgatagarbha.

### ❌ 5.3 "Originally Not by Conditions" ≠ Denying Dependent Origination

"Originally neither caused by conditions nor arising from a self-nature" does not deny dependent origination (*pratītyasamutpāda*). The Buddha is not saying that dharmas do not arise through conditions — that would directly overturn the twelve links. He is saying that if you take "conditions" as the *ultimate* explanation — believing dharmas are "nothing but" assemblages of conditions — you miss the tathāgatagarbha dimension.[^36]

Likewise, "not from a self-nature" does not deny *dharmatā* (things being as they are). The Buddha is saying that if you understand dharmas' existence as a kind of "naturally-so" independent emergence, you turn the tathāgatagarbha into a non-Buddhist concept of inherent nature.

The double negation points toward the Middle Way: the tathāgatagarbha can neither be reduced to a product of conditions nor understood as a self-existing entity requiring no conditions.

### ⚠️ 5.4 Classification ≠ Entities

The four categories and seven elements classify all dharmas into four systems plus seven elements — five aggregates, six entrances, twelve sense-bases, eighteen realms, earth, water, fire, wind, space, seeing, and consciousness. But these classifications are themselves pedagogical tools, not ontological claims.

The Buddha is not saying these five aggregates genuinely "exist" as such — he is borrowing classification frameworks already familiar to you (the five aggregates are learned in the Āgamas) and pointing out the tathāgatagarbha within your familiar framework. The classification is the raft for crossing the river — it should be set down upon reaching the far shore. If you cling to "five aggregates" as a classification with some special ontological status, you have returned to dharma-attachment (*dharma-grāha*).

### ⚠️ 5.5 Prajñā-Emptiness ≈ Śūraṅgama-Tathāgatagarbha, but the Skillful Means Differ

Section 3.5 demonstrated that the Heart Sūtra's "illuminated the emptiness of all five aggregates" and the Śūraṅgama's "the five aggregates are originally tathāgatagarbha" are two sides of the same coin. But this does not mean they can be conflated.

The Prajñā system's skillful means is "emptiness" — entering through negation, dismantling attachment to dharmas. The tathāgatagarbha system's skillful means is "existence" (the "existence" of the non-empty tathāgatagarbha) — entering through affirmation, pointing out what the nature of dharmas actually is. For someone heavy with attachment, Prajñā's "emptiness" is the medicine; for someone leaning toward nihilism, the tathāgatagarbha's "non-empty" is the medicine. The two skillful means treat different diseases — one cannot be said to be "more correct" than the other.

The Śūraṅgama itself recognizes this. Jiāoguāng's three-storehouse exegesis progresses through empty tathāgatagarbha → non-empty tathāgatagarbha → neither-empty-nor-non-empty tathāgatagarbha — the final integration transcends both emptiness and non-emptiness.[^37]

## 六 Conclusion: All Dharmas Are Thus; You Are Within

The teaching of the four categories and seven elements can be distilled to a single statement: **wherever you look, you find the tathāgatagarbha; whatever you look with, that tool is also the tathāgatagarbha; even the act of "looking" itself is the tathāgatagarbha.**

This completes the progressive closure of S6's inner circle (P02–P04). P02's seven locations of mind told you the mind is not in the seven locations — destroying your false positioning of "mind." P03's ten demonstrations of seeing led you to recognize the seeing-nature — you are not merely what you see; there is a "capacity to see" that does not perish with its objects. P04's four categories and seven elements extend this seeing-nature to all dharmas — not only the seeing-nature is tathāgatagarbha, but all dharmas are so. From "the mind is not there" to "the seeing-nature is here" to "all dharmas are none other than this" — a perspective progressively expanding.

But between "knowing" that all dharmas are tathāgatagarbha and "living" all dharmas as tathāgatagarbha — what stands in the way? Ānanda, after hearing the four categories and seven elements, made a great vow but also admitted that "subtle delusions" remain. He knew, but he had not yet come home.

The way home is not a single road. Twenty-five great ones each found their own door. The next paper's question (S6-P05): **Where is yours?**

[^35]: See note [^25]. The Laṅkāvatāra's statement is the Buddha's own protective declaration against the misreading of the tathāgatagarbha teaching as *ātman*.
[^36]: See note [^27].
[^37]: Jiāoguāng's three-storehouse exegesis: empty tathāgatagarbha (four categories + ten demonstrations) → non-empty tathāgatagarbha (seven elements) → neither-empty-nor-non-empty tathāgatagarbha (fascicle 4's complete revelation). The present paper addresses the first two layers; the third belongs to Pūrṇa's question in fascicle 4 and will be treated in subsequent S6 papers.
