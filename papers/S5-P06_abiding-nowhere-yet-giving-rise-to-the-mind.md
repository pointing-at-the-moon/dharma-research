# Abiding Nowhere, Yet Giving Rise to the Mind

**Āśraya-parāvṛtti and the Simultaneity of Non-Abiding**

## Abstract

"Abiding nowhere, yet giving rise to the mind" (應無所住而生其心, *yīng wú suǒ zhù ér shēng qí xīn*) is the most widely known line of the Diamond Sūtra—and the line upon hearing which the Sixth Patriarch Huineng awakened. Yet the Chinese conjunction 而 (*ér*) is easily misread as sequential: first achieve non-abiding, then give rise to the mind. This paper argues that non-abiding and giving rise to the mind occur simultaneously: non-abiding is not the absence of mind (that would be nihilism), and giving rise to mind is not abiding (that would be ordinary consciousness). Non-abiding mind is the correct functioning of *paratantra-svabhāva*.

Through the Yogācāra lens of *āśraya-parāvṛtti* (transformation of the basis), relinquishing *parikalpita* (non-abiding) and realizing *pariniṣpanna* (giving rise to the mind) are two aspects of one operation, with *paratantra* as the unchanging basis. The fundamental non-discriminating wisdom (*nirvikalpaka-jñāna*) and subsequently-attained wisdom (*pṛṣṭha-labdha-jñāna*) are "one in essence, differentiated in function"—precisely mirroring the simultaneity of non-abiding and giving rise to the mind.

The paper further examines the *trisvabhāva* structure of §10's "adorning buddha-lands," the doctrinal link to Vimalakīrti's "when the mind is pure, the buddha-land is pure," the Śūraṅgama Sūtra's sevenfold examination as evidence that the mind has never abided anywhere, and the Sixth Patriarch's awakening as a demonstration of simultaneous non-abiding and mind-arising.

**Keywords:** non-abiding mind, *āśraya-parāvṛtti*, *trisvabhāva*, *prajñā*, simultaneous operation, Diamond Sūtra §10

## I. The Problem

Of the Diamond Sūtra's roughly five thousand characters, ten are the most famous, the most cited, and the most misread: 應無所住而生其心—"one should give rise to a mind that abides nowhere."[^1]

The Sixth Patriarch Huineng awakened upon hearing this line. The Platform Sūtra's account made these ten characters the most iconic moment of awakening in Chinese Buddhist history. But fame breeds distortion. Chan anthologies read them as a pithy mind-instruction. Modern self-help literature reads them as a relaxation technique. And the ambiguity of Chinese grammar leads countless readers to read them as a two-step practice formula: first achieve non-abiding, *then* give rise to the mind.

This paper's central thesis is that non-abiding and giving rise to the mind occur simultaneously, not sequentially. The Chinese conjunction 而 (*ér*) functions here as parallel coordination, not temporal succession. "Abiding nowhere" is not a prerequisite; "giving rise to the mind" is not a result.

From the Yogācāra perspective, this simultaneity has a precise technical basis: *āśraya-parāvṛtti* (transformation of the basis)—relinquishing *parikalpita-svabhāva* (non-abiding) and realizing *pariniṣpanna-svabhāva* (giving rise to the mind) are two aspects of one operation, with *paratantra-svabhāva* as the unchanging basis. The Diamond Sūtra says in everyday language what the *Cheng weishi lun* says in technical language.

§10, the "Adorning Pure Lands" chapter, is the core locus of this line. Structurally, §10 follows §9's dismantling of attachment to the four fruits (even *arhatship* cannot be clung to) and precedes §11's merit comparison (measuring the immeasurable against the measurable).[^2] That §10 places "adorning buddha-lands" and "abiding nowhere" in the same passage is not coincidental—the Vimalakīrti Sūtra's teaching that "when the mind is pure, the buddha-land is pure" is its doctrinal source. The pure land is not external construction; it is the mind's transformation of its basis.

Within the S5 series, P06 follows P05's analysis of the merit-comparison pedagogical arc—§10's "giving without abiding in marks" is the heart-method underlying the merit comparisons—and is positioned within the dual-layer structure established by P07: §10 belongs to the first half (dismantling self-grasping / *kleśāvaraṇa*), but the concept of "non-abiding" undergoes deeper development in the second half, where even attachment to "non-abiding" is subjected to negation.[^3]

## II. Scriptural Foundations

### 2.1 The Diamond Sūtra: Three Occurrences of "Abiding Nowhere"

"Abiding nowhere, yet giving rise to the mind" appears three times in the Diamond Sūtra, each in a different context:

**§4 (Wondrous Practice of Non-Abiding)**—first occurrence, in the context of giving:

> 菩薩於法應無所住行於布施，所謂不住色布施，不住聲、香、味、觸、法布施。
> A bodhisattva should practice giving without abiding in anything—not abiding in form when giving, not abiding in sound, smell, taste, touch, or dharmas when giving.[^4]

**§10 (Adorning Pure Lands)**—the core occurrence, in the context of buddha-lands:

> 莊嚴佛土者，則非莊嚴，是名莊嚴。……不應住色生心，不應住聲、香、味、觸、法生心，應無所住而生其心。
> "Adorning buddha-lands" is not adorning; it is called adorning. ... One should not give rise to a mind abiding in form, nor give rise to a mind abiding in sound, smell, taste, touch, or dharmas. One should give rise to a mind that abides nowhere.[^5]

**§14 (Quiescence Beyond Marks)**—restated with a counter-proof:

> 應生無所住心。若心有住，則為非住。
> One should give rise to a mind that abides nowhere. If the mind has something it abides in, that is precisely non-abiding.[^6]

The three occurrences form a progressive structure: §4 introduces non-abiding in the concrete activity of giving; §10 unfolds the complete proposition within the *jífēi* (即非, "is not / is called") structure of adorning buddha-lands; §14 provides the logical self-negation—the opposite of "abiding" is not another kind of "abiding," but abiding's own self-cancellation.

The character 住 ("abide") appears approximately twenty-one times across the entire sūtra, spanning four semantic categories: settled abiding ("how should one abide"), non-attachment ("not abiding in form"), attachment-generated ("a mind that abides in form"), and dialectical self-negation ("if the mind abides, it precisely does not abide").[^7] From §2's question "how should one abide?" to §10's answer "one should abide nowhere," the sūtra's semantic arc of "abiding" traces a complete pedagogical trajectory—the only way to abide is to not abide.

### 2.2 The Vimalakīrti Sūtra: "From Non-Abiding as Root, All Dharmas Are Established" and "When the Mind Is Pure, the Buddha-Land Is Pure"

The Diamond Sūtra's "non-abiding" is not an isolated practice instruction. The Vimalakīrti Sūtra's "Contemplating Sentient Beings" chapter elevates non-abiding to an ontological declaration:

> 顛倒想孰為本？——無住為本。——無住孰為本？——無住則無本。文殊師利！從無住本，立一切法。
> What is the root of inverted thinking? — Non-abiding is the root. — What is the root of non-abiding? — Non-abiding has no root. Mañjuśrī, from non-abiding as root, all dharmas are established.[^8]

All dharmas take non-abiding as their root, and non-abiding itself has no root—a more thoroughgoing metaphysical statement than the Diamond Sūtra's. The Diamond Sūtra says non-abiding is a practice method; the Vimalakīrti Sūtra says non-abiding is the ontological ground of all dharmas.

The "Buddha Lands" chapter directly connects to §10's theme of adorning pure lands:

> 若菩薩欲得淨土，當淨其心；隨其心淨，則佛土淨。
> If a bodhisattva wishes to obtain a pure land, they should purify their mind; as their mind is pure, so the buddha-land is pure.[^9]

The Buddha further demonstrated by pressing the ground with his toe, revealing the innate splendor of the trichiliocosm—splendor that was always there, merely unseen because beings' defilements obscured it.[^10]

### 2.3 The Great Perfection of Wisdom Sūtra: The Prajñā Source of "Non-Abiding"

The Diamond Sūtra is a condensation of the Great Perfection of Wisdom Sūtra. Fascicle 570 contains what may be regarded as the *prajñā* source-text for "abiding nowhere, yet giving rise to the mind":

> 雖常如是心無所住，而能通達諸法性相，以無礙智、無功用心，為諸有情宣說諸法。
> Though the mind constantly abides nowhere, it can penetrate the nature and characteristics of all dharmas, and with unobstructed wisdom and effortless mind, expound all dharmas for sentient beings.[^11]

"The mind abides nowhere" yet "penetrates the nature of all dharmas"—non-abiding and penetration occur simultaneously, not sequentially. "Effortless mind" further indicates that this is not deliberate "non-attachment" but the natural operation of wisdom.

### 2.4 The Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra: The Ocean-Wave Analogy

The Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra uses the analogy of ocean waves to illustrate the relationship between the store-consciousness and the active consciousnesses:

> 猶如猛風，吹大海水。外境界風，飄蕩心海，識浪不斷。
> Like fierce winds blowing the great ocean waters—the winds of external objects toss the mind-ocean, and the waves of consciousness arise without ceasing.[^12]

The store-consciousness is the ocean, the active consciousnesses are the waves, and external objects are the wind. When the wind stops, the waves subside, but the ocean-water does not disappear. This is a precursor expression of *āśraya-parāvṛtti*: not the destruction of consciousness, but the transformation of its basis (wind stops → waves subside → ocean remains).

The Laṅkāvatāra also establishes the comprehensive Yogācāra framework in its summation verse: "The five dharmas, three self-natures, eight consciousnesses, and two kinds of selflessness—all are contained in the Great Vehicle."[^13]

### 2.5 The Śūraṅgama Sūtra: Sevenfold Examination of the Mind

In fascicle one of the Śūraṅgama Sūtra, the Buddha systematically challenges Ānanda's answer to "where is your mind?"—seven proposed locations, seven refutations: "there is no such place."[^14] At the seventh attempt, Ānanda answers "non-attachment to everything—that is what I call mind," almost a verbatim echo of "abiding nowhere." Yet the Buddha still refutes it—defining the mind as "non-attached" turns "non-attachment" itself into a mark, and one is once again "abiding."

The sevenfold examination provides a diagnostic foundation for non-abiding: the mind is not located anywhere, not because you *should* not abide, but because the mind *has never* abided anywhere.

### 2.6 The Cheng weishi lun and the Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra: Transformation of the Basis and the Three-Nature Framework

The *Cheng weishi lun* (fascicle 9) introduces *āśraya-parāvṛtti* with a verse: "Inconceivable, beyond attainment, this supramundane wisdom / relinquishes the two kinds of heaviness, thereby realizing transformation of the basis."[^15] Its definition: *paratantra* is the basis, which supports both defiled and purified dharmas; transformation means relinquishing *parikalpita* overlaid on *paratantra*, and realizing *pariniṣpanna* within *paratantra*.[^16]

Fascicle 10 unfolds four aspects of *āśraya-parāvṛtti*: the transforming path (*prajñā*), the transformed basis (seed-holding basis + delusion-awakening basis), what is relinquished (seeds of the two obstructions), and what is attained (nirvāṇa as "that which is revealed" + bodhi as "that which arises").[^17] Crucially, the fundamental non-discriminating wisdom and subsequently-attained wisdom are described as: "In relation to *tathatā*, it is non-discriminating; in relation to other objects, it belongs to subsequently-attained wisdom. One in essence, differentiated in function."[^18]

The Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra provides the epistemological foundation: knowing *parikalpita* as empty upon *paratantra* enables the cessation of defilement and the realization of purity.[^19] Fascicle 5 explicitly links transformation to the Dharma-body: "When transformation of the basis is complete, this is called the characteristic of the Tathāgata's Dharma-body."[^20]

### 2.7 Kuiji's Commentary and the Platform Sūtra: Exegesis and Awakening

Kuiji's *Jingang bore jing zanshu* is the crucial bridge between the Diamond Sūtra and Yogācāra. He reads "abiding nowhere, yet giving rise to the mind" explicitly as a simultaneous middle-way structure: "The earlier instruction to not abide in form and so on negates existence; the later instruction to give rise to the mind negates nothingness. Free of both existence and nothingness is the Middle Way."[^21] He offers the exquisite analogy of a bird in the sky: "Not abiding yet abiding is abiding in true emptiness—like a bird that does not abide in the sky yet is able to abide in the sky; if it abided in the sky, it would not be abiding in the sky."[^22] Kuiji further states: "The non-abiding mind is precisely right wisdom; know that a mind arising from abiding is nothing but deluded consciousness."[^23]

The Platform Sūtra records the most famous instance of awakening through hearing a sūtra: upon hearing "abiding nowhere, yet giving rise to the mind," Huineng instantly awakened and expressed his realization in five exclamations beginning "Who would have thought that self-nature..."—"is originally pure," "is originally unborn and undying," "is originally complete," "is originally unmoving," "can give rise to the myriad dharmas."[^24]

## III. Core Argument

### 3.1 Simultaneity: 而 Is Not Sequential

The doctrinal core of "abiding nowhere, yet giving rise to the mind" hinges on a single character: 而 (*ér*).

In Chinese, 而 can express temporal sequence ("learn and practice it regularly"), contrast ("learning without thinking is perilous"), or parallel coordination ("broadly learned and firmly committed"). Kumārajīva's translational genius lies in selecting a polysemous conjunction that lets the reader discover for themselves whether the relationship is sequential or simultaneous. Doctrinally, only simultaneity is coherent:

First, if non-abiding is a prerequisite and giving rise to the mind is a result, this presupposes a stage of "first achieving non-abiding"—but what is the mind doing during that stage? Not arising? A non-arising mind is annihilationism, not non-abiding.

Second, if giving rise to the mind is an independent step, what kind of mind arises? If it is a mind that abides, it is an ordinary mind; if it is a mind that does not abide, then "non-abiding" and "giving rise to the mind" are simply two descriptions of the same event.

Third, §14 provides a definitive counter-proof: "If the mind has something it abides in, that is precisely non-abiding."[^25] The moment of "abiding" is precisely not genuine abiding—a logically self-cancelling structure. By the same logic, "non-abiding" is not a state that can be arrived at (for then one would be "abiding" in "non-abiding"); it is the manner in which the mind operates.

Kuiji reads this explicitly as a simultaneous middle-way structure: "The earlier instruction to not abide in form and so on negates existence; the later instruction to give rise to the mind negates nothingness. Free of both existence and nothingness is the Middle Way."[^26] Negating existence and negating nothingness are completed simultaneously, not sequentially. Non-abiding negates existence (not grasping things as truly existent); giving rise to the mind negates nothingness (not falling into annihilationist emptiness). These two negations operate simultaneously, constituting the Middle Way.

✅ **Doctrinally established**: the 而 in "abiding nowhere, yet giving rise to the mind" is parallel coordination, not temporal causation. Non-abiding and giving rise to the mind are two descriptions of the same operation of mind—just as water's flowing and wetness are not two sequential steps but the simultaneous presentation of water being water.

### 3.2 The Jífēi Structure of §10: Three-Nature Mapping of Adorning Buddha-Lands

The text of §10 does not present "abiding nowhere, yet giving rise to the mind" in isolation. These ten characters appear within a complete *jífēi* (即非) structure:

> "Adorning buddha-lands" is not adorning; it is called adorning. — Therefore, Subhūti, all bodhisattva-mahāsattvas should give rise to a pure mind in this way... abiding nowhere, yet giving rise to the mind.[^27]

"Adorning buddha-lands is not adorning; it is called adorning"—this is precisely the *jífēi* tripartite formula mapped onto the theme of pure lands, as demonstrated in S5-P01.[^28] Mapped onto the three natures (*trisvabhāva*): "adorning buddha-lands" is *paratantra-svabhāva*—dependent arising functioning as it should, a bodhisattva practicing the six perfections. "Is not adorning" negates *parikalpita-svabhāva*—denying that a substantial buddha-land is adorned by a substantial bodhisattva. "Is called adorning" is *pariniṣpanna-svabhāva*—knowing adornment as illusory yet functional, neither real nor unreal.

"Abiding nowhere, yet giving rise to the mind" is the practice-face translation of this *jífēi* structure: knowing that adorning buddha-lands is *paratantra* (giving rise to the mind), not grasping it as *parikalpita* (non-abiding), thereby seeing its *pariniṣpanna* nature (pure mind).

Kuiji illuminates this with his "gold-into-vessel" analogy: "As gold is made into a vessel, the vessel does not come from outside—the vessel itself adorns the gold in return."[^29] The vessel is not brought from outside to decorate the gold—the vessel *is* the gold. Adornment is not added to the buddha-land from outside—a pure mind *is* adornment. This "is" is the simultaneity of non-abiding and giving rise to the mind expressed in the register of pure lands.

### 3.3 "Not Abiding in Form When Giving Rise to the Mind": The Six Sense-Fields and Yogācāra Epistemology

§10's complete instruction is: "One should not give rise to a mind abiding in form, not give rise to a mind abiding in sound, smell, taste, touch, or dharmas. One should give rise to a mind that abides nowhere."[^30] The Buddha enumerates the six sense-fields one by one, then summarizes with "abiding nowhere."

This is not a casual enumeration. In Yogācāra, the six sense-fields correspond to the perceived aspects (*nimittabhāga*) of the first five consciousnesses and the sixth consciousness.[^31] "Abiding in form when giving rise to the mind" means precisely grasping the perceived aspect as truly existent—taking the images projected by consciousness to be independently existing external objects. This is the operating mechanism of *parikalpita-svabhāva*: superimposing an "actually existing external object" onto the *paratantra* cognitive process.

The Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra describes this precisely:

> 由有情於依他起自性及圓成實自性上，增益遍計所執自性……
> Because sentient beings superimpose *parikalpita-svabhāva* upon *paratantra-svabhāva* and *pariniṣpanna-svabhāva*... through verbal habituation, verbal apprehension, and verbal latencies...[^32]

"Superimposition" (*samāropa*)—adding what is not there—is the epistemological mechanism of "abiding." You see form and hear sound; this is the normal cognitive activity of *paratantra*. But when you superimpose the judgment "this external object truly exists" onto that activity, *that* is abiding. Thus the Yogācāra reading of "not abiding in form when giving rise to the mind" is: cognitive activity operates as normal (giving rise to the mind), but no judgment of truly existing externals is superimposed onto it (non-abiding). The six fields are enumerated one by one because every cognitive channel can become an entry point for superimposition—*prajñā*'s illumination must cover all channels, not just one.

### 3.4 Āśraya-parāvṛtti and the Two Wisdoms: The Yogācāra Mechanism

"Abiding nowhere, yet giving rise to the mind" says in the Diamond Sūtra's language what *āśraya-parāvṛtti* says in the technical language of the *Cheng weishi lun*.

The *Cheng weishi lun* (fascicle 9) defines *āśraya-parāvṛtti*:

> 「依」謂所依，即依他起，與染、淨法為所依故……轉謂二分，轉捨、轉得。……故能轉捨依他起上遍計所執，及能轉得依他起中圓成實性。
> "Basis" means that which is depended upon, namely *paratantra*, since it serves as basis for both defiled and purified dharmas. "Transformation" has two aspects: relinquishing and attaining. Through repeatedly cultivating non-discriminating wisdom... one can relinquish *parikalpita* overlaid on *paratantra*, and attain *pariniṣpanna* within *paratantra*.[^33]

The structure is exquisitely precise. *Paratantra*—dependent arising functioning as it does—simultaneously serves as basis for both defiled and purified dharmas. This means that *āśraya-parāvṛtti* does not change *paratantra* itself but changes our *relationship* to it. Relinquishing *parikalpita*—releasing the reifying grasping superimposed on *paratantra*—is "non-abiding." Attaining *pariniṣpanna*—manifesting the *tathatā* inherent in *paratantra*—is "giving rise to the mind."

Relinquishing and attaining are not two sequential steps but two aspects of one operation—the moment *parikalpita* is released, *pariniṣpanna* manifests. As the *Cheng weishi lun* states, *tathatā* "though pure in nature, is defiled in appearance; therefore when defilement is removed, it is provisionally called newly pure"[^34]—*tathatā* has always been pure; it merely appears defiled due to adventitious obstructions. The moment obstruction is removed, purity manifests. Not first clearing and then attaining, but clearing *as* attaining.

The *Cheng weishi lun* (fascicle 10) further unfolds four aspects:[^35] the transforming path (*prajñā*), the transformed basis (seed-holding + delusion-awakening), what is relinquished (seeds of the two obstructions), and what is attained (nirvāṇa as revealed + bodhi as arisen). That nirvāṇa is "revealed" rather than "produced" is crucial—it was always there but obscured. Kuiji's "the vessel does not come from outside" is exactly this point.

The transforming path—*prajñā*—requires further elaboration. The *Cheng weishi lun* distinguishes fundamental non-discriminating wisdom (*nirvikalpaka-jñāna*) and subsequently-attained wisdom (*pṛṣṭha-labdha-jñāna*), a distinction that opens a more precise dimension for "non-abiding mind." From fascicle 9:

> 若時菩薩於所緣境，無分別智都無所得……智與真如平等平等，俱離能取、所取相故。
> When the bodhisattva, regarding the object of contemplation, gains nothing whatsoever through non-discriminating wisdom... wisdom and *tathatā* are equal and equal, both free of the marks of grasper and grasped.[^36]

Fundamental non-discriminating wisdom "gains nothing"—this is the deepest level of non-abiding: even the awareness "I am contemplating" is absent; grasper and grasped are both extinguished. Subsequently-attained wisdom is the other face: it "discriminates the specific and general characteristics of all dharmas, observes the different capacities of sentient beings in order to teach... manifests bodies and lands to expound the true Dharma for sentient beings."[^37] It knows differentiation, reads capacities, manifests forms—this is the bodhisattva dimension of "giving rise to the mind." Non-abiding does not mean hiding in emptiness and never coming out, but rather, on the basis of non-abiding, generating the wisdom and activity of liberating beings.

The *Cheng weishi lun* (fascicle 10) offers a supremely concise summary:

> 緣真如故，是無分別；緣餘境故，後得智攝。其體是一，隨用分二。
> In relation to *tathatā*, it is non-discriminating; in relation to other objects, it belongs to subsequently-attained wisdom. One in essence, differentiated in function.[^38]

"One in essence, differentiated in function"—fundamental and subsequently-attained wisdom are not two separate wisdoms but two aspects of a single wisdom. When oriented toward *tathatā*: fundamental wisdom (non-abiding). When oriented toward differentiated objects of the world: subsequently-attained wisdom (giving rise to the mind). One in essence, two in function—this is the precise Yogācāra articulation of "abiding nowhere, yet giving rise to the mind."

### 3.5 "When the Mind Is Pure, the Buddha-Land Is Pure": The Pure-Land Dimension of Āśraya-parāvṛtti

That §10 places "adorning buddha-lands" and "abiding nowhere, yet giving rise to the mind" in the same passage is not accidental textual arrangement—it is doctrinal necessity.

The Vimalakīrti Sūtra's "Buddha Lands" chapter provides §10's direct doctrinal source: "If a bodhisattva wishes to obtain a pure land, they should purify their mind; as their mind is pure, so the buddha-land is pure."[^39] The purity of the buddha-land is not external renovation but transformation of the mind. The Buddha further demonstrated by pressing the ground with his toe—the splendor was always there; beings simply could not see it due to their defilements.[^40]

This aligns perfectly with "what is attained" in the *Cheng weishi lun*'s *āśraya-parāvṛtti*: nirvāṇa is not newly produced but "originally pure in self-nature, merely not manifest due to adventitious obstructions. When the true holy path arises and severs those obstructions, its characteristic becomes manifest—this is called attaining nirvāṇa."[^41]

Kuiji directly links these two threads: "The characteristics of body and land are nothing but reflections of mind; only when the mind is pure can they be manifested."[^42] He then cites the Vimalakīrti Sūtra as a gloss on §10: "To purify the mind is to be free of both existence and nothingness."[^43]

"Free of existence" is non-abiding (not grasping marks as truly existent); "free of nothingness" is giving rise to the mind (not falling into annihilationist emptiness). Purifying the mind and purifying the buddha-land are not a causal chain in which the mind *changes* the land, but the middle way of the mind's *āśraya-parāvṛtti* revealing the buddha-land's original face.

The Vimalakīrti Sūtra's Upāli chapter adds: "When the mind is defiled, beings are defiled; when the mind is pure, beings are pure. The mind is not inside, not outside, not in between."[^44] "Not inside, not outside, not in between"—this directly connects to the Śūraṅgama Sūtra's sevenfold examination in the next section.

### 3.6 Sevenfold Examination of the Mind: The Mind Has Never Abided Anywhere

The Diamond Sūtra says "one should give rise to a mind that abides nowhere"—this is a practice instruction. The Śūraṅgama Sūtra approaches the same question from the opposite direction: the mind has *always* been without abiding—it is not that you *should* not abide, but that the mind has *never* abided anywhere.

In fascicle one of the Śūraṅgama Sūtra, the Buddha systematically challenges Ānanda on where the mind is located:[^45]

| Ānanda's Answer | The Buddha's Refutation |
|----------------|----------------------|
| Mind is inside the body | You should first see your heart and liver — "there is no such place" |
| Mind is outside the body | Body and mind unrelated — "there is no such place" |
| Hidden within the sense organs | If you see the eye, the eye becomes an object — "there is no such place" |
| Seeing darkness means seeing inside | Seeing darkness is not seeing inside — "there is no such place" |
| Mind arises wherever contact occurs | Mind without substance cannot make contact — "there is no such place" |
| In between | What is between is neither inside nor outside — "there is no such place" |
| Non-attachment to everything is mind | No mark means nothing; if not nothing, then a mark — "there is no such place" |

Seven answers, seven refutations. The seventh is especially significant: Ānanda's "non-attachment to everything—that is what I call mind" is almost verbatim "abiding nowhere." Yet the Buddha still refutes it: if "non-attachment" becomes the mind's definition, then "non-attachment" becomes a mark, and one is once again "abiding."[^46]

This refutation is crucial for the paper's core thesis. "Non-abiding" is not a state that can be named, established, or grasped. The moment you confirm "I have achieved non-abiding," you are abiding in "non-abiding." The sevenfold examination's conclusion is not "the mind is in an eighth place" but that the question "where is the mind?" is itself *parikalpita*—the mind is not an object that can be located.

The Śūraṅgama Sūtra's sevenfold examination is a diagnosis; the Diamond Sūtra's "abiding nowhere, yet giving rise to the mind" is a prescription—both point to the same fact: the mind's nature *is* non-abiding. This plants a seed for S6, which will take the sevenfold examination as its starting point for the Śūraṅgama Sūtra's complete practice map.

### 3.7 The Sixth Patriarch's Awakening: A Public-Case Demonstration

The Platform Sūtra records the most famous instance of awakening through hearing a sūtra:

> 至「應無所住而生其心」，惠能言下大悟，一切萬法，不離自性。
> Upon reaching "abiding nowhere, yet giving rise to the mind," Huineng instantly awakened: the myriad dharmas do not depart from self-nature.[^47]

"Instantly awakened"—not gradually understanding after hearing the whole sūtra, but awakening the moment this single line was heard. Huineng expressed his awakening in five exclamations:

> 何期自性，本自清淨；何期自性，本不生滅；何期自性，本自具足；何期自性，本無動搖；何期自性，能生萬法。
> Who would have thought that self-nature is originally pure! ... originally unborn and undying! ... originally complete! ... originally unmoving! ... can give rise to the myriad dharmas![^48]

"Originally pure" is non-abiding—self-nature has never abided in any defilement. "Can give rise to the myriad dharmas" is giving rise to the mind—self-nature inherently possesses the capacity to generate all dharmas. The five exclamations are the Sixth Patriarch's first-person awakening report, confirming this paper's core thesis: non-abiding (originally pure) and giving rise to the mind (generating the myriad dharmas) are not sequential but the simultaneous presentation of self-nature.

A subtle point of pedagogical design: the Sixth Patriarch did not first cultivate non-abiding and *then* give rise to the mind—he "instantly awakened," with non-abiding and giving rise to the mind arriving at once. This corroborates the public-case mirror property discussed in S5-P02: the Diamond Sūtra is a mirror—if you can see, you see; if you cannot, you cannot.[^49] The Sixth Patriarch's capacity (*ālaya-vijñāna* seeds ripening in that moment) coincided with the text—merit, roots, and conditions aligned simultaneously.

The Platform Sūtra's "Prajñā" chapter further states: "Self-nature inherently possesses the wisdom of prajñā; one uses one's own wisdom in constant illumination, without relying on written words."[^50] Prajñā is not imported from outside; it is innate. The Diamond Sūtra's words were merely a trigger, not prajñā itself. The Sixth Patriarch's awakening was not "given" by the sūtra but "revealed"—just as in *āśraya-parāvṛtti*, nirvāṇa is "that which is revealed," not "that which is produced."

### 3.8 Non-Abiding Mind in Daily Life: An Analogy

You are driving on the highway.

Hands on the steering wheel, eyes on the road, foot precisely controlling speed. You are fully engaged—judging distance, observing traffic, making decisions. Every action is "giving rise to the mind": the mind is operating, cognition is active, decisions are being made.

But have you considered: throughout this entire process, where does your mind "abide"?

You are not abiding in the steering wheel—you hold it but do not cling to it. You are not abiding in a speed—you control it but do not fixate on a number. You are not abiding in "I am a person who is driving"—you are simply driving.

This is the everyday version of "abiding nowhere, yet giving rise to the mind": fully engaged yet without clinging. The mind is operating (giving rise to the mind), but there is no additional grasping of "I am doing this" (non-abiding).

But note—the moment you begin to think "I am really focused right now," you have become distracted. Attention has shifted from the road to self-evaluation. This is the subtle entry point of "abiding": not abiding in external objects, but abiding in "my state."

This analogy also demonstrates the core danger addressed in this paper's Middle Way corrections: the thought "I have achieved non-abiding" is itself the most refined form of abiding. Just as in the Śūraṅgama Sūtra's seventh examination—Ānanda's "non-attachment to everything is mind" is refuted as "there is no such place"—self-awareness of "non-abiding" is precisely another layer of abiding.

⚠️ This analogy is a heuristic illustration. The "non-clinging" of driving is a temporary psychological state; *āśraya-parāvṛtti* is a permanent transformation of the consciousness-basis. Everyday "non-abiding" still operates within the range of defiled consciousness; post-transformation "non-abiding" is the natural state of undefiled wisdom. The analogy holds only on the single point of "simultaneity" and must not be extended to theoretical equivalence.

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Resonances

### 4.1 Heidegger's Gelassenheit

In his later work, Heidegger proposed *Gelassenheit* ("releasement" or "letting-be")—facing the technological world with neither blind embrace nor passive rejection, but with an attitude of letting things be. This bears structural resemblance to "abiding nowhere, yet giving rise to the mind" at the level of attitude: not clinging (non-abiding) while remaining engaged (giving rise to the mind), neither fleeing the world nor being bound by it.

⚠️ This is a heuristic analogy. Heidegger's *Gelassenheit* describes an existential attitude but lacks the epistemological mechanism of Yogācāra *āśraya-parāvṛtti*—it says "let things be" but does not explain what consciousness-structure makes letting-be possible. Non-abiding mind is not merely an attitude but a transformation of the consciousness-basis itself. The analogy holds only on the structural point of "non-clinging engagement."

### 4.2 Flow States in Cognitive Science

Csikszentmihalyi's flow state—total immersion in an activity, disappearance of self-consciousness, altered sense of time—bears phenomenological resemblance to "non-abiding mind": the mind is operating at full capacity (giving rise to the mind), but the meta-awareness of "I am doing this" temporarily ceases (resembling non-abiding).[^51]

⚠️ This is a heuristic analogy. Flow is a temporary psychological state with identifiable triggers and dissipation; *āśraya-parāvṛtti* is a permanent transformation. Flow occurs within the range of defiled consciousness—it is a temporary well-functioning of *paratantra*, not a fundamental elimination of *parikalpita*. The analogy holds only on the phenomenological feature of "full engagement with dissolution of self" and must not be equated with *āśraya-parāvṛtti*.

## V. Middle Way Corrections

❌ **Nihilist reading: "Abiding nowhere" means doing nothing, the mind like dead ash.** This is the most common misreading. The complete text is ten characters, not five. "Yet giving rise to the mind" is the safety valve against nihilism. Non-abiding does not mean not giving rise to the mind—a non-arising mind is annihilationism, not prajñā. *Paratantra* is illusory yet existent, not nihilistically void. As Kuiji states: "The later instruction to give rise to the mind negates nothingness"[^52]—giving rise to the mind guards precisely against the danger of annihilationism.

❌ **Sequential misreading: first practice non-abiding, then give rise to the mind.** This is a grammatical trap created by the Chinese 而. Reading "non-abiding → giving rise to the mind" as two steps converts the Diamond Sūtra's sudden teaching into a gradualist formula. The Sixth Patriarch's awakening was not first achieving non-abiding and then giving rise to the mind, but both arriving at once in a single awakening. The *Cheng weishi lun*'s "one in essence, differentiated in function" confirms that fundamental and subsequently-attained wisdom are not sequential but two aspects of a single wisdom.

❌ **Pretending to non-abide: verbally claiming non-abiding while inwardly clinging to "I have achieved non-abiding."** This is the most insidious form of dharma-grasping—taking "non-abiding" as something real. The moment you confirm "I have achieved non-abiding," you are abiding in "non-abiding." The Śūraṅgama Sūtra's seventh examination has already refuted this: Ānanda's "non-attachment to everything is mind" is deemed "there is no such place." The Diamond Sūtra's second half (§17 onward) specifically addresses this form of grasping—even "dharma" cannot be abided in, much less "non-abiding." S5-P07's "self-reenactment" structure treats this dimensional upgrade.[^53]

❌ **Confusing āśraya-parāvṛtti with suppression: thinking transformation of the basis means suppressing afflictions.** *Āśraya-parāvṛtti* transforms the basis; it does not suppress. Suppression uses a stronger willpower to press down afflictions—but the thought "I must control my afflictions" is itself another form of *parikalpita* (there is still an "I" that is "controlling"). Transformation does not suppress waves; it lets the wind stop—the Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra's ocean-wave analogy illustrates this precisely: when the wind stops, waves naturally subside; they are not pressed flat by another force.[^54]

## VI. Conclusion

"Abiding nowhere, yet giving rise to the mind" is the Diamond Sūtra's most concise and most profound practice instruction—ten characters, two aspects, one simultaneity.

The core argument of this paper can be distilled into three layers:

First, the textual layer: 而 is simultaneous, not sequential. The three occurrences across §4, §10, and §14 form a progressive structure, with §14's "if the mind abides, it precisely does not abide" providing the definitive logical argument. The semantic spectrum of "abide" across the entire sūtra—from "how should one abide?" to "abide nowhere" to "abiding is precisely not abiding"—traces a complete pedagogical arc.

Second, the mechanism layer: *āśraya-parāvṛtti* is the Yogācāra precise articulation of "non-abiding mind." Relinquishing *parikalpita* (non-abiding) and attaining *pariniṣpanna* (giving rise to the mind) are two aspects of one operation, with *paratantra* as the unchanging basis. Fundamental and subsequently-attained wisdom are "one in essence, differentiated in function"—non-abiding and giving rise to the mind are not two wisdoms but two aspects of one prajñā.

Third, the corroboration layer: the Vimalakīrti Sūtra's "from non-abiding as root, all dharmas are established" provides the metaphysical foundation; "when the mind is pure, the buddha-land is pure" explains why §10 places pure-land adornment and non-abiding in the same passage; the Śūraṅgama Sūtra's sevenfold examination argues from the reverse that the mind has never abided anywhere; and the Platform Sūtra's "instant awakening" and five "who would have thought" exclamations demonstrate the simultaneous arrival of non-abiding and giving rise to the mind.

§10 is located in the Diamond Sūtra's first half (dismantling self-grasping / *kleśāvaraṇa*), but the teaching of non-abiding does not end here. In the second half (§17 onward), the Buddha will subject the teaching itself to the *jífēi* operation—even "dharma" cannot be abided in, even "non-abiding" cannot be abided in. "Abiding nowhere, yet giving rise to the mind" opens a door, and behind that door is a mirror: you believe you have understood "non-abiding"? The mirror will tell you whether the "non-abiding" you have understood has itself become another form of abiding.
