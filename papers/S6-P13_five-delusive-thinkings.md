## Abstract

Why are the Fifty Skandha-Māras of the *Śūraṅgama-sūtra* not simply fifty independent warnings, but a structural map of the entire path of awakening? Immediately after the teaching of the Fifty Māras concludes in Fascicle Ten, Ānanda rises again to ask three questions about the root causes of the five aggregates (*pañcaskandha*), the manner of their exhaustion, and the boundaries between them. The Buddha's reply is unexpected: rather than addressing fifty phenomena one by one, he collapses the entire structure into a single sentence — "the root causes of the five aggregates are all delusive thinkings" (*pañcaskandha-mūla-hetavaḥ sarve vikalpa-saṃjñā eva*).[^1] Taking this sentence as its entry point, this paper argues that the Fifty Skandha-Māras constitute an exhaustive catalogue of ten typical traps corresponding to each of five progressively subtler delusive thinkings: (1) **solid** (*dṛḍha*, 堅固) for the form aggregate, (2) **illusively bright** (*ākāra-prabhāsa*, 虛明) for the feeling aggregate, (3) **fluid** (*saṃplava*, 融通) for the perception aggregate, (4) **hidden** (*gūḍha*, 幽隱) for the formations aggregate, and (5) **vacuous and inverted** (*nirākāra-viparyāsa*, 罔象虛無顛倒) for the consciousness aggregate. These five delusive thinkings form a refinement series structured by the Buddha's own dictum "**overlapping arising, born through consciousness, extinguished from form down**" (五陰元重疊生起,生因識有,滅從色除), and the remedial principle throughout is a five-stage refinement of the single rule "do not form a holy interpretation" (不作聖心).

The paper makes five interlinked structural contributions. First (§3.2), it presents the **five-stage refinement gradient of delusive thinking** with "the ratio of unreal to real in the cognitive object" as the internal parameter. Second (§3.3), it analyzes the **two-pole structure of each aggregate's boundary** following Jiāoguāng Zhēnjiàn's 交光真鑑 commentary: each aggregate is defined by a pair of mutually constituting opposites (form/space, contact/separation, retention/forgetting, arising/ceasing, immersed-stillness/conjoined-stillness), and exhausting only one pole leaves the meditator still inside that aggregate. Third (§3.4), it presents the **five-stage refinement genealogy of "do not form a holy interpretation"** as the inner spine of the entire S6 series from P08 through P12. Fourth (§3.5), it formalizes the **"sudden in principle, gradual in phenomena"** (理頓事漸) doctrinal classification as a third path between pure subitism and pure gradualism. Fifth (§3.6), it develops Jiāoguāng's claim that **Avalokiteśvara's six-knot release in Fascicle Six is structurally identical** ("全同") to the sequential exhaustion of the five aggregates in Fascicle Ten — two formulations of the same topology of awakening.

Following the structural arguments, the paper executes the *Śūraṅgama-sūtra*'s own injunction at the close of Fascicle Ten — "transmit this to the practitioners of the latter dharma age" (傳示將來末法之中諸修行者) — with a direct address to the contemporary reader who has read the entire fifty-māra map. As the formal closure of the S6 series, the paper also constructs the bridge to S7 (the *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka* / *Lotus Sūtra* series): the *Śūraṅgama*'s "do not establish any ultimate position" and the *Lotus*'s "there is only one ultimate position, the One Buddha Vehicle" (*ekayāna*) are two unfoldings of the same structure — the former negates all intermediate positions, the latter positively establishes the unique terminal position.

**Keywords:** five delusive thinkings (*pañca-vikalpa-saṃjñā*); fifty skandha-māras; aggregate boundaries; sudden principle and gradual phenomena (*li-dun shi-jian*); Avalokiteśvara's perfect penetration; do-not-form-a-holy-interpretation; one Buddha vehicle (*ekayāna*); *Śūraṅgama-sūtra*

## I. Introduction: Why Does Ānanda Ask Again?

### 1.1 The Structural Recursion at the Close of Fascicle Ten

From the latter half of Fascicle Nine through the bulk of Fascicle Ten, the *Śūraṅgama-sūtra* delivers, in unbroken sequence, the teaching of the **Fifty Skandha-Māras** (五十陰魔) — ten phenomena each across the five aggregates of form (*rūpa*), feeling (*vedanā*), perception (*saṃjñā*), formations (*saṃskāra*), and consciousness (*vijñāna*). Fifty meditative pitfalls have been catalogued in detail; according to ordinary expository logic, the teaching should now conclude with a brief summary, dedication of merit, and circulation formula. But the *Śūraṅgama-sūtra* does not do this. After the fifty māras are fully expounded, an unexpected scene unfolds:

> "Ānanda then rose from his seat. Having heard the Buddha's instruction, he made obeisance and respectfully received it, retaining it without loss. Within the great assembly he again addressed the Buddha: 'Just as the Tathāgata has said, "within the marks of the five aggregates, the root mind of thinking is fivefold falsity" — we ordinarily have not received the Tathāgata's subtle exposition of this. Moreover, are these five aggregates extinguished simultaneously, or exhausted in sequence? And of these five gradations, what constitutes the boundary of each? May the Tathāgata, out of great compassion, illuminate the minds and eyes of this assembly, and serve as a future eye for all sentient beings of the latter age!'"[^2]

Ānanda's renewed question is structurally crucial. He is not asking "why do these fifty pitfalls exist?" — that has already been addressed in the teaching itself. Nor is he asking "how do I avoid them?" — that has been addressed in the per-phenomenon remedies. He is asking three deeper questions:

**First**, the phrase "the root mind of thinking is fivefold falsity" (五陰相中,五種虛妄為本想心) was used by the Buddha during the fifty-māra exposition, but only in passing. Ānanda asks for a systematic unfolding of what these "five delusive thinkings" actually are, and why this designation is appropriate.

**Second**, "are the five aggregates extinguished simultaneously or exhausted in sequence?" (此五陰為併銷除?為次第盡?) — this is one of the most fundamental questions in the doctrinal classification of meditative practice. Subitism would say "simultaneously"; gradualism would say "in sequence." The choice between these two positions has historically defined entire schools of East Asian Buddhism.

**Third**, "of these five gradations, what constitutes the boundary of each?" (如是五重,詣何為界?) — what is the criterion for declaring one aggregate exhausted and the next entered? Without clear boundaries, the entire fifty-māra map dissolves into a vague continuum.

These three questions form a structural triad: the **first** asks about *substance* (what the five aggregates fundamentally are), the **second** asks about *manner* (how they are extinguished), and the **third** asks about *demarcation* (where the boundaries lie). Substance, manner, and boundary together constitute Ānanda's structural interrogation of the entire fifty-māra teaching.

It is worth noting that Ānanda explicitly frames his question as being asked "for all sentient beings of the latter age, to serve as a future eye." The audience for his question is not the assembly of his contemporaries; it is, by his own designation, the readers of the latter dharma age (*saddharma-pratirūpaka* and *saddharma-vipralopa*) — that is, those reading this passage two thousand years later. This explicit projection of audience will be picked up again in §6.3 of this paper.

### 1.2 The Force of the Answer "All Are Delusive Thinking"

The Buddha's response to Ānanda's three questions is extraordinarily compressed yet comprehensive. He begins with the substantive question:

> "The Buddha said to Ānanda: 'The pure, true, wondrously bright, originally enlightened nature is perfectly pure, retaining neither birth-and-death nor any defilement. Even space itself arises from delusive thinking. This original enlightenment, wondrous, bright, true, and pure, deludedly gives rise to the receptacle worlds [*bhājana-loka*]. … Therefore the Tathāgata is showing you that the root causes of the five aggregates are all delusive thinking.'"[^3]

The phrase "the root causes of the five aggregates are all delusive thinking" (五陰本因,同是妄想) is the axis of the entire response. The Buddha does not address fifty phenomena one by one, does not re-explain the cause of each pitfall, does not list new criteria for discrimination. He collapses the entire fifty-māra teaching into this single eight-character sentence.

Where does the force of this collapse lie?

First, it folds the number "fifty" back to "five": beneath fifty phenomena lies a structure of five, and that structure of five is named the five delusive thinkings. Second, it folds "five" back to "one": all five delusive thinkings share a single nature — they are all delusive thinking. The Buddha's reply thus presents a three-tier collapsing structure: **fifty phenomena → five structures → one principle**. Any reader who can hold these three layers simultaneously possesses the entire map of the fifty-māra teaching.

This is precisely why the present paper must take this passage as its point of entry. Reading only the specific contents of the Fifty Skandha-Māras yields fifty phenomena and fifty remedies — valuable, but missing the structural skeleton. Reading only the conclusion "all are delusive thinking" misses the specific unfolding of each phenomenon within each aggregate. **Only by holding both levels simultaneously can one perceive the inner spine of the entire S6 series from P08 to P13** — a spine which proceeds from fifty phenomena to five structures to one principle, where the principle has different unfoldings at different levels of subtlety.

### 1.3 Position within the S6 Series and the Bridge to S7

This paper is the thirteenth and final installment of the S6 *Śūraṅgama* series. From P01 to P13, the series has constructed a systematic phenomenology of meditative practice, demonstrating how the *Śūraṅgama-sūtra* incorporates every conceivable meditative pitfall into a single doctrinal classification system. Specifically:

- **P01–P07** treat the practice doctrines of Fascicle Six and earlier — the twenty-five perfect penetrations (*viṃśati-pañca-samāpattayaḥ*), Mañjuśrī's selection of the ear-faculty as the optimal path, and the foundational meditative architecture.
- **P08–P12** treat the Fifty Skandha-Māras section by section: P08 on the form-aggregate ten phenomena (照妖鏡的開光, "the consecration of the demon-revealing mirror"); P09 on the feeling-aggregate ten phenomena (第二支箭, "the second arrow"); P10 on the perception-aggregate ten phenomena (求大師, "seeking the great teacher"); P11 on the formations-aggregate ten māras (鏡像學生, "the mirror-student"); P12 on the consciousness-aggregate ten māras (兩楹之間, "between the two pillars").

P13, as the series capstone, must complete two levels of formal closure.

**The first level** is internal to S6: collapsing the five sub-treatises (P08–P12) into a single general treatise. This collapse is organized around the **gradient of the five delusive thinkings**, and §III of the present paper argues that the five sub-treatises are five expressions of a single principle (do-not-form-a-holy-interpretation) at five different levels of subtlety, where each level corresponds to one of the five delusive thinkings.

**The second level** is the bridge from S6 to S7: the transition from the *Śūraṅgama* series to the *Lotus Sūtra* series (法華經系列). The crucial question for this transition is: if the entire teaching of the *Śūraṅgama* is "do not establish any ultimate position" (the Fifty Māras repeatedly warn against mistaking any meditative position for the final goal), then where is the reader supposed to go after finishing all of S6? Is meditative practice nothing but perpetual negation, perpetual non-establishment, perpetual deconstruction?

It is not. §VI of this paper argues that the *Śūraṅgama*'s "do not establish any ultimate position" and the *Lotus*'s "there is only one ultimate position — the One Buddha Vehicle" are two unfoldings of the same structure. The *Śūraṅgama* speaks the via negativa of meditative phenomenology: no intermediate position is the ultimate. The *Lotus* speaks the via positiva of narrative structure: the ultimate position is unique. These two voices are not contradictory but complementary — one excludes errors from the negative side, the other establishes uniqueness from the positive side. The bridge between the close of S6 and the opening of S7 is constructed precisely upon this complementarity.

## II. Canonical Sources

### II.A Sūtra Sources (*sūtra-pramāṇa*)

#### 2.1 *Śūraṅgama* Fascicle Ten: Ānanda's Renewed Question and the Buddha's Answer

The full passage of Ānanda's renewed question reads:[^4]

> "Ānanda then rose from his seat. Having heard the Buddha's instruction, he made obeisance and respectfully received it, retaining it without loss. Within the great assembly he again addressed the Buddha: 'Just as the Tathāgata has said, "within the marks of the five aggregates, the root mind of thinking is fivefold falsity" — we ordinarily have not received the Tathāgata's subtle exposition of this. Moreover, are these five aggregates extinguished simultaneously, or exhausted in sequence? And of these five gradations, what constitutes the boundary of each? May the Tathāgata, out of great compassion, illuminate the minds and eyes of this assembly, and serve as a future eye for all sentient beings of the latter age!'
>
> "The Buddha said to Ānanda: 'The pure, true, wondrously bright, originally enlightened nature is perfectly pure, retaining neither birth-and-death nor any defilement. Even space itself arises from delusive thinking. This original enlightenment, wondrous, bright, true, and pure, deludedly gives rise to the receptacle worlds. It is like Yajñadatta mistaking a reflection for his head. Delusion is fundamentally without cause; within delusive thinking, causal nature is posited. Those who lose track of causality call it spontaneity. The nature of empty space is itself an illusory production; causality and spontaneity are both calculations of the deluded mind of sentient beings. Ānanda, knowing where delusion arises, one speaks of the causes of delusion; if delusion is fundamentally without cause, then speaking of the causes of delusion means there are originally none — how much less should one resort to spontaneity? Therefore the Tathāgata shows you that the root causes of the five aggregates are all delusive thinking.'"

This passage has four crucial features. **First**, Ānanda himself frames his question as being for "the latter age, to serve as a future eye" — an explicit projection that resonates with the Buddha's closing instruction at the end of the chapter (see §2.4) to "transmit this to the practitioners of the latter dharma age." **Second**, the Buddha begins by establishing the purity of original enlightenment (*mūla-bodhi*), then expands the scope of "delusive thinking" to include even space itself — an extraordinary maximalization of the category. **Third**, the reference to "Yajñadatta mistaking a reflection for his head" invokes a parable established earlier in the sūtra (Fascicle Four), in which a man named Yajñadatta runs about wildly, taking his own reflection for his head and unable to find his head — a parable whose function is to demonstrate that delusion requires no cause; delusion itself is causeless. **Fourth**, "delusion is fundamentally without cause" (妄元無因) is the most important verdict in this passage. Its argumentative structure is: if delusion had a first cause, that first cause would either be real (in which case it would not be delusion) or itself a prior delusion (requiring an even earlier cause, leading to infinite regress). Both alternatives fail; therefore delusion must be "fundamentally causeless."

#### 2.2 The Five Delusive Thinkings, Itemized

The Buddha then expounds each of the five delusive thinkings in turn. Each has a distinctive argumentative structure.

**Form aggregate = solid (堅固) first delusive thinking:**[^5]

> "Your body originally arose from the thinking of your parents; if your mind were not itself thinking, it could not come into the thinking that transmits life. As I said before: 'Thinking of a sour taste, saliva arises in the mouth; thinking of climbing a high place, the soles of the feet ache.' The cliff is not really there, the sour fruit has not yet arrived — if your body were not of the same kind as falsity, how could the saliva flow from merely speaking of sourness? Therefore know that your present physical body is named the **first delusive thinking, called solid**."

The argumentative structure here is *the unreal can produce the real*: the sexual desire (delusive thinking) of one's parents condenses into a physical embryo; speaking of sour fruit produces real saliva; thinking of high places produces real ache in the feet. These phenomena demonstrate that the physical body (form aggregate) and thinking (which is unreal) belong to the same category — therefore the body itself is "thinking-condensed-into-solidity."

**Feeling aggregate = illusively bright (虛明) second delusive thinking:**[^6]

> "Just as I said, the thinking of climbing a high place can really cause your body to feel a stinging ache. From cause arises feeling, which can move the physical body. Your present condition of being driven by the dual phenomena of benefit-conformity and harm-violation is named the **second delusive thinking, called illusively bright**."

The argumentative structure is *the dual driving of benefit-conformity and harm-violation*: the feeling aggregate has no substance of its own; it consists only of the continuous oscillation between pleasant (conforming to benefit) and unpleasant (violating welfare) phenomena. The phrase "illusively bright" means: feeling has no substance (illusive) but possesses receptive function (bright).

**Perception aggregate = fluid (融通) third delusive thinking:**[^7]

> "Your reflective thoughts drive your physical body; the body is not of the same kind as thinking — why then does your body, driven by thinking, take various forms? When mind arises, form is grasped; the two correspond. While awake it is the thinking mind; while asleep it becomes various dreams. Thus your thinking and reflection, agitating delusive feeling, are named the **third delusive thinking, called fluid**."

The argumentative structure is *the correspondence of unreal and real*: thoughts (unreal) can drive the physical body (real). This "unreal-driving-real" phenomenon can only be explained by the "fluidly fusing" nature of the perception aggregate — perception is the intermediate structure that fuses unreal and real into a single continuum.

**Formations aggregate = hidden (幽隱) fourth delusive thinking:**[^8]

> "The principle of transformation does not stay still; it shifts secretly moment by moment. Nails grow, hair sprouts, vital energy decays, the face wrinkles — day and night they alternate without your awareness. Ānanda! If this were not you, how could your body change? If it were truly you, how is it that you do not perceive it? Then your formations [*saṃskārāḥ*], moment after moment without ceasing, are named the **fourth delusive thinking, called hidden**."

The argumentative structure is *double interrogation*: if the formations aggregate is not you, why does your body undergo continuous change? If it is you, why are you unaware of this change? Both alternatives fail, demonstrating that the formations aggregate is delusive. "Hidden" means: the flux of formations is so subtle that it cannot be perceived without sustained meditative cultivation.

**Consciousness aggregate = vacuous and inverted (罔象虛無顛倒) fifth delusive thinking:**[^9]

> "Furthermore, the place where your refined brightness, settled and unmoving, is called permanent — within the body it does not exceed seeing, hearing, sensing, and knowing. If it were truly refined and real, it would not admit of habituated delusion. Yet why is it that, having seen some marvelous object years ago, after years and years with neither memory nor forgetting, when you suddenly see that very thing again, your memory of it is intact and not lost? Within this refined and unmoving brightness, moment after moment receives perfuming — what calculation can encompass this? Ānanda, you should know: this stillness is not real, like a swift current that appears tranquil to the eye — its swiftness is not seen, but it is not the absence of flow. If this were not the source of thinking, how could it receive deluded perfuming? Unless your six faculties open and merge in mutual functioning, this delusive thinking has no time of cessation. Therefore at this moment, in your seeing, hearing, sensing, and knowing, what is intermittently practiced becomes the **inverted fine-and-subtle refined thinking** — the **fifth, vacuous and inverted** within the still-and-clear inwardness."

The argumentative structure is *evidentiary refutation by memory*: if the consciousness aggregate (specifically the *ālaya-vijñāna*, eighth consciousness) were truly permanent and unchanging, it could not have the capacity to "receive deluded perfuming." But the empirical fact that one can recall, years later, a previously seen object — and this memory is intact — proves that the consciousness aggregate is "moment by moment perfumed," which is to say, in continuous flux. The "swift current" simile is one of the *Śūraṅgama-sūtra*'s most precise descriptions of the *ālaya-vijñāna*: surface appears tranquil; the actual flow is too swift to be perceived.

The five passages, taken together, exhibit a graded refinement of argument: the form aggregate is established by *concrete physical phenomena* (saliva from speaking of sourness); the feeling aggregate by *dual driving* (conforming/violating); the perception aggregate by *unreal-real correspondence* (thought-driving-body); the formations aggregate by *double interrogation* (neither-you-nor-not-you); the consciousness aggregate by *evidentiary refutation* (memory's persistence). The argumentative method becomes increasingly abstract and subtle, paralleling the increasing subtlety of the aggregates themselves.

#### 2.3 The Boundaries of the Five Aggregates

The Buddha next answers the third question — "what constitutes the boundary of each?":[^10]

> "Ānanda! These five received aggregates are constituted by five delusive thinkings. If you wish to know the depth and shallowness of their boundaries: only form and space are the boundary of form; only contact and separation are the boundary of feeling; only retention and forgetting are the boundary of perception; only ceasing and arising are the boundary of formations; immersed-stillness and conjoined-stillness — these belong to the boundary of consciousness."

This passage gives five **paired categories** as the boundaries of the five aggregates: form/space (色/空), contact/separation (觸/離), retention/forgetting (記/忘), ceasing/arising (滅/生), immersed-stillness/conjoined-stillness (湛入/合湛). These five pairs share a single structural form: each aggregate is defined not by a single term but by a pair of poles, and the entire range between (and including) the two poles constitutes the "boundary" of that aggregate.

This **two-pole structure** is the central insight of the boundary section. It implies that each aggregate is not a unitary category but a pair of mutually constituting opposites. The form aggregate is not merely "form" but the entire range of "form-and-space"; the feeling aggregate is not merely "contact" but the entire range of "contact-and-separation"; and so on. The practical implications of this insight will be developed in §3.3.

#### 2.4 The Structure of Sudden Principle and Gradual Phenomena, and the Closing Injunction

Finally, the Buddha addresses the second question — "are they extinguished simultaneously or sequentially?":[^11]

> "These five aggregates fundamentally arise in overlapping fashion: their birth occurs through consciousness, their extinction follows from form. **Principle is awakened all at once, and with that awakening all is dissolved together; phenomena cannot be removed all at once, but are exhausted in sequence**. I have already shown you the knots of the kalpa-cloth — what is still unclear, that you ask again? You should take this root of delusive thinking, having gained understanding of it, and **transmit it to the practitioners of the latter dharma age**, so that they may recognize delusion, develop deep aversion, know there is *nirvāṇa*, and not cling to the three realms."

This passage contains the doctrinal heart of the entire chapter and three of the most important phrases in the *Śūraṅgama-sūtra*'s soteriological vocabulary:

**(a) "Overlapping arising, born through consciousness, extinguished from form down"** (五陰元重疊生起,生因識有,滅從色除): the five aggregates arise in a layered manner, with consciousness as the cause and form as the outermost manifestation. Their extinction proceeds in the reverse direction — from outer (form) to inner (consciousness). This establishes the asymmetry between the directions of generation and dissolution.

**(b) "Principle is awakened all at once, and with that awakening all is dissolved together; phenomena cannot be removed all at once, but are exhausted in sequence"** (理則頓悟,乘悟併銷;事非頓除,因次第盡): this is the *Śūraṅgama-sūtra*'s formulation of the relationship between sudden insight and gradual cultivation. *Principle* (the realization that all five aggregates are delusive thinking) can be grasped instantly; *phenomena* (the actual extinction of the latent dispositions in the *ālaya-vijñāna*) must proceed in stages. This will be analyzed in detail in §3.5 as the doctrinal classification known as **"sudden principle and gradual phenomena"** (理頓事漸).

**(c) "Transmit it to the practitioners of the latter dharma age"** (傳示將來末法之中諸修行者): this closing injunction is the Buddha's explicit designation of the audience for this entire teaching. The recipients are not the assembled disciples but the meditators of *saddharma-vipralopa*, the latter dharma age — that is, those reading this passage centuries or millennia after its composition. This injunction will be picked up again and treated as the structural anchor of §6.3, where the present paper executes the *Śūraṅgama-sūtra*'s own command by addressing the contemporary reader directly.

#### 2.5 Avalokiteśvara's Perfect Penetration and the Knots of the Kalpa-Cloth

Two earlier passages must be cited here as essential context for §3.6, in which the structural identity between Avalokiteśvara's six-knot release (Fascicle Six) and the sequential exhaustion of the five aggregates (Fascicle Ten) is established.

The first is Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva's account, in Fascicle Six, of his own attainment of perfect penetration (*samāpatti*) through the ear-faculty:[^12]

> "I, recalling that countless eons ago a Buddha appeared in the world named Avalokiteśvara, with that Buddha gave rise to *bodhicitta*. That Buddha taught me to enter *samādhi* through hearing-contemplation-cultivation. **At first, in hearing, I entered the stream and lost the place where I entered. The place of entry being still, the two characteristics of motion and rest were both clearly not produced.** Thus gradually advancing, **the hearing and the heard were both exhausted. Not abiding in exhausted hearing, the awareness and the aware became empty. Emptiness and awareness becoming perfectly complete, the empty and the emptied were dissolved. Birth and ceasing being already ceased, quiescence appeared.** Suddenly I transcended the worldly and the supramundane, and the ten directions became perfectly bright."

This passage describes a seven-stage progression: (1) entering the stream and losing the place of entry; (2) the dual phenomena of motion and rest no longer arising; (3) hearing and the heard both exhausted; (4) not abiding in exhausted hearing — awareness and the aware becoming empty; (5) emptiness and awareness becoming perfectly complete — the empty and the emptied dissolved; (6) birth and ceasing already ceased, quiescence appearing; (7) sudden transcendence of the worldly and supramundane, ten directions becoming perfectly bright. This is what later commentators (most notably Jiāoguāng) refer to as the **"six knots, with the one also gone"** structure.

The second is the parable of the **kalpa-cloth knots** (劫波巾結), in which the Buddha demonstrates the principle of "release at the heart of the knot" through a physical demonstration with a cloth offered by deities of the Kalpa heaven:[^13]

> "The Buddha sat on the lion-throne … took the flower-cloth offered by the Kalpa-deva, and in front of the great assembly tied it into a single knot. He showed this to Ānanda, saying: 'What is this called?' Ānanda and the great assembly all said: 'This is called a knot.' Then the Tathāgata tied the cloth into successive knots, asking each time, until there were six knots in total. … The Buddha then said: 'Ānanda! The cloth is originally one. Through six tyings, six knots arise. Examine carefully: the substance of the cloth is the same; only through the knots is there difference.' … 'If you wish to remove the knot, **you must work at the heart of the knot**. … These knots arose in sequence by tying; today they must be released in sequence. Six knots share one substance, but the knots are not simultaneous — when releasing them, how can they be released simultaneously?' … 'The releasing of the six faculties is likewise. **When this faculty is first released, the emptiness of person is first attained. When the nature of emptiness is perfectly bright, the liberation from dharmas is achieved. When the liberation from dharmas is itself accomplished, both emptinesses no longer arise — this is what is called a bodhisattva attaining the patience of non-arising from samādhi.**'"

This parable establishes three critical principles: (a) the six faculties are constructed knots in a single substance, not separate substances; (b) release must proceed through "the heart of the knot," not by mere external pulling; (c) the knots must be released in the same sequence in which they were tied, but their final release leads to the "six released, the one also gone" state in which no enumeration remains. These three principles are structurally identical to the principles governing the sequential exhaustion of the five aggregates in Fascicle Ten — a parallel that Jiāoguāng's commentary (cited in §2.10) identifies as a "complete identity" (全同).

#### 2.6 Āgama Parallels: The Five Aggregates as the Fundamental Object of Meditation

The *Śūraṅgama-sūtra*'s treatment of the five delusive thinkings stands within a much older tradition of meditative analysis of the five aggregates, extending back to the early Buddhist *Āgama* literature. Three passages from the *Saṃyukta-āgama* (T0099) are essential reference points.

**First**, the foundational formula of "right contemplation" of the impermanence (*anitya*) of the five aggregates, from sūtras 1–2:[^14]

> "At one time the Buddha was at the Jeta Grove in Śrāvastī. The Bhagavān addressed the bhikṣus: '**Contemplate form as impermanent. One who contemplates thus has right contemplation. Right contemplation produces aversion; aversion exhausts delight and craving; the exhausting of delight and craving is called liberation of mind.** Contemplate feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness as impermanent in the same way. … As with impermanent, so also with suffering, empty, and non-self.'"

**Second**, sūtras 33–34, in which the Buddha teaches the formula of triple negation: not-self, not-mine, not-in-relation:[^15]

> "Form is not the self. If form were the self, sickness and suffering would not arise from form, and one could direct form to be thus or not thus. Because form is non-self, sickness and suffering arise in form, and one cannot direct form. … Therefore, all form whatsoever — past, future, present, internal, external, coarse, fine, fair, ugly, far, near — **all that is not-self, not-mine, not-in-relation**. Thus contemplate. The same is true of feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness. The well-instructed noble disciple, with respect to these five aggregates of clinging, sees them as not-self, not-mine. Contemplating thus, he does not grasp at any worldly thing; not grasping, he is not attached; not attached, he becomes self-awakened to *nirvāṇa*."

**Third**, sūtra 265, the famous five similes:[^16]

> "It is like the great river Ganges, surging in flood, gathering foam as it flows. A clear-eyed person carefully examines and analyzes — at the moment of careful examination, there is nothing there, nothing solid, nothing real, nothing firm. … Likewise all forms whatsoever, past, future, present … **Contemplate form as a mass of foam, feeling as a water bubble, perception as a heat-shimmer in spring, formations as a plantain trunk, consciousness as a magician's display.**"

These three *Āgama* passages establish three points that are presupposed by the *Śūraṅgama*'s "five delusive thinkings": (a) the five aggregates are the fundamental object of liberative contemplation; (b) the contemplation method is "right contemplation as not-self, not-mine, not-in-relation"; (c) each aggregate has a characteristic simile of insubstantiality (foam, bubble, mirage, plantain, magic). The *Śūraṅgama*'s reformulation as "five delusive thinkings" is not a replacement of this Āgama foundation but a re-organization of it for the purpose of providing a doctrinal framework for the Fifty Skandha-Māras.

### II.B Commentarial Sources (*śāstra-vyākhyā*)

#### 2.7 Zǐxuán's Commentary on the Five Delusive Thinkings

The Northern Song dynasty commentator **Zǐxuán** 子璿 (965–1038), in his *Śūraṅgama義疏注經* (T1799), provides the most precise exposition of each of the five delusive thinkings.[^17] His readings of the five are as follows:

**On the form aggregate as solid delusive thinking** (T1799, fascicle 9a): "The phrase 'transcending the kalpa-defilement' refers to: the kalpa-defilement is the substance of the form aggregate. The very first instant in which subject and object are established is the undifferentiated emptiness-and-seeing — this is called the kalpa-defilement. From nothing, suddenly there is being; that being is form. Now that the form aggregate is broken, the kalpa-defilement is therefore transcended. As for 'solid delusive thinking': enlightenment-bright firmly grasping, the resistance of substance becomes the body of form, hence 'solid.'"

**On the feeling aggregate as illusively bright delusive thinking** (T1799, fascicle 9b): "Because feeling is reception, taking and grasping arise consequently … When the feeling aggregate is gone, the seeing-defilement is transcended. The enlightenment-bright mind illusively pervades, receiving and admitting — hence 'illusively bright delusive thinking.'"

**On the perception aggregate as fluid delusive thinking** (T1799, fascicle 9b): "All afflictions take perception as their root, disturbing body and mind, polluting the true nature — hence called defilement. … The fluid delusive thinking: perception can fluidly transform; the body follows the mind. Thinking of sour fruit, saliva flows in the mouth — fluidly merging the resistance of substance, hence called fluid."

**On the formations aggregate as hidden delusive thinking** (T1799, fascicle 10a): "The arising-and-ceasing of formations is extremely subtle and difficult to perceive, hence called hidden."

**On the consciousness aggregate as vacuous-and-inverted delusive thinking** (T1799, fascicle 10a): "'Vacuous and inverted': this is the appearing-image phase of the very arising of enlightenment-brightness. Grasping at this delusive vacuous appearing-image as the substance of consciousness — this is the *karma-saṃskāra* and *vijñāna* phases [of the *ālaya-vijñāna*'s functioning]."

The unifying thread of Zǐxuán's commentary is the identification of each delusive thinking with a specific *mode of grasping*: solid thinking grasps resistant substance as body; illusively-bright thinking grasps reception as self; fluid thinking grasps the trans-domain merging of unreal and real as connection; hidden thinking grasps imperceptible flux as continuous self; vacuous-inverted thinking grasps the appearing-image at the very root of the *ālaya-vijñāna* as ultimate reality.

#### 2.8 Zǐxuán on the Boundaries and the Sudden-Gradual Structure

On the doctrinal heart of the chapter — "principle is awakened all at once, with that awakening all is dissolved together; phenomena cannot be removed all at once, but are exhausted in sequence" — Zǐxuán writes:[^18]

> "*Principle is awakened all at once*: if it concerns realizing the perfect principle, then 'one cut, all cut' — there is no before-and-after. … *With that awakening, all is dissolved together*: this is the dissolution of the inverted thoughts of countless eons. As when in dark night one mistakes a stump for a ghost, runs about wildly in confusion — once another person breaks open the misconception, the ghost-thought is entirely dissolved. … *Phenomena cannot be removed all at once*: 'phenomena' refers to the matters of breaking and cultivating; not removable all at once means cannot be removed in a single moment. *Exhausted in sequence*: one must proceed through the five aggregates' sequence and gradually remove them. Even though the ghost-thought is entirely dissolved, the path of the wild running cannot be reversed instantly; one must go back along the path traveled to return to the original place. **In sum: the meaning of sudden awakening with gradual cultivation** (頓悟漸修)."

This is a remarkably clear statement of the doctrinal structure. The dissolution of error at the level of *view* (the ghost-thought) is sudden, because the ghost was never really there — once the misperception is broken, there is nothing left to dissolve. But the dissolution of error at the level of *karmic momentum* (the path of wild running) is gradual, because that momentum is real even though its cause was illusory.

#### 2.9 Jiāoguāng on the Two-Pole Structure of the Aggregate Boundaries

The Ming dynasty commentator **Jiāoguāng Zhēnjiàn** 交光真鑑, in his *Śūraṅgama Zhèngmài Shū* (X0275), offers what is perhaps the single most penetrating reading of the boundary passage. Where earlier commentators read the five paired terms as descriptive markers, Jiāoguāng reads them as defining a *two-pole topology* in which exhausting only one pole leaves the meditator still inside that aggregate. His reading is structured as a sequence of five identical observations, one per aggregate:[^19]

> "*Form boundary*: in the form aggregate, the formed is form, the unformed is space. If one departs from all form-marks and rests the mind in pure emptiness — what the Chan school calls 'one side of form,' what Yogācāra calls 'space as a single revealed form' — one knows that exhausting form without exhausting space, all forbearance toward emptiness, is not yet ultimate.
>
> "*Feeling boundary*: in the feeling aggregate, attachment is contact, repugnance and abandonment is separation. If one severs all attachments but does not let go of repugnance and abandonment, one still abides in neutral feeling. Hence the Buddha, after teaching 'separation from illusion,' further teaches 'separation from separation' — exhausting contact without exhausting separation, all backturning and abandonment, is not yet ultimate.
>
> "*Perception boundary*: in the perception aggregate, having a thought is retention, having no thought is forgetting. If one removes all thoughts but does not forget no-thought, one still abides in the midst of still no-thought. Hence the Buddha says: 'Whether having or not having a thought, all return to delusion.' The patriarchs say: 'Do not say no-mind is the Way — no-mind is still one barrier away.' Exhausting retention without exhausting forgetting is not yet ultimate.
>
> "*Formations boundary*: in the formations aggregate, with confused-position scattered-mind coarse formations as the arising-mark (as in Fascicle 2's simile of the rushing torrent), and with cultivation-position concentrated-mind fine formations as the ceasing-mark (as in this fascicle's simile of the wild horse [mirage]). Yet these fine formations, seeming-ceased but not ceased, are still subtle disturbance and fine flux. Like a person in *samādhi*, still subject to the growth of nails and hair. Exhausting arising without exhausting ceasing is not yet ultimate.
>
> "*Consciousness boundary*: in the consciousness aggregate, having entry is immersed-stillness; having no entry is conjoined-stillness. … Exhausting immersed-stillness without exhausting conjoined-stillness is not yet ultimate. **All 'clear and bright dharma-body' remains short of the ultimate.**"

The closing line — "all clear and bright dharma-body remains short of the ultimate" (一切明白法身,俱未究竟) — is one of the most arresting verdicts in the entire commentarial tradition. It places even the highest meditative attainments short of the *Śūraṅgama-sūtra*'s final goal, refusing to allow any specific realization to claim the title of "ultimate." This single line is the heart of the *Śūraṅgama*'s soteriological severity, and it will be returned to in §6.2.

#### 2.10 Jiāoguāng's "Complete Identity" Thesis

The most structurally significant of Jiāoguāng's contributions, however, is his thesis that **Avalokiteśvara's six-knot release in Fascicle Six is "completely identical" (全同) to the breaking of the five aggregates and entering the first abiding (初住) in Fascicle Ten**:[^20]

> "Investigating the principle, one should know clearly that **Avalokiteśvara's previous releasing of the six knots and entering perfect penetration is completely identical to the present breaking of the five aggregates and entering the first abiding** — only the dharma-numbers, six previously and five here, differ in their grouping and division, making detailed correspondence difficult, hence I roughly distinguish two segments. From form-and-space through immersed-stillness covers the previous three knots; only the two characters of conjoined-stillness cover the previous latter three knots."

This single sentence accomplishes a remarkable structural unification: the entire architecture of cultivation laid out in the fifty-māra map is shown to be the same architecture as the perfect-penetration practice of Avalokiteśvara taught in Fascicle Six. The two presentations differ in vocabulary (knots vs. aggregates), in narrative voice (the bodhisattva's first-person account vs. the Buddha's third-person exposition), and in numerical structure (six vs. five) — but the underlying topology of awakening is identical. This thesis will be developed in detail in §3.6.

## III. Core Arguments

### 3.1 The Structural Significance of "All Are Delusive Thinking" — A Three-Tier Collapse

The entire interpretive enterprise of the present paper rests on a single observation: the Buddha's reply to Ānanda's renewed question collapses the fifty phenomena of the Fifty Skandha-Māras into a structure of five, and then collapses that five into a single principle. This three-tier collapse — **fifty phenomena → five structures → one principle** — is not merely a stylistic compression. It is a doctrinal claim about the architecture of meditative phenomenology.

The claim has three components.

The **first component** is that the fifty phenomena are not independent objects. Each of the ten phenomena within each aggregate, and each of the five aggregates considered as a class, are not isolated units that must be separately learned and separately defended against. They are *manifestations* — different surface-forms of the same underlying error. To learn the fifty phenomena as fifty separate items is to miss the point of their grouping.

The **second component** is that the five aggregates correspond to five distinct *kinds* or *modes* of delusive thinking. The Buddha does not say merely "the five aggregates are all delusion" (which would be the standard *Āgama* formulation). He says "the five aggregates are five delusive thinkings" (五受陰,五妄想成) — that is, five differentiated *types* of the same underlying activity. Each type has its own structural mark: solidity, illusory brightness, fluidity, hiddenness, vacuity-and-inversion. The differentiation is not arbitrary; it tracks the increasing subtlety of the cognitive object that the deluded mind is grasping at.

The **third component** is that beneath the five differentiated types lies a single principle — *delusive thinking itself*. This principle is what makes the five types comparable, what makes them members of a single series, and what makes the remedial principle ("do not form a holy interpretation") applicable across all of them. Without this single underlying principle, the five aggregates would be five disconnected meditative objects, and the fifty phenomena would have no internal coherence.

The reader who can hold all three components simultaneously possesses the entire fifty-māra map. Reading only the fifty phenomena yields a long catalogue with no organizing principle; reading only the conclusion ("all are delusive thinking") yields a slogan with no specifying content. **It is the holding of all three layers at once that constitutes mastery of this teaching** — and providing the conceptual scaffolding for that simultaneous holding is the central task of the present paper.

### 3.2 ⭐ The Refinement Gradient of the Five Delusive Thinkings

Original structural contribution. This section presents the five delusive thinkings as a gradient ordered by an internal parameter — *the ratio of unreal to real in the cognitive object* — and shows that this gradient is not a doctrinal stipulation but a phenomenological observation that the Buddha is reporting.

Consider the five thinkings in order:

| Aggregate | Delusive Thinking | Cognitive Object | Unreal:Real Ratio | Mode of Grasping |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Form (*rūpa*) | Solid (堅固) | Concrete physical body | Lowest unreal proportion | Resistance taken as substance |
| Feeling (*vedanā*) | Illusively bright (虛明) | Pleasant/unpleasant qualia | Low | Reception taken as self |
| Perception (*saṃjñā*) | Fluid (融通) | Mental images and concepts | Medium | Trans-domain merging taken as continuity |
| Formations (*saṃskāra*) | Hidden (幽隱) | The flux of mental processes | High | Imperceptible flow taken as continuous self |
| Consciousness (*vijñāna*) | Vacuous and inverted (罔象虛無顛倒) | The "swift current" of *ālaya-vijñāna* | Highest unreal proportion | Apparent stillness taken as ultimate reality |

The internal parameter is straightforward: as one moves from form to consciousness, the cognitive object becomes increasingly *removed from gross sensory verifiability* and increasingly *internal*. The form aggregate has the highest proportion of "real" content (a body can be touched, seen, weighed); the consciousness aggregate has the lowest (the *ālaya-vijñāna* cannot be directly perceived even by the meditator who has crossed the previous four aggregates — its motion can only be inferred from the persistence of memory across years, as the Buddha demonstrates in §2.2's evidentiary refutation).

But — and this is the *Śūraṅgama-sūtra*'s decisive insight — the phenomenological *force* of the grasping does not decrease as the unreal proportion increases. On the contrary, it *increases*. The grasping at the form aggregate as solid is comparatively easy to dissolve; once one understands that the body is "thinking-condensed-into-substance," its hold on the meditator weakens. The grasping at the consciousness aggregate as ultimate reality is extraordinarily difficult to dissolve, precisely because the consciousness aggregate looks like ultimate reality. Jiāoguāng's verdict is uncompromising: "the swift current looks tranquil; ordinary persons and the two vehicles do not perceive it at all; even those before the tenth ground perceive but cannot exhaust it." The most subtle delusive thinking is the most dangerous, because it most closely resembles its opposite.

This produces a two-axis structure: as one moves through the five aggregates, the cognitive object becomes *less real* (lower in solidity, more illusory) but the grasping becomes *more dangerous* (harder to detect, more closely resembling realization). The *Śūraṅgama-sūtra*'s presentation of the fifty māras tracks both axes simultaneously: the form-aggregate māras involve relatively crude phenomena (light-displays, paranormal experiences) that any disciplined teacher can identify; the consciousness-aggregate māras involve extremely subtle realizations (the "permanent self," "the absolute beginning") that even highly accomplished meditators can mistake for ultimate awakening.

The refinement gradient is therefore not a hierarchy of value (consciousness is not "more important" than form), nor a hierarchy of progress (the meditator does not graduate from form to consciousness as if from one level of school to the next). It is a *typology of grasping* — a systematic catalogue of the different ways in which the meditative mind can mistake an intermediate object for the ultimate. Reading the five aggregates this way unifies the *Śūraṅgama-sūtra*'s presentation with the broader Buddhist tradition's analysis of *avidyā* (ignorance): ignorance is not a single error but a family of structurally related errors, each appropriate to a different level of cognitive subtlety.

### 3.3 The Two-Pole Structure of Each Aggregate's Boundary

This section develops Jiāoguāng's reading of the five paired boundary terms (form/space, contact/separation, retention/forgetting, ceasing/arising, immersed-stillness/conjoined-stillness) as defining a *two-pole topology*, and draws out its implications for meditative practice.

Jiāoguāng's central observation is that each aggregate is bounded not by a single term but by a pair of mutually constituting opposites. The form aggregate is not bounded by "form" alone, but by the entire range stretching from "form" (occupied space) to "space" (unoccupied space) — and exhausting only one of these poles leaves the meditator still inside the form aggregate. The form-aggregate practitioner who breaks attachment to physical objects but still rests in pure emptiness has not yet exited the form aggregate; he has merely moved from one of its poles to the other.

Why is this insight important? Because it diagnoses an extremely common error in meditative practice: the meditator who has broken one mode of clinging within an aggregate believes he has exited that aggregate, when in fact he has only moved to the opposite pole within the same aggregate.

Consider the feeling aggregate. The meditator who has dissolved attachment to pleasant feelings (broken "contact") but who still experiences a strong sense of equanimity defined as the *absence* of attachment has not exited the feeling aggregate; he has moved from "contact" to "separation," but separation is itself defined within the feeling aggregate's two-pole structure. True exit from the feeling aggregate requires that *both* contact and separation cease to function as defining poles.

Or consider the perception aggregate. The meditator who has dissolved discursive thinking (broken "retention") but who rests in a state of "no-thought" (forgetting) has not exited the perception aggregate. Both retention and forgetting are poles of the same aggregate. The patriarchs' famous saying — "Do not call no-mind the Way; no-mind is still one barrier away" — is precisely this point. To exit the perception aggregate, one must transcend both having-thoughts and not-having-thoughts as defining categories.

The same logic applies to the formations aggregate (arising/ceasing) and the consciousness aggregate (immersed-stillness/conjoined-stillness). In each case, there are two opposing modes that look like opposites but are in fact two sides of the same coin — both are constituted by the aggregate's own internal grammar, and exhausting one without the other leaves the practitioner inside the aggregate.

The two-pole structure also explains why the *Śūraṅgama-sūtra*'s presentation of the fifty māras is symmetrically organized. Many of the māras within each aggregate come in pairs of opposites: a positive grasping and a negative grasping, an attached state and a detached state, a presence-state and an absence-state. These pairs are not coincidental; they reflect the two-pole architecture of the aggregate itself. To learn the fifty māras as fifty independent traps is to miss this architecture. To learn them as paired oscillations within a five-fold two-pole structure is to grasp the *Śūraṅgama-sūtra*'s actual teaching.

The practical upshot is severe: **at every moment of meditative practice, ask not only "have I let go of this clinging?" but also "have I taken refuge in the opposite of what I let go of?"** Letting go of attachment can become attachment to non-attachment; letting go of thought can become attachment to no-thought; letting go of arising can become attachment to ceasing. The two-pole structure forces the meditator to recognize that real exit from any aggregate requires the dissolution of *both* its defining poles, not the migration from one pole to the other.

### 3.4 ⭐ The Five-Stage Genealogy of "Do Not Form a Holy Interpretation"

Original structural contribution. This section gathers the remedial principles developed across S6 papers P08–P12 into a single refinement genealogy and demonstrates that they form a coherent series.

The *Śūraṅgama-sūtra*, at the head of each aggregate's section in the Fifty Māras, repeats a single general principle: "**If one forms a holy interpretation, one immediately receives all the demonic hosts; if one does not form a holy interpretation, this is called a wholesome state**" (若作聖解,即受群邪;不作聖心,名善境界). The principle is repeated word-for-word across all five aggregates. This repetition is not redundancy — it is structural marking. The Buddha is indicating that the remedial principle is *single*, even though the cognitive objects to be remedied are five.

But this single principle has different concrete *forms* in each aggregate, because the cognitive objects become progressively subtler. P08 through P12 of the present series have unfolded these five concrete forms; §3.4 of P13 collapses them into a single genealogy.

**Form aggregate: do not grasp light-marks** (P08)

The ten phenomena of the form aggregate involve concrete meditative experiences: the body becoming able to pass through obstructions, internal vision of bodily organs, hearing the dharma in space, seeing the Buddhas appear, the ten directions appearing as seven jewels. These are all *concrete light-displays* (*ālokā*) and *manifestations* arising during deep concentration. The form aggregate's cognitive object is what I call *unreal-cause-real-effect* (虛因實果): the cause (the meditative concentration) is unreal in the sense of being a mental construction, but the effect (the visual/sensory display) appears with full concrete vividness.

The remedial form of "do not form a holy interpretation" at this level is **"do not grasp at light-marks"** (不取光相): do not take these concrete displays as evidence of attainment. They are a side-effect of meditative concentration's ability to refine perception — they are signs that the form aggregate is being penetrated, not signs that the goal has been reached. The meditator who learns to let these displays come and go without grasping them passes through the form-aggregate phase without being captured by its characteristic māras.

**Feeling aggregate: do not grasp at attachments — the second arrow** (P09)

The ten phenomena of the feeling aggregate involve intense emotional states: limitless sorrow, excessive ardor, great spiritual pride, deep tranquility and joy, various craving-states. These are all *strong feelings* arising during meditative practice. The feeling aggregate's cognitive object is the dual driving of conformity and violation — emotions have no substance of their own but consist of the oscillating drive between "what helps me" and "what hurts me."

The remedial form here is **"do not grasp at attachments"** (不取著). The defining metaphor of P09 is the *second arrow* of *Saṃyukta-āgama* sūtra 470: the first arrow is the bodily pain that any sentient being cannot avoid; the second arrow is the psychological reaction to the first arrow (anger, regret, self-pity), and *this* second arrow is the one that can be avoided. "Do not grasp at attachments" means: do not shoot the second arrow. Let the first arrow (the original feeling) arise naturally, but do not add a second-order reaction to it. The feeling-aggregate practitioner who masters this passes through the feeling-aggregate māras without being captured by them.

**Perception aggregate: seek the great teacher** (P10)

The ten phenomena of the perception aggregate involve states in which the meditator seeks something *outside himself* — seeking ingenious teachings, seeking travels, seeking accord with hidden powers, seeking knowledge of past lives, seeking quietude. These are all *projections onto external others*. The perception aggregate's cognitive object is the *intermediate fusion* of inner and outer.

The remedial form here is **"seek the great teacher"** (求大師) — actively seek out an authentic *kalyāṇamitra* (true spiritual friend) who can serve as the proper landing-point for the meditator's outward-projecting tendency. P10 argues at length that the projecting tendency itself is not the error; it is a phase of meditative development that cannot be eliminated. The error is when the projection lands on a false target (a self-claimed sage, a possessing demon, a charismatic teacher without realization). Seeking the great teacher gives the projection a correct landing-point, channeling it productively rather than attempting to suppress it.

**Formations aggregate: do not form a holy interpretation — the mirror-student** (P11)

The ten phenomena of the formations aggregate involve doctrinal misjudgments: the two-causeless-views, the four-permanence-views, the four-partial-permanence-views, the four-finite-views, the various views of inverted-and-undying. These are all *philosophical verdicts* that the meditator forms about the flow of formations he has begun to perceive. The formations aggregate's cognitive object is the *bare flow itself* — the meditator has reached a level of perceptual subtlety at which he can see the moment-by-moment arising and ceasing of mental processes, and the temptation is to form a final philosophical verdict about what this flow means.

The remedial form here is **"do not form a holy interpretation"** (不作聖解) — keep verdicts at the level of *description*, do not allow them to escalate into *conclusions*. P11's central argument is that the formations-aggregate practitioner has acquired the *capacity* to make philosophical judgments about meditative phenomena, and this capacity is not the error; the error is mistaking philosophical capacity for ultimate insight. "Do not form a holy interpretation" allows the flow to continue flowing without being captured by any verdict.

**Consciousness aggregate: do not establish supreme understanding, turn the mind toward the great** (P12)

The ten phenomena of the consciousness aggregate involve "final graspings" at extremely subtle objects: the *cause-non-cause* grasping, the *able-non-able* grasping, the *permanent-non-permanent* grasping, and so on through the eighth grasping, followed by the ninth and tenth — *fixed-nature śrāvaka* and *fixed-nature pratyekabuddha*. P12 argues that these ten graspings have a two-segment topological structure: the first eight are graspings at the *nature of subtle objects* (corresponding to non-Buddhist meditative traditions), and the last two are graspings at the *height of attainment* (corresponding to the two vehicles).

The remedial form here is twofold: **"do not establish supreme understanding"** (不立勝解) for the first eight (do not form an ultimate verdict about any subtle object that resembles "the one and the permanent"); and **"turn the mind toward the great"** (迴心向大) for the last two (do not let a real intermediate attainment be mistaken for the final goal). This is the most refined form of the remedial principle, because the cognitive objects at this stage are most subtle and most closely resemble the goal.

**The internal logic of the five-stage genealogy:**

Arranging these five remedial forms in sequence reveals a clear refinement vector:

| Aggregate | Remedial Form | Object Type | Internalization |
|---|---|---|---|
| Form | Do not grasp light-marks | Concrete experience | External (toward object) |
| Feeling | Do not grasp attachments | Strong emotion | Semi-external (toward feeling) |
| Perception | Seek the great teacher | Projection onto other | Mediational (toward other) |
| Formations | Do not form a holy interpretation | The flow itself | Reflexive (toward one's own verdict) |
| Consciousness | Do not establish supreme understanding + turn the mind | Subtle object resembling realization | Two-pillar (toward an unfinished position) |

Three structural features stand out. **First**, the *internalization of the remedy increases stage by stage*: from form-aggregate's correction of grasping at external objects to consciousness-aggregate's correction of grasping at one's own unfinished position. The remedy moves progressively closer to the meditator himself. **Second**, the *subtlety of the formulation increases stage by stage*: from "do not grasp" (form) to "do not grasp at attachments" (feeling) to "seek the great teacher" (perception) to "do not form a holy interpretation" (formations) to "do not establish supreme understanding" (consciousness). The formulations become progressively closer to the ultimate principle of "not establishing any position whatsoever." **Third**, the *root principle remains invariant*: regardless of how the formulation is refined, the underlying principle is always "do not form a holy interpretation" — do not establish any meditative position as ultimate.

This genealogy is the inner spine of the entire S6 series from P08 to P13. Each of the five intervening papers corresponds to one stage of the genealogy. P13's structural contribution is to gather these five into a single coherent series and demonstrate that their endpoint is the most refined possible expression of the single principle that has been operating throughout.

### 3.5 ⭐ The Formal Structure of "Sudden Principle, Gradual Phenomena"

Original structural contribution. The *Śūraṅgama-sūtra*'s answer to the question "are the five aggregates extinguished simultaneously or sequentially?" is neither pure subitism (*dùnjiào* 頓教, in which both principle and phenomena are sudden) nor pure gradualism (*jiànjiào* 漸教, in which both principle and phenomena are gradual). It is a two-layer structure called **"sudden principle and gradual phenomena"** (理頓事漸). This section formalizes the structure and demonstrates its doctrinal significance.

The first layer — "**principle is awakened all at once, with that awakening all is dissolved together**" — has two components.

The first component is *principle can be awakened suddenly*. The view-level realization that "the five aggregates are all delusive thinking" requires no sequence; it can occur in a single moment of insight. Zǐxuán, citing the *Yuánjué Jīng*'s formula "knowing illusion is itself separation from illusion; separation from illusion is itself awakening — there is no further gradation," supports this point. The action of "knowing" the illusion is itself the action of "separating from" it; there is no need to perform a separate "separation" action after the "knowing" has occurred. The dharma-eye opens at the speed of recognition.

The second component is *awakening can dissolve delusion*. Once the view is opened, the "inverted thinking of countless eons" can be dissolved instantly along with it. Zǐxuán's *ghost-thought* simile makes this vivid: at night one sees a tree-stump and mistakes it for a ghost, runs about in panic; once another person says "that's not a ghost, just a stump," the ghost-thought is entirely dissolved — there is no need to dissolve each detail of the imagined ghost separately. The opening of view has the power of "one cut, all cut" (一斷一切斷).

The second layer — "**phenomena cannot be removed all at once, but are exhausted in sequence**" — also has two components.

The first component is *phenomena cannot be removed suddenly*. The actual elimination of the five aggregates as functioning structures cannot occur in a single moment; it must proceed in sequence. Why? Because the five aggregates, although unified at the view level as "all delusive thinking," operate at the phenomenal level with the structure of *overlapping arising* — the consciousness aggregate is the cause of the form aggregate, the form aggregate is the manifest result of the consciousness aggregate. To dismantle this structure, one must begin from the result-end (form) and proceed back toward the cause-end (consciousness). Zǐxuán uses the similes of *sunrise* and *child-growth*: at sunrise the light gradually expands, not instantaneously to noon; the child gradually grows, not instantaneously to adulthood. These are *natural gradations*, not stages constructed by human convention.

The second component is *the sequence has a direction*. "Extinguished from form down" — that is, from coarse to subtle, from outer to inner. This direction is *opposite* to the direction of arising: arising is from consciousness (innermost, subtlest) outward to form (outermost, coarsest); extinction is from form (outer) inward to consciousness (inner). Zǐxuán's images of *putting on clothes* (from inner garments outward) and *taking off clothes* (from outer garments inward) capture this asymmetry exactly.

The doctrinal significance of "sudden principle, gradual phenomena" lies in three points.

**First, it reformulates the sudden-vs-gradual debate as a two-layer relation rather than an either-or choice.** The traditional doctrinal classification placed subitism and gradualism as opposing schools, each claiming the true Buddhist path. The *Śūraṅgama-sūtra*'s position is neither: it distributes subitism and gradualism to *different layers* of the same path. View-layer is sudden; phenomenon-layer is gradual. This is not eclecticism or compromise; it is a re-statement of the question itself. Suddenness and gradualness are not two competing paths; they are two aspects of the same path operating at different levels of analysis.

**Second, it strictly separates "the opening of view" from "the exhaustion of seeds."** View-opening can be sudden because views are about doctrinal recognition, and recognition has no quantity. Seed-exhaustion must be gradual because *seeds* (*bīja* — the latent dispositions stored in the *ālaya-vijñāna*) have quantity, layering, and accumulated momentum. To conflate these two is one of the most common errors in meditative doctrinal classification: conflating sudden view-opening with sudden seed-exhaustion produces *premature self-certification* (the meditator who has had a glimpse of insight believes he has finished the path); conflating gradual seed-exhaustion with gradual view-opening produces *denial of sudden insight* (the school that requires kalpas of preparation before any view-realization is possible). The "sudden principle, gradual phenomena" structure prevents both errors simultaneously.

**Third, it provides the structural slot for the Fifty Skandha-Māras.** All fifty māras belong to the *gradual-phenomena* layer — they are obstacles in the seed-exhaustion process, not errors in the view-realization. This distinction is decisive, because it prevents misdiagnosing the māras as "view errors." A meditator may have entirely correct view (principle-layer is fully open) and still encounter the māras — this is not a contradiction but the necessary consequence of the "sudden principle, gradual phenomena" structure. View can be open while seeds are still being exhausted; in fact, this is the *normal* state of an advanced practitioner. The māras are not signs of view-failure but of view-grounded gradual progress.

This insight reverses the common assumption that the appearance of māras indicates meditative failure. The opposite is true: **the appearance of māras is evidence of meditative progress**. Only meditators whose view has opened and who have begun the gradual seed-exhaustion can possibly encounter the māras; the meditator who is still at the beginning of the form-aggregate has not yet begun to move and therefore has nothing to encounter. The māras are evidence that one is "on the path," not evidence of failure on the path. The remedy for the māras is therefore not "stop entering this path" but "do not stop at any position along the path" — which is precisely the "do not form a holy interpretation" principle in its phenomenon-layer specification.

### 3.6 ⭐ Avalokiteśvara's Six-Knot Release as Structurally Identical to the Sequential Exhaustion of the Five Aggregates

Original structural contribution. This section develops Jiāoguāng's thesis (cited in §2.10) that the practice of perfect penetration through the ear-faculty taught by Avalokiteśvara in Fascicle Six is "completely identical" (全同) to the breaking of the five aggregates and entering the first abiding taught in Fascicle Ten.

Jiāoguāng's claim is structurally remarkable. He is asserting that two passages of the *Śūraṅgama-sūtra* — separated by four fascicles, presented in entirely different rhetorical modes (one a first-person account by a bodhisattva, the other a third-person exposition by the Buddha), and using different numerical structures (six knots vs. five aggregates) — are *the same teaching* in two different forms. If this claim is correct, it accomplishes a significant unification: the entire fifty-māra map of Fascicle Ten can be read as a *systematic phenomenology of what Avalokiteśvara describes briefly* in Fascicle Six. The earlier passage gives the destination; the later passage gives the detailed map.

The mapping between the two structures is as follows.

| Avalokiteśvara's Six Knots (Fascicle 6) | Five-Aggregate Exhaustion (Fascicle 10) | Topological Function |
|---|---|---|
| (1) Entering the stream and losing the place of entry | Form-aggregate exhaustion | Crossing from outer object to subject |
| (2) Motion-and-rest both unproduced | Feeling-aggregate exhaustion | Dissolution of dual driving |
| (3) Hearing-and-the-heard both exhausted | Perception-aggregate exhaustion | Dissolution of subject-object correspondence |
| (4) Awareness-and-aware emptied | Formations-aggregate exhaustion | Dissolution of reflexive flow |
| (5) Empty-and-emptied dissolved | Consciousness-aggregate exhaustion | Dissolution of the final stillness |
| (6) Birth-and-ceasing already ceased; quiescence appears | The "six released, one also gone" final transition | Topological closure |

Jiāoguāng's own grouping is slightly different (he attributes the first three knots to form-through-immersed-stillness and the last three to conjoined-stillness alone), and his phrasing is "rough division." The detailed correspondence is not the point; the point is that *both presentations describe the same six-stage topology* — a sequential dissolution from outer to inner, from coarse to subtle, from multi-pole to single-pole to no-pole.

Three structural features confirm the identity.

**First, both presentations are sequential and irreversible.** Avalokiteśvara explicitly describes his progression as "gradually advancing" (如是漸增); the Buddha explicitly says the five aggregates are "exhausted in sequence" (因次第盡). The kalpa-cloth parable (cited in §2.5) provides the underlying logic: knots that are tied in sequence must be released in sequence; one cannot bypass the order. Both presentations therefore presuppose the same temporal asymmetry between binding and release.

**Second, both presentations identify the same crucial release-mechanism — "release at the heart of the knot."** Avalokiteśvara's description repeatedly returns to the word *exhausted* (盡): "hearing and the heard both exhausted," "empty-and-emptied dissolved." The Buddha's kalpa-cloth parable explicitly answers Ānanda's question about how to release a knot: not by pulling left or right, but "**at the heart of the knot**" (當於結心). Both presentations agree that the release-mechanism is internal to each stage, not applied from outside.

**Third, both presentations terminate in the same state — "six released, one also gone" (六解一亦亡).** Avalokiteśvara's final stage is "suddenly transcending the worldly and supramundane, the ten directions becoming perfectly bright" (忽然超越世出世間,十方圓明). The Buddha's final stage is the entry into "the first abiding, witnessing perfect penetration" (入初住證圓通). Both terminate in a state in which the *enumeration itself disappears* — the very framework of "this many stages" no longer applies. This terminal state is not "the sixth stage" or "the fifth aggregate" but the dissolution of the framework that made these counts possible.

The implications of Jiāoguāng's "complete identity" are significant for how the *Śūraṅgama-sūtra* should be read. The sūtra is not a collection of disparate teachings loosely stitched together; it has a deep architecture in which the early fascicles (the practice doctrines) and the late fascicles (the māras and their remedies) describe the same structure from different angles. **The early fascicles present the structure from the inside** — the practitioner's first-person account of how the path unfolds. **The late fascicles present the structure from the outside** — the systematic catalogue of every way the path can be misread along the way. Both presentations are necessary, and reading them as a unified structure is the only way to grasp what the *Śūraṅgama-sūtra* is doing.

This structural identity also illuminates why the *Śūraṅgama-sūtra* devotes so much space (the entirety of Fascicle Ten and the latter half of Fascicle Nine) to a teaching that, on the surface, seems redundant after Avalokiteśvara has already described the path. The redundancy is only apparent. Avalokiteśvara describes *what success looks like*; the Fifty Māras describe *what the failure modes look like at every step of the success*. Without the Fifty Māras, Avalokiteśvara's account would be inspiring but useless for actual practice — the practitioner would have no way to recognize when he has gone off track. With the Fifty Māras, every step of Avalokiteśvara's account is annotated with the specific errors that can occur at that step. The two together constitute the *Śūraṅgama-sūtra*'s complete teaching: the destination plus the map of the path's pitfalls.

### 3.7 From "Do Not Establish Any Ultimate Position" to "There Is Only One Ultimate Position": The S6→S7 Bridge

The closing section of §III addresses the question that any reader of the Fifty Skandha-Māras eventually asks: if every meditative position is to be refused as ultimate, where is the meditator supposed to go? Is the path nothing but perpetual negation?

The *Śūraṅgama-sūtra* itself gives a partial answer through its description of the post-māra trajectory: "**when the consciousness aggregate is exhausted, the present six faculties function in mutual interchange. From mutual interchange, one is able to enter the bodhisattva's vajra-dry wisdom**" (識陰若盡,則汝現前諸根互用。從互用中,能入菩薩金剛乾慧). Jiāoguāng's commentary identifies these phrases with the entry into the first abiding (初住) and the subsequent rapid traversal of the bodhisattva path to the position of "vajra-dry wisdom" preceding wonderful enlightenment (妙覺後心). The post-māra trajectory has two segments: from satisfied contemplation to first abiding (one-leap, no intermediate stops), and from first abiding to equal enlightenment (one-leap, no intermediate stops). Jiāoguāng's verdict — "**throughout, there is no taking-of-attestation in the middle**" (中間更不取證) — extends the "do not form a holy interpretation" principle from the fifty-māra phase to the entirety of the fifty-five positions of the bodhisattva path.

But this extension does not by itself answer the deeper question. If "no intermediate position is to be taken as ultimate" is the principle that runs through the fifty-five positions, what about the ultimate position itself — the position of wonderful enlightenment (*samyak-saṃbodhi*)? Is wonderful enlightenment also a position to be refused under the rubric of "do not form a holy interpretation"? If so, the entire path collapses into infinite regress; if not, the principle has an exception that the *Śūraṅgama-sūtra* does not explain.

The *Śūraṅgama-sūtra*'s answer to this question is structural rather than positive: wonderful enlightenment is not an exception to "do not form a holy interpretation" because it is *not a position* in the sense in which the intermediate stages are positions. The intermediate positions are *objects of grasping*; wonderful enlightenment is *the dissolution of grasping itself*. To "form a holy interpretation" of wonderful enlightenment would mean to *take it as an object*, which is precisely what wonderful enlightenment refuses. The principle does not need to be applied to wonderful enlightenment because wonderful enlightenment is not the kind of thing to which the principle applies.

But this answer remains negative: it tells us why "do not form a holy interpretation" does not need to be applied to the goal, but it does not give us a positive specification of the goal. The positive specification — what the goal *is*, rather than what it is not — is left for later sūtras to develop.

The sūtra that takes up this task is the *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka* (法華經), the *Lotus Sūtra*. The *Lotus*'s answer to "what is the unique ultimate position?" takes two structural forms.

The first form is **"opening the provisional, revealing the real"** (開權顯實): the three vehicles previously taught (*śrāvaka*, *pratyekabuddha*, *bodhisattva*) are *upāya* — skillful means — and the actual reality is *one vehicle only* (*ekayāna*, the One Buddha Vehicle). The three vehicles are "provisional" (*權*) — temporary, methodological; the one vehicle is "real" (*實*) — true, ultimate. All skillful means point toward the same ultimate, and that ultimate is *unique*.

The second form is the parable of the **conjured city and the treasure-place** (化城寶所, *Lotus Sūtra* fascicle 3): a guide leading a tired group of travelers conjures a city for them to rest in midway; they believe they have reached their destination; after they have rested, the guide tells them "this is only a conjured city — the real treasure-place is still ahead," and the conjured city vanishes, and they continue on. The conjured city is *upāya*; the treasure-place is real; and the treasure-place is *unique*.

The *Lotus*'s two forms are, on the surface, in tension with the *Śūraṅgama*'s "do not form a holy interpretation" principle: the *Lotus* affirms a unique ultimate position (the treasure-place), whereas the *Śūraṅgama* refuses every intermediate ultimate position (the conjured cities). But this tension is only apparent. **The two are two unfoldings of the same structure.** The *Śūraṅgama* speaks from the side of "every intermediate position is not the ultimate" (negation of the intermediate); the *Lotus* speaks from the side of "there is only one ultimate position" (affirmation of the terminal). Taken together, they form the complete teaching: **all intermediate conjured cities are not the ultimate, but there is one real treasure-place**.

The structural identity has four correspondence points. **First**, the conjured cities of the *Lotus* correspond to the fifty māras of the *Śūraṅgama* — each māra is a "conjured city" that arises during the actual meditative process and looks like the destination. **Second**, the guide's reminder that "the real treasure-place is still ahead" corresponds to "do not form a holy interpretation" — both are the same act of pulling the practitioner past the false destination. **Third**, the unique treasure-place corresponds to *samyak-saṃbodhi* — both *Lotus* and *Śūraṅgama* converge on the same terminal state, defined as "the position that is not a position" or "the dissolution of the framework of positions." **Fourth**, the disappearance of the conjured city corresponds to "the exhaustion of an aggregate" — when the meditator actually crosses an aggregate, the entire phenomenology of that aggregate (including its māras) loses its position-like character and becomes pure *upāya*.

P13's argument here serves as the formal preparation for the transition from the S6 *Śūraṅgama* series to the S7 *Lotus* series. S6 uses *meditative phenomenology* to establish "no intermediate position is the ultimate"; S7 will use *narrative structure* to establish "there is only one ultimate position." Together, the two series constitute the complete teaching of "the map of cultivation plus the direction of the ultimate." Without S6, readers would stop at the first conjured city; without S7, they would not know in which direction the treasure-place lies.

P13 is the formal construction of this bridge. From the close of P13 onward, the S6 series is formally complete, and the S7 series formally begins.

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Resonances

This section presents two structural analogies from contemporary scientific and mathematical traditions that illuminate aspects of the five delusive thinkings and the asymmetry between aggregate-arising and aggregate-extinction. Both analogies are explicitly marked as ⚠️ *heuristic* (启发性), not *reductive* (理论等价). Any attempt to reduce the *Śūraṅgama-sūtra*'s meditative phenomenology to claims of contemporary science would miss the teaching's basic intent.

### 4.1 ⚠️ Premature Convergence and Local Optima — The Formal Description of the Fifty Māras

In optimization theory and machine learning, two closely related concepts describe a common failure mode of search algorithms: **premature convergence** and **local optima**. *Premature convergence* refers to the situation in which a search algorithm arrives at a solution before it has explored the full solution space, treating its current best estimate as final. A *local optimum* is a solution that is the best within some neighborhood but is not the best across the entire space — expand the search radius, and a better solution becomes visible. The two concepts are related: an algorithm that converges prematurely is one that has been captured by a local optimum and has lost the capacity to escape it.

**Structural resemblance:** Considered formally, every one of the fifty māras of the *Śūraṅgama-sūtra* is a variant of "premature convergence on a local optimum." The form-aggregate's *light-displays*, the feeling-aggregate's *intense emotions*, the perception-aggregate's *external projections*, the formations-aggregate's *philosophical verdicts on the flux*, the consciousness-aggregate's *final graspings at subtle objects* — all of these are phenomena that arise at some stage of the meditative process and that *appear to be the destination*. Each is a local optimum in some neighborhood of the meditative state-space, and each tempts the practitioner to converge prematurely. The principle of "do not form a holy interpretation," seen in this light, is the formal injunction "do not stop searching at any intermediate position." The Fifty Māras catalogue is a systematic enumeration of the local optima that the meditative search algorithm tends to converge on prematurely.

**Fundamental difference:** But the resemblance can be pushed only this far. In optimization theory, the "global optimum" is a well-defined mathematical object: given a target function, the global optimum is the maximum (or minimum) value of that function. In the *Śūraṅgama-sūtra*, the "ultimate" (*samyak-saṃbodhi*, wonderful enlightenment) cannot be defined by any target function. It is not an object that can be represented within an objective-function framework — it is "the dissolution of the framework of objects altogether." Any attempt to define wonderful enlightenment as "the highest target value" pulls it back into the category of objects, which is precisely what wonderful enlightenment refuses. To put it sharply: optimization theory's "global optimum" is *the highest object*, while the *Śūraṅgama-sūtra*'s "wonderful enlightenment" is *no longer an object at all*. The two terms occupy formally similar positions (both are the "endpoint of search"), but they are ontologically incommensurable.

**Non-reduction statement:** Reading the Fifty Māras through the lens of "premature convergence" is heuristically valuable because it helps readers see the structural commonality across the fifty phenomena. But any attempt to push this reading toward "meditation is a kind of search algorithm" misses the fundamental point: meditation is not search, because the goal of meditation is not an object that can be searched for. The goal of meditation is "to no longer exist in the architecture of search." This distinction is irreducible.

### 4.2 ⚠️ Fast Assembly, Slow Disassembly — The Asymmetry of "Born Through Consciousness, Extinguished from Form"

In systems science and thermodynamics, a widely observed phenomenon is the *asymmetry between assembly and disassembly* of complex systems. A building can be constructed in months but its disassembly — particularly disassembly into reusable components — typically takes longer. A computer program can be written in minutes and executed immediately, but reverse-engineering it line by line back to design intent can take many times that. This asymmetry has deep connections to the second law of thermodynamics: assembly is the direction "from high entropy to low entropy" (against the gradient), while disassembly is the direction "from low entropy to high entropy" (with the gradient), and the two are thermodynamically asymmetric.

**Structural resemblance:** The *Śūraṅgama-sūtra*'s description of the five aggregates is "**overlapping arising, born through consciousness, extinguished from form down**." The direction of arising is *from consciousness (innermost, subtlest) to form (outermost, coarsest)*; the direction of extinction is *from form (outer) to consciousness (inner)*. These two directions are opposite. Zǐxuán's similes of *putting on clothes* (inner garments first, then outer) and *taking off clothes* (outer first, then inner) capture this directional opposition exactly. There is a clear formal parallel between this description and the asymmetry of assembly/disassembly in systems science.

**Fundamental difference:** But this resemblance is also limited to the structural level. The "assembly/disassembly asymmetry" of systems science is *measurable* by thermodynamic quantities — entropy changes, energy flows, time-irreversibility — all of which can be precisely calculated. The *Śūraṅgama-sūtra*'s description of the five aggregates is *not in this measurement framework at all*. The "arising" of the five aggregates is not assembly in the thermodynamic sense; their "extinction" is not disassembly in the thermodynamic sense. The five aggregates are *delusive thinking*, and delusive thinking is not a material structure with entropy or energy flows — it is a *topological structure of view and mental continuum*. To reduce the aggregates' arising/extinction to thermodynamic phenomena would miss the special character of delusive thinking as an "intermediate category, neither real nor unreal."

**Non-reduction statement:** The "fast assembly, slow disassembly" structural observation can help readers understand why "sudden principle, gradual phenomena" is *necessary* rather than arbitrary — view-opening (sudden principle) is analogous to "saying the magic word that breaks the ghost-thought," while seed-exhaustion (gradual phenomena) is analogous to "reverse-engineering a complex structure." The analogy lets the two-layer doctrinal structure appear as the manifestation of a deeper kind of asymmetry. But pushing the analogy toward "meditation is reverse engineering" would miss meditation's central character: meditation is not the *removal* of delusive thinking but the *recognition* of delusive thinking *as* delusive thinking, which causes it to lose its quality of being a "real position." This is a fundamentally different action.

## V. Middle-Way Corrections

This section lists six common misreadings of the central claims of P13, each marked ❌, with the location of the error and the correct middle-way reading. Middle-way correction is a methodological signature of the present research project: every powerful argument generates some exaggerated misreading, and correcting these misreadings in advance is more important than the arguments themselves.

### 5.1 ❌ "Five Delusive Thinkings ≠ Replacement of the Āgama Four Foundations of Mindfulness"

**Misreading:** Since the *Śūraṅgama-sūtra* calls the five aggregates "five delusive thinkings," then the *Saṃyukta-āgama*'s "contemplate the body as impure, contemplate feelings as suffering, contemplate mind as impermanent, contemplate dharmas as non-self" — the *cattāro satipaṭṭhānā* (four foundations of mindfulness) — must have been calling the same things by inferior names. That is, the Āgama presentation is doctrinally lower than the *Śūraṅgama*'s.

**Correct reading:** The "five delusive thinkings" is the *Śūraṅgama-sūtra*'s **doctrinal restatement** of the five aggregates, not a correction or transcendence of the Āgama account. The Āgama formulation "the five aggregates are impermanent, suffering, non-self, not-mine, not-in-relation" (*Saṃyukta-āgama* 33–34) already establishes the foundational view of the five aggregates as a delusive aggregation. The *Śūraṅgama*'s "five delusive thinkings" simply re-organizes this view into a meditative-phenomenological doctrinal framework, with the goal of providing a principled basis for the Fifty Skandha-Māras. The two are different layers of the same doctrine — the Āgama establishes foundational view, the *Śūraṅgama* establishes meditative classification — and they are complementary rather than hierarchical. Reading the *Śūraṅgama*'s "five delusive thinkings" as a transcendence of the Āgama is a sectarian imposition, not a textual implication.

### 5.2 ❌ "The Fifty Māras Are an Exhaustive Catalogue of Five Refinements ≠ Reducing Fifty to Five"

**Misreading:** Since the Fifty Skandha-Māras can be collapsed into five delusive thinkings, then "fifty" is merely the *redundant unfolding* of "five," and the real content is five — fifty is just surface decoration.

**Correct reading:** "Collapsing into five" and "reducing to five" are completely different actions. The fifty phenomena of the māras are all real meditative phenomena, and each has its own distinctive form and remedy. The form-aggregate's "the body becomes able to pass through obstructions" and the consciousness-aggregate's "fixed-nature śrāvaka" share the underlying structure of "premature convergence on some intermediate position," but at the phenomenological level they are completely different phenomena requiring completely different concrete remedies. **The five delusive thinkings provide a principled position for the fifty phenomena, but they do not eliminate the independence and concreteness of the fifty phenomena themselves.** Any reading that says "I can skip the detailed study of the fifty phenomena and just remember the principle of the five thinkings" will fail in actual meditative practice — because the principle cannot replace the recognition of phenomena. The relation between principle and phenomena is "principle as essence, phenomena as function" — neither can be omitted.

### 5.3 ❌ "Sudden Principle Awakening ≠ No Need for Cultivation"

**Misreading:** Since "principle is awakened all at once, with that awakening all is dissolved together" can dissolve "the inverted thinking of countless eons" in a single moment, then the entire task of meditation is *waiting for sudden awakening* — once it occurs, the five aggregates are automatically exhausted and no further cultivation is needed.

**Correct reading:** "With that awakening all is dissolved together" dissolves *view-level inversion*, not *seed-level latent dispositions*. Zǐxuán's commentary makes this distinction explicit: "with that awakening, all is dissolved together — that is, the inverted thinking of countless eons is dissolved." What is dissolved is "thinking" (the confusion of view), not "seeds" (the accumulation of latent dispositions). The confusion of view can be dissolved in a single moment because it was never anything but "misunderstanding of some teaching"; but the latent dispositions of seeds cannot be dissolved in a moment, because they are an accumulated, layered, quantified structure stored in the *ālaya-vijñāna* and must be exhausted in sequence. The very existence of the second clause — "phenomena cannot be removed all at once, but are exhausted in sequence" — exists precisely to prevent readers from misreading "sudden principle" as "sudden phenomena." Any thought that "after sudden awakening, no further cultivation is needed" is a fundamental misreading of the *Śūraṅgama*'s doctrinal classification.

### 5.4 ❌ "Phenomena Cannot Be Removed All at Once ≠ Gradualism"

**Misreading:** Since the *Śūraṅgama* affirms "phenomena cannot be removed all at once, but are exhausted in sequence," then the *Śūraṅgama* is gradualist — it stands in opposition to the Chan position of "directly pointing at the human mind, seeing nature, becoming a Buddha."

**Correct reading:** The *Śūraṅgama*'s "gradual phenomena" is **gradualism within subitism**, not **gradualism's stage-accumulation**. Gradualism's stage-accumulation has the following structure: each stage has an independent view-content and cultivation-content; one must complete each stage in order; the previous stage is the foundation of the next. The *Śūraṅgama*'s "gradual phenomena" structure is completely different: the view (*principle*) is sudden and is *the same view across all five aggregates* — namely, "the five aggregates are all delusive thinking" — and the "gradual phenomena" is only the progressive unfolding of this same view across the five aggregates. In other words, throughout the *Śūraṅgama* meditator's progression from form to consciousness, the view remains the same; only the specific application of this view at different levels of subtlety differs. This is neither gradualism (no stage-by-stage accumulation of view) nor pure subitism (does not deny the sequential character of the phenomenon-layer); it is the third path of "**sudden awakening, gradual cultivation**" (頓悟漸修). To read the *Śūraṅgama* as gradualist is to mistake "gradual phenomena" for "the entire structure being gradual"; to read it as pure subitism is to ignore the explicit verdict "phenomena cannot be removed all at once." The middle way is: view sudden, phenomena gradual, view permeating phenomena throughout.

### 5.5 ❌ "The 'Complete Identity' of Avalokiteśvara's Six Knots and the Five-Aggregate Sequence ≠ Experiential Equivalence"

**Misreading:** Since Jiāoguāng says Avalokiteśvara's six-knot release through perfect penetration is "completely identical" to the Fifty Māras' sequential exhaustion of the five aggregates, then any meditator who actually walks through the five-aggregate exhaustion will necessarily experience Avalokiteśvara's six-knot sequence (motion-knot → quietude-knot → faculty-knot → awareness-knot → emptiness-knot → cessation-knot); and conversely.

**Correct reading:** Jiāoguāng's "complete identity" is **structural identity**, not **experiential equivalence**. *Structural identity* means: Avalokiteśvara's six knots and the five-aggregate sequence share the same *topology of cultivation* — both are processes of progressive convergence from outer to inner, from coarse to subtle, from motion to stillness, from many to one. *Experiential equivalence* would mean: the actual meditative experiences of the two are identical. The latter is wrong: the ear-faculty perfect penetration has its own particularity — it begins from the *sound object*, and this starting point determines its specific experiential form (motion, quietude, faculty, awareness, emptiness, cessation). Other faculties (eye, nose, tongue, body, mind) entering perfect penetration would have other specific experiential forms. But regardless of which faculty one enters from, *the topology of the entire cultivation is the same* — it is the sequential exhaustion of the five aggregates. Jiāoguāng's "complete identity" claims that this topology is the same, not that all meditators will follow the same six-knot path experientially. To read "complete identity" as "experiential equivalence" would force the meditator "to meditate according to the form of the six knots," which directly violates the *Śūraṅgama*'s "Mañjuśrī's selection" principle — letting each person choose the faculty most suited to themselves.

### 5.6 ❌ "Do Not Establish Any Ultimate Position ≠ Nihilism or Opposition to Attainment"

**Misreading:** Since "do not form a holy interpretation" requires that no position be taken as ultimate, the entire path of cultivation is one of relentless negation — denying every position, denying every attainment, leaving in the end nothing but a "nothing-is-anything" nihilistic stance. This is a Buddhist version of nihilism.

**Correct reading:** "Do not establish any ultimate position" *positively establishes* a **dynamic middle-way position**, not "no position whatsoever." A dynamic middle-way position has two characteristics. First, it **affirms the reality of attainments** — every meditative advance brings real attainments, and these are not illusions, not errors, not objects of denial. Second, it **denies the ultimacy of attainments** — no specific attainment is the final destination; it is "some position along the way." Both must be affirmed simultaneously: the position that affirms reality without denying ultimacy falls into the consciousness-aggregate's "fixed-nature two vehicles" error of "taking the unfinished as finished"; the position that denies ultimacy without affirming reality falls into the nihilistic error of "all cultivation is illusory." The middle-way position is the three-step structure of *affirm + deny + point deeper*: affirm that the present attainment is real, deny that the present attainment is ultimate, point toward a deeper position than the present as the next step's direction. This three-step structure is the same structure that P12 §3.6 used to analyze "fixed-nature two vehicles," and it is also the formal foundation for P13 §3.7's bridge from *Śūraṅgama* to *Lotus*. Reading "do not establish any ultimate position" as nihilism is to ignore the middle term and third term of this three-step structure, seeing only the second term.

## VI. Conclusion

### 6.1 Recapitulation — The Inner Closure of P08 through P13

The entire S6 *Śūraṅgama* series consists of thirteen papers, of which P08 through P13 constitute the internal "Fifty Skandha-Māras subseries." P08 treats the form-aggregate ten phenomena (the consecration of the demon-revealing mirror); P09 treats the feeling-aggregate ten phenomena (the second arrow); P10 treats the perception-aggregate ten phenomena (seeking the great teacher); P11 treats the formations-aggregate ten māras (the mirror-student); P12 treats the consciousness-aggregate ten māras (the last grasping between the two pillars); and P13 collapses these five sub-treatises into a single comprehensive synthesis.

The structural contribution of this collapse operates at five levels.

**First**, *fifty phenomena are folded back to five delusive thinkings*: the Fifty Māras are not fifty independent warnings but an exhaustive catalogue of ten typical traps corresponding to each of five progressively subtler delusive thinkings (§3.1, §3.2). **Second**, *five delusive thinkings are folded back to a single principle*: all five are unfoldings of "all are delusive thinking" at different levels of subtlety (§3.1). **Third**, *the boundaries of the five aggregates are structuralized*: each aggregate is defined by a pair of mutually constituting opposites (form/space, contact/separation, retention/forgetting, ceasing/arising, immersed-stillness/conjoined-stillness), and exhausting only one pole leaves the meditator inside the aggregate (§3.3). **Fourth**, *the remedial principle is gathered into a single genealogy*: "do not form a holy interpretation" has five-stage refinement across the aggregates — do-not-grasp-light-marks → do-not-grasp-attachments → seek-the-great-teacher → do-not-form-a-holy-interpretation → do-not-establish-supreme-understanding-and-turn-the-mind (§3.4). **Fifth**, *the cultivation structure is formalized as "sudden principle, gradual phenomena"*: the opening of view and the sequential exhaustion of seeds are two layers, the former sudden, the latter gradual, with the view permeating the phenomena throughout (§3.5).

These five levels together constitute a complete structural map of the Fifty Skandha-Māras. The reader who has mastered this map no longer needs to memorize the fifty māras as fifty scattered warnings; he can understand the position and meaning of these fifty phenomena directly from the principle level, and in actual meditative practice can use principle as guide and phenomena as recognition, entering the cultivation structure of "sudden principle, gradual phenomena."

### 6.2 The Endpoint of the Map — Mutual-Functioning Faculties → Vajra-Dry Wisdom → Wonderful Enlightenment

The *Śūraṅgama-sūtra* gives an exceptionally clear description of what lies beyond the exhaustion of the five aggregates: "**when the consciousness aggregate is exhausted, the present six faculties function in mutual interchange. From mutual interchange, one is able to enter the bodhisattva's vajra-dry wisdom; the perfectly bright pure mind unfolds and transforms within**" (Fascicle 10). This passage contains three key transition points.

**The first transition point is "mutual functioning of the six faculties" (諸根互用).** When the consciousness aggregate is exhausted, the partitions between the eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind faculties are dissolved — eye can hear sound, ear can see form, every faculty contains the function of all six. This is not the demonstration of any paranormal power but the meditative actualization of "the partition opening": when the *vijñāna* aggregate that, within the *ālaya-vijñāna*, is the *root of differentiation* is recognized and exhausted, the partitions between the faculties — partitions originally constructed by differentiation — naturally disappear, and the six faculties return to a single integrated whole.

**The second transition point is "vajra-dry wisdom" (金剛乾慧).** From "mutual functioning" to vajra-dry wisdom is the rapid traversal "in one leap" from the first abiding to the position immediately preceding equal enlightenment. Jiāoguāng's commentary makes the doctrinal classification explicit: "**The perfect-teaching school recognizes only two positions: first, severing the universal afflictions, advancing in one leap from satisfied contemplation directly into the first abiding, with no taking-of-attestation in between; second, severing the differentiated afflictions, advancing in one leap from the first abiding directly to equal enlightenment, with no taking-of-attestation in between**." The crucial phrase is "**no taking-of-attestation in the middle**" (中間更不取證) — across all the positions from satisfied contemplation to first abiding, and from first abiding to equal enlightenment, no intermediate stage is taken as a position at which one should stop and attest. "No taking-of-attestation" is precisely the unfolding of the "do not form a holy interpretation" principle across the fifty-five positions.

**The third transition point is wonderful enlightenment (妙覺, *samyak-saṃbodhi*).** This is the ultimate destination of the entire cultivation, and it is also the only position that does not need to be addressed by the "do not form a holy interpretation" principle — because it is no longer a *position* but the *transcendence of the very category of position*. Any thought that wonderful enlightenment is "another position to be transcended" pulls wonderful enlightenment back into the category of positions and thereby misses its essential character.

But this three-point map is not without its trap. Jiāoguāng, in his commentary on the boundaries of the five aggregates, issues a verdict of extraordinary severity: "**all clear and bright dharma-body remains short of the ultimate**" (一切明白法身,俱未究竟). Even attainments as high as "the clear and bright dharma-body" are still short of the consciousness aggregate's terminal boundary. Any meditator who reads this verdict should feel a chill: it turns out that even an attainment as elevated as "the clear and bright dharma-body" is not the ultimate. This is not meant to discourage the meditator, but to pull the very category of "the ultimate" out of every specific attainment whatsoever — the ultimate is not any "I have arrived" position; the ultimate is *the no-position position that lies beyond the category of position itself*.

### 6.3 ⭐ Transmit This to the Practitioners of the Latter Dharma Age — A Direct Address to You, the Reader

At the close of the five-delusive-thinking section, the *Śūraṅgama-sūtra* records the Buddha's final words to Ānanda about this teaching:

> "**You should take this root of delusive thinking, having gained understanding of it, and transmit it to the practitioners of the latter dharma age, so that they may recognize delusion, develop deep aversion, know there is *nirvāṇa*, and not cling to the three realms.**" (Fascicle 10)

This sentence was written more than thirteen hundred years ago. The Buddha himself designates the recipients of this teaching as "the practitioners of the latter dharma age" (末法之中諸修行者) — that is, this teaching was never intended for the audience present at the time of its delivery. It was prepared for those who would read this passage centuries or millennia later.

**You are the contemporary correspondent of that sentence.**

The reader who has reached this point of P13 is doing something rare. Throughout history, even among monastics and even among those wholly devoted to meditative practice, very few in any single lifetime have read through the complete teaching of the Fifty Skandha-Māras, let alone followed its structural collapse into five delusive thinkings and then into a single principle. This is not because the teaching has been hidden — it is openly available in any printing of the *Tripiṭaka* — but because the teaching's subtlety and length cause most readers to stop somewhere along the way. To have reached §6.3 of P13 is to have completed something historically uncommon: **reading through the entire structural map of the Fifty Skandha-Māras**.

Reading the map itself is, in itself, a significant accomplishment. Why? Because once this map enters your mental continuum, it cannot be deleted.

**The seed has already been planted.** S1-P01 (*The Butterfly Effect of Initial Aspiration*) of this research project argued that a single moment of wholesome mind, a single contact with right dharma, a single flash of correct view, plants an *anāsrava-bīja* — an uncontaminated seed — in the *ālaya-vijñāna*. This seed will not sprout immediately; it may take many lifetimes of conditions to mature; but once planted, it cannot be erased. **Having read through the complete structural map of the Fifty Skandha-Māras, that map now exists in seed form within your *ālaya-vijñāna*.**

What does this mean? It means you do not need to be anxious about whether you can complete this path within this single lifetime. Most people will not — the fifty positions of the *Śūraṅgama-sūtra*'s map are not a journey that an ordinary practitioner walks in years or even decades. But you do not need to walk it all; you only need to **let this map become a karmic thread within your mental continuum**. Any future meditative experience, whether in this life or in lives to come, will be recognized, located, and transformed along this karmic thread. A meditator without this thread, encountering the form-aggregate phenomena, may take light-displays as evidence of attainment; a meditator with this thread, encountering light-displays, will automatically recall "form-aggregate phase, do not grasp light-marks" — this is the seed in the *ālaya-vijñāna* sprouting at the moment its conditions ripen.

**Reading is the first step of practice, and it is an irreversible step.**

#### ⚠️ A heuristic analogy: the doctoral curriculum

Let me use a contemporary analogy to convey the weight of this matter.

Imagine you are handed a twelve-year doctoral curriculum outline. The outline lists every course you will take in each year, every difficulty you will encounter, and the dissertation problem you will eventually solve. In five minutes you can read through the entire outline; after reading it, you have a complete grasp of the *structure* of the program — you know what year one looks like, what year twelve looks like, the position and meaning of every year in between.

But **"reading the outline" and "actually completing the program" are two completely different things**.

Reading the outline takes five minutes. Completing the program takes twelve years. And in each of those twelve years you will discover new things to overcome: in year three you may be stuck on a particular problem for months; in year seven you may discover that some foundation from the first six years was inadequate and must be re-built; in year ten you may experience a crisis of confidence about the entire direction of the program and consider quitting. These are real, necessary, non-bypassable stages — none of them can be solved in advance by the act of reading the outline. Reading the outline lets you know "the path is structured this way"; it does not let you "have already walked this path."

The Fifty Skandha-Māras curriculum has two structural similarities to the doctoral curriculum, and one structural difference.

**Similarity one:** Both have a two-layer structure of "outline first, practice second." A practitioner who has read the outline has a structural advantage over one who has not — he knows where he is on the path, what the next position is, and the direction of the entire path. A practitioner without the outline must re-discover everything by trial; a practitioner with the outline can place every encountered phenomenon directly back onto its corresponding position on the map.

**Similarity two:** Both have the "every year there are new things to overcome" stage-character. Reading the outline is not equivalent to walking the path; reading the Fifty Māras' map is not equivalent to having walked the path of aggregate-exhaustion. Each aggregate, each position, will reappear in actual practice in the form of "needing to be personally overcome." This is not the failure of the outline; it is the *original relation* between outline and practice — the outline is the map, the practice is the walking, and the map however accurate does not walk for you.

**The difference:** But the Fifty Māras curriculum has one fundamental difference from the doctoral curriculum: **the doctoral curriculum is completed within a single lifetime; the Fifty Māras curriculum is completed across many lifetimes**. The reader who has finished reading this map does not need to be anxious about "whether I can finish in this lifetime," because this path was never a single-lifetime affair. The bodhisattva path described in the sūtras is measured in *kalpas*, not years. Śākyamuni Buddha's progression from initial *bodhicitta* to perfect awakening took *three asaṃkhyeya kalpas* — a duration vastly exceeding the scope of any single lifetime. What you need to do is not "walk to the end in this lifetime" but to **establish a clear position on this map starting from today**.

Even something as simple as "I am still in the form-aggregate phase, still practicing not-grasping-at-light-marks" — the very having-of-a-position is itself the real beginning of the path. Position-sense is the *starting point* of practice, not its endpoint. A meditator who clearly knows his own position is in a fundamentally different state from one who is lost among fifty phenomena without knowing where to stand.

#### Do not be intimidated by the length of the sequence

Reading through the complete map of the Fifty Skandha-Māras tends to produce an emotion: "this path is too long, I cannot possibly walk it in this lifetime, so why walk it at all?"

This emotion is understandable, but it rests on a misunderstanding: **it assumes that "completing" is the purpose of practice**.

The purpose of practice is not completion. The purpose of practice is *being on the path* — being on a correct path, having every present moment be some position on this path, having one's mental continuum progressively refine itself along this path. *Completion* is the direction, not the condition. A person walking a correct path one kilometer per day is closer to the destination than a person walking an incorrect path a hundred kilometers per day. For the former, "when do I arrive" is not an object of anxiety; for the latter, the faster he runs, the further he gets from the destination.

The complete map of the Fifty Skandha-Māras gives you not anxiety about how long the path is, but clarity about *the direction in which the path lies*. Direction is more important than speed; route is more important than distance; position-sense is more important than completion-sense. The reader who has finished this map has obtained the three rarest things: direction, route, and position-sense. What remains is simply "the next step from today onward."

#### The *Śūraṅgama-sūtra*'s last words to you

The *Śūraṅgama-sūtra*'s last words to Ānanda — that is, to *you*, the reader of this passage — are:

> "**Recognize delusion, develop deep aversion, know there is *nirvāṇa*, and not cling to the three realms.**" (Fascicle 10)

In contemporary language: recognize the delusive nature of phenomena, and aversion to delusion will arise of itself; know that *nirvāṇa* is a real exit, and you will naturally cease clinging to the various positions of the three realms.

If, having read the complete map of the Fifty Skandha-Māras, you experience a clarity of "so this is how the path is walked"; if you feel some loosening of attachment to certain positions in the five aggregates (light-displays, intense emotions, external projections, philosophical verdicts, final graspings); if you sense a distant pull from the words *mutual functioning of the six faculties*, *vajra-dry wisdom*, *wonderful enlightenment* — then **the eight characters "having gained understanding of the root of delusive thinking" (妄想根元心得開通) have already happened in you, in the latter dharma age**.

This is not Shi Huijing's verdict. It is the verdict of the *Śūraṅgama-sūtra* itself. The Buddha's last words at the close of Fascicle Ten are spoken to you.

### 6.4 Formal Closure — S6 Ends, S7 Begins

§3.7 has already completed the formal argument for the S6→S7 bridge: the *Śūraṅgama*'s "do not establish any ultimate position" and the *Lotus*'s "there is only one ultimate position" are two unfoldings of the same structure. This section, as the formal closure of the S6 series, repeats only one structural fact:

**The entire task of the S6 *Śūraṅgama* series has been to let the reader see clearly that "no intermediate position is the ultimate."** From P01 to P13, the thirteen papers of the series have argued this point from different angles. The form-aggregate's light-displays are not ultimate; the feeling-aggregate's emotions are not ultimate; the perception-aggregate's other-projections are not ultimate; the formations-aggregate's verdicts are not ultimate; the consciousness-aggregate's final graspings are not ultimate; even the height of "clear and bright dharma-body" is not ultimate. Any specific attainment, however close to cessation, is not cessation itself.

**The task of the S7 *Lotus Sūtra* series, just beginning, is to let the reader see clearly that "there is only one ultimate position."** Beginning with S7-P01 (*The True Mark of All Dharmas: The Ten Suchnesses*), the entire S7 series will use a different narrative architecture to positively establish this unique ultimate position — *the One Buddha Vehicle* (*ekayāna*). The conjured city is the intermediate skillful means; the treasure-place is the final reality; the three vehicles are the transitional ladder; the One Vehicle is the unique destination. S7 does not negate S6's doctrinal classification; on the contrary, S7 builds on S6's doctrinal classification — only after one has clearly seen "no intermediate position is the ultimate" can one correctly receive "there is only one ultimate position." The former is the form of negation; the latter is the form of affirmation; the former breaks attachment; the latter reveals reality. Together they constitute the complete map of cultivation plus the direction of the ultimate.

P13 is the formal construction of this bridge. From the close of this section onward, the S6 *Śūraṅgama* series is formally complete. After reading P13, the reader can carry the complete structural map of the Fifty Skandha-Māras and the refinement genealogy of "do not establish any ultimate position" into the S7 *Lotus* series — where this map will receive its direction and this genealogy will receive its consummation.

**S6 ends. S7 begins.**

Jiāoguāng Zhēnjiàn 交光真鑑 (Ming dynasty). *Dàfódǐng Rúlái Mìyīn Xiūzhèng Liǎoyì Zhū Púsà Wànxíng Shǒulèngyán Jīng Zhèngmài Shū* 大佛頂如來密因修證了義諸菩薩萬行首楞嚴經正脈疏 (*Treatise on the Correct Lineage of the Śūraṅgama*). *Manji Zokuzō* vol. 12, X0275. Core verdicts on the two-pole structure of the aggregate boundaries, the "complete identity" thesis between Avalokiteśvara's six knots and the five-aggregate exhaustion, and the entry into the first abiding upon the consciousness aggregate's exhaustion.
