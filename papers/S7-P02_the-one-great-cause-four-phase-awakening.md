## Abstract

This paper centers on the pivotal passage in the *Lotus Sūtra*'s second chapter, *Upāyakauśalya* ("Skillful Means"): "The Buddhas, the World-Honored Ones, appear in the world only for the sake of one great matter and cause." It argues that the four verbs the sūtra immediately unfolds — *to open, to reveal, to awaken to, and to enter* the buddha-knowledge-and-vision — together constitute the most fully articulated "cosmic awakening loop" in the Buddhist literary corpus: a closed four-phase circuit rather than a linear ladder, structurally identical to the four-phase contemplative loop that this project has previously identified in the *Heart Sūtra* (illuminating → examining → entering deeply → liberating from suffering). By collating Vasubandhu's *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka-upadeśa* (T1519/T1520), Zhiyi's *Fahua wenju* (T1718), and Kuiji's *Fahua xuanzan* (T1723), the paper traces how the Lotus's doctrine of "merging the three vehicles into one" (*huì sān guī yī* 會三歸一) was structurally read by Indian and Chinese commentators working within the broader Yogācāra horizon. It further locates two seed-lines in the early Āgama corpus that anticipate this teaching at the level of rhetorical form and argumentative structure: the "single-vehicle path" (*ekāyana mārga* 一乘道) of *Saṃyukta-āgama* 607 — referring there specifically to the four foundations of mindfulness — and the "*dharmadhātu* ever-abides whether the Tathāgata appears in the world or not" (*Saṃyukta-āgama* 299). Using a *homonymous-distinction* principle, the paper carefully separates the Āgama's "single-vehicle path" from the Lotus's "one buddha-vehicle" — they share rhetoric and structure but differ in referent — while at the same time arguing that the Lotus's doctrine is best read as a *cosmological dramatization* of the Āgama's *dharmadhātu*-abiding structure. The final synthesis: the Āgamas supply the methodological seed (the four foundations of mindfulness), the Lotus supplies the cosmological amplification (one matter, one vehicle, all buddhas), and the Yogācāra commentaries supply the fine-grained mechanism (the four verbs as the four phases of *āśraya-parāvṛtti*, the transformation of the basis). All three layers answer the same question, at different scales: *what, exactly, is awakening?*

**Keywords:** one great cause; four-phase awakening (*open, reveal, awaken, enter*); one buddha-vehicle; *ekāyana*; convergence of the three vehicles; *dharmadhātu* ever-abiding; Vasubandhu's *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka-upadeśa*; Kuiji's *Fahua xuanzan*; three natures (*trisvabhāva*); homonymous distinction

## I. Introduction: What, exactly, did the Buddha come into the world for?

Imagine you have practiced for thirty years. You have read the texts, you have sat through countless retreats, you have taught your students well. And then someone — a serious person, not a casual interlocutor — sits down across from you and asks one question: "Why did the Buddha come into this world? What was he here *for*?"

You will likely reach for one of the standard answers. To liberate sentient beings. To end suffering. To turn the wheel of Dharma. These answers are all correct, and yet the moment you say them aloud you may notice a strange hollowness — they are too large, too smooth, too obviously the answers everyone gives. They do not answer the question so much as they allow you to step gracefully past it.

The *Lotus Sūtra*'s second chapter, *Upāyakauśalya*, gives an answer that is at once startlingly simple and totally non-evasive: the Buddha came into the world for the sake of *one matter*. One — not two, not seven, not eighty-four thousand. Every skillful means, every parable, every teaching given to the three vehicles, all of it converges, in the end, on this one matter. And the sūtra immediately unpacks what that matter is, in four verbs:

> "The Buddhas, the World-Honored Ones, appear in the world only for the sake of one great matter and cause… Because they wish to cause sentient beings *to open* the buddha-knowledge-and-vision and obtain purity, they appear in the world; because they wish *to reveal* the buddha-knowledge-and-vision to sentient beings, they appear in the world; because they wish to cause sentient beings *to awaken to* the buddha-knowledge-and-vision, they appear in the world; because they wish to cause sentient beings *to enter into* the path of buddha-knowledge-and-vision, they appear in the world."[^1]

Four verbs, in a single uninterrupted breath: *open, reveal, awaken, enter*. There is no second passage in the entire Buddhist canon that compresses the entire purpose of buddha-teaching into a four-step circuit with this kind of authority. The Tiantai school recognized this immediately and made the *Upāyakauśalya* chapter the structural pivot of the Lotus's "trace gate" (迹門) — the entire first half of the sūtra collapses into this passage.

There are three reasons this passage matters as much as it does. First, it is the *doctrinal hinge* of the Lotus: from this point forward the sūtra is committed to the doctrine of the one buddha-vehicle and is willing to say, in the most exclusive language available in Classical Chinese, that "there are no other vehicles, neither two nor three." Second, it formally inaugurates the convergence-of-the-three-vehicles teaching for which the *Lotus* is famous, and does so in language whose grammatical force we will need to take seriously in §3.1. Third, and most easily missed: it raises a question about the *appearance-in-the-world* of buddhas that turns out, on closer inspection, to be in subtle tension with what the early Āgamas had already said — and that tension, when properly worked through, becomes the deepest doctrinal payoff of the whole passage.

This paper sets out to answer three questions. *First*, what is the structure of the four verbs *open, reveal, awaken, enter*? Are they four parallel stages? Four sequential rungs of a ladder? Or do they form a closed circuit of the kind this project has previously identified in S2-P02's reading of the *Heart Sūtra*'s Avalokiteśvara verse — *illuminating → examining → entering deeply → liberating from suffering* — as a four-phase contemplative loop? *Second*, do these teachings have antecedents in the Āgamas? S7's preliminary plan listed two candidate seed-lines under "the one matter" thesis: the phrase *yīchéng dào* (一乘道, "single-vehicle path") and the phrase "what I have spoken is only this one matter." A systematic *grep* over the local Āgama corpus has established that the first seed-line exists and is even richer than expected — but that the second seed-line, as such, *does not exist* in the *Mahāparinirvāṇa-sūtra*'s recension of the Buddha's last words. In its place, however, we have found something deeper: a verse from *Saṃyukta-āgama* 299 about the *dharmadhātu*-abiding-whether-the-tathāgata-appears-or-not, which turns out to be a much stronger structural antecedent than the lost candidate. *Third*, how did the millennium-long commentarial history from Vasubandhu (5th c. India) to Kuiji (7th c. Tang) progressively articulate this loose four-verb formula into a fully structured awakening equation that maps cleanly onto the Yogācāra doctrine of the three natures?

The argument moves through three layers. The Āgamas supply a *methodological seed* — the four foundations of mindfulness as the original referent of "single-vehicle path." The *Lotus Sūtra* performs a *cosmological dramatization* — taking the seed and amplifying it from a single contemplative method into the unique purpose of every buddha's appearance in every world. The commentarial tradition from Vasubandhu through Zhiyi to Kuiji then performs a *fine-grained mechanical refinement* — articulating the four verbs as the four phases of *āśraya-parāvṛtti* (transformation of the basis), with the Yogācāra three natures serving as the structural skeleton.

This paper is the second in series S7 and directly extends the work begun in S7-P01 ("The True Mark of All Dharmas: The Ten Suchnesses"). In P01 I proposed a *three-tier genealogy thesis*: Vasubandhu's *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka-upadeśa* T1519 → Jizang's *Fahua yishu* T1721 → Kuiji's *Fahua xuanzan* T1723, with the common pivot being the other-dependent nature (*paratantra*) and the common goal being the perfectly accomplished nature (*pariniṣpanna*). §3.5 of the present paper will demonstrate that this genealogy thesis receives its strongest possible confirmation precisely on the *Upāyakauśalya* "one great matter" passage: Kuiji in *Fahua xuanzan* fascicle 4b states explicitly, in language that admits no ambiguity, that "the [pure portion of] other-dependent nature *is* awakening, and the perfectly accomplished nature *is* nirvāṇa, together constituting the one vehicle." This is the strongest text-anchored support for command-thesis I that the Lotus commentarial tradition contains.

The paper is also the *first formal use* of S7's third thesis — *the Lotus as the dramatic completion of Āgama seeds*. Beginning here, every paper in the series whose central image has an Āgama seed will use a two-level structure in §II, dividing the textual sources into "甲1 Āgama seed" and "甲2 Lotus development." This is more than an aesthetic preference: it is a structural response to the latent worry, sometimes raised in modern scholarship and sometimes raised in modern Sangha communities, about whether the *Mahāyāna sūtras* are "what the Buddha actually taught." We do not need to enter that debate frontally. We only need to do the patient philological work of laying the seed-lines side by side, so that the Lotus quietly returns to its place along the continuous transmission of the Dharma.

The remaining sections proceed as follows. §II establishes the textual sources (sūtra first, then the commentarial corpus). §III is the core argument, in six subsections moving from textual structure to the three-layer synthesis. §IV attempts a careful interdisciplinary bridge to closed-loop feedback systems in cybernetics, with full irreducibility caveats. §V handles five corrective adjustments necessary to keep the argument on the middle path. §VI concludes and prepares the ground for S7-P03 on the parable of the burning house.

## II. Textual Sources

Following this project's *sūtra-first* methodology, this section lists primary scriptural sources first (甲), and only then commentarial sources (乙). The scriptural sources themselves are further divided into two strata: the Lotus base text proper (甲1) and the Āgama seed-lines (甲2). The commentarial sources will be sketched briefly here and then unfolded systematically in §3.5.

### 甲. Sūtra

#### 甲1. The Lotus base text: the core declaration of the *Upāyakauśalya* chapter

The "one great matter" passage occurs in fascicle 1, *Upāyakauśalya* chapter 2, of Kumārajīva's translation:

> 「諸佛世尊唯以一大事因緣故出現於世。舍利弗!云何名諸佛世尊唯以一大事因緣故出現於世?諸佛世尊,欲令眾生開佛知見使得清淨故,出現於世;欲示眾生佛之知見故,出現於世;欲令眾生悟佛知見故,出現於世;欲令眾生入佛知見道故,出現於世。舍利弗!是為諸佛以一大事因緣故出現於世。」
>
> "The Buddhas, the World-Honored Ones, appear in the world only for the sake of one great matter and cause. Śāriputra, what is meant by 'the Buddhas, the World-Honored Ones, appear in the world only for the sake of one great matter and cause'? The Buddhas, the World-Honored Ones, appear in the world because they wish to cause sentient beings to *open* the buddha-knowledge-and-vision and obtain purity; appear in the world because they wish *to reveal* the buddha-knowledge-and-vision to sentient beings; appear in the world because they wish to cause sentient beings *to awaken to* the buddha-knowledge-and-vision; appear in the world because they wish to cause sentient beings *to enter into* the path of buddha-knowledge-and-vision. Śāriputra, this is what is meant by 'the Buddhas appear in the world for the sake of one great matter and cause.'"[^1]

The passage is followed almost immediately by a second declaration that pushes the doctrine to its strongest universal-quantifier form:

> 「諸佛如來但教化菩薩,諸有所作,常為一事,唯以佛之知見示悟眾生。舍利弗!如來但以一佛乘故,為眾生說法,無有餘乘,若二、若三。舍利弗!一切十方諸佛,法亦如是。」
>
> "The Buddhas, the Tathāgatas, only teach and transform bodhisattvas; whatever they do, they do constantly for one matter, namely, to reveal and to awaken sentient beings to the buddha-knowledge-and-vision. Śāriputra, the Tathāgata teaches the Dharma to sentient beings only by means of the one buddha-vehicle; there is no other vehicle, neither two nor three. Śāriputra, the Dharma of all the Buddhas in all ten directions is also like this."[^2]

A subsequent verse-passage tightens the universal quantifier even further: "Among the buddha-lands of the ten directions, there is only the one-vehicle Dharma; there is neither two nor three, except where the Buddha teaches by way of skillful means."[^3]

The passage rewards close grammatical attention. The lexical choices are extraordinarily strong. *Wéi* (唯, "only") functions as an exclusive adverb that explicitly forecloses every other possible reason for buddhas to appear in the world. *Cháng wéi yī shì* (常為一事, "constantly for one matter") makes this not an occasional emphasis but a structural invariant. *Dàn yǐ* (但以, "only by means of"), *wú yǒu yú chéng* (無有餘乘, "no other vehicle"), and *yīqiè shífāng zhū fó* (一切十方諸佛, "all the buddhas in all ten directions") together build three successive layers of exclusive quantification. We will return to this grammatical work in §3.1.

Dharmarakṣa's earlier translation, the *Zheng fahua jing* (T0263) — preserved in the same vault under fascicle 2 of his *Shanquan* (善權) chapter — uses subtly different terminology. Dharmarakṣa retains more of the dynamic vocabulary of "three vehicles," "skillful expedience" (善權方便), and "driving forward and exhorting" (驅勸); he uses "one buddha-vehicle" much less prominently and tends instead to say "one vehicle" or "the great vehicle" (大乘). This translation difference is itself instructive: the very concept of "one buddha-vehicle" passed through at least two layers of translator decision-making on its way from Central Asia to East Asia. The present paper takes Kumārajīva's version as primary and uses Dharmarakṣa's as a comparative check.

#### 甲2. Āgama seeds: the single-vehicle path and the *dharmadhātu* ever-abiding

The Lotus's "one great matter" passage has two important seed-lines in the early Āgama corpus. S7's initial planning document had identified two candidates: a *lexical* seed (the phrase *yīchéng dào*) and a *thematic* seed (a putative passage in which the Buddha says "what I have spoken is only this one matter"). After a systematic *grep* over the Āgama vault, the first seed-line is robustly confirmed (though with an important correction to its location) and the second is not — but the failed search uncovered a more powerful structural seed than the one we had been looking for.

**The first seed: 一乘道, "the single-vehicle path."** This phrase occurs in *four* places in the *Saṃyukta-āgama* (T0099). The shortest and most canonical statement is in fascicle 24, sūtra 607:

> 「爾時,世尊告諸比丘:『有一乘道,淨諸眾生,令越憂悲,滅惱苦,得如實法,所謂四念處。何等為四?身身觀念處,受、心、法法觀念處。』」
>
> "At that time the World-Honored One addressed the bhikṣus: 'There is a single-vehicle path that purifies sentient beings, that enables them to surmount sorrow and grief, to extinguish anguish and suffering, and to attain the *dharma-as-it-truly-is* — namely, the four foundations of mindfulness. What are the four? Contemplating the body as body, contemplating feeling, mind, and dharmas as dharmas.'"[^4]

The longest unfolding occurs in fascicle 19, sūtra 535 (the Anuruddha *dhyāna* passage), where the same formula is given a much richer doxological treatment. Sūtra 550 in fascicle 20 (the Mahākātyāyana six-recollections passage) repeats the formula "the single-vehicle path purifies sentient beings, separates them from anguish and suffering, completely extinguishes sorrow and grief, and obtains the *dharma-as-it-truly-is*" *six times*, in correspondence with the six recollections (Buddha, Dharma, Sangha, *śīla*, generosity, and the heavens).[^5] Sūtra 561 in fascicle 21 applies the same formula to the four bases of supernormal power (*ṛddhipāda*).

A point that requires special note: T0026 *Madhyama-āgama*, in its *Smṛtyupasthāna-sūtra* (sūtra 98), uses the slightly different phrase *yǒu yī dào* (有一道, "there is a single path") rather than *yī chéng dào* — that is, it omits the "vehicle" (*chéng* 乘).[^6] In other words, the three-character compound "single-vehicle path" is, in extant Chinese translations of the Āgamas, primarily found in the *Saṃyukta-āgama*, not the *Madhyama*. The S7 plan's initial citation needs to be corrected: the primary citation should be *Saṃyukta-āgama* 607, with the *Madhyama-āgama*'s "single path" cited as a textual variant.

⚠️ A critical *homonymous distinction* must be flagged immediately. The "single-vehicle path" of the Āgamas refers, with no ambiguity whatsoever, to the *four foundations of mindfulness* (*catvāri smṛtyupasthānāni*) — that is, to a specific, operational, contemplative method belonging to the order of "practice" (*xíng* 行). The "one buddha-vehicle" of the *Lotus* refers to the encompassing teaching that comprehends all three vehicles into their final convergence — that is, to a structural, totalizing concept belonging to the order of "doctrine" (*jiào* 教). One is "what to *practice*"; the other is "what the *teaching is*." One is a method-level instruction at the scale of an individual practitioner; the other is a doctrine-level statement at the scale of the entire economy of buddha-teaching. §3.4 will argue that the relationship between these two is one of *structural and rhetorical continuity*, not doctrinal identity. The Āgama sūtra establishes the legitimacy of the *exclusive rhetorical figure* "only this one"; the *Lotus* extends that figure from method-level to cosmology-level. This is a *seed of language*, not an equation of meaning.

**The second seed: 法界常住, "the *dharmadhātu* ever-abides."** This second seed-line was *not* on the original S7 planning table. We had originally hoped to find a passage in the *Dīrgha-āgama*'s *Mahāparinirvāṇa-sūtra* in which the Buddha says "what I have spoken is only this one matter" — and found that no such passage exists. The Buddha's *actual* last words in the *Mahāparinirvāṇa-sūtra* concern *non-negligence* (*apramāda*): "Therefore, bhikṣus, do not be negligent. It was through non-negligence that I myself attained perfect awakening. Innumerable good things are likewise obtained through non-negligence. All conditioned things are impermanent. *This is the Tathāgata's last teaching.*"[^7] The closest approximation to "only this one matter" is a passage in fascicle 2 — "Ānanda, the Dharma I have taught, both inner and outer, is now finished" — but this expresses the *completeness* of the teaching corpus, not the *singularity* of its content.

What the *grep* did surface, however, was something genuinely better. *Saṃyukta-āgama* 299 contains the following exchange:

> 「佛告比丘:『緣起法者,非我所作,亦非餘人作。然彼如來出世及未出世,法界常住,彼如來自覺此法,成等正覺,為諸眾生分別演說,開發顯示。』」
>
> "The Buddha addressed the bhikṣu: 'The teaching of dependent arising was not made by me, nor by anyone else. Whether tathāgatas appear in the world or do not appear in the world, the *dharmadhātu* ever-abides; the tathāgata, having himself awakened to this Dharma, attains complete and perfect awakening, and for the sake of sentient beings he discriminates and expounds, opens up and reveals it.'"[^8]

Sūtra 854 in fascicle 30 (the *Dharma-mirror sūtra*) contains a parallel passage: "Whether tathāgatas appear in the world or do not appear in the world, the *dharma-nature* (*fǎxìng* 法性) ever-abides; the tathāgata, having known this himself, attains perfect awakening, and reveals, expounds, discriminates, and opens it forth."[^9] The two sūtras together establish the core proposition with no ambiguity: *the teaching of dependent arising exists in itself; the* dharmadhātu *abides regardless of whether any tathāgata appears; what the tathāgata uniquely contributes is to* discriminate, expound, open, and reveal *that which already is.*

Now read the two sūtras together with the *Lotus*'s "one great matter" passage. On the surface they look opposed: one says that the appearance of buddhas is the unique cause of one great matter; the other says that buddhas' appearance adds nothing to what was already there. But look more carefully and the relationship is not opposition, it is *complementarity in a structure of essence and function* (體用 *tǐ-yòng*). The *dharmadhātu*-abiding clause supplies the *essence* — what is already in place. The "one great matter" clause supplies the *function* — *why* the tathāgata bothers to appear, given that nothing needs to be added. The two clauses together describe the same situation from two angles: from the angle of the abiding Dharma itself (the Āgama angle), and from the angle of the pedagogical event (the Lotus angle). §3.4 will develop this in detail.

### 乙. Commentary

This section sketches the four commentarial sources whose detailed analysis is reserved for §3.5.

**Vasubandhu, *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka-upadeśa*** (T1519 in Bodhiruci's translation; T1520 in the Ratnamati translation). The earliest extant Indian commentary on the *Lotus*, and the most structurally consequential. On the "one great matter" passage Vasubandhu proposes a *fourfold-meaning analysis*:

> "[The phrase] 'one great matter' should be understood according to four meanings. What are the four? (1) The *unsurpassed meaning* — only the all-knowing wisdom of the tathāgata can know it, there is no other matter. (2) The *meaning of sameness* — namely, that the dharma-bodies of the śrāvakas, pratyekabuddhas, and buddhas are equal. (3) The *meaning of not-knowing* — namely, that the śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas cannot know its true place. (4) [The meaning of] *causing them to attain the stage of non-retrogression*."[^10]

These four meanings map directly onto the four verbs *open, reveal, awaken, enter*. Crucially — and this is the structural finding S7-P01 had already established — Vasubandhu's framework here is *dharma-body equality*, not the three-natures framework of his other works. There is no mention of *parikalpita*, *paratantra*, or *pariniṣpanna* in the *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka-upadeśa*. The use of "*dharma-body equality*" rather than "three natures" is a deliberate *register-selection* on Vasubandhu's part — he selects different doctrinal vocabularies for different scriptural contexts — and this selection itself becomes a key piece of evidence for the three-tier genealogy thesis.

**Zhiyi, *Fahua xuanyi*** (T1716) and ***Fahua wenju*** (T1718). Tiantai's treatment of the "one great matter" passage is the richest in the entire commentarial history. In *Fahua xuanyi* fascicle 1, Zhiyi makes "destroying the discardable expedient and opening to the true buddha-knowledge-and-vision" the doctrinal pivot of the Lotus[^11]. In *Fahua wenju* fascicle 2, Zhiyi develops a *fourfold gloss* of *open, reveal, awaken, enter* (by stage, by wisdom, by gate, by contemplation) that is, structurally, the most fine-grained analysis of the four verbs in any Buddhist commentary[^12]. Zhiyi's teacher Huisi (慧思) had already proposed a *four-position gloss* — "*open* the buddha-knowledge-and-vision is the ten dwellings; *reveal* is the ten practices; *awaken to* is the ten dedications; *enter into* is the ten grounds and the stage of equal awakening"[^13] — which became the foundation of Tiantai's doctrinal-staging schema.

**Kuiji, *Fahua xuanzan*** (T1723). The Tang Dynasty Faxiang school's Yogācāra reading of the *Lotus*. §3.5 will demonstrate that Kuiji not only inherits Vasubandhu's fourfold-meaning framework but also, in *Fahua xuanzan* fascicle 4b, explicitly reads the "one vehicle" through the structural lens of the three natures: "Now, the [pure portion of] the *paratantra* is awakening, and the *pariniṣpanna* is nirvāṇa — together they constitute the one vehicle."[^14] This is the strongest text-anchored support that the three-tier genealogy thesis I established in S7-P01 receives anywhere in the Lotus commentarial tradition.

**Jizang, *Fahua yishu*** (T1721). The Sanlun (Madhyamaka) perspective on the same passage. Jizang's contribution to the "one great matter" exegesis is structural rather than doctrinal: he sets out *three parallel readings* of *open, reveal, awaken, enter* (an "old gloss," the gloss based on Vasubandhu's *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka-upadeśa*, and a third gloss drawing on the *Mahāparinirvāṇa-sūtra* and the *Da zhidu lun*), making him the most complete cataloguer of the commentarial state of the question at the threshold of the Tang.[^15]

## III. Core Argument

### 3.1 The textual structure and pedagogical weight of the "one great matter" passage

We begin by reading the passage closely. The pedagogical weight of the sentence "the Buddhas, the World-Honored Ones, appear in the world only for the sake of one great matter and cause" derives almost entirely from three layers of grammatical choice.

**The first layer is the word *wéi* (唯, "only").** Classical Chinese *wéi* is an exclusive adverb of the strongest possible kind: to say "*wéi* X is Y" is to say "with the exception of X there is no Y at all." The Buddha is not saying "buddhas mostly appear in the world for one great matter"; he is not saying "buddhas often appear in the world for one great matter"; he is saying that buddhas appear *only* for one great matter. The moment this *wéi* enters the sentence, every other possible motivation for buddhas to appear in the world is foreclosed: not for the display of supernormal powers, not for being honored and supported, not for the conversion of any particular individual or country, not for the polemical defeat of non-Buddhist views, not for the establishment of monastic institutions. None of these. *Only* that one matter.

**The second layer is the compound *yī dà shì* (一大事, "one great matter").** Chinese *shì* (事) can mean both "event" and "affair" or "business." Kumārajīva's lexical choice in this passage uses *shì* in its heaviest semantic register: not an isolated event, but the entire *business* of the buddha's appearing-in-the-world, taken as a single project with a single endpoint. Kuiji in *Fahua xuanzan* fascicle 3b glosses the term:

> "'One great matter' — 'matter' may here be taken in any of its senses: thing, substantive matter, meaning of the matter, principle. It is for the sake of this great matter and cause that the buddha appears in the world, proclaiming his epithets and broadly benefiting sentient beings. Were it not so, *he would simply enter nirvāṇa as the two vehicles do.*"[^16]

Note carefully Kuiji's last clause. If it were not for this one great matter, the Buddha could perfectly well — *immediately upon his own awakening* — simply enter *parinirvāṇa* as the *śrāvakas* and *pratyekabuddhas* do. *The Buddha's choice not to enter nirvāṇa is itself a pedagogical decision*, and the single thing supporting that decision is the existence of this one great matter. The "one great matter" is therefore not some abstract doctrinal concept; it is *the reason the Buddha is here*. If you take it away, the Buddha goes home.

**The third layer is the universalization that follows immediately.** "The Tathāgatas only teach and transform bodhisattvas." "All the Buddhas in all ten directions are likewise." These two clauses extend the proposition from "the historical Śākyamuni" to "every buddha in every world-system." It is not that this particular Buddha happened to have one purpose; it is structurally the case that *no buddha in any world ever appears for any other reason*. This is a universal quantifier under no exceptions.

Stack the three layers together: buddhas appear *only* for *one* matter, and *every* buddha *always* appears *only* for *that same* matter. The passage drives three doors of exclusive quantification shut, one after another, until the reader is forced to stop and ask: *and what, exactly, is that one matter?* The sūtra's answer is the four-verb structure that constitutes our next subsection.

### 3.2 Open, reveal, awaken, enter as a cosmic-scale awakening loop

The four verbs *kāi* (開), *shì* (示), *wù* (悟), *rù* (入) are extraordinarily precise lexical choices. Each verb corresponds to a specific phase of the awakening event, and the four verbs together constitute a *closed loop* — not a linear ladder, not a parallel listing, but an internal circuit of the same kind we already identified in S2-P02's reading of the *Heart Sūtra*'s Avalokiteśvara verse.

**Open** (*kāi*) — to remove a covering, to make accessible what was previously enclosed. The buddha-knowledge-and-vision is, for sentient beings as they ordinarily exist, covered over by ignorance, by karma, by the cognitive obstruction. The Buddha's first action is to *uncover* this covering, allowing what was already there to come into view. Note carefully: the object of "opening" is not something *given* to sentient beings; it is something *already in them* that is being *unveiled*. The use of the verb *kāi* therefore already presupposes a doctrinal commitment — that sentient beings *intrinsically possess* the buddha-knowledge-and-vision in some sense. Kuiji glosses *open* in his *Fahua xuanzan* fascicle 3b as follows: "The buddhas appear in the world wishing to cause sentient beings to sever the obstruction-of-the-knowable along with the karmic actions and karmic results that follow from it, and to fully realize *bodhi*, opening the *appearance* of the knowledge-and-vision so that they obtain purity."[^17] The content of "opening" is the severing of the obstruction-of-the-knowable; the *direction* of opening is from coveredness toward purity.

**Reveal** (*shì*) — to *display* the contents of what has been opened to a viewer. *Open* and *reveal* may at first glance seem to collapse into the same operation, but there is a crucial difference: *opening* can be silent and structural (as one might silently draw back a curtain), whereas *revealing* must be *displayed-to-someone* — there must be both an object that is displayed and a subject who beholds the display. Vasubandhu glosses *reveal* as the *meaning of sameness*: "namely, that the dharma-bodies of the śrāvakas, pratyekabuddhas, and buddhas are equal. The dharma-bodies are equal because the buddha-nature and the dharma-body know no differentiation."[^18] What the Buddha *displays* in this phase is not something the sentient being lacks; it is the *equal dharma-body* that all three vehicles have always possessed. The Buddha shows you what you have always already had, but did not know you had.

**Awaken to** (*wù*) — the subject *recognizes* what is being shown. The first two verbs were the Buddha's actions; with the third verb, the locus of agency begins to migrate to the practitioner. Vasubandhu glosses *awaken to* as the *meaning of not-knowing*: "namely, that all the *śrāvakas* and *pratyekabuddhas* cannot know its true place. To 'not know its true place' means: not to know that there is, in the end, only the one buddha-vehicle."[^19] The problem the verb *awaken to* addresses is not that sentient beings *fail to see* something — they may already see it — but that they *fail to recognize what they are seeing*. They see the buddha-knowledge-and-vision and mistake it for the *śrāvaka*'s liberation; they see the one buddha-vehicle and mistake it for one vehicle among three. *Awaken to* is precisely the verb of *recognition* — of seeing-as.

**Enter** (*rù*) — to actively step into, to take up and carry. With the first three verbs we are still in the order of *knowing*: open the covering, display the content, recognize what is displayed. With *enter*, the action moves from knowing to *doing*: the practitioner does not simply receive information about the buddha-knowledge-and-vision but *takes that content as part of her own existence*. Vasubandhu glosses *enter* as *causing them to attain the stage of non-retrogression, displaying the wish to bestow on them the karma of immeasurable wisdom*.[^20] The "stage of non-retrogression" (*avaivartika*) is the structurally pivotal stage of the bodhisattva path (the first ground and beyond), at which the practitioner cannot fall back to the two vehicles or to the worldling. The mark of *entering* is the *irreversibility* of what has been undertaken.

**The four verbs together constitute a closed circuit, not a linear ladder.** Why a circuit? Because the last verb, *enter*, is not a terminal endpoint at which the practitioner stops; it is the entry into a new vantage point from which the *first* verb, *open*, immediately acquires new content. A practitioner who has just entered the first ground will, in working her way to the second, third, and tenth grounds, repeat the entire *open–reveal–awaken–enter* circuit on every successive layer of refinement. Zhiyi's *Fahua wenju* fascicle 1 makes this explicit with a striking simile drawn from the *Mahāprajñāpāramitāśāstra*: "Just as the [Sanskrit] letter *a* at the beginning [of the alphabet] possesses the merit of all forty-one letters, so too the letter *ṭha* at the end possesses the merit of all the others, and the letters in the middle likewise; the letters are equal, the words are equal, the merits are equal."[^21] A bodhisattva at the *first* dwelling already possesses *all* the merits of the stage of equal awakening; conversely, a bodhisattva at equal awakening is still doing the *same* operation as the bodhisattva at the first dwelling. The progression through stages is not the addition of new content but the *more refined repetition of the same circuit*.

This loop structure is *structurally identical* to the four-phase contemplative loop that S2-P02 identified in the *Heart Sūtra*'s Avalokiteśvara verse: *illuminate the five aggregates as empty → examine → enter deeply into prajñā → liberate from all suffering*. The two structures map onto each other cleanly:

| Lotus: open/reveal/awaken/enter | Heart Sūtra: illuminate/examine/enter-deeply/liberate | Common structural position |
|---|---|---|
| *open* (uncover the covering) | *illuminate* (notice arising) | The starting point: from not-knowing to knowing |
| *reveal* (display content) | *examine* (the content stands forth) | The clarification of the object |
| *awaken to* (subject recognizes) | *enter deeply* (active deepening) | The migration of agency |
| *enter* (take up irreversibly) | *liberate from suffering* (results land) | The irreversibility of action |

The two structures are structurally identical not because the *Lotus* borrowed from the *Prajñāpāramitā* literature, nor because the *Prajñāpāramitā* anticipated the *Lotus*, but because *both are describing the same thing — the internal structure of the awakening event*. Awakening is not a one-time occurrence but a closed loop; the loop has four phases, none of which can be omitted; and the four phases occur in a definite order (uncover → display → recognize → take up). The Lotus's version amplifies this loop to the cosmological scale of *every buddha appearing in every world*; the *Heart Sūtra*'s version contracts the same loop to the moment-by-moment scale of *a meditator practicing the perfection of wisdom*. One contemplative loop, two scales.

Zhiyi's *Fahua wenju* fascicle 2 develops the structure even further with his *fourfold gloss* of *open, reveal, awaken, enter*: by stage (位), by wisdom (智), by gate (門), and by contemplation (觀). His conclusion is the dense formula: ***seeing the principle depends on the stage; the stage is established by wisdom; wisdom unfolds through the gate; the gate becomes accessible through contemplation.***[^22] The four glosses are not parallel alternatives; they are four orthogonal slices of the same loop. *Stage* gives us the temporal ordering of the bodhisattva's progress; *wisdom* gives us the cognitive capacity at each phase; *gate* gives us the entry points provided by different teachings; *contemplation* gives us the immediate first-person practice. Four dimensions, mutually supporting, reading the same circuit.

### 3.3 The argumentative structure of "merging the three vehicles into one"

The four-verb awakening loop is one of the two main arguments running through the *Upāyakauśalya* chapter; the other is the doctrine of *converging the three vehicles into one* (會三歸一). The two arguments do not merely happen to occur in the same chapter — they are two faces of a single doctrinal commitment.

The Lotus's argument for "converging the three vehicles into one" is constructed with extraordinary subtlety. It is not the Madhyamaka argument (which would directly negate the substantial existence of the three vehicles), and it is not the standard Yogācāra argument (which would ground the three vehicles in differences of innate disposition or *gotra*). It is a *dramatic-historical* argument with the following structure: *in the past* the Buddha taught the three vehicles, because the listeners' capacities were not uniform and required graduated guidance; *now* he teaches the one vehicle, because the time is ripe and the expedient teaching can be opened to reveal the true. The three vehicles were never *wrong*; they were simply *not ultimate*. Their not-being-ultimate is not because they had a defect intrinsic to themselves, but because they were *steps along a path whose endpoint was the one vehicle*, and to mistake them for the endpoint was to commit the only mistake possible.

The sūtra's grammatical sequencing follows this argumentative shape exactly. *Affirmation*: "the Tathāgatas only teach and transform bodhisattvas; whatever they do, they do constantly for one matter." *Exclusion*: "the Tathāgata teaches the Dharma to sentient beings only by means of the one buddha-vehicle; there is no other vehicle, neither two nor three." *Universalization*: "the Dharma of all the Buddhas in all ten directions is also like this." *Historical retrospection*: "the buddhas of the past, by means of innumerable countless skillful means, by means of various causes, parables, and verbal expressions, taught the Dharma to sentient beings — and *all those teachings were for the sake of the one buddha-vehicle*." The historical retrospection does the crucial work: it places the three vehicles back in their *role as historical pedagogy*, explicitly acknowledged as having occurred, but reframes their content in light of the now-disclosed endpoint.

The decisive move is to move "skillful means" from a *logical* register into a *narrative* register. If the relationship between three vehicles and one vehicle were a *logical* relationship, we would conclude "the three vehicles are false and the one vehicle is true," and we would instantly slide off into the trap the Lotus is most worried about: dismissing the historical *śrāvaka* and *pratyekabuddha* paths as worthless. But the relationship is *narrative*: the three vehicles are the *real history of pedagogy*, the one vehicle is the *real endpoint of that history*. The three vehicles *as history* are real; *as endpoints* they are not ultimate. This distinction is the heart of the *Lotus*'s concept of skillful means: ***skillful means are not deception; skillful means are the unavoidability of pedagogical history itself.***

From the standpoint of the Yogācāra three natures, this structure maps cleanly onto the *parikalpita → paratantra → pariniṣpanna* relationship:

- **The three vehicles (*śrāvaka, pratyekabuddha, bodhisattva*) = *parikalpita-svabhāva*.** The "substantiality" of these three vehicles is constructed by sentient beings on top of their own attachments and discriminations; from the buddha's perspective, the three vehicles are differences of pedagogical accommodation, not differences of any ultimate reality.
- **Parable, expedient teaching = *paratantra-svabhāva*.** The parable is not real and is not unreal; it is *dependently arisen, mutually conditioned, situational* — when a particular sentient being meets a particular parable at a particular moment, the parable performs its pedagogical function.
- **The one buddha-vehicle, the true mark of all dharmas = *pariniṣpanna-svabhāva*.** The perfectly accomplished is not an independent "thing"; it is *the perfection of the other-dependent*. When the imputational portion of the other-dependent is stripped away, what remains is the perfectly accomplished.

This three-nature mapping is precisely the core architecture of S7-P01's command-thesis I. §3.5 will demonstrate that this mapping is *explicitly and verbatim* established in Kuiji's *Fahua xuanzan* fascicle 4b for the "one great matter" passage. But before we move into the commentarial history, we need to address a subtle question: what *exactly* is the relationship between the *Lotus*'s "one buddha-vehicle" and the Āgamas' "single-vehicle path"?

### 3.4 Āgama seeds: the single-vehicle path and the *dharmadhātu* ever-abiding

As established in §II.甲2, the Lotus's "one great matter / one buddha-vehicle" teaching has two important seed-lines in the early Āgama corpus. One is a *lexical* seed (the phrase "single-vehicle path"); the other is a *structural* seed (the formula "the *dharmadhātu* ever-abides whether the tathāgata appears or not"). This subsection argues that these two seeds can neither be inflated into "doctrinal identity" with the Lotus's teaching nor deflated into "irrelevant coincidence." They are best understood as the *raw material* on which the Lotus performs a *cosmological dramatization*.

#### 3.4.1 The "single-vehicle path": a lexical seed under the principle of homonymous distinction

*Saṃyukta-āgama* 607 reads: "There is a single-vehicle path that purifies sentient beings, that enables them to surmount sorrow and grief, to extinguish anguish and suffering, and to attain the *dharma-as-it-truly-is* — namely, the four foundations of mindfulness." The grammatical parallelism with the Lotus's "one great matter" passage is striking when we lay them side by side:

| *Saṃyukta-āgama* 607 | *Lotus*, *Upāyakauśalya* |
|---|---|
| There is **one** single-vehicle path | **Only** for the **one** great matter and cause |
| Purifies sentient beings | Causes sentient beings to obtain purity |
| Surmounts sorrow, extinguishes suffering | Opens the buddha-knowledge-and-vision |
| Attains the *dharma-as-it-truly-is* | Enters the *path of buddha-knowledge-and-vision* |
| Namely, the four foundations of mindfulness | (One buddha-vehicle) |

The lexical correspondence is almost word-by-word: *one*, *purifies sentient beings*, *attains the dharma-as-it-truly-is*, and the rhetorical structure of "*X* — namely, *Y*" all appear in both. From the standpoint of rhetorical figure, the Lotus's "one great matter / one buddha-vehicle" can be read as *the same exclusive figure as the Āgama's "single-vehicle path / four foundations of mindfulness," lifted from the level of contemplative method to the level of total doctrine*. The Lotus enlarges *purifies sentient beings* from a goal of meditative training into the entire purpose of buddha-appearance; it replaces the specific *four foundations of mindfulness* with the totalizing *one buddha-vehicle*.

But — and this is the central point — *the referents of the two formulas are entirely different*. The Āgama's "single-vehicle path" refers, with no ambiguity, to the four foundations of mindfulness: a specific, operable, contemplative method situated at the level of *practice*. The Lotus's "one buddha-vehicle" refers to a structural, totalizing, comprehensive teaching-concept situated at the level of *doctrine*. The first answers "*what should I practice*"; the second answers "*what is the teaching about*." One is a method-level instruction at the scale of an individual practitioner; the other is a doctrine-level statement at the scale of buddha-pedagogy as a whole.

⚠️ It would be a serious error simply to identify the two. This is a textbook example of *homonymous distinction* (同名異義 *tóng míng yì yì*) — the same lexical compound, occurring in two different doctrinal systems, refers to entirely different content. This project encountered an exactly parallel case in S3-SUP01, where the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra*'s "four preparatory stages" (四加行) share their name with the Yogācāra's "four wholesome roots" (四善根) but carry fundamentally different content. The standard methodological response in such cases is *not* to force the two into identity, *nor* to dismiss the connection as accidental, but to display them as two cases of the *same structural form filled with different content*.

So in what sense, then, is the Āgama's "single-vehicle path" a "seed" of the Lotus's "one buddha-vehicle"? It is a seed in the sense of providing three things: *first*, the legitimacy of an *exclusive rhetorical figure* in the Buddha's own speech (only this one path, no other); *second*, the syntactic position of *purifies sentient beings* as a teleological verb attached to that figure; *third*, the traditional pairing of *one* with *vehicle* (or *path*). What the Lotus does is to *expand* this figure from the contemplative-method level to the doctrinal-totalization level, replacing the specific four foundations with the abstract one buddha-vehicle. *This expansion is itself the dramaturgical move characteristic of the* Mahāyāna sūtra *literature*: take a structurally tight formulation from the early teaching and elevate it from individual practice to cosmic theology, without losing the original structure.

#### 3.4.2 The *dharmadhātu* ever-abiding: a structural seed and its productive tension

The second seed-line is the deeper one. Recall the passage from *Saṃyukta-āgama* 299:

> "The teaching of dependent arising was not made by me, nor by anyone else. Whether tathāgatas appear in the world or do not appear in the world, the *dharmadhātu* ever-abides; the tathāgata, having himself awakened to this Dharma, attains complete and perfect awakening, and for the sake of sentient beings he discriminates and expounds, opens up and reveals it."

This passage carries doctrinal weight that the lexical seed does not. It directly addresses a philosophical question: *if the* dharmadhātu *ever-abides, if dependent arising is intrinsically there, what exactly does the appearance of a tathāgata in the world add to it?* The answer the Āgama gives is precise and clean. The tathāgata does *not create* dependent arising; he *self-awakens to* it (becomes conscious of it), and then *for the sake of sentient beings discriminates, expounds, opens up, and reveals* it (explains it to others). The existence of the Dharma does not depend on any tathāgata; but the *expounding, opening, and revealing* of the Dharma — which is to say, *the pedagogical event* — does require the tathāgata. The tathāgata's appearance is *pedagogically* necessary, not *ontologically* necessary.

Carry this structure back to the Lotus's "one great matter" passage and a remarkable thing happens. The Lotus's *gù* (故, "for the sake of," "because") in the sentence "the buddhas appear in the world *only for the sake of* one great matter" turns out to occupy *exactly the same syntactic position* as the Āgama's transition from "self-awakened to this Dharma" to "discriminates and expounds for sentient beings." The tathāgata's appearance is not for the *creation* of something that did not exist, but for the *displaying* of something that already exists. The Lotus's four verbs *open, reveal, awaken to, enter*, properly understood, correspond structurally to the Āgama's *discriminate, expound, open up, reveal*:

| *Saṃyukta-āgama* 299 | *Lotus*, *Upāyakauśalya* |
|---|---|
| The tathāgata self-awakens to this Dharma | (The buddha-wisdom; the buddha-knowledge-and-vision itself, presupposed but not stated) |
| Discriminates for sentient beings | *Open* the buddha-knowledge-and-vision (uncover the covering) |
| Expounds | *Reveal* the buddha-knowledge-and-vision (display the content) |
| Opens up | *Awaken to* the buddha-knowledge-and-vision (cause sentient beings to recognize) |
| Reveals | *Enter into* the path of the buddha-knowledge-and-vision (cause them to take it up) |

The correspondence is not word-for-word — the Āgama does not contain the four characters "*open, reveal, awaken, enter*" — but the *structure* is isomorphic. In both passages, the Tathāgata in the context of an already-abiding Dharma performs four sequential pedagogical actions; the four actions have the same semantic direction; the same agent and the same recipient; and the same goal (the recipient's coming-into-purity / coming-into-the-buddha-knowledge-and-vision).

The deeper convergence: *the Āgama's "*dharmadhātu* ever-abiding" is exactly the answer to the question* §3.1 *raised but did not answer — namely, why does the Buddha not enter parinirvāṇa immediately upon his own awakening?* Kuiji's gloss said "were it not so, he would simply enter nirvāṇa as the two vehicles do" — meaning that the only thing preventing the Buddha from immediately entering nirvāṇa upon his own awakening is the presence of "this one great matter." *Why?* Because *the Dharma is already complete* (the *dharmadhātu* ever-abides). The Buddha's not-entering-nirvāṇa is not because the Dharma is incomplete and needs him to finish it; it is because *sentient beings do not yet know that the Dharma is already complete*, and need him to display it to them. The Āgama emphasizes that the Dharma is already-there (the dimension of *essence* / *abiding*); the Lotus emphasizes why the buddha appears anyway (the dimension of *function* / *teaching*); the two together constitute the full *essence-function* (體用) description of the same situation.

❌ Here a common misreading must be corrected at once. There exists a certain modern reading of the *Lotus*'s "the buddhas appear in the world only for the sake of one great matter" that takes "appear in the world" to mean "*are sent into the world*" — as if the buddhas were envoys dispatched by some higher reality, called forth by an external "one great matter" that exists independently of them. This is a slide that imports the explanatory framework of theistic incarnation into a Buddhist text where it does not belong. *Saṃyukta-āgama* 299's "*dharmadhātu* ever-abiding" is the doctrinal shield against this slide: dependent arising is *not made by me, nor by anyone else*. The tathāgata's appearance does not *create* anything new; it is the meeting-point of his compassion and sentient beings' capacities at a particular historical moment. The "cause" in "great matter and cause" is *bidirectional resonance* between the Buddha and sentient beings, *not* a unilateral summons from outside.

#### 3.4.3 The latent dissolution of the "*Mahāyāna* is not the Buddha's word" worry

One of S7's methodological purposes — explicit in the planning document but quietly operational throughout — is *not* to enter the historical debate about whether the Mahāyāna sūtras are "what the Buddha actually taught," but to lay out the Āgama seed-lines for each major Lotus teaching one by one, so that the Lotus quietly returns to its rightful place along the continuous line of Dharma transmission. The work of the present subsection is the first formal exercise of that methodology in the S7 series.

After working through the two seed-lines, any reader familiar with the Āgamas will see that the Lotus's teaching of "one great matter / open-reveal-awaken-enter / one buddha-vehicle" is *neither* a Mahāyāna invention dropped from the sky *nor* a deviation from the early teaching, but *a dramatic amplification of certain core rhetorical figures, argumentative structures, and teleological framings that already exist in the Āgamas*. The word "dramatic" needs to be read precisely: it does not mean "exaggerated" or "distorted"; it means "*lifted from the order of practice to the order of cosmology and doctrine.*" The Āgama's "single-vehicle path" is contemplative advice given to individual *bhikṣus*; the Lotus's "one buddha-vehicle" is a cosmological declaration to all sentient beings in all worlds in all times. The first is at the scale of individual practice, the second at the scale of universal pedagogy — but *both say the same thing: there is only one path*.

### 3.5 Commentarial history and the three-natures mapping: Vasubandhu → Zhiyi → Kuiji

From Vasubandhu (5th-century India) to Kuiji (7th-century Tang Chang'an), the *open-reveal-awaken-enter* passage was reworked through at least three distinct layers of commentarial elaboration. Each layer used the conceptual tools of its own tradition to reorganize the four verbs, and the cumulative effect of three layers stacked on top of one another is to align this loose four-verb formula tightly with the Yogācāra structure of *āśraya-parāvṛtti* across the three natures. This subsection unfolds the three layers in chronological order and argues that the common pivot of all three is the other-dependent nature, while the common goal of all three is the perfectly accomplished — exactly the three-tier genealogy structure that S7-P01 first established.

#### 3.5.1 Vasubandhu's *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka-upadeśa*: the fourfold-meaning architecture

Vasubandhu's analysis of "one great matter" in *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka-upadeśa* fascicle 2 reads:

> "[The phrase] 'one great matter' should be understood according to four meanings. What are the four? (1) The *unsurpassed meaning*: only the all-knowing wisdom of the tathāgata can know it, there is no other matter… (2) The *meaning of sameness*: namely, that the dharma-bodies of the *śrāvakas*, *pratyekabuddhas*, and buddhas are equal. As the sūtra says, 'Wishing to reveal the buddha-knowledge-and-vision to sentient beings, [the buddhas] appear in the world.' Dharma-body equality means: the buddha-nature and the dharma-body know no differentiation. (3) The *meaning of not-knowing*: namely, that the *śrāvakas*, *pratyekabuddhas*, and so on cannot know its true place. To 'not know its true place' means: not to know that there is, in the end, only the one buddha-vehicle… (4) [The meaning of] *causing them to attain the stage of non-retrogression*, displaying the wish to bestow on them the karma of immeasurable wisdom."[^23]

The four meanings map onto the four verbs:

- *Open* = the unsurpassed meaning: buddha-wisdom is unsurpassed, knowable to none other than buddhas
- *Reveal* = the meaning of sameness: the dharma-bodies of all three vehicles are equal, originally so, only covered over and not yet displayed
- *Awaken to* = the meaning of not-knowing: sentient beings do not know that there is, ultimately, only the one vehicle, and need to be brought to recognition
- *Enter* = causing them to attain the stage of non-retrogression: bringing sentient beings into the stage at and beyond the first ground from which they cannot fall back

What deserves the closest attention is this: ***Vasubandhu's entire passage uses no three-natures language whatsoever.*** His structural framework is "*dharma-body equality*" — taking the dharma-body as a common substrate, glossing *reveal* as *sameness*, and attributing the differences among the three vehicles to "not-knowing" rather than to "inequality." This is the characteristic vocabulary of the Indian *tathāgatagarbha* and *dharmakāya* tradition. S7-P01 had already proposed: Vasubandhu chooses *different doctrinal vocabularies* in different scriptural contexts (the three natures in his *Mahāyānasaṃgraha-bhāṣya*, dharma-body equality in the *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka-upadeśa*), and this choice is a *register-conscious* decision rather than a self-contradiction.

The four-meaning architecture Vasubandhu provides becomes the foundational frame that every subsequent Chinese commentator inherits. Zhiyi, Jizang, and Kuiji *all* cite these four meanings as their starting point. The differences among them are differences of how one *builds onto* Vasubandhu's frame.

#### 3.5.2 Zhiyi's *Fahua wenju*: the fourfold gloss

Zhiyi's treatment of *open, reveal, awaken, enter* in *Fahua wenju* fascicle 2 first criticizes earlier "partial glosses" of the four verbs, then accepts Vasubandhu's four meanings as the basic frame, and then adds his own *fourfold gloss* under four orthogonal categories: *by stage, by wisdom, by gate, by contemplation*. Each category produces a new four-step parallel:

- **By stage**: *open* = the ten dwellings (*shízhù*, breaking ignorance and opening the *tathāgatagarbha*); *reveal* = the ten practices (the various merits of the *dharmadhātu* are displayed); *awaken to* = the ten dedications (event and principle merge); *enter* = the ten grounds (sovereign self-flowing).
- **By wisdom**: *open* = path-wisdom (*dào huì*); *reveal* = path-species wisdom (*dào zhǒng huì*); *awaken to* = the all-knowing wisdom (*yī qiè zhì*); *enter* = the all-knowing-of-all-aspects wisdom (*yī qiè zhǒng zhì*).
- **By gate**: *open* = the gate of emptiness; *reveal* = the gate of being; *awaken to* = the gate of both-emptiness-and-being; *enter* = the gate of neither-emptiness-nor-being.
- **By contemplation**: *open* = clarified purity; *reveal* = the discrimination of empty/conventional/middle; *awaken to* = the three as one; *enter* = the simultaneous illumination of empty, conventional, and middle.[^24]

The conclusion of this fourfold gloss is the dense formula: ***seeing the principle depends on the stage; the stage is established by wisdom; wisdom unfolds through the gate; the gate becomes accessible through contemplation.***[^25] The four glosses are not parallel alternatives but *mutually supporting orthogonal slices* of the same loop. *Stage* is the temporal axis (the bodhisattva's career through the doctrinal levels). *Wisdom* is the cognitive axis (the increasing precision of insight). *Gate* is the doctrinal axis (the different teaching-entry-points). *Contemplation* is the immediate first-person practice axis. Four dimensions, mutually supporting, all reading the same single circuit.

Zhiyi's teacher Huisi had already proposed the simpler *four-position gloss*: "*Open* the buddha-knowledge-and-vision is the ten dwellings; *reveal* is the ten practices; *awaken to* is the ten dedications; *enter* is the ten grounds and the stage of equal awakening. Both 'knowledge' and 'vision' are spoken of as buddha because [the practitioner] attains the all-knowing-of-all-aspects wisdom and the buddha-eye."[^26] This four-position gloss became the foundation of Tiantai's doctrinal-staging system.

Two key differences from Vasubandhu deserve attention. First, Zhiyi uses *staging language* — he assigns the four verbs to specific bodhisattva stages (the ten dwellings, ten practices, etc.). Vasubandhu's four meanings are abstract doctrinal judgments; Zhiyi's four positions are concrete progress-markers along the bodhisattva path. Second, Zhiyi uses *multiple slicing* — the same four-verb structure can be cut by stage, by wisdom, by gate, or by contemplation. Each slicing is complete on its own terms but they are all complementary. This is the characteristic Tiantai methodology of *yuán róng* (perfect interfusion).

But Zhiyi is himself careful to insist that the four positions, four wisdoms, four gates, and four contemplations are *not* a linear ladder. In *Fahua wenju* fascicle 1 he says explicitly: "A bodhisattva at the first dwelling can divide his body into a hundred world-systems and become a buddha there… A bodhisattva at the first dwelling, having opened the buddha-knowledge-and-vision, has knowledge-and-vision the same as that of the buddhas, and is therefore praised by the buddhas."[^27] At the *first* dwelling — the very moment of "opening" — the bodhisattva's knowledge-and-vision is *already the same as the buddhas'*. The subsequent stages of ten practices, ten dedications, and ten grounds are not "places not yet reached"; they are "the same single circuit unfolding at progressively finer layers." This is exactly the loop-not-ladder reading of §3.2.

#### 3.5.3 Kuiji's *Fahua xuanzan*: reading the one vehicle through the three natures

Kuiji's *Fahua xuanzan* (T1723) treats the "one great matter" passage in two crucial places. In fascicle 3b he cites Vasubandhu's four meanings verbatim and glosses each one in detail. In fascicle 4b he then performs a register-shift unprecedented in the prior commentarial tradition: he reads the entire "one vehicle" doctrine through the structural lens of the three natures. This second move provides the strongest text-anchored support for command-thesis I that the entire Lotus commentarial tradition contains.

The core passage in fascicle 4b reads:

> "Suchness — the marvelous principle — has a substance and nature that ever-abides; the buddha is able to realize-and-know it. The *imagined* nature — the substantial nature of the two selves of person and dharma — is non-existent; because the buddha realizes the principle of selflessness, he also knows that this non-existence is what worldlings empty-falsely cling to. As for 'the buddha-seed arises from conditions': this means that the *uncontaminated other-dependent* — the seed of the reward-body buddha — is born from causes-and-conditions and arises from conditions, because it is shaped by cultivation. By means of realizing the principle of suchness, [the buddha] severs the contaminated portion of the other-dependent that the cognitive mind clings to, knows that what was clung to is non-existent, and from the *pure portion* of the other-dependent — the conditions for the cultivation of the buddha-seed — proceeds for the sake of the one vehicle… ***Now, this other-dependent is awakening, and the perfectly accomplished is nirvāṇa, together constituting the one vehicle; because the perfectly accomplished is the more excellent of the two, it alone is named the one vehicle.*** By severing the contaminated other-dependent and dispelling the imputed-and-clung-to, [the buddha] realizes these two fruits."[^28]

This passage requires phrase-by-phrase unpacking.

*"Suchness — the marvelous principle — has a substance and nature that ever-abides."* This is Kuiji building the foundational floor of the analysis: suchness (the perfectly accomplished, *pariniṣpanna*) is ever-abiding, intrinsically there. Note that this language deliberately echoes the Āgama's "*dharmadhātu* ever-abiding" — Kuiji is doing the same structural move as *Saṃyukta-āgama* 299.

*"The imagined nature — the substantial nature of the two selves of person and dharma — is non-existent."* The buddha realizes suchness and therefore *knows* that the imputational clinging to a self of person and a self of dharma is empty. The character *parikalpita* is now explicitly named.

*"The buddha-seed arises from conditions: the *uncontaminated other-dependent*, the seed of the reward-body buddha, is born from causes-and-conditions."* The line "the buddha-seed arises from conditions" is taken directly from the verse-section of the *Upāyakauśalya* chapter ("All the buddhas, two-footed honored ones, know that the dharmas are constantly without nature; the buddha-seed arises from conditions; therefore [we] speak of the one vehicle"[^29]). Kuiji glosses *the buddha-seed* with extreme precision as "*the uncontaminated other-dependent*" — that is, the *pure portion* of the *paratantra*. The buddha's seed is not arising out of nowhere; it is *paratantra* — but not the contaminated portion, the *pure portion*.

*"By severing the contaminated portion of the other-dependent that the cognitive mind clings to, [the buddha] knows that what was clung to is non-existent."* The path to becoming a buddha is to *sever the contaminated other-dependent*, *dispel the imputed-and-clung-to*, and *realize these two fruits*.

The decisive sentence is: ***"Now, this other-dependent is awakening, and the perfectly accomplished is nirvāṇa, together constituting the one vehicle; because the perfectly accomplished is the more excellent of the two, it alone is named the one vehicle."***

*The other-dependent is awakening* — meaning that, once the contaminated portion has been severed, the *pure portion of the other-dependent* = *bodhi* (the wisdom of awakening itself).
*The perfectly accomplished is nirvāṇa* — meaning that, once the imputational has been dispelled, the *pariniṣpanna* = *nirvāṇa* (the cessation realized as suchness).
*Together constituting the one vehicle* — these two together constitute "the one vehicle."
*The perfectly accomplished is the more excellent of the two; it alone is named the one vehicle* — but because the *pariniṣpanna* is more ultimate, the name "one vehicle" is, in the end, given to it alone.

Carry this structure back to the *Upāyakauśalya* passage's four verbs:

| *Open / reveal / awaken / enter* | Three-natures correspondence | Kuiji's language |
|---|---|---|
| *Open* | dispel the imputed | "sever the obstruction-of-the-knowable, the karmic actions and karmic results that follow… fully realize *bodhi*" |
| *Reveal* | display the pure portion of the *paratantra* | "reveal to sentient beings the nature of the buddha-knowledge-and-vision; all three vehicles equally have it" |
| *Awaken to* | recognize that *paratantra* is *bodhi* | "awaken to the fact that, in the end, there is only this one buddha-bodhi-wisdom" |
| *Enter* | realize the *pariniṣpanna* = *nirvāṇa* | "in order to cause [them] to attain the stage of non-retrogression" |

This correspondence is supported directly by Kuiji's own commentarial words — it is not a retroactive gloss imposed on the text but a reading that the text itself sustains. The three-tier genealogy of S7-P01 — Vasubandhu → Jizang → Kuiji, with the common pivot of the other-dependent and the common goal of the perfectly accomplished — is, in the case of the "one great matter" passage, *directly textually demonstrable*.

#### 3.5.4 The common pivot: from *paratantra* to *pariniṣpanna*

Place the three commentators side by side and a clear pattern emerges:

| Commentator | Conceptual framework | Reading of "one great matter" |
|---|---|---|
| Vasubandhu (India, 5th c.) | dharma-body equality | *open / reveal / awaken / enter* = four meanings (unsurpassed / sameness / not-knowing / non-retrogression) |
| Zhiyi (China, 6th c., Tiantai) | fourfold gloss by stage/wisdom/gate/contemplation | *open / reveal / awaken / enter* = the perfectly interfused multidimensional slicing of a single circuit |
| Kuiji (China, 7th c., Faxiang) | three natures structure | *open / reveal / awaken / enter* = "the other-dependent is *bodhi*, the perfectly accomplished is nirvāṇa" |

The three commentators use *different* conceptual tools — Vasubandhu uses dharma-body equality, Zhiyi uses staging/wisdom/gate/contemplation, Kuiji uses the three-natures structure of *āśraya-parāvṛtti* — but ***all three share the same pivot, the other-dependent nature, and the same goal, the perfectly accomplished nature.*** Vasubandhu's "sameness" (i.e., dharma-body equality) is in fact just the *pure portion of the other-dependent* (uncontaminated *paratantra*); Zhiyi's "stages" are in fact just the *differential phases of the other-dependent* (the various levels of *xíng* / practice); Kuiji's "the other-dependent is *bodhi*, the perfectly accomplished is nirvāṇa" makes this hidden line completely explicit.

This is the second confirmation of the three-tier genealogy that S7-P01 first proposed. Command-thesis I — that the Lotus's *expedient → real* relation maps onto the Yogācāra *parikalpita → paratantra → pariniṣpanna* with *paratantra* as pivot — is not a postmodern overlay imposed by this project on the *Lotus*; it is a hermeneutic result that the three layers of commentary themselves drove forward, one step at a time, over a millennium.

⚠️ One boundary needs to be marked at once. This three-tier genealogy is a *hermeneutic* continuity, *not an authorial-intent* continuity. Vasubandhu in the *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka-upadeśa* did not himself use three-natures language; the dharma-body-equality framework is not "waiting" for Kuiji to "complete" it with the three natures. Zhiyi was not "preparing the way" for Kuiji's three-natures reading. Each of the three was working independently within his own tradition; the convergence to which they all contributed arose because *they were all responding to the same passage, and the structure of the passage itself directed their readings*. To describe this genealogy as "evolution" or as "Indian Yogācāra extending into China" would be over-interpretation. The more accurate description is *structural convergence*: a single passage subjected to independent commentarial labor in different traditions, finally producing a hermeneutic confluence. Command-thesis I points to the convergence; it does not invent a linear historical development from Vasubandhu to Kuiji.

### 3.6 The three-layer synthesis: Āgama, Lotus, Yogācāra

We can now state a compact synthesis. *On the question of "what, exactly, is awakening," the Āgamas, the* Lotus, *and the Yogācāra commentarial tradition each provide one face of the answer; only when the three are taken together do we have the full description.*

- **The Āgama layer** provides the *methodological seed*. Awakening is *practicing the four foundations of mindfulness* — "there is one path, the single-vehicle path, that purifies sentient beings… namely, the four foundations of mindfulness." This is the most concrete, most immediately operable answer. The Āgamas tell us *how* to do it: the specific contemplative method, the specific mental objects, the specific bodily and mental disciplines.
- **The *Lotus* layer** provides the *cosmological amplification*. Awakening is the unique purpose of every buddha's appearance in every world — "the Buddhas, the World-Honored Ones, appear in the world only for the sake of one great matter and cause… to cause sentient beings to open, reveal, awaken to, and enter the buddha-knowledge-and-vision." This is the most expansive, most dramatically charged answer. The *Lotus* tells us *why* it matters: its place in the cosmological order, its universal significance for all sentient beings, its essential relation to the very fact of buddha-appearance.
- **The Yogācāra commentarial layer** provides the *fine-grained mechanism*. Awakening is the *transformation of the basis* (*āśraya-parāvṛtti*): the dispelling of the imputational portion of the contaminated other-dependent, and the manifestation of the pure other-dependent that is *bodhi* and the perfectly accomplished that is *nirvāṇa*. This is the most precise, most structurally articulated answer. The Yogācāra tells us *the internal mechanism*: which mental structures are operating, where the contaminated-to-pure transition occurs, why an event must have exactly four phases and not more or fewer.

The three layers constitute, taken together, a complete description of the awakening event from practice (Āgama) through vision (*Lotus*) to internal mechanics (Yogācāra). No single layer suffices on its own. With only the Āgamas, you understand the method but not why it matters. With only the *Lotus*, you grasp the vision but not what to *do*. With only Yogācāra, you have the structure but lose the historical and pedagogical meaning of the teaching.

The reason the *Upāyakauśalya* chapter's "one great matter" passage is the *meeting-point* of all three layers is precisely that it touches all three at once: it is *method* (it points back to the four foundations and forward to all subsequent practices), it is *vision* (the single purpose of all buddha-appearance), and it is *structure* (it can be read precisely through the three-natures map). This is also why Tiantai chose this passage as the structural pivot of the Lotus's "trace gate" — it is not the *Lotus*'s prelude but its *structural genome*. The seven famous parables and the twenty-odd subsequent chapters of pedagogy are *all the unfolding of this passage*.

## IV. Interdisciplinary Bridge: Closed-Loop Feedback in Cybernetics and the Awakening Loop

A reader familiar with systems theory or cybernetics may, at this point in the reading, recognize a familiar structure within the four-phase loop of *open, reveal, awaken, enter*: the *closed-loop feedback system*.

The closed-loop feedback system has four components in a cycle: **sensor → comparator → effector → output → back to sensor**. The system is not linear from input to output; rather, it feeds the output back into the sensor so that the system can continuously self-correct on the basis of actual results. The classical illustration is the thermostat: a temperature sensor reads the room temperature, compares it with the setpoint, and if there is a deviation activates the heating or cooling system; the heating or cooling changes the room temperature; the changed temperature is read back into the sensor. The system is a closed loop with no clear "beginning" or "end."

The *open-reveal-awaken-enter* structure has several points of correspondence with this:

| Closed-loop feedback | Awakening loop |
|---|---|
| sensor (perception) | *open* (uncover the covering, receive) |
| comparator | *reveal* (display content, comparison) |
| effector | *awaken to* (subject acknowledges) |
| output | *enter* (take up irreversibly) |
| feedback | After *enter*, a new round of *open* begins on a refined layer |

Both structures are non-linear, both are circular and self-correcting, both require all four components without omission, and both demand that the final component return to the first in order for the system to maintain itself. From this angle, the *Lotus*'s *open-reveal-awaken-enter* has, in the contemporary systems-theoretic vocabulary, a structural analogue — it is not isolated, mysterious, or uniquely religious.

Three layers of fundamental difference must, however, be flagged immediately.

*First, the goals are different*. The closed-loop feedback system's goal is to *keep the system near the setpoint* — that is, to maintain stability. The awakening loop's goal is *not* to maintain stability; it is to *irreversibly transform the very obstruction-of-the-knowable structure of the system itself*. Awakening is *transformation of the basis*, not thermostat regulation. The mark of awakening's success is not "remaining at a state" but "*irreversibly leaving* a state" (entering the stage of non-retrogression). These are two diametrically opposite design goals.

*Second, the subject-object structure is different*. Cybernetics treats the system as a black box: the observer (designer) is *outside* the system, and the system itself does not need to "know" that it is being observed. The awakening loop's *awaken to* phase, however, requires *the observer to be the observed*. The subject capable of observing and the object being observed must, in their structure, *coincide* — otherwise *awakening to* cannot occur, because the content of "awakening to" is precisely "*the observing subject itself*." These are two completely different epistemic structures.

*Third, the content of "enter" is different*. The "output" of the closed-loop feedback system is the transmission of information or a physical action. The "enter" of the awakening loop is *the assumption of action*: not merely outputting a result, but *taking the entire content as one's own mode of existence*. The verb *enter* in "enter the path of buddha-knowledge-and-vision" is dynamic, irreversible, and committal. There is no concept in cybernetics that corresponds.

⚠️ ***Irreducibility statement***: the closed-loop feedback system in cybernetics and the *open-reveal-awaken-enter* awakening loop are *structural heuristic analogues*, *not theoretical equivalents*. Awakening cannot be reduced to a feedback-control model; the closed-loop feedback is only one entry-point through which a contemporary reader, working within a familiar conceptual vocabulary, may glimpse the *loop-rather-than-ladder* structural intuition. Once that intuition is established, the cybernetic scaffolding must be dismantled and the reader allowed to encounter the *Lotus* directly. Any attempt to reduce "awakening" to "self-correcting system" will lose precisely what is most important in the *Upāyakauśalya* chapter.

## V. Middle-Path Corrections

**❌ Correction 1: The one buddha-vehicle ≠ Lotus sectarian supremacy.**

The Lotus's "there is no other vehicle, neither two nor three" has been read, throughout history, as "the *Lotus* is the king of all sūtras, and all other sūtras are merely the second and third vehicles serving as preparation." This is a structural misreading. The Lotus's "one" is "*one*" in the sense of *pedagogical function* — pointing to the one root purpose of all buddha-pedagogy (causing sentient beings to *open, reveal, awaken, enter* the buddha-knowledge-and-vision). It is *not* "*one*" in the sense of *ranking sūtras higher and lower*. As S7's planning document has already argued, the seven major sūtras (Prajñāpāramitā, *Avataṃsaka*, *Śūraṅgama*, *Lotus*, *Saṃdhinirmocana*, *Laṅkāvatāra*, *Mahāparinirvāṇa*) each carry distinct pedagogical functions; the *Lotus*'s "convergence" (攝) is not a hierarchical superiority but a functional gathering. To read "no other vehicle" as "Lotus is higher than the other sūtras" is precisely to commit the very error the Lotus is itself trying to correct — what Vasubandhu in his analysis of the seven topsy-turvy graspings names "the conceited overestimation of having actual merit."[^30]

**❌ Correction 2: *Open, reveal, awaken, enter* ≠ a linear ladder.**

Zhiyi's "by-stage" gloss in *Fahua wenju* (open = ten dwellings, reveal = ten practices, awaken = ten dedications, enter = ten grounds) is easily misread as a linear ladder from the tenth dwelling up to the tenth ground — as if a practitioner had to *finish* "opening" before being allowed to begin "revealing," and must finish that before "awakening to," and so on. This is wrong. As §3.2 already argued, *open, reveal, awaken, enter* is a *closed loop, not a linear ladder*; every stage is itself a complete *open-reveal-awaken-enter* loop, only with different content at different depths. Zhiyi himself was explicit on this: a bodhisattva at the *first* dwelling, at the very moment of "opening," already has knowledge-and-vision the same as the buddhas'. The advance through the stages is not "what the next stage has that the previous stage lacked" but "the same loop running again at a more refined layer of content."

**❌ Correction 3: The Āgama "single-vehicle path" ≠ the *Lotus* "one buddha-vehicle."**

As argued in detail in §3.4.1, this is a textbook *homonymous distinction*: the Āgama's "single-vehicle path" refers to the four foundations of mindfulness (a specific contemplative method); the *Lotus*'s "one buddha-vehicle" refers to the encompassing teaching that comprehends all three vehicles (a structural doctrinal concept). To identify the two as doctrinally equivalent is wrong; to dismiss the connection between them as accidental is also wrong. The standard methodological response is to display them as two cases of the same *structural form filled with different content*, neither collapsing nor severing the relationship.

**❌ Correction 4: Kuiji's three-natures reading ≠ Vasubandhu's authorial intent.**

S7-P01 had already argued: Vasubandhu in the *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka-upadeśa* did not use three-natures language; his core framework was "dharma-body equality." Kuiji's reading of the "one vehicle" through the three natures in *Fahua xuanzan* fascicle 4b is the hermeneutic achievement of Chinese Yogācāra (Faxiang), not the original intention of Vasubandhu himself. The "Vasubandhu → Zhiyi → Kuiji" three-tier genealogy proposed in §3.5 is *structural convergence*, not "evolutionary continuation." To describe this genealogy as "Indian Yogācāra extending its line into China" is over-interpretation. The more accurate description: "the same passage was independently reworked in different commentarial traditions and finally arrived at a structural confluence." Command-thesis I has its value in *pointing to the convergence*, not in inventing a linear historical development from Vasubandhu to Kuiji.

**❌ Correction 5: "Appearing in the world" ≠ a creationist or theistic incarnation.**

The *Lotus*'s "the Buddhas, the World-Honored Ones, appear in the world only for the sake of one great matter and cause" can, in the hands of certain modern readers, generate a misreading: the "appearing in the world" gets read as "*the descent of a deity*," as if the Buddha were an envoy dispatched from some higher reality and summoned by an external "great matter." This is an interpretive framework imported from Christian or Hindu mythology and *does not belong to Buddhism*. *Saṃyukta-āgama* 299's "*dharmadhātu* ever-abiding" is precisely the doctrinal shield against this slide: dependent arising is *not made by me, nor by anyone else*; the tathāgata's appearance does *not create* any new *dharma*; he merely "*self-awakens to this Dharma, and for the sake of sentient beings discriminates and expounds, opens up and reveals it.*" The tathāgata's appearance is a *pedagogical event*, not an *ontological event*; it is the meeting-point of compassion and capacity at a particular historical moment, not the descent of a higher being. To read "one great matter and cause" as "sacred summons" is to misalign the entire doctrinal structure of the *Upāyakauśalya* chapter — and the correct reading is that "one great matter" *is* the inherent weight of the very fact of *sentient beings' awakening*; it is not external to the buddha, nor external to sentient beings.

## VI. Conclusion

This paper has, taking the *Upāyakauśalya* chapter's "one great matter and cause" passage as its core, completed three pieces of work. First, it has argued that the four verbs *open, reveal, awaken, enter* form a *closed awakening loop rather than a linear ladder*, and has established the structural isomorphism between this loop and the four-phase contemplative loop S2-P02 identified in the *Heart Sūtra*'s Avalokiteśvara verse. Second, it has located two seed-lines of the Lotus teaching in the early Āgama corpus — the *lexical seed* of "single-vehicle path" and the *structural seed* of "the *dharmadhātu* ever-abides" — and used the principle of homonymous distinction to clarify that the Lotus and the Āgamas are connected by structural continuity, not by doctrinal identity. Third, it has unfolded the three layers of the commentarial history from Vasubandhu through Zhiyi to Kuiji and demonstrated that the common pivot of this genealogy is the other-dependent nature, the common goal is the perfectly accomplished, and Kuiji's *Fahua xuanzan* fascicle 4b — "the [pure portion of] the other-dependent is awakening, the perfectly accomplished is nirvāṇa, together constituting the one vehicle" — is the strongest text-anchored support for command-thesis I that can be found anywhere on the "one great matter" passage.

**Practical guidance.** For the daily practitioner, the most direct gift of this paper is the structural insight of *loop-rather-than-ladder*. When you sit in meditation and encounter an affliction, a stray thought, a stretch of dullness — do not read it as "I have regressed" or "I have not yet reached that stage." Read it as "*the loop is starting again*": the affliction itself is a new *opening*; the awareness that follows is *revealing*; the recognition that follows is *awakening to*; the letting-go that follows is *entering*. Every affliction, every distraction, every fall is the same awakening loop running again at a finer layer. The advance through the stages is not "becoming further from affliction"; it is "*on each occasion of affliction, running the loop more skillfully, more cleanly, more effortlessly.*" This is the *Lotus*'s most direct gift to daily practice: it does not require you to attain some distant stage; it requires you, in this single instant of awareness, to walk a complete loop.

**Open question.** This paper has argued for the structure of "one buddha-vehicle" and "convergence of the three vehicles," but it has not handled one crucial sub-question: *if there is, ultimately, only the one buddha-vehicle, why did the buddhas spend so long "discriminating and teaching three"? Why is skillful means unavoidable?* The tension between skillful means and the real requires a concrete parable to dramatize. The next paper in this series, S7-P03, will handle the most representative — and the most easily misread — of the seven Lotus parables: the parable of the burning house. The parable of the burning house will answer directly the question this paper has left open: *skillful means is not deception; skillful means is the only communication channel available within the burning house*.
