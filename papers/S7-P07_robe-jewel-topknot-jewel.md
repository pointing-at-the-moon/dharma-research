## Abstract

This paper examines two parable-jewels of the *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka-sūtra*: the Robe-Jewel parable (Ch. 8) and the Topknot-Jewel parable (Ch. 14), advancing three theses. **Thesis I**: the Robe-Jewel is not merely a lyrical trope for buddha-nature but the Lotus Sūtra's most structurally articulated expression of *tathāgatagarbha* thought — its three-moment form (intrinsic → occluded → disclosed) is isomorphic with the *Laṅkāvatāra* and the *Ratnagotravibhāga*, and is explicitly positioned within Vasubandhu's seven-conceit taxonomy in the *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka-upadeśa*; Kuiji's Yogācāra reading further identifies robe with the sixth consciousness (*manovijñāna*), fastening with perfuming (*vāsanā*), and jewel with the intrinsic uncontaminated seeds of the four wisdoms. **Thesis II**: the Topknot-Jewel encodes a doctrine of timing, not esoterism — the wheel-turning king's restraint is protection of the listener's capacity to bear the supreme dharma; Vasubandhu assigns it to the sixth conceit (*overweening of supposed merit*), while Kuiji distinguishes two strata of bestowal: the *teaching of the jewel* (verbal transmission) and the *substance of the jewel* (realization requiring entry into the ten stages). **Thesis III**: the two jewels constitute the complete bilateral structure of awakening — learner-side occlusion (intrinsic but unknown) and teacher-side occlusion (known but not-yet-given); neither is a defect but both are built into awakening as encounter. The paper's original contribution is explicitly pairing the two jewels as a bilateral structure of occlusion — an alignment no classical commentary makes — and using this pairing to disclose the underlying pedagogical logic of the Lotus Sūtra.

**Keywords:** Robe-Jewel Parable, Topknot-Jewel Parable, *tathāgatagarbha*, Intrinsic Seeds (*本有種子*), Doctrine of Timing, Vasubandhu's Seven-Conceit System, Kuiji's Yogācāra Reading, Awakening as Encounter, *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka-sūtra*, *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka-upadeśa*

## I. Introduction: Two Parables, One Structure

Two jewels run through the twenty-eight chapters of the *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka-sūtra*.

The first appears in "The Prediction for the Five Hundred Disciples" (Ch. 8). After receiving their predictions of buddhahood, five hundred arhats prostrate before the Buddha and confess: a man once visited a friend's house, drank to drunkenness, and fell asleep. The friend, called away on official business, tied a priceless jewel (*anargha-maṇi*) into the hem of his robe and left. The drunken man, oblivious, awoke and wandered to another land, toiling bitterly for food and clothing, contenting himself with meager gain — until he met his old friend again, who told him: *"The jewel I fastened in your robe long ago is still there now, and you did not know."*[^01] This is the **Robe-Jewel parable** (衣珠喻).

The second appears in "The Practice of Ease" (Ch. 14). The Buddha tells Mañjuśrī: a mighty wheel-turning king, subduing rebellious states, rewards his victorious generals — with fields, villages, gold, silver, horses, chariots. **Only the bright jewel in his topknot he will not give.** Why? *"Only the king bears this one jewel atop his head. Were he to bestow it, his entire retinue would be astonished."* Not until he "sees one of great merit" — one who has broken the five-aggregate-māra, the defilement-māra, and the death-māra, who has transcended the three realms and severed the three poisons — does the king finally unbind his topknot and present the jewel. The Buddha then declares: so too does the Tathāgata reserve *this Lotus Sūtra*, "the secret treasury of all buddhas … long guarded and not rashly proclaimed, only today expounded to you."[^02] This is the **Topknot-Jewel parable** (髻珠喻).

Two jewels, two parables, two postures. On the surface: one is "intrinsically present yet unknown" (the jewel lies in the robe; the drunkard thinks himself poor); the other is "known yet not-yet-given" (the jewel sits on the king's head, awaiting merit). One concerns the learner's situation; the other, the teacher's timing. But under the Pointing-at-the-Moon reading, these two parables answer two faces of a single question: **Why must awakening take the form of an encounter?**

This question is the structural pivot by which Series 7 moves from Phase II (the Four Great Parables) to Phase III (the prediction chapters). P03 (Burning House), P05 (Medicinal Herbs), P06 (Conjured City) answered *how* the Buddha teaches: the nature of *upāya*, the differentiation of capacities, the distinction between real and provisional expedients. At P07 the question reverses: **Why must the Buddha teach at all?** Why can sentient beings not open and unfold on their own? Why is the Lotus Sūtra disclosed only at the end?

Behind this reversal lies a deeper proposition. If all beings intrinsically possess buddha-nature and uncontaminated seeds, why does a Buddha need to appear in the world? And if his appearance is necessary, in what sense is anything "intrinsic"? This is the crucial step from an *epistemology of teaching* to an *ontology of potentiality* — the foundation the Lotus Sūtra must lay before it can hand its baton to the Huayan doctrine of *dharmadhātu-pratītyasamutpāda*.

This paper advances three theses. **Thesis I**: the Robe-Jewel is not a lyrical trope for buddha-nature but the Lotus Sūtra's most structurally articulated statement of *tathāgatagarbha* thought — its three-moment pattern (intrinsic → occluded → disclosed) is isomorphic with the *Laṅkāvatāra* and the *Ratnagotravibhāga*, and already has an explicit systematic position in Vasubandhu's *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka-upadeśa*. **Thesis II**: the Topknot-Jewel is not about *secrecy* but about *timing* — the supreme teaching is disclosed only in the Lotus not because earlier teachings were false but because the moment was not yet ripe; and this principle of timing is one the Buddha has practiced since the Āgama period. **Thesis III**: read together, the two jewels form the complete bilateral structure of awakening — a learner-side occlusion (intrinsic but unknown) and a teacher-side occlusion (known but not-yet-given). Neither occlusion is a defect; both are built into awakening *as encounter*.

A boundary must be drawn before entering the sūtras. The Robe-Jewel overlaps visibly with the **Lost Son parable** (Ch. 4) at the level of "intrinsic treasure unknown to its owner," but their narrative foci differ. The Lost Son concerns *external inheritance* recognized only gradually — twenty years of hired labor, a slow re-identification with the father. The Robe-Jewel concerns *internal treasure* disclosed instantaneously — the jewel never left, a single meeting suffices. The former addresses psychological resistance to identity; the latter, the cognitive closure of a blind spot. Complementary, not redundant. This paper treats the Robe-Jewel; the Lost Son is reserved for S7-P04.

## II. The Lotus Sūtra: The Textual Shape of Both Jewels

### A. Sūtra

#### 1. The Robe-Jewel (Ch. 8)

**Setting.** The parable appears *after* the prediction of the five hundred arhats. Strikingly, they do not erupt in joy. Instead they rise, prostrate, and *"reproach themselves in repentance,"* saying: *"We always thought we had attained ultimate extinction. Now we know that we were like those without wisdom."*[^01] That word *"thought"* — *"we always thought"* — is the emotional key of the parable. It is not malice, not mere error, but cognitive self-closure: believing one has reached the terminus and therefore ceasing to walk. Repentance begins not from what one did but from what one took to be already complete.

**The parable proper.** A man visits a friend's house, drinks, and falls asleep. The friend, called to duty, *"fastens a priceless jewel into the lining of his robe"* and departs. The man, drunk, *"is wholly unaware."* He wanders to another country, toils for food and clothing, *"content with any small gain."* Meeting his friend again, he hears the revelation: *"The jewel is still there, and you did not know. Take it now; you will lack nothing."*[^02]

Six narrative nodes unfold: **fastening** (intrinsic) → **drunken sleep** (occlusion) → **non-cognition** (closure) → **wandering in another land** (the arhats' laborious search) → **content with little** (mistaking it for final extinction) → **reunion and disclosure** (*kalyāṇamitra*) → **use** (actualization). Not a linear fable but a six-step loop — the complete arc from "intrinsic but unmanifest" to "encountered and enacted."

**The application.** The five hundred disciples immediately voice the Buddha's hermeneutic: *"The Buddha too, when he was a bodhisattva, taught us and aroused in us the mind of omniscience. But we forgot and did not know. Having attained the arhat-way, we thought we had extinction … **yet the vow of omniscience still remained, not lost**."*[^03]

*"The vow of omniscience still remained, not lost"* — this is the most direct textual statement within the Lotus Sūtra of something like "intrinsic seed." What is intrinsic is not a permanent entity lodged in consciousness but the *original vow*, which never vanished from the *ālayavijñāna*; it was merely occluded by the cognitive closure of "thinking one had attained extinction." The jewel's "intrinsic" character is a matter of vow, seed, and function — not of substance. We return to this in §III.1 and §V.

> **Sanskrit–Chinese collation note.** In Dharmarakṣa's earlier recension (*Zhengfahua jing*, T0263), the jewel is fastened not into the "lining" but into a *knot*, and is named *"bright-moon jewel"* rather than "priceless jewel."[^04] The image of the *knot* reinforces structural anchorage — the jewel is not casually stowed but structurally bound to a site, readable alongside the Yogācāra image of seeds lodged in the *ālaya*. Kumārajīva's "robe-lining" instead accents immediacy — "upon you, never separated." The two renderings are complementary.

#### 2. The Topknot-Jewel (Ch. 14)

**The parable proper.** To Mañjuśrī the Buddha says: a mighty wheel-turning king subdues rebellious states; his generals of merit receive lands, cities, robes, ornaments, gold, lapis, agate, coral, amber, elephants, horses, chariots, retainers. *"Only the bright jewel in his topknot he will not give. Why? Only the king bears this one jewel; were he to bestow it, his entire retinue would be astonished."*[^05]

The decisive phrase is *"will not give"* (不以與之). Kuiji's commentary insists: this is *active safeguarding*, not passive oversight. The king *knows* the jewel is in his topknot; he *knows* his generals desire it; yet he chooses not to bestow it. Not miserliness but discernment — prematurely given, the jewel would occasion alarm and its dignity would collapse.

**The application.** *"Mañjuśrī! So too does the Tathāgata … among the fourfold assembly he has expounded many sūtras, gladdened their hearts, bestowed upon them meditation, liberation, the uncontaminated faculties and powers, the wealth of all dharmas, and the city of nirvāṇa, calling it extinction … **but he has not expounded this Lotus Sūtra to them**. … As the wheel-turning king, seeing one of great merit, rejoices and finally bestows the jewel long kept in his topknot — so too the Tathāgata. This Lotus Sūtra is the secret treasury of all buddhas, supreme among sūtras, **long guarded and not rashly proclaimed; only today is it expounded to you**."*[^06]

Two terms demand emphasis. First, **"great merit"** (大功) — not generic virtue, but a precise condition: severing the three poisons, transcending the three realms, rending the net of Māra. The threshold for receiving the jewel is the listener's capacity to *bear* its weight. Second, **"only today"** (今日乃與) — not an incidental moment but the mark of ripened timing. The governing word of the entire "Practice of Ease" chapter is *time*: *"only at the last do I expound this Lotus"; "now is the very time to expound it to you."*[^07] The core of the parable is not *secrecy* but *timeliness*.

A first-layer contrast: the Robe-Jewel narrative begins with *fastening* — the Buddha's act is already complete, awaiting only the learner's awakening; the Topknot-Jewel climaxes in *unbinding* — the Buddha's act (bestowal) is not yet complete, awaiting ripeness. In the former, agency lies with the learner (to awaken); in the latter, with the teacher (to bestow). But this is surface. The real dialogue between them unfolds in §III.3.

### B. Treatise and Commentary

#### 1. Vasubandhu's *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka-upadeśa*: The Two Jewels in the Seven-Conceit System

The earliest systematic classification of both parables comes from Vasubandhu's *Upadeśa* (T1519, trans. Bodhiruci; T1520, trans. Ratnamati). Vasubandhu establishes two parallel classifying axes — *seven forms of overweening conceit × seven parables*, and *ten supreme meanings × corresponding parables* — and both jewels have clear positions on each.[^08]

On the seven-conceit axis, the Robe-Jewel is assigned to the fifth conceit, *the overweening of scattered mind*: *"One whose scattered mind lacks concentration, who **though possessing Mahāyāna roots of goodness from the past does not know it**, does not seek the Mahāyāna, but within a narrow and inferior mind generates false understanding, taking it for the first vehicle."*[^09] This is surgically precise — it names the core structure of the Robe-Jewel: not absence of roots, but the *presence* of roots *unknown*. Vasubandhu's corrective is equally exact: *"Show him the roots of goodness he already has, cause him to remember, and then lead him into samādhi."* **Remembering** (憶念) is the key verb — the Robe-Jewel gives the learner nothing new; it returns to him what was already his.

The Topknot-Jewel is assigned to the sixth conceit, *the overweening of supposed possession of merit*: *"Those who, with merit, hear Mahāyāna dharma but grasp it as non-Mahāyāna — thus taking it in inverted fashion."* The corrective: *"The teaching of Mahāyāna, whose dharma-gate is equal to the fulfillment of the ten stages — by which all buddhas secretly confer prediction."*[^10] This discloses something easy to miss: the Topknot-Jewel is bestowed on those *who already have merit*, who have already transcended the three realms. Their problem is not deficiency of practice but the last cognitive threshold — habituated to an earlier doctrinal framework, they hear the Lotus as a variant of something already familiar, failing to catch its distinct grammar as *One Vehicle*. Timing here is not a matter of *qualification* but of *the last aperture of perception*.

On the ten-supreme-meanings axis, the Robe-Jewel corresponds to the fourth, *supreme understanding* (令解無上), and the Topknot-Jewel to the sixth, *supreme proclamation* (說無上).[^11] One concerns *understanding*, the other *proclamation* — one an inner cognitive event, the other an outer act of bestowal. Vasubandhu does not himself pair the two as a "bilateral structure" (that insight belongs to §III.3 below), but his taxonomy supplies its conceptual scaffolding: *learner-side understanding* and *teacher-side proclamation* are two distinct axes, and the two jewels map onto them respectively.

#### 2. Kuiji's *Fahua xuanzan*: A Yogācāra Reading of Both Jewels

Kuiji (T1723) extends Vasubandhu's classification into a refined Yogācāra reading.

**On the Robe-Jewel**, Kuiji reads at two levels. At the general level, commenting on "awakening" (悟) within the four-verb formula of the opening of the sūtra, he invokes the jewel directly: *"'Awakening' distinctively reveals the **intrinsic seed of the saṃbhogakāya's ultimate bodhi**, causing its fruition to arise."*[^12] The phrase *intrinsic seed* (本有種子) is Kuiji's first-layer identification of the jewel's nature — jewel = the intrinsic seeds of the four wisdoms (pure, uncontaminated, naturally there). He further cites the *Laṅkāvatāra*: *"The ālaya-vijñāna is called the empty tathāgatagarbha; possessed of the perfuming of uncontaminated dharmas, it is called the non-empty tathāgatagarbha."*[^13] The non-empty *tathāgatagarbha* thus *equals* the store of uncontaminated seeds — providing a Yogācāra grammar legitimating "Robe-Jewel = tathāgatagarbha."

At the local level of Chapter 8, Kuiji turns to a finer psychology of consciousness: *"The priceless jewel is the mind of great bodhi … **'robe' is the sixth consciousness, 'fastening' is perfuming (vāsanā)**."*[^14] The mapping is exact. The "robe" is not the body in general but specifically the *manovijñāna*; the "fastening" is not static attachment but the dynamic process of *perfuming*. A consequence follows: the jewel's intrinsicality does not mean it pre-exists in some mystical storehouse outside consciousness. It is intrinsic in the sense that, as a perfumed seed, it is lodged "naturally and as-it-is" (法爾而住) within the *ālayavijñāna* that underlies the sixth consciousness. *Intrinsic* names the post-perfuming mode of dwelling in consciousness, not a pre-perfuming *a priori* substance.

For the image of *drunken sleep*, Kuiji is equally precise: *"Here the non-shared ignorance (**āveṇika-avidyā**) of the sixth consciousness is itself the substance of drunkenness, inasmuch as it wanders in the state of the ordinary being."*[^15] Not root ignorance in the abstract, but the specifically *sixth-consciousness non-shared ignorance* — what is drunk is not "existence itself" but the *evaluative capacity* of consciousness upon existence. This coupling is decisive. Drunkenness is not metaphysical fall but the functional de-calibration of an ordinary being's *manovijñāna*: present, yet unreliable. *Because* the function is present, it can be re-awakened; *because* it is de-calibrated, it concludes prematurely that it has attained extinction.

**On the Topknot-Jewel**, Kuiji offers what is, for this paper, the most unexpected move. He divides the jewel into two strata:

> *"Those not yet entered into the ten stages receive the **teaching of the bright jewel**; only upon entering the ten stages and partially breaking the four māras of transformation-birth is the **substance of the bright jewel** bestowed."*[^16]

| Level | Content | Condition of bestowal |
|---|---|---|
| **Teaching of the bright jewel** (明珠之教) | The verbal expounding of the Lotus Sūtra | Even those of lesser merit may receive it (at the Lotus Assembly itself) |
| **Substance of the bright jewel** (明珠之體) | The One-Vehicle reality itself | Requires entry into the ten stages and partial breaking of the four māras |

In other words, "only today is it expounded" does not mark a single complete handover but a *two-phase* structure — **teaching first, substance later**. This is a crucial refinement to Thesis II. Timing is not a flat gate ("when the moment arrives, everything is given") but a layered sequence: *verbal disclosure may be given early; realization must await maturation of the path*. Kuiji's reading expands the Topknot-Jewel from "a single final bestowal" into "the final phase of progressive disclosure" — and sharpens its contrast with the Robe-Jewel: the Robe-Jewel is *immediate once known* (the jewel is already in the robe); the Topknot-Jewel is *verbally receivable now, substantively awaited later* (the jewel is still on the king's head).

#### 3. The Tathāgatagarbha Backdrop: *Laṅkāvatāra* and *Ratnagotravibhāga*

The Robe-Jewel's "intrinsic → occluded → disclosed" structure is no outlier in the Indian *tathāgatagarbha* literature. The *Laṅkāvatāra* declares: *"The Tathāgatagarbha, intrinsically pure, bearing the thirty-two marks, has entered into the bodies of all sentient beings — **like a great precious jewel wrapped in a soiled garment**."*[^17] A priceless jewel wrapped in filth — the image is almost a mirror of the Lotus. The *Ratnagotravibhāga*, among its nine similes, gives us the **Poor Man's Buried Treasure** — a structural twin: *"As in the ground beneath a poor man's hut lies a treasure of jewels, yet the man does not know, and the treasure cannot speak … so too beings bear within their own mind-houses an inexhaustible treasury of dharma-jewels, yet cannot know it, and in not knowing suffer the poverty of birth-and-death."*[^18] Treasure present but unknown to its owner — this is the *Ratnagotra* version of the Robe-Jewel.

But the most vital statement of the *tathāgatagarbha* literature is its preemptive self-demarcation against the charge of being Brahmanical *ātman*. In the *Laṅkāvatāra*, Mahāmati asks plainly: *"How can the World-Honored One speak of a tathāgatagarbha, as the non-Buddhists speak of an ātman?"* The Buddha's reply is unambiguous: *"The tathāgatagarbha I teach is not the ātman taught by non-Buddhists … For the sake of foolish beings who, on hearing 'no-self,' become frightened, I speak of a tathāgatagarbha. To draw in those who cling to self, I speak of tathāgatagarbha so that they may depart from the clinging of the mind's delusion of a self-soul and enter the three gates of liberation."*[^19] The *tathāgatagarbha* is *upāya*: a concession made so that those terrified by *anātman* do not recoil in fear. It must still be understood through emptiness, ultimate reality, nirvāṇa, signlessness, and wishlessness — *not* as a permanent entity but as a *functional potentiality of purity*.

This passage matters immensely, because it settles once and for all the misreading "Robe-Jewel = ātman." The jewel is not a permanent unchanging self. The jewel is *the name skillfully bestowed on the seed of goodness so that listeners, hearing "no-self," will not be startled into retreat*. §V returns to this as the first Middle-Way correction.

#### 4. The Āgama Prototype: Luminous Mind and Teaching by Capacity

One final layer is easy to overlook. The doctrinal foundations of *both* parables are already in place in the Chinese Āgamas — though the famous ten-character formula *"the mind is intrinsically pure, stained by adventitious defilements"* (心性本淨,客塵所染) does *not* appear verbatim in the Āgamas themselves, being a later doctrinal crystallization first articulated in the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*'s commentary on *pabhassara-citta* in AN 1.49–52.

*Saṃyuktāgama* sūtra 267 says plainly: *"Bhikṣus, examine the mind well. **Through the long night the mind is stained by greed, hatred, and delusion.** When the mind is troubled, beings are troubled; when the mind is pure, beings are pure."*[^20] The structure is complete: the mind is *stained* by adventitious greed, hatred, and delusion; the staining is "through the long night" — persistent but not original — presupposing a nature of the mind capable of being purified. *Saṃyuktāgama* 1246, the **parable of refining raw gold**, is more direct still: raw gold bears the nature of gold, mixed with sand and impurities; layer by layer the impurities are washed away and pure gold naturally appears.[^21] This is the mature expression, in earliest Buddhism, of the "intrinsic → occluded → disclosed" structure.

The Topknot-Jewel's doctrine of timing has equally firm Āgama roots. *Bieyi za ahan* sūtra 4 records the Buddha's teaching sequence to the Brahmin Bharadvāja: *"**In order, he taught the discourses on giving, on morality, on rebirth in the heavens** … When the Buddha saw that the young man's mind was softened, uplifted, rejoicing, free of doubt, **fit as a dharma-vessel, ready to receive and understand** — as is the way of all buddhas — he expounded for him the Four Noble Truths."*[^22] Three steps, crystal-clear: (1) progressive expedient teaching, (2) observation of ripeness, (3) bestowal of the supreme teaching. **"Fit as a dharma-vessel"** (堪任法器) is the key term — the Āgama prototype of "seeing one of great merit, then bestowing the jewel." *Saṃyuktāgama* 485 records the Buddha's own declaration: *"Sometimes I teach one feeling, sometimes two, three, four, five, six … sometimes up to one hundred and eight feelings, sometimes even immeasurable feelings — the world does not understand, and so they quarrel."*[^23] The principle that dharma has no fixed form and is taught according to capacity is already settled at the Āgama layer.

This Āgama foundation is crucial. It permits us to read neither jewel as a "Mahāyāna innovation," but as *the narrative maturation, in the Lotus period, of principles the Buddha had practiced from the beginning*: luminous mind is the prototype of the Robe-Jewel; teaching by capacity is the prototype of the Topknot-Jewel. Both threads already existed in the Āgamas; only at the Lotus are they woven into a single narrative architecture.

## III. Three Theses

### 3.1 Thesis I: The Robe-Jewel as the Lotus Sūtra's Structural Articulation of *Tathāgatagarbha*

The alignment of the Robe-Jewel with *tathāgatagarbha* thought proceeds not by analogy but by structural isomorphism. To avoid the illusion that "the Lotus invented intrinsicality," we begin from the Āgamas.

The Āgamas establish two premises. (1) The mind can be stained, but staining is *adventitious* — coming from without, later, accumulating through long time, not the original nature of mind (SĀ 267). (2) The intrinsic pure nature can be disclosed through progressive removal of stains (SĀ 1246, the raw-gold simile). Together these form a complete *intrinsic → occluded → disclosable* structure, though in the language of gradual path rather than of "intrinsic seeds."

It bears explicit note: *"the mind is intrinsically pure, stained by adventitious defilements"* **does not appear verbatim** in the Chinese Āgamas. It is a later crystallization, first seen in the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*'s gloss on *pabhassara-citta*. This scholarly honesty answers the charge "tathāgatagarbha = ātman" more convincingly than any apologetic: if *intrinsic purity* is a gradually crystallized doctrinal expression rather than a primordial substantialist claim, then *tathāgatagarbha* thought is not, at root, a substance ontology but *a stage in the history of doctrinal articulation*.

From the Āgamas into Mahāyāna, the structure is rearticulated with refined grammar in the *Laṅkāvatāra* and the *Ratnagotra*. The *Laṅkāvatāra*'s "priceless jewel wrapped in a soiled garment" renders occlusion as *filth wrapping treasure* — nearly interchangeable with Lotus's "jewel fastened in the robe's lining." The *Ratnagotra*'s nine similes — *golden buddha-image wrapped in rotten cloth, gold in filth, buried treasure in a poor house, seed in husk* — are all variations of one structure beneath differing narrative skins. The *Ratnagotra*'s distinction between **two purities** is especially important: *natural purity* (*prakṛti-viśuddhi*: originally unassociated with defilement) and *purity-from-stain* (*vaimalya-viśuddhi*: distance from adventitious stain attained through the path).[^24] These correspond exactly to the two states of the Robe-Jewel: jewel-in-robe (natural purity — occluded but substantively uncompromised) and jewel-disclosed-and-used (purity-from-stain — intrinsic function actualized).

Entering Yogācāra, Kuiji provides the most refined theorization. The intrinsic jewel = **the seed dwelling in its original nature** (本性住種, *Yogācārabhūmi* vocabulary) = **intrinsic uncontaminated seeds** (本有無漏種子, *Vijñaptimātratā-siddhi* vocabulary). The *Cheng weishi lun*, Book 2, reports Dharmapāla's orthodox position: *"Seeds are of two kinds. First, **intrinsic**: naturally present since beginningless time in the maturation-consciousness, producing the differentiated functions of aggregates, bases, and elements. Second, **newly arising**: perfumed into existence through repeated manifest activity from beginningless time."*[^25] The Robe-Jewel corresponds to *intrinsic* — seeds not produced by practice but naturally present since beginningless time, stored in the *ālaya*. Kuiji embeds this within the Lotus: jewel = mind of great bodhi = the intrinsic seeds of the four wisdoms; robe = *manovijñāna*; fastening = perfuming.[^14] This completes the Robe-Jewel's translation from narrative into theory.

Yet a decisive Middle-Way point must be secured. **Intrinsic does not entail actualized.** The *Cheng weishi lun* states plainly: *"Uncontaminated seeds are subtle and concealed, hard to know; they are distinguished only through the obstructions they confront."*[^26] Before encountering condition, intrinsic seeds are *subtle and concealed* — just as the jewel in the robe, before being pointed out, has no effect whatsoever on the poor man's sense of poverty. *Intrinsic* is only *one half* of the causal picture (the *hetu-pratyaya*); it still requires *adhipati-pratyaya* (the friend's disclosure, the perfuming of hearing dharma) to trigger actualization. The full theoretical form of the Robe-Jewel is **the four-condition structure narrativized**:

> intrinsic seed (*hetu-pratyaya*) × reunion with friend (*adhipati-pratyaya*) × consciousness's acceptance (*samanantara-pratyaya*) × present objective sphere (*ālambana-pratyaya*) = jewel discovered and used.

This is why intrinsicality does not guarantee liberation. It guarantees only that liberation is *structurally possible*, not that it *occurs in time*.

### 3.2 Thesis II: The Topknot-Jewel as a Doctrine of Timing, Not a Doctrine of Secrecy

Two dangerous misreadings of the Topknot-Jewel must be headed off. The first reads it as *esoterism* — as if the Lotus harbored a secret teaching reserved for a privileged few. The second reads it as *hierarchism* — as if the Lotus were proclaimed supreme over other sūtras and therefore disclosed last. Both mistranslate "timing" into "rank."

The clarification begins at the Āgama layer. The Buddha's principle of *teaching-by-capacity* is already complete in *Bieyi za ahan* 4: he teaches the Brahmin first the discourses on giving, morality, and heavenly rebirth — teachings familiar and accessible to a householder — and only after *"seeing that his mind was softened and fit as a dharma-vessel"* does he expound the Four Noble Truths. The sequence is not because the Four Noble Truths are "higher grade" but because they require a listener who has *already loosened* his attachment to the conventional world. Gradual expedient is not deception; it is *the concrete form of compassion*. *Saṃyuktāgama* 485 has the Buddha himself declare: *"Sometimes I teach one feeling, sometimes two … sometimes immeasurable feelings. The world does not understand, and so they quarrel."*[^23] The variability of dharma is not the Buddha's indecision but his *responsive adaptation* to capacities; its appearance as inconsistency is the world's failure of understanding.

The Topknot-Jewel is the *Lotus-period* maturation of this same principle. Its logic is continuous with the Āgamas. The wheel-turning king withholds the jewel not because the jewel is a secret, but because *before his generals have broken the five-aggregate-māra, the defilement-māra, and the death-māra*, bestowing it would cause them to be unable to bear its weight — to be "astonished," to fail to honor it, to collapse both the gift and themselves. The timing here is purely *structural*: the listener must first possess the capacity to bear the weight of dharma, or the act of giving becomes, at best, empty and, at worst, harmful.

Vasubandhu's placement of the Topknot-Jewel under the *sixth conceit* (*supposed possession of merit*) reveals something easily missed: **those to whom the jewel is given already possess merit**. They have already severed the three poisons, already transcended the three realms, already rent the net of Māra. Their problem is not deficiency of cultivation but that, *"hearing Mahāyāna dharma, they take it in non-Mahāyāna fashion"* — they have enough practice to *listen* to the Lotus, but, habituated to the doctrinal grammar of earlier teachings, they hear it as a variant of what they already know, missing its distinct grammar as *One Vehicle*. The Topknot-Jewel's timing thus does not address insufficient merit; it addresses *the final cognitive threshold of perception*. Timing, in this frame, is not a question of *qualification* but of *the last aperture through which the listener's already-present capacity can cohere into a new grammar*.

Kuiji's two-strata reading — "teaching of the jewel" vs. "substance of the jewel" — pushes the doctrine of timing to a finer resolution.[^16] At the Lotus Assembly, the Buddha has already bestowed the **teaching** of the bright jewel — the sūtra's text, narrative, parables, predictions. But to receive the **substance** of the bright jewel — the reality-body of the One Vehicle, the fulfillment of the ten stages, the transcendence of *pariṇāmiki-jāti* — the practitioner must himself *"enter the ten stages and partially break the four māras of transformation-birth."* The disclosure of the Lotus is therefore not a one-stroke event but a *sequence*: **speech first**, so that the listener may be planted with understanding; **realization after**, so that the practitioner may enter it through the cultivation of the ten stages. Timing is not binary (give or withhold) but *stratified* (speech now, realization later).

This stratification offers a far more compassionate placement of the Lotus within the broader canon. Other sūtras are not "one rank below the Lotus"; they are "the laying-down of the listening-conditions for the Lotus's teaching of the jewel." The Lotus's teaching of the jewel is not "one rank below the substance"; it is "the laying-down of the narrative frame through which the substance can be realized." The entire teaching system is a carefully designed *progressive drawing-in* culminating in final realization — each link necessary, each link compassionate. The emotional force of "one and only one bestowal" does not lie in the Lotus being *higher than* other teachings, but in its being the *last link* of the whole progressive drawing-in. Without all the earlier teachings, this last link could not be given at all.

### 3.3 Thesis III: Two Occlusions Constitute the Complete Bilateral Structure of Awakening

Here lies the paper's core original contribution. The Robe-Jewel and the Topknot-Jewel are *never explicitly paired as a bilateral structure of occlusion* in the classical commentaries. Vasubandhu assigns them to the fifth and sixth conceits. Kuiji treats them within separate chapter commentaries (Ch. 8 and Ch. 14). Zhiyi and Jizang also handle them separately. The Pointing-at-the-Moon reading places them side by side — not for novelty, but because the moment they are placed side by side, the Lotus Sūtra's entire pedagogical structure reveals a complete bilateral form invisible under separate treatment.

**3.3.1 The Learner-Side Occlusion: The Robe-Jewel as "Intrinsic but Unknown"**

The structure on the learner's side:

- **Intrinsic** (*structural condition*). The learner already bears the jewel — the intrinsic uncontaminated seeds, the mind of great bodhi, the vow of omniscience. This is the non-negotiable substrate. Without intrinsicality there is no possibility of *"finding"*: finding presupposes that there is something to be found.
- **Unknown** (*temporal dimension*). The learner does not know he bears it. This ignorance is not accidental lapse but *structural*: "drunken sleep" is the ordinary state of *sāsrava* consciousness; "thinking one has attained extinction" is the ordinary being's natural appraisal of his own condition. Non-cognition is not error but *the default state of the cognitive system in the absence of adhipati-pratyaya*.
- **Encounter** (*event*). The friend comes and tells. This moment is neither the learner's own devising nor the friend's preplanned strategy ("he *happened* to meet him"); it is the intersection of two lines of condition at a particular moment. *Encounter is the actualization of adhipati-pratyaya.* Without *adhipati-pratyaya*, intrinsicality remains forever "subtle and concealed."

The lesson of this side: **awakening cannot proceed on "intrinsicality" alone**. Even bearing a jewel of incalculable value on one's person, without someone to tell, one will live out an entire lifetime of poverty. This is a structural check on any *self-power triumphalism* — the Buddha's advent is not superfluous, the *kalyāṇamitra*'s teaching is not optional. It is equally a structural check on any *lyrical tathāgatagarbha sentimentalism* that treats "having buddha-nature" as sufficient.

**3.3.2 The Teacher-Side Occlusion: The Topknot-Jewel as "Known but Not-Yet-Given"**

The structure on the teacher's side:

- **Known** (*the precondition of compassion*). The teacher knows where the jewel lies, knows its value, knows who will ultimately need it. *Knowing is the precondition of compassion*: teaching without knowledge of the student's condition is not compassion but blind action.
- **Not-yet-given** (*the form of protection*). Knowing and yet not immediately giving is not miserliness but discernment about timing and capacity. To bestow the supreme teaching before the listener has "broken the five-aggregate-māra, the defilement-māra, and the death-māra" is not help but harm — the dharma will be misheard, the jewel scorned, and both parties damaged. Not-yet-given is *the discipline that locks compassion into its effective window*.
- **Timing** (*the structure of correspondence*). *"Only upon seeing one of great merit"* — timing is not subjective preference but the *objective ripening of structure*. When the height of the learner's cultivation meets the place from which the jewel can be borne, the teacher's bestowal becomes real giving rather than failed projection.

The lesson of this side: **awakening cannot proceed on "the teacher's wish to teach" alone**. Even if the teacher holds the full answer, if the student's structure is not yet in place, teaching is as if unheard — or worse than unheard. This is a structural check on any *totalizing-transmission triumphalism* — the "late appearance" or "secret preservation" of a scripture is not stinginess but protection of its listeners. It is equally a structural check on any *lyrical scripture-worship* that treats "having the text" as sufficient.

**3.3.3 The Closure of Bilateral Occlusion: Awakening as Encounter**

Taken together, the Lotus Sūtra's pedagogical structure now stands complete:

| Dimension | Robe-Jewel (learner-side) | Topknot-Jewel (teacher-side) |
|---|---|---|
| Subject of occlusion | The learner himself (intrinsic but unknown) | The teacher (known but not-yet-given) |
| Content of occlusion | Intrinsic seeds (interior) | Supreme teaching (exterior) |
| Condition of release | Disclosure by a *kalyāṇamitra* (*adhipati-pratyaya*) | Maturation of the path-stage (*hetu-pratyaya*) |
| Temporal structure | Immediate disclosure (knowing = having, at once) | Stratified disclosure (teaching first, realization after) |
| Misreading corrected | Against "no intrinsic" nihilism | Against "reserved withholding" miserliness |
| Form of compassion | Fastening (active preparation) | Guarding (active safeguarding) |

The table makes it clear: *the two occlusions are not two defects in the structure of awakening but two necessary sides of awakening as an event of encounter*. Without the Robe-Jewel (learner-intrinsic), there is nothing to disclose; without the Topknot-Jewel (teacher-guarding), there is no final completion of disclosure. Without the Robe-Jewel, teaching is mere external implantation (the consequence of nihilism); without the Topknot-Jewel, teaching is undifferentiated broadcast (the consequence of miserliness-phobia). Together, the two occlusions guarantee that awakening is *an event in which two lines of condition converge at a particular moment*, not an act that either side can accomplish alone.

This reading severs two traps simultaneously. The intrinsic is not Brahmanical *ātman* because intrinsicality *does not guarantee actualization*: it must wait for *adhipati-pratyaya*. An eternal substance would need no conditions; the Robe-Jewel needs the friend's telling to function. Timing is not hierarchy because timing is *the correspondence between the structure of cultivation and the structure of teaching*, not a one-sided ranking by either party. A hierarchical secret teaching would be an active withholding as a marker of power; the Topknot-Jewel's not-yet-given is the protection of the listener's capacity to bear.

This is also why §3.3 cannot rest content with juxtaposition. Juxtaposition is only the point of departure. The real claim is this: *neither occlusion is a defect to be "improved away"; both are mechanisms internal to the structure of awakening itself.* If the Robe-Jewel were not occluded, there would be no emotional force of "rediscovery"; if the Topknot-Jewel were given too early, there would be no reverence of "corresponding timing." *Occlusion is not the opposite of compassion. Occlusion is one of compassion's forms.*

This insight echoes across the series. It mirrors S2-P02 (the Avalokiteśvara awakening-loop) and S7-P06 (the Conjured City loop): what the Lotus Sūtra treats is never a static ontological proposition but a dynamic *structure of encounter*. Awakening is not a state but an event; the event requires structure; the structure requires encounter; the encounter requires timing. This is what the Lotus Sūtra spends twenty-eight chapters of parable trying to say.

## IV. Series Continuity: From the Jewel to the Predictions

### 4.1 The Encounter-Loop Takes Shape

P07 establishes the series's most complete six-step loop so far, isomorphic with the Avalokiteśvara-loop of S2-P02:

**Robe-Jewel encounter-loop**: intrinsic (fastening) → forgetting (drunken sleep) → wandering (laboring poverty) → encounter (reunion with friend) → disclosure (unbinding the jewel) → actualization (use).

**Avalokiteśvara-loop (S2-P02)**: observing (reflexive gaze) → illumining (seeing) → practicing (depth) → seeing (emptiness) → ferrying (across suffering) → liberation.

Both are six-step loops. Both move from non-knowing to knowing. Both move from static intrinsicality to dynamic actualization. But they differ in *where agency lies*: the Avalokiteśvara-loop's agency rests with the practitioner (self-reflection); the Robe-Jewel's agency rests with the encounter (the emergence of *adhipati-pratyaya*). Together they form the complete bilateral of awakening: sometimes by self-power reflexive gaze (Avalokiteśvara), sometimes by other-power encounter (Robe-Jewel); the former is an *interior loop*, the latter an *exterior loop*. The Lotus Sūtra supplies here the side that the Heart Sūtra did not unfold — awakening can begin not from inward reflection but from *being told*.

### 4.2 Foreshadowing P08: The Prediction Chapters

The P07 articulation of "intrinsic treasure" lays the foundation for the logic of prediction in P08 (Devadatta). *Prediction presupposes intrinsic seed*: without the seed, there is nothing to predict. The Robe-Jewel answers *why prediction is possible* (because of intrinsicality); the Topknot-Jewel answers *why prediction must be given* (because intrinsicality requires a corresponding event of disclosure). P08 will then address *who may be predicted* — including Devadatta (the extreme evildoer) and the Dragon Girl (non-human, non-male, not-adult). These cases will push the *universality* of intrinsicality to its limit. P09 (Sadāparibhūta) will convert this universality into concrete practice: bow to every sentient being, because every sentient being bears a jewel in the robe.

### 4.3 Preparing S8: From Star to Black Hole

The Lotus's "intrinsic treasure" is the necessary precondition of Huayan's *dharmadhātu-pratītyasamutpāda*. The *dharmadhātu* can be "one-is-all, all-is-one" only because each being intrinsically possesses the potential to *mutually identify* with the whole. In the Lotus this potential is called the Robe-Jewel; in the Huayan it is called *"manifesting inexhaustible ocean-lands within a single pore."* The "intrinsic–encounter" structure treated in P07 will be rearticulated in S8 as *Indra's Net* — each node intrinsically possessing the capacity to mirror every other, awaiting the corresponding arising of conditions. Without the Lotus first building the structures of *intrinsic* and *encounter*, Huayan's *inexhaustibility* would collapse into lyrical imagery without structural support.

## V. Middle-Way Corrections

### ❌ The Robe-Jewel is not Brahmanical ātman.

Throughout the Chinese intellectual tradition, the jewel in the robe has repeatedly been misread as "a permanent unchanging true self" — equated with the *brahman-ātman* of the Upaniṣads or the "true ruler" of Neo-Daoist metaphysics. This misreading must be severed explicitly. **The jewel is a dependently arisen dharma, not a substantial entity.** Three reasons. (1) The jewel is *fastened* (by the friend); this very act means it is produced by conditions, not self-standing — a substantial self could not be "fastened." (2) Intrinsicality does not guarantee actualization: the "reunion with the friend" (*adhipati-pratyaya*) is required for any function to appear — a substantial self would be self-luminous, needing no external condition. (3) The *Laṅkāvatāra* preempts the charge unambiguously: *"The tathāgatagarbha I teach is not the ātman taught by non-Buddhists … To draw in those who cling to self, I speak of tathāgatagarbha."*[^19] The *tathāgatagarbha* (the Robe-Jewel) is *upāya*, a concession so that those frightened by *anātman* do not retreat. It is not the repackaging of an old substance under a new name.

### ❌ The Robe-Jewel is not the Christian soul.

The Robe-Jewel resembles the Christian immortal soul in surface structure but differs at the root. Both posit "an internal, persisting, soteriologically relevant subjectivity." But three differences are decisive. *First*: the jewel is **functional** (seed, vow, capacity); the soul is **substantial** (an indivisible metaphysical individual). *Second*: the jewel **dwells in consciousness** (the *ālayavijñāna* is its substrate); the soul **stands independent** (requiring no bearer). *Third*: the jewel **is fully actualized, not preserved, in liberation** (once manifest, it is the Buddha's wisdom; no "preserved individual" remains); the soul **continues as an individual in liberation** (someone is still someone in heaven). These concepts are *not reducible to each other*; at the depth level, they are different ontologies.

### ❌ The Topknot-Jewel is not esoterism.

The Topknot-Jewel is not "a teaching reserved for a privileged initiated few." Three clarifications. *First*: the jewel's "safeguarding" is not exclusion of outsiders but waiting for timing; when the time arrives, the bestowal is public — there is no long-term secret circle. *Second*: the condition "to see one of great merit" is a *path-stage condition* (breaking the three māras), not an *identity-qualification* condition (monastic/lay, male/female, caste, lineage). This is a structural condition, not a distribution of power. *Third*: once the Lotus Sūtra *is* proclaimed at its ripe moment, it is proclaimed openly to the entire assembly — there is no "only-for-the-few" esoteric procedure. The "secrecy" of the jewel is a secrecy of *timing*, not a secrecy of *circulation*.

### ❌ Timing is not hierarchy.

The Topknot-Jewel is not a doctrinal proclamation of "Lotus supreme, others inferior." *Temporal position is not hierarchical position.* Other sūtras are, *functionally, irreplaceable preconditions*: without the discourses on giving, morality, and heavenly rebirth, there would be no audience for the Four Noble Truths; without the Āgamas, no basis for Prajñāpāramitā; without Prajñāpāramitā, no soil for the Lotus. The Lotus's position "at the end" is not "at the top" but *"at the final point of joining."* The whole system of progressive drawing-in is a compassionate structure designed around one final encounter; no link's value can be assessed in isolation. To read the Topknot-Jewel as a proclamation of "Lotus > others" is to mistake a structural position for a hierarchical one — the single misreading that most needs recalibration, not only in Lotus hermeneutics but across the entire tradition of Mahāyāna sūtra-classification.

## VI. Conclusion: Two Jewels, One Compassion

The Robe-Jewel and the Topknot-Jewel, placed side by side, disclose two faces of a single compassion.

The jewel in the robe tells us: *the seed of awakening has never departed; forgetting is not the structure's failure but the structural vacancy that waits for encounter.* The jewel in the topknot tells us: *the supreme teaching has never been withheld out of stinginess; safeguarding is not the concealment of power but the preservation of the integrity of the moment of encounter.* Together, the two jewels answer the Lotus Sūtra's deepest question — **Why must awakening take the form of an encounter?** Because intrinsicality requires disclosure; disclosure requires timing; timing requires the ripening of conditions. Neither side alone suffices; only together do they form a real teaching event.

The significance of this reading lies not only in locating a bilateral structural correspondence for the two jewels that the classical commentators never paired explicitly. It lies more deeply in disclosing the underlying logic of the entire Lotus pedagogy. **The Lotus Sūtra is not a sūtra that proclaims itself "supreme." The Lotus Sūtra is a sūtra that articulates *how the supreme happens*.** The supreme is not a property but an *event*; the event requires bilateral structure, requires ripening of timing, requires the joint correspondence of intrinsicality and safeguarding. This is why the Lotus tells parables instead of offering abstract propositions — parables are the form of *narrative*, and only narrative can express events that unfold *in time*. Abstract propositions can only say *is* or *is-not*; parables can say *when*, *how*, and *for whom*.

From P03 (Burning House) through P07 (Robe-Jewel and Topknot-Jewel), we have traversed the *upāya-to-reality* logic of the Lotus pedagogy, and have now entered, at P07, the *intrinsicality-to-encounter* logic of prediction. The next step, P08, will push the *universality* of intrinsicality to its limit: Devadatta and the Dragon Girl. Is there truly a jewel in the robe of every being? The Lotus Sūtra is about to give its boldest answer.

*The intrinsic treasure is thus: thus it has always been. The moment of encounter has its own conditions.*
