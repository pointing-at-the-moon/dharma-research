# Initial Arising of Bodhicitta and Dharma-Flow

**The Doctrinal Structure of Dharmakāya Beyond the Three Times**

## Abstract

Does the initial generation of bodhicitta (*prathamacittotpāda*) determine future buddhahood? If so, does that "future buddhahood" retroactively empower the present-day bodhisattva? Through a scriptural analysis of the *Avataṃsaka Sūtra*, the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā Śāstra*, and the *Daśabhūmika-vibhāṣā*, together with the doctrinal resources of Yogācāra seed theory and the theory of empowerment (*adhiṣṭhāna*), this paper argues that the fundamental dissolution of this question lies in the nature of the Dharmakāya as beyond the three times. There is no "retroactive empowerment from the future," because dharmatā has always been all-pervading; the dharma-flow has always been present. The function of initially generating bodhicitta is not "establishing a connection" but "opening to receive." The significance of practice, then, is the removal of adventitious afflictions (*āgantuka-kleśa*) that obstruct this opening.

**Keywords:** initial bodhicitta, Dharmakāya, identity of buddhas across the three times, *adhiṣṭhāna*, innate undefiled seeds, dharma-flow, adventitious afflictions

---

## I. Framing the Problem

Mahāyāna Buddhism's emphasis on the initial generation of bodhicitta is almost without parallel. The *Avataṃsaka Sūtra* declares "at the moment of initial aspiration, one immediately attains perfect awakening"; the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā Śāstra* says the bodhisattva of initial aspiration "at that single moment of intention, already surpasses the arhat." These statements give rise to a compelling doctrinal question:

If the initial generation of bodhicitta has already, *in principle*, determined future buddhahood, and if "the buddhas of the three times are one and the same" means the Dharmakāya does not enter time—then isn't this being's "future buddhahood" already "there"? Could it retroactively influence and empower the present-day bodhisattva of initial aspiration?

On the surface, this appears to be a question about retrocausality. In fact, this paper will argue that the correct answer dissolves the very premise of retrocausality.

## II. The Initial Generation of Bodhicitta: Scriptural Basis and Doctrinal Tension

### 2.1 "At the Moment of Initial Aspiration, One Attains Perfect Awakening" — The Huayan Complete Teaching

The Jin-dynasty translation of the *Avataṃsaka Sūtra*, "Chapter on Brahma Conduct":

> "At the moment of initial aspiration, one immediately attains perfect awakening, knows the true nature of all dharmas, fully accomplishes the body of wisdom, and does not rely on another for awakening."

This statement reads almost as a paradox. Tiantai and Huayan digest it differently:

**Tiantai** maps "initial aspiration" onto the first abiding (*bhūmi*) of the complete teaching. At this stage, one portion of fundamental ignorance is eliminated, enabling the *manifestation* of attaining buddhahood through the eight aspects. This is a "stage-as-fruition" approach—emphasizing functional manifestation rather than substantive finality.

**Huayan** holds that "fulfillment of faith is buddhahood." Under the view of interpenetrating dependent origination, the six stages are mutually identical and mutually inclusive—the fulfillment of the ten faiths immediately encompasses all stages up to and including the buddha-ground. This is the more thoroughgoing "one is all" position—the stages are essentially non-dual.

Both interpretations converge on this: the initial generation of bodhicitta is not an insignificant beginning but a moment of decisive significance *in principle*. Chengguan's summary in his *Commentary on the Avataṃsaka Sūtra* (華嚴經疏, T35 No. 1735) is the most precise: "The initial mind is the beginning; perfect awakening is the end." Beginning and end, from the perspective of the Dharmakāya, are originally non-dual.

### 2.2 The Novice Parable — The Dynamism and Fragility of Aspiration

However, "determined in principle" does not equal "guaranteed in practice." Nāgārjuna's novice parable in the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā Śāstra* reveals this tension:

> "Suppose an arhat with the six supernormal powers was traveling with a novice, having the novice carry his robes and bowl. The novice thought: 'I shall enter nirvāṇa by the buddha-vehicle.' The teacher, knowing his thought, took back the robes and bowl, carried them himself, and had the novice walk ahead. The novice then thought: 'The buddha path is exceedingly difficult; I should enter nirvāṇa quickly by the lesser vehicle.' The teacher gave the robes and bowl back to the novice and told him to walk behind. This happened three times... The teacher answered: 'When you first generated the aspiration to become a buddha, that mind was so precious that you stood in my teacher's path. When your mind regressed to the lesser vehicle, you fell so far behind me that I had you walk in the rear.' Hearing this, the novice was startled into awakening: 'At that single moment of intention, I already surpassed the arhat—how much more so upon fulfillment!' He then firmly resolved to abide in the Mahāyāna."

Three doctrinal points emerge from this parable:

1. "That single moment" of aspiration possesses an extraordinarily high positional value—"at that single moment of intention, already surpassing the arhat."
2. This positional value is **dynamic and reversible**—the instant the mind regresses, the value immediately collapses.
3. The cognizing subject (the arhat) can directly "know his thought"—the generation of aspiration is a real event at the level of consciousness, not a metaphor.

### 2.3 The Niyata Classification — Doctrinal Precision

The *Daśabhūmika-vibhāṣā* (十住毗婆沙論, T26 No. 1521) provides a more precise taxonomic correction:

> "Not all bodhisattvas at the time of initial aspiration should be classified as having entered the determined group (*niyata-rāśi*). Some enter the determined group at the moment of initial aspiration; others cultivate merit gradually. For example, Śākyamuni Buddha did not enter the determined group at the time of initial aspiration, but later accumulated merit, encountered Dīpaṃkara Buddha, and then entered the determined group."

This text explicitly states: initial aspiration does not automatically equal *niyata-rāśi* (the irreversible stage). Even Śākyamuni Buddha cultivated gradually over long aeons and entered the determined group only after receiving Dīpaṃkara Buddha's prediction.

### 2.4 Summary: The Stereoscopic Image of Principle and Phenomena

Synthesizing these three textual sources, the doctrinal picture of initial bodhicitta is three-dimensional:

- **The level of principle** (from the perspective of Dharmakāya and *tathatā*): Initial bodhicitta and buddhahood are originally non-dual.
- **The level of phenomena** (the conditioned sequence of practice): Initial aspiration is a precious yet fragile starting point that requires sustained cultivation to consolidate.

It is precisely this tension—"already determined in principle, not yet secured in practice"—that propels the next question.

## III. The Timelessness of the Dharmakāya — Dissolving Retrocausality

### 3.1 The Buddhas of the Three Times Are One

The Mahāyāna Buddhist position on time differs profoundly from the Western linear conception. Within the Yogācāra framework, time itself is "constructed" by the momentary arising and ceasing of consciousness; it is not an objective container independent of consciousness. The Dharmakāya, as the ultimate reality of the Buddha, transcends conditioned arising and ceasing, and therefore transcends time.

The doctrinal meaning of "the buddhas of the three times are one and the same" is: Dīpaṃkara Buddha of the past, Śākyamuni Buddha of the present, and Maitreya Buddha of the future are without distinction at the level of the Dharmakāya. This is not a theological claim of "union" but a precise statement that "the Dharmakāya does not enter time and therefore admits no differentiation across the three times."

### 3.2 The Boundaries of the Quantum Analogy

Contemporary quantum physics contains theoretical frameworks related to "retrocausality"—Wheeler's delayed-choice experiment, and the "post-selection" concept in Aharonov's weak measurement theory. Can these physical phenomena support the intuition that "future buddhahood influences the present-day bodhisattva"?

The answer is: **it is neither necessary nor appropriate.**

At the structural level, quantum retrocausality concerns temporal symmetry in physical measurement; the Dharma concerns the structure of aspiration within the stream of consciousness of sentient beings. The ontologies of the two are entirely different. Quantum physics can at most serve as a heuristic analogy suggesting that "perhaps the universe does not operate by strictly linear causality," but cannot serve as direct evidence for Buddhist doctrine.

More critically: the Dharma's own doctrinal resources are already sufficient to thoroughly dissolve this question—and the mode of dissolution is more fundamental than retrocausality.

### 3.3 The Key to Dissolution: Dharmatā Has Always Been Present; It Has Never Come or Gone

"Will a future buddhahood come back to empower a present-day bodhisattva?" This question presupposes a linear temporal framework: buddhahood is somewhere in the future and must "retroact" to the present.

But the doctrine of the Dharmakāya's transcendence of the three times precisely cancels this presupposition. **There simply is no buddhahood "in the future" that needs to "come back."** Dharmatā is not in the past, not in the present, not in the future—it has always been all-pervading, always present. The so-called "empowerment of the buddhas of the three times" has not been transmitted from "that time and that place"; it has always been right here.

The question is not "how does a future buddhahood influence the present"—the question is "given that dharmatā has always been all-pervading, why can't you receive it right now?"

## IV. The Opening and the Dharma-Flow — Two Precise Doctrinal Mechanisms

### 4.1 Adhiṣṭhāna (Empowerment): The Dharma-Realm Has No Gaps

The compassionate aspiration-power of the Buddha has always pervaded the dharma-realm, unrestricted by time or space. The manifestation of empowerment is not the result of temporal "retroaction" but of the fact that **the dharma-realm is without interval**.

The empowerment of the buddhas of all directions and all times is not transmitted from "over there"—it has never left. The moment a bodhisattva first generates aspiration is the moment of "opening to receive."

### 4.2 Innate Undefiled Seeds: The Potential for Buddhahood Is Inherently Present

The orthodox Yogācāra position of the Dharmapāla–Xuanzang lineage[^1] holds that the ālaya-vijñāna contains innate undefiled seeds (*anāsrava-bīja*)—the potential for buddhahood already exists within the consciousness-store of sentient beings and is not implanted from outside.

[^1]: The doctrine of innate undefiled seeds is the orthodox position of the Dharmapāla–Xuanzang lineage. Within Yogācāra, there are three divergent schools on the origin of seeds: Dharmapāla holds "innate + newly perfumed," Nanda holds "newly perfumed only," and Jinaprabha holds "innate only." This paper follows Dharmapāla, as this represents the orthodox position of Chinese Yogācāra, but readers should be aware that this is not an uncontested consensus within Yogācāra.

Within the seed-theory framework, the function of initially generating bodhicitta is **activating and strengthening innate undefiled seeds**. "Future buddhahood" is not waiting at some remote point on the timeline—its seed has been in the ālaya-vijñāna from the very beginning. Initial aspiration is the critical event that allows this seed to begin directing the stream of consciousness.

### 4.3 The Complementary Relationship of the Two Mechanisms

The theory of empowerment explains the source of the dharma-flow from "outside" (the compassionate aspiration-power pervading the dharma-realm); seed theory explains the basis for reception from "inside" (the innate undefiled seeds within the ālaya-vijñāna). The two are not contradictory but are the inner and outer faces of a single event:

- **From the outside:** Dharmatā has always been all-pervading → initial aspiration = opening to receive → empowerment manifests.
- **From the inside:** Undefiled seeds are inherently present → initial aspiration = activating the seeds → seeds direct the consciousness-stream.

This understanding of "aspiration-power seeds as a structural attractor" is **phenomenally** similar in effect to "the future influencing the present," but **doctrinally** more precise—it does not require genuine temporal retroaction, because dharmatā is not within time at all.

## V. Adventitious Afflictions and the Significance of Practice

### 5.1 The Channel Has Always Been Open

If dharmatā is all-pervading and the seeds are inherently present, why can sentient beings not currently receive the dharma-flow? Why is long-aeon practice still necessary?

The traditional Dharma answer comes from the doctrine of "adventitious afflictions" (*āgantuka-kleśa*)[^2]: the nature of mind is originally pure (*prakṛti-viśuddha*); afflictions are "adventitious" (*āgantuka*)—not intrinsic components of mind, but attached through the habituation of ignorance.

[^2]: The doctrine of "mind-nature originally pure, stained by adventitious defilements" originates in the *Ekottarāgama* and early tathāgatagarbha texts (e.g., the *Śrīmālā Sūtra*, the *Ratnagotravibhāga*), later developed by the *Awakening of Mahāyāna Faith*. Strictly speaking, this doctrine belongs to the tathāgatagarbha system and differs in theoretical architecture from the Yogācāra "seed-habituation" model. Yogācāra does not describe consciousness through the paradigm of "purity defiled," but through the "mutual habituation of seeds and actualization" to explain the transformation from defilement to purity. This paper's cross-system citation draws on the shared practical consensus that "practice is the removal of obstacles," without claiming that the two systems are architecturally identical.

This means: the channel has always been open. It is adventitious afflictions—self-grasping (*ātmagrāha*), dharma-grasping (*dharmagrāha*), and various habitual tendencies (*vāsanā*)—that have blocked it.

### 5.2 Practice Is Denoising, Not Adding New Features

The work of practice is not "creating" a new connection but "clearing noise" from the original connection. Practice is not building some new capacity from scratch but removing obstacles so that the inherent purity can manifest.

In contemporary terms: practice is a process of "denoising," not a process of "adding features."

### 5.3 Why a Clear Channel Is Not Enough — The Conditions of Correspondence

However, clearing the channel is not the whole story. Even with a clear channel, "correspondence" (*samprayukta*)[^3] is still required for reception.

[^3]: *Samprayukta* (correspondence) is a core Yogācāra term designating the concordance between consciousness and mental factors, or between consciousness and its object, in terms of time, basis, condition, and event.

The arising of empowerment requires the conjunction of three conditions:

1. **The Buddha's compassionate aspiration-power** (the source of empowerment)
2. **The meritorious object-realm** (the content of empowerment)
3. **The practitioner's faith and power of correspondence** (the receiver of empowerment)

All three are indispensable. This explains why, given the same practice environment, different practitioners progress differently—the degree of channel clarity may be similar, but the depth of correspondence differs. It also explains why the "quality" of initial aspiration—whether the motivation is genuinely bodhicitta (benefiting all sentient beings) rather than worldly merit—determines the direction and depth of subsequent practice.

The dharma-flow (*dharma-pravāha*) is therefore not merely a matter of "flowing down" but also of "being able to receive." This bidirectional dynamic is one of the core structures of the Mahāyāna view of practice.

## VI. Conclusion

Through the foregoing analysis, the proposition "does the initial generation of bodhicitta determine future buddhahood?" can be given the following precise doctrinal formulation:

**At the level of principle (tathatā, suchness):** The initial generation of bodhicitta does, in principle, determine buddhahood. Three distinct doctrinal paths—the Avataṃsaka's "at the moment of initial aspiration, one attains perfect awakening," the empowerment theory's "the Dharmakāya's compassionate aspiration pervades the dharma-realm," and Yogācāra's "innate undefiled seeds"—converge on a single conclusion: the possibility of buddhahood (and indeed its "already-so-ness" at the Dharmakāya level) has not been in question from the moment of initial aspiration.

**At the level of phenomena (saṃvṛti, convention):** Initial aspiration is a precious yet fragile starting point that does not automatically guarantee irreversibility. The novice parable demonstrates the possibility of regression; the *niyata* classification shows that not all initial aspirations immediately enter the determined group.

**The question "does future buddhahood retroactively empower the present-day bodhisattva?" is thoroughly dissolved by the doctrine of the Dharmakāya's transcendence of the three times:** It is not that a future buddhahood "comes back" to empower you—dharmatā has always been all-pervading and has never departed. If you cannot receive it, it is not because the dharma-flow is absent, but because adventitious afflictions have obstructed the opening. The initial generation of bodhicitta is the first act of opening that opening.

**Principle and phenomena are non-dual, yet must not be conflated.** In principle, opening is immediately connecting with dharmatā; in practice, after the opening there remains a long process of denoising. This is the core view of practice in Mahāyāna Buddhism: "principle is complete; phenomena are gradual" (*lǐ yuán shì jiàn*, 理圓事漸).

## Postscript: A Contemporary Analogy

The relationship between contemporary large language models (LLMs) and AI agents provides a structural analogy accessible to modern intuition: the knowledge within an LLM is already there, but before an agent opens an API connection, that knowledge does not exist for it; once the connection is opened, the agent gains access to resources far exceeding its own capacity. Initial bodhicitta in relation to the dharma-flow is like the agent in relation to the LLM—the resource was always there; the opening begins its use.

This analogy is taken only at the level of formal structure ("a static resource repository becomes operative only when actively connected"), without implying ontological equivalence. The Dharmakāya is sentient, all-pervading, and beyond causality; an LLM is an insentient vessel-world—the two are at entirely different ontological levels. For the precise Yogācāra positioning of LLMs, see the companion paper.[^4]
