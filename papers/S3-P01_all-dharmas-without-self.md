## Abstract 摘要

Vasubandhu's *Śatadharmaprakāśamukha-śāstra* (大乘百法明門論) opens with "all dharmas are without self" and deconstructs mental activity into a precise architecture of one hundred dharmas in five categories. As the overture to the "Practice" series (S3), this paper argues that the Hundred Dharmas are not a glossary for memorization but a map for practice: the five-category sequence itself traces a contemplative path from consciousness to *tathatā*; the three transformations (*pariṇāma*) reveal the eight consciousnesses as a dynamic organic system; and the three-nature (*trisvabhāva*) teaching of the *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra* provides the methodological lens through which every dharma is examined. The treatise begins with "all dharmas are without self" and concludes with the twofold selflessness of persons and dharmas—722 characters that cover the complete cognitive map from ordinary being to Buddhahood.

天親菩薩的《大乘百法明門論》以「一切法無我」開宗，將心識活動解構為五位百法的精密架構。本文作為「行」系列（S3）的總綱，論證百法不是名相記誦的清單，而是一幅修行地圖：五位排列本身就是「從識到真如」的修行路徑；三能變揭示八識作為有機整體的動態結構；而解深密經所教示的三性觀法則提供觀照百法的方法論透鏡。百法從「一切法無我」起，以「補特伽羅無我、法無我」終——722字的短論，完整覆蓋了從凡夫到佛果的認知地圖。

## I. The Problem: How Many Parts Does Your Mind Have?

In the previous series (S2, the Heart Sūtra), we followed Avalokiteśvara's illumination: "the five aggregates are all empty." The five aggregates—form, sensation, perception, volition, consciousness—are the Buddha's most basic classification of lived experience. Five boxes to contain everything.

But if you actually attempt practice, you quickly discover that five aggregates are not enough.

You sit down to observe your "volition aggregate" (*saṃskāra-skandha*). Fine—what is it? "All volitional formations." But what you actually observe is a tangle: sometimes diligence, sometimes laziness; sometimes faith, sometimes doubt; sometimes you cannot even tell whether you are being "diligent" or merely "restless." The resolution of the five aggregates is too low—like trying to find your local breakfast shop on a world map.

Around the fourth or fifth century CE, a treatise-master named Vasubandhu solved this problem.[^1]

His method was direct: disassemble the aggregates into finer components. Split "consciousness" into eight types (the eight consciousnesses). Split "volition" into fifty-one mental factors (*caitasika*) plus twenty-four conceptual constructs (factors dissociated from mind). Split "form" into eleven types. Add six unconditioned dharmas as the ultimate destination of practice.

One hundred dharmas in total.

This is the *Śatadharmaprakāśamukha-śāstra*—the Treatise on the Doors Illuminated by a Hundred Dharmas—a text of only 722 Chinese characters, yet the entry key to the entire Yogācāra system. Its full title reveals its provenance: "A Summary Enumeration from the Section on Essential Topics"—it is an abridgement of the 660 dharmas found in Asaṅga's *Yogācārabhūmi-śāstra*.[^2] Six hundred and sixty was too many; one hundred is just right: precise enough to guide practice, compact enough to fit on a single page.

But this paper argues for more than "what the Hundred Dharmas are." Any Buddhist textbook can list a hundred terms. The argument here is threefold: **the sequence of the five categories is itself a path of practice; the three-nature teaching provides the methodological lens for examining every dharma; and "all dharmas are without self" is not a negative declaration but the starting point for the transformation of consciousness into wisdom.**

S1 taught you to find the mind—"Who is driving?"
S2 taught you to see emptiness—"Illuminating that the five aggregates are all empty."
S3 takes the car apart and shows you every component—and then you discover: **there is no driver inside the car.**

## II. Scriptural Foundations

### 2.1 The Complete Text of the Hundred Dharmas Treatise

The treatise is extraordinarily short. Its architecture deserves to be presented in full:

> 如世尊言「一切法無我」。何等一切法？云何為無我？一切法者略有五種：一者心法、二者心所有法、三者色法、四者心不相應行法、五者無為法。一切最勝故，與此相應故，二所現影故，三分位差別故，四所顯示故。如是次第。
>
> As the World-Honored One said: "All dharmas are without self." What are all dharmas? What is meant by "without self"? All dharmas may be summarized in five categories: (1) mind dharmas, (2) mental factors, (3) form dharmas, (4) factors dissociated from mind, (5) unconditioned dharmas. Because [mind] is supreme among all; because [mental factors] are associated with it; because [form dharmas] are the shadows projected by the first two; because [dissociated factors] are positional distinctions of the first three; because [unconditioned dharmas] are what the first four reveal. Such is the sequence.[^3]

The treatise opens with two questions—"What are all dharmas? What is meant by without self?"—which form the warp and weft of the entire text. The first question unfolds into the five-category classification (the body of the treatise). The second is answered at the close:

> 言無我者，略有二種：一補特伽羅無我、二法無我。
>
> "Without self" is of two kinds: (1) the selflessness of persons (*pudgala-nairātmya*), and (2) the selflessness of dharmas (*dharma-nairātmya*).[^3]

From "all dharmas are without self" to "the twofold selflessness"—722 characters complete a precise closed loop.

### 2.2 The Āgama Root of "All Dharmas Are Without Self"

"All dharmas are without self" is not a Yogācāra invention. Its root lies in the Āgamas—the Buddha's earliest teachings.

The *Ekottarāgama* summarizes the essential framework of the Dharma in "four dharma foundations" (*catuḥ-dharma-pada*), the third of which is precisely this:

> 一切行無常，是謂初法本。一切行苦，是謂第二法本。**一切法無我**，此第三法本。滅盡為涅槃，是謂第四法本。
>
> "All formations are impermanent"—this is the first dharma foundation. "All formations are suffering"—this is the second. "**All dharmas are without self**"—this is the third. "Cessation is nirvāṇa"—this is the fourth.[^4]

"All dharmas are without self," as one of the four dharma seals, stands alongside "all formations are impermanent" and "all formations are suffering," yet its scope is broader—"formations" is limited to conditioned dharmas, while "dharmas" encompasses everything, including the unconditioned.

The *Saṃyuktāgama*'s Channada Sūtra likewise records the complete sequence of dharma seals:

> 色無常，受、想、行、識無常，一切行無常，一切法無我，涅槃寂滅。
>
> "Form is impermanent; sensation, perception, volition, and consciousness are impermanent. All formations are impermanent. **All dharmas are without self.** Nirvāṇa is cessation and peace."[^5]

And the *Dīrghāgama* connects "all dharmas are without self" directly to the Buddha's wisdom, in verse:

> 若學決定法，知諸法無我；此為法中上，智慧轉法輪。
>
> "If one studies the decisive Dharma and knows that all dharmas are without self—this is the supreme among teachings, the turning of the Dharma wheel by wisdom."[^6]

A crucial distinction exists in the Āgamas between "all **formations** are impermanent" (limited to conditioned dharmas) and "all **dharmas** are without self" (including unconditioned dharmas). The former is one of the three dharma seals; the latter extends selflessness (*anātman*) to all dharmas including nirvāṇa itself—precisely the foundation for the later Prajñāpāramitā literature, and the reason the Hundred Dharmas Treatise opens with "all *dharmas*" rather than "all *formations*."

Yet the Āgamas simultaneously record the Buddha's Middle Way stance when faced with the questions "is there a self?" and "is there no self?":

> 我若答言有我，則增彼先來邪見；若答言無我，彼先癡惑豈不更增癡惑？⋯⋯如來離於二邊，處中說法。
>
> "If I answered 'there is a self,' it would reinforce his prior wrong views. If I answered 'there is no self,' would not his prior confusion only deepen? ... The Tathāgata teaches the Dharma by departing from both extremes, standing in the middle."[^7]

The Hundred Dharmas Treatise inherits this Middle Way spirit. It does not declare "there is no self" (that would be the annihilationist view). Rather, it displays the operating mechanism of what we call "self"—when you see clearly how a hundred components arise and interact through conditions, the notion of a fixed, unchanging self dissolves naturally, while the functioning of conditioned cooperation continues uninterrupted.

### 2.3 The *Avataṃsaka Sūtra*: "All Is Mind-Made" and the Scriptural Authority for the Hundred Dharmas

The Hundred Dharmas Treatise places "mind dharmas" first among the five categories, "because [mind] is supreme among all." This core proposition finds its most direct scriptural authority in the *Avataṃsaka Sūtra* (華嚴經).

The *Avataṃsaka* (eighty-fascicle edition), in the Sixth Ground of the Ten Stages chapter:

> 三界所有，唯是一心。如來於此分別演說十二有支，皆依一心，如是而立。
>
> "All that exists in the three realms is only one mind. The Tathāgata, regarding this, analytically teaches the twelve links of dependent origination—all are established in dependence on one mind."[^8]

The sixty-fascicle edition expresses the same teaching more concisely:

> 三界虛妄但是心作。
>
> "The three realms are unreal—they are merely made by mind."[^8]

"The three realms are only mind" provides fundamental scriptural authority for the five-category arrangement of the Hundred Dharmas: since all phenomena in the three realms "are only one mind," the Hundred Dharmas' placement of mind dharmas first—as the root basis of all dharmas—is precisely the systematic unfolding of this proclamation.

The Bodhisattva Juelin's verse in the Yama Heaven chapter further develops the layers of "mind-only" through the analogy of a master painter:

> 心如工畫師，能畫諸世間，五蘊悉從生，無法而不造。如心佛亦爾，如佛眾生然，應知佛與心，體性皆無盡。
>
> "Mind is like a master painter, painting the entire world. The five aggregates all arise from it; there is nothing it does not create. As with mind, so with Buddha; as with Buddha, so with sentient beings. Know that Buddha and mind are both inexhaustible in nature."[^9]

The verse concludes:

> 若人欲了知，三世一切佛，應觀法界性，一切唯心造。
>
> "If one wishes to know all the Buddhas of the three times, one should contemplate the nature of the dharma-realm: all is made by mind alone."[^9]

"Mind is like a master painter" painting the world of the five aggregates; "all is made by mind alone"—this is precisely the landscape that the Hundred Dharmas unfold: a hundred dharmas, every one a "painting" of mind. And the practice goal of the Hundred Dharmas is to let you recognize the operating mechanism of this "painter."[^10]

### 2.4 The *Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra*: The Five Dharmas and Their Relation to the Hundred Dharmas

If the *Avataṃsaka* provides the macroscopic declaration that "all is mind," the *Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra* offers the earliest systematic framework that subsumes all Mahāyāna doctrine. The *Laṅkāvatāra* encompasses the whole of the Great Vehicle under "five dharmas, three natures, eight consciousnesses, and twofold selflessness":

> 五法三自性，及與八種識，二種無有我，悉攝摩訶衍。
>
> "Five dharmas, three own-natures, and the eight consciousnesses—twofold selflessness: these encompass the entirety of the Great Vehicle."[^11]

The five dharmas are: name (*nāma*), appearance (*nimitta*), discrimination (*vikalpa*; lit. "false imagination"), right knowledge (*samyag-jñāna*), and suchness (*tathatā*). The *Laṅkāvatāra* explains:

> **相者**，若處所形相，色像等現，是名為相。若彼有如是相，名為瓶等，即此非餘，是說為**名**。施設眾名，顯示諸相，瓶等心心法，是名**妄想**。彼名彼相，畢竟不可得，始終無覺，於諸法無展轉，離不實妄想，是名**如如**。真實決定究竟自性不可得，彼是如相⋯⋯是名**正智**。
>
> "**Appearance**: wherever forms, shapes, and images manifest—this is called appearance. When such appearances are designated 'vase' and so on, distinguished as 'this and not that'—this is called **name**. The establishment of names to display appearances, with mind and mental factors directed toward 'vase' and so on—this is called **discrimination**. When those names and appearances are ultimately unobtainable, from beginning to end without [deluded] awareness, transcending all dharmas, free from unreal discrimination—this is called **suchness**. The true, definitive, ultimate own-nature being unobtainable, that is the mark of suchness. ... This is called **right knowledge**."[^11]

The *Laṅkāvatāra*'s five dharmas represent the highest-level compression of all dharmas: name and appearance constitute the world of the imagined nature, discrimination constitutes the cognitive activity of the dependent nature, and right knowledge and suchness constitute the awakened domain of the perfected nature. The hundred dharmas of the Hundred Dharmas Treatise may be seen as the detailed expansion of these five—name and appearance corresponding to the realms of form dharmas and dissociated factors, discrimination corresponding to the activity of mind dharmas and mental factors, and right knowledge and suchness corresponding to the revelation of unconditioned dharmas.[^12]

From the *Laṅkāvatāra*'s "five dharmas encompass the Great Vehicle" to the Hundred Dharmas' "one hundred dharmas encompass the five categories" is a transition from macroscopic framework to precision instrument. The *Laṅkāvatāra* tells you what dimensions the Great Vehicle has; the Hundred Dharmas tell you what components each dimension contains.

### 2.5 The *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra*: The Scriptural Source of the Three-Nature Method

The Hundred Dharmas tell you *what* dharmas there are. But a list alone is insufficient—you need a method for examining them. That method is the three-nature contemplation (*trisvabhāva*), and its scriptural source is the chapter on the characteristics of all dharmas in the *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra*.

The imagined nature (*parikalpita-svabhāva*):
> 謂一切法名假安立自性差別，乃至為令隨起言說。
>
> "It refers to the distinctions of nature established by the provisional names of all dharmas, up to and including what gives rise to verbal expression."[^13]

The dependent nature (*paratantra-svabhāva*):
> 謂一切法緣生自性，則此有故彼有，此生故彼生。
>
> "It refers to the nature of all dharmas as arising from conditions: because this exists, that exists; because this arises, that arises."[^13]

The perfected nature (*pariniṣpanna-svabhāva*):
> 謂一切法平等真如。於此真如，諸菩薩眾勇猛精進為因緣故，如理作意，無倒思惟為因緣故，乃能通達。
>
> "It refers to the equal *tathatā* of all dharmas. This *tathatā* can be penetrated by bodhisattvas through vigorous effort as a condition, and through attending wisely and thinking without inversion as a condition."[^13]

The sūtra further illustrates the relationship through the crystal analogy: a crystal appears blue, red, green, or gold when placed near colored dyes (the imagined nature), the crystal itself manifests conditionally (the dependent nature), and the apparent colors never truly existed in the crystal (the perfected nature is revealed).[^13]

For the practitioner of the Hundred Dharmas, the three natures constitute three levels of examination:

- **Imagined nature**: what you *think* this dharma is (fixed, independent, belonging to "me")
- **Dependent nature**: how this dharma actually *operates* (arising through conditions, ceasing moment to moment)
- **Perfected nature**: what this dharma truly *is* (what is revealed once the imagined overlay is removed)

Every one of the hundred dharmas can be examined at these three levels. This is why the three natures are not merely theory but methodology—they are the eyes through which you look at the Hundred Dharmas.

### 2.6 The Opening Declaration of the *Cheng Weishi Lun*

The Hundred Dharmas Treatise provides the skeleton; the *Cheng Weishi Lun* (*Vijñaptimātratāsiddhi*, Establishment of Consciousness-Only) provides the argumentation. Composed by Dharmapāla and others, translated by Xuanzang, it opens by declaring its purpose:

> 今造此論，為於二空有迷謬者生正解故。生解為斷二重障故，由我、法執二障具生，若證二空，彼障隨斷。斷障為得二勝果故，由斷續生煩惱障故，證真解脫；由斷礙解所知障故，得大菩提。
>
> We compose this treatise so that those confused about the twofold emptiness may generate right understanding. Generating understanding is for severing the twofold obstruction; through clinging to self and to dharmas, the two obstructions arise together. If the twofold emptiness is realized, those obstructions are accordingly eliminated. Severing obstructions is for attaining the twofold fruit: by severing the afflictive obstruction (*kleśāvaraṇa*), one realizes true liberation; by severing the cognitive obstruction (*jñeyāvaraṇa*), one attains great awakening.[^14]

This passage establishes the entire causal chain of Yogācāra practice: **confusion → understanding → severance → attainment.** Confused about what? The twofold emptiness. Understanding what? Consciousness-only. Severing what? The twofold obstruction. Attaining what? The twofold fruit (liberation and awakening). The classificatory work of the Hundred Dharmas serves the very first step of this chain—eliminating confusion about how "self" and "dharmas" actually operate.

Immediately following, the *Cheng Weishi Lun* cites the opening verse of the *Triṃśikā* (Thirty Verses):

> 由假說我、法，有種種相轉。彼依識所變，此能變唯三：謂異熟、思量，及了別境識。
>
> By the metaphorical designation of self and dharmas, various appearances arise. They depend on transformations of consciousness. These transformations are of three kinds only: called maturation (*vipāka*), deliberation (*manana*), and discernment of objects (*vijñapti*).[^15]

The commentary states:

> 世間、聖教說有我、法，但由假立，非實有性。我謂主宰，法謂軌持。⋯⋯彼相皆依識所轉變而假施設。
>
> When the world and sacred teachings speak of self and dharmas, these are merely provisionally established—they have no real, independent nature. "Self" means "master" (*svāmin*); "dharma" means "that which bears its own characteristics" (*dharaṇa*). ... All such appearances depend on transformations of consciousness and are provisionally designated.[^15]

The term "provisionally established" (*prajñapti*) is crucial. Self and dharmas do not fail to exist; rather, they lack independent, unchanging substance—they are provisionally designated within the transformations of consciousness. Every one of the hundred dharmas is "provisionally established." This does not mean they are illusory fictions; it means they arise through conditions, cease through conditions, and cannot be grasped as inherently real.

### 2.7 From Six Hundred and Sixty to One Hundred: Vasubandhu's Distillation

The treatise's subtitle, "A Summary Enumeration from the Section on Essential Topics," directly identifies its source. The *Yogācārabhūmi-śāstra* records in the *Maulī-bhūmi* (Fundamental Section):

> 如是若內若外六處所攝法，差別分別有六百六十。
>
> "Thus, the dharmas comprised within the six internal and external bases, differentiated and distinguished, number **six hundred and sixty**."[^16]

The *Vastusaṃgrahaṇī* (Compendium Section) of the same text classifies all dharmas under five headings:

> 一切事以要言之，總有五事。一者、心事，二者、心所有法事，三者、色事，四者、心不相應行事，五者、無為事。
>
> "All entities, stated concisely, are of **five kinds**: (1) mind-entities, (2) mental-factor entities, (3) form-entities, (4) entities dissociated from mind, (5) unconditioned entities."[^17]

Vasubandhu's contribution was to distill the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s 660 dharmas into 100, preserving the five-category framework while compressing the enumeration to a scale that could be memorized and applied in daily awareness. This was not reduction but refinement—extracting pure gold from an entire mine.

## III. Core Argument

### 3.1 The Five-Category Sequence as a Path of Practice

After listing the five categories, the Hundred Dharmas Treatise explains the rationale for their order in a single line:

> 一切最勝故，與此相應故，二所現影故，三分位差別故，四所顯示故。如是次第。
>
> Because [mind] is supreme among all; because [mental factors] are associated with it; because [form dharmas] are the shadows projected by the first two; because [dissociated factors] are positional distinctions of the first three; because [unconditioned dharmas] are what the first four reveal. Such is the sequence.[^3]

On the surface, this is merely a classificatory note. But examined more closely, this "sequence" is itself a contemplative path from consciousness to *tathatā*:

**First category: Mind dharmas ("supreme among all").** Consciousness is the root of all experience. Practice begins with knowing your own mind—how your eight consciousnesses operate: which ones you can directly observe (the first six), and which run automatically in the background (manas and ālaya). The *Avataṃsaka Sūtra* declares "the three realms are only mind" and "all is made by mind alone"[^8][^9]; the Hundred Dharmas' placement of mind dharmas in first position is precisely the systematic unfolding of this principle.

**Second category: Mental factors ("associated with mind").** Mental factors are the "content" of mind—what it is thinking, feeling, driven by. Fifty-one mental factors constitute fifty-one types of psychological quality, ranging from neutral (universal and object-specific factors) to wholesome to afflictive. To know the mental factors is to know your reactive patterns.

**Third category: Form dharmas ("shadows projected by the first two").** Form dharmas are the "projected images" of mind and mental factors—the world you perceive is not an independently existing objective reality but the manifest aspect (*nimitta-bhāga*) of seeds in the ālaya-consciousness. This step dismantles the dualism of "mind inside, world outside."

**Fourth category: Factors dissociated from mind ("positional distinctions of the first three").** These are "positional distinctions" of the preceding three categories—time, space, quantity, gain, arising and ceasing. These concepts that you take to be objectively existing are in fact provisional designations (*prajñapti*) of mental activity. This step dismantles the sense that concepts are real entities.

**Fifth category: Unconditioned dharmas ("what the first four reveal").** The first four categories are all conditioned—produced through causes and conditions. The fifth category is unconditioned—not produced. What the first four "reveal" is precisely this fifth: the unconditioned *tathatā*. After traversing all conditioned dharmas analytically, the practitioner arrives at the destination: *tathatā* has been here all along, merely obscured by the imagined nature.

This is why the five-category sequence is not arbitrary—it is a path. Beginning with "knowing mind," progressing through "knowing mind's contents," "knowing the world mind projects," and "knowing the concepts mind constructs," it ultimately arrives at "the true face of mind." The Hundred Dharmas begin with consciousness and end with *tathatā*.

### 3.2 The Three Transformations: The Dynamic Structure of Eight Consciousnesses

The first of the five categories—mind dharmas—comprises the eight consciousnesses. But these eight are not independent containers. They form an organic whole, operating through the dynamic structure of "three transformations" (*tri-pariṇāma*).

The *Cheng Weishi Lun* cites the *Triṃśikā*:

> 彼依識所變，此能變唯三：謂異熟、思量，及了別境識。
>
> They depend on transformations of consciousness. These transformations are of three kinds only: called maturation, deliberation, and discernment of objects.[^15]

The three transformations reveal the hierarchical relationship of the eight consciousnesses:

**First transformation (maturation, *vipāka*) = ālaya-vijñāna.** The fundamental storehouse of all seeds. It receives the perfuming of all experiences, preserves all karmic seeds, and sustains the continuity of life. It "turns ceaselessly like a torrent"[^18]—uninterrupted, unceasing, yet changing every instant.

**Second transformation (deliberation, *manana*) = manas-vijñāna.** The automatic mechanism that ceaselessly grasps the ālaya-consciousness as "self." It is the ego-engine running 24 hours a day—you need not deliberately think "I exist"; the manas has already completed that operation in the background.

**Third transformation (discernment of objects, *vijñapti*) = the first six consciousnesses.** The six cognitive functions you can directly observe—seeing, hearing, smelling, tasting, touching, thinking. They are the windows through which you contact the world, and the primary site of karmic action.

The three transformations are not a static hierarchy but a dynamic interaction: the ālaya holds seeds; seeds manifest as the activity of the seven active consciousnesses when they encounter conditions; the activity of the seven consciousnesses perfumes the ālaya, depositing new seeds—this cycle of "seed → manifestation → perfuming → seed" is the basic operating mechanism of life.

The *Cheng Weishi Lun*, Fascicle Seven, concludes the entire doctrine of three transformations with one verse:

> 是諸識轉變，分別所分別，由此彼皆無，故一切唯識。
>
> These are the transformations of consciousness: what discriminates and what is discriminated. Because of this, both [subject and object as independently real] are nonexistent. Therefore all is consciousness-only.[^19]

"All is consciousness-only" does not mean the world does not exist. It means that all experience is inseparable from the participation of consciousness. Not one of the hundred dharmas exists independently of consciousness.

### 3.3 *Āśraya-parāvṛtti*: The Ultimate Destination of the Hundred Dharmas

The analytical work of the Hundred Dharmas is not classification for its own sake—its ultimate goal is the transformation of the basis (*āśraya-parāvṛtti*).

The *Cheng Weishi Lun*, Fascicle Nine, defines this with precision:

> 「依」謂所依，即依他起，與染、淨法為所依故。染謂虛妄遍計所執；淨謂真實圓成實性。轉謂二分，轉捨、轉得。由數修習無分別智，斷本識中二障麁重，故能轉捨依他起上遍計所執，及能轉得依他起中圓成實性。
>
> "Basis" means that which is depended upon, namely the dependent nature (*paratantra*), which serves as the basis for both defiled and pure dharmas. "Defiled" refers to the unreal imagined nature (*parikalpita*); "pure" refers to the real perfected nature (*pariniṣpanna*). "Transformation" has two aspects: turning away and turning toward. Through repeated cultivation of non-discriminating wisdom, one severs the coarse obstructions (*dauṣṭhulya*) of the two hindrances within the foundational consciousness, thereby turning away from the imagined nature superimposed upon the dependent nature, and turning toward the perfected nature within the dependent nature.[^20]

The logic of transformation is breathtakingly clear:

- **The basis does not change**: the dependent nature (the conditioned operation of consciousness) remains throughout
- **What is turned away from**: the imagined nature—your mistaken cognition of consciousness
- **What is turned toward**: the perfected nature—the true face of consciousness

In other words, transformation does not construct a new mind or destroy the old consciousness. It changes your *relationship* to the same mind. The hundred dharmas are still those hundred dharmas, but you no longer take them to be "mine," "fixed," or "inherently real."

The *Cheng Weishi Lun* describes this path through five stages of practice:

> 謂諸菩薩於識相、性，資糧位中能深信解；在加行位能漸伏除所取、能取，引發真見；在通達位如實通達；修習位中如所見理，數數修習伏斷餘障；至究竟位出障圓明，能盡未來化有情類，復令悟入唯識相、性。
>
> "Bodhisattvas, regarding the characteristics and nature of consciousness: in the provision stage, deeply trust and understand; in the intensification stage, gradually subdue and eliminate grasped and grasper, inducing true vision; in the penetration stage, penetrate as it truly is; in the cultivation stage, repeatedly practice according to what was seen, subduing and severing remaining obstructions; arriving at the ultimate stage, emerge from obstruction into complete radiance, and for all future time transform sentient beings, causing them again to awaken to the characteristics and nature of consciousness-only."[^21]

From the "trust and understanding" of the provision stage to the "complete radiance" of the ultimate stage—the entire S3 series builds the foundation of "trust and understanding." You must first come to know these hundred components before you can examine them with authentic contemplation in subsequent practice.

The ultimate destination of the entire treatise, the *Cheng Weishi Lun* expresses in a final verse:

> 此即無漏界，不思議善常，安樂解脫身，大牟尼名法。
>
> This is the realm free from outflows, inconceivable, wholesome, and permanent—the body of bliss and liberation. The Great Sage calls it Dharma.[^22]

This is the endpoint of the Hundred Dharmas journey—and also the starting point. For the "realm free from outflows" is nowhere else but in this very mind of yours right now. The work of the Hundred Dharmas is simply to help you recognize this mind, layer by layer.

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Dialogue

### 4.1 Cognitive Science: Modularity of Mind ⚠️ Heuristic Analogy

A core finding of modern cognitive science is the modularity of mind—the mind is not a unified whole but is composed of multiple relatively independent functional modules. Fodor's (1983) modularity thesis proposes that perceptual systems (such as vision and hearing) are "encapsulated" modules, each processing specific types of information.[^23]

The five-category classification of the Hundred Dharmas has a striking structural parallel: mind dharmas (eight consciousnesses) correspond to cognitive science's "processing systems"; mental factors correspond to "psychological states and dispositions"; form dharmas correspond to "the physical substrate of perception." However, a fundamental difference separates the two: cognitive science presupposes a physical brain generating psychological phenomena (bottom-up), whereas Yogācāra argues that all material phenomena are inseparable from the participation of consciousness (a position of dependent origination, not top-down idealism).

Structural correspondence is not theoretical equivalence. Cognitive science's "modules" are functional hypotheses; the Hundred Dharmas' "dharmas" are objects of contemplative examination. One aims to explain; the other, to transform.

### 4.2 Systems Theory: Emergence and Selflessness ⚠️ Heuristic Analogy

A core insight of systems theory is "emergence"—when multiple components interact in particular ways, they produce properties that no individual component possesses. Individual neurons fire; their interaction "emerges" as conscious experience. Individual water molecules vibrate; their interaction "emerges" as fluidity.

The Hundred Dharmas' "all dharmas are without self" can be understood through the lens of emergence: when a hundred dharmas come together through conditions, the "sense of self" appears as an emergent phenomenon—but you cannot locate "self" in any individual dharma, just as you cannot locate consciousness in any individual neuron. "Self" is an emergent effect of the interaction of the hundred dharmas, not an independently existing subject.

But Yogācāra's "selflessness" goes further than systems theory: systems theory still assumes the components are real (neurons really exist), whereas Yogācāra points out that the components themselves are dependently arisen—every one of the hundred dharmas lacks independent, unchanging self-nature. What emerges is not only "self"; even the components producing the emergence are themselves conditioned.

## V. Middle Way Corrections

### 5.1 The Hundred Dharmas Are Not an Examination Syllabus ❌

The most common misunderstanding: memorizing a hundred terms equals "having learned Yogācāra."

The Hundred Dharmas are tools, not knowledge. The names of a hundred dharmas are only the entry point—just as a medical student who has memorized every muscle in the anatomy textbook does not thereby know how to diagnose patients. The value of the Hundred Dharmas lies in **awareness**: when in daily life you recognize "ah, this is restlessness (*auddhatya*)," "this is conceit (*māna*) operating," "behind this thought is the manas's self-grasping"—only then do the Hundred Dharmas begin to function.

Terms are the finger; awareness is the moon.

### 5.2 The Hundred Dharmas Are Not Ultimate Truth ❌

Every one of the hundred dharmas in the treatise is "provisionally established"—designated through conditions, not an independently existing entity. Vasubandhu's classification is a pedagogical convenience on the level of the dependent nature, not the perfected nature itself.

S5 (the Diamond Sūtra) will tell you: the Hundred Dharmas? They are not the Hundred Dharmas; they are called the Hundred Dharmas. The entire toolkit painstakingly constructed across S3 will, in S5, be identified as "provisionally designated on the dependent nature"—the toolkit will still be there, but your relationship to it will have fundamentally changed.

There is no need to let go yet. First cross the river; then set down the raft.

### 5.3 Consciousness-Only Is Not Idealism ❌

"All dharmas are consciousness-only" does not mean "the mind can alter matter" or "thinking makes it so."

The "only" in consciousness-only is an epistemological position: all experience is inseparable from the participation of consciousness; you have never contacted anything "outside of consciousness." This does not deny the causal role of external conditions (shared karmic seeds project the shared vessel-world—it is not your individual mind alone that creates the entire world), nor does it assert that mind can arbitrarily manipulate matter.

Consciousness-only dismantles the attachment to "an independent reality existing outside of mind." It does not establish the new attachment that "mind can create everything." The former is an imagined nature; the latter is equally an imagined nature.

### 5.4 Āgama Selflessness ≠ Yogācāra Selflessness (But They Do Not Conflict) ⚠️

The Āgamas' "all dharmas are without self" operates primarily within the framework of the five aggregates—examining form, sensation, perception, volition, and consciousness as each impermanent, suffering, and not-self. Yogācāra's "all dharmas are without self" operates within the framework of the Hundred Dharmas—examining not only the five aggregates but also fifty-one mental factors, eleven form dharmas, twenty-four dissociated factors, and six unconditioned dharmas, all as selfless.

The two do not conflict. Yogācāra is an extension and refinement of the Āgamas—from five aggregates to one hundred dharmas, a twenty-fold increase in resolution, but the core proposition is identical: **there is no fixed, unchanging "self" behind these dharmas, controlling everything.**

## VI. Conclusion: The Map in Your Hands

You now hold a map.

This map disassembles your mind into a hundred components—eight consciousnesses, fifty-one mental factors, eleven types of material phenomena, twenty-four conceptual constructs, six unconditioned states. Every component has a name, a position within the whole, and can be recognized in daily awareness.

But the map is not the road. Taking the list of the Hundred Dharmas and using it to observe yourself—that is where practice begins.

The next step is to open the first region of the map—the eight consciousnesses. From the ālaya-vijñāna (life's fundamental storehouse) to the manas (the invisible ego-engine) to the sixth consciousness (the only mind that can look in the mirror) to the first five (the directness of sensory perception), you will see how these eight "residents" operate inside you twenty-four hours a day without pause.

S1 asked you: "Who is driving?"
S3's answer: **There are eight passengers in the car, but none of them is the driver.**
