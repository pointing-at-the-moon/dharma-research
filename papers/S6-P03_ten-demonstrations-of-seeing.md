## Abstract

Close your eyes. What do you see? If you answer "nothing," you have already skipped over a fact: you are seeing. If you answer "black," you have skipped over the same fact — what is it that sees the black? The ten demonstrations of seeing (*shí fān xiǎn jiàn* 十番顯見) in the Śūraṅgama Sūtra address precisely this perpetual oversight: you forgot that you are seeing. From ten angles — immovability, imperishability, non-loss, nothing to return, non-mixture, non-obstruction, beyond size, beyond is-and-is-not, neither caused nor uncaused — the Buddha demonstrates the same truth: the seeing-nature (*jiàn xìng* 見性) has always been functioning, but sentient beings fabricate names and forms (*nāma-rūpa*) so quickly that they leap past the seeing itself. Drawing on Jiaokuang Zhenjian's classification of the ten demonstrations as "empty tathāgatagarbha" (*kōng rúlái zàng* 空如來藏) and employing the Yogācāra four-aspect model (*sì fēn shuō* 四分說) as an analytical instrument, this paper examines the doctrinal architecture of each demonstration and argues that the seeing-nature is not something to be found — you have been using it all along; you simply forgot.

閉上眼睛，你看到什麼？如果你回答「什麼都沒看到」，你已經跳過了一個事實——你正在看。楞嚴經十番顯見的教學核心，正是這件你每時每刻都在做、卻從未注意到的事：你忘了你在看。本文以交光真鑑「空如來藏」的判教定位為架構，以唯識四分說為分析工具，逐層解讀十番顯見的義理結構，並指出：見性不是需要你去「找到」的東西——你一直在用它，只是你忘了。

**Keywords:** Ten Demonstrations of Seeing 十番顯見, Seeing-Nature 見性, Empty Tathāgatagarbha 空如來藏, Revealing Truth While Bearing Delusion 帶妄顯真, Four-Aspect Model 四分說, Svasaṃvedana 自證分

## I. Introduction: You Forgot That You Are Seeing

Close your eyes.

What do you see?

Most people answer "nothing." But this answer has already skipped over a fact — you *are* seeing. The very ability to report "nothing" presupposes that you looked, evaluated what you saw, and judged the content to be absent.

If you are slightly more honest, you might say "black." But this, too, skips over the same fact — you are seeing your eyelids, and you have named that visual experience "black." The fabrication of name-and-form (*nāma-rūpa*) operates so rapidly that it outruns your attention: you see first, then name, then forget that you saw.[^1]

This is precisely the problem that the Śūraṅgama Sūtra's ten demonstrations of seeing set out to address.

In the preceding paper (S6-P02), the Buddha employed the seven locations inquiry (*qī chù zhēng xīn* 七處徵心) to dismantle every claim Ānanda made about where the mind resides — not inside the body, not outside, not in the sense organ, not between light and dark, not arising with contact, not in the middle, not in non-attachment. The seven locations stripped the false mind (*wàng xīn* 妄心) of all its disguises.[^2]

But what comes after demolition? Ānanda now knows what the mind is *not*, yet he has not seen what the seeing-nature *is*. He possesses negation without revelation.

This is the entrance to the ten demonstrations. The Buddha no longer tells Ānanda what is not the case. Instead, he directly displays, from ten angles, the characteristics of the seeing-nature — not by defining it conceptually (that would merely produce another name-form), but by ten live demonstrations that point toward what Ānanda has been using all along but keeps forgetting.

Jiaokuang Zhenjian 交光真鑑, in his *Śūraṅgama Sūtra Commentary on the True Pulse* (*Zhèngmài Shū* 正脈疏), precisely maps the relationship between the seven locations and the ten demonstrations:

> At the level of consciousness (*shí* 識): complete demolition of delusion. At the level of the root (*gēn* 根): mostly revealing truth, with minor demolition of delusion.[^3]

The seven locations operate at the consciousness level — "complete demolition." The ten demonstrations operate at the root level — "revealing truth while bearing delusion" (*dài wàng xiǎn zhēn* 帶妄顯真). This does not mean exhibiting a perfectly purified true suchness, but rather pointing to truth under the real conditions of an ordinary person who still carries delusion.

These four characters — *dài wàng xiǎn zhēn* — are the methodological foundation of the entire paper.

## II. Scriptural Foundations

### A. Sūtra Sources: The Śūraṅgama's Ten Demonstrations

#### 2.1 From the Seven Locations to the Ten Demonstrations: The Transitional Teaching of Guest-Dust

The seven locations inquiry does not end with a pause. The Buddha immediately employs the metaphor of "guest and dust" (*kè chén* 客塵) as a transitional teaching. Kauṇḍinya, who first awakened at the Buddha's initial turning of the Dharma wheel, offers the analogy of a traveler at an inn: a guest departs but the innkeeper remains; dust swirls but space stays still.[^4]

This passage bridges demolition and revelation. Immediately following, the Buddha presents the first of the ten demonstrations — **demonstrating that seeing is a function of mind** (*xiǎn jiàn shì xīn* 顯見是心):

> "The eye can manifest color; thus the seeing-nature is mind, not eye."[^5]

A lamp illuminates colors, but one does not say "the lamp sees." The eye manifests colors, but "seeing" does not belong to the eye — it belongs to mind. This first demonstration establishes the keynote: the seeing-nature is not a sensory function but a function of mind itself.

The second demonstration — **seeing does not move** (*xiǎn jiàn bù dòng* 顯見不動) — follows immediately in Fascicle One. The Buddha opens and closes his fist; Ānanda sees the hand opening and closing. The Buddha asks: does the hand open and close, or does your seeing-nature open and close?

> "The Buddha's hand does not rest, yet my seeing-nature does not even possess stillness — who could be without rest?"[^6]

The hand exhibits motion and stillness, but the seeing-nature possesses neither — it does not belong to the category of "motion and stillness" at all. The Buddha then summarizes for the assembly:

> "From beginning to end, thought after thought arising and perishing, you have lost your true nature. Acting in inversion, your nature-mind has lost its truth; you take objects for yourself. Cycling within this, you seize your own transmigration."[^7]

You take what moves as yourself, what changes as reality — and the unchanging true nature is forgotten.

#### 2.2 The Core Four Demonstrations: Imperishability, Nothing to Return, Non-Mixture, Non-Obstruction

**Third Demonstration: Seeing does not perish.** The dialogue with King Prasenajit is the most emotionally resonant passage among the ten. The king recounts his aging: at twenty already declining from ten, at thirty declining from twenty, now at sixty-two, moment by moment never ceasing to change. The Buddha asks: does your body contain something that does not perish? The king does not know. The Buddha then introduces the Ganges River — you saw this river at age three, at age thirteen, at age sixty-two. The water is the same. Your face has wrinkled, but has your *seeing* of the river aged?

> "Your face may wrinkle, but this seeing-essence has never wrinkled. What wrinkles is subject to change; what does not wrinkle is not subject to change. What changes undergoes destruction; that which does not change has fundamentally never arisen or perished — why would it be subject to your birth and death?"[^8]

**Fifth Demonstration: Seeing has nothing to return (The Eight Returns).** This is the most logically rigorous of the ten. The Buddha opens with the famous finger-and-moon analogy — taking his voice as your mind is like taking the pointing finger as the moon itself; you lose not only the moon but even the finger.[^9]

He then enumerates eight phenomenal qualities: brightness returns to the sun, darkness to the moonless night, openness to doors and windows, obstruction to walls, discrimination to mental differentiation, blankness to empty space, murkiness to dust, clarity to a clear sky. Each can be "returned" to its causal source. But:

> "Whatever can be returned is naturally not you; that which cannot be returned to anything — if not you, then who?"[^10]

Notably, before commencing the eight returns, the Buddha calibrates expectations regarding what this exercise reveals:

> "This seeing, though not the wondrous, luminous essence of mind, is like the second moon — not a mere reflection of the moon."[^11]

The "seeing-essence" (*jiàn jīng* 見精) that can be observed at this stage is not the full tathāgatagarbha; it is like the visual doubling one sees when pressing the eye — not the moon itself, but not a mere reflection either. It is truth bearing delusion, not pure delusion.

**Sixth Demonstration: Seeing does not mix.** The Buddha instructs Ānanda to survey everything from near to far — sun, moon, mountains, rivers, birds, dust, trees, people. All of these are "objects" (*wù* 物), not "you." Yet the seeing-essence that observes all of them:

> "The myriad objects each have their distinctions, yet the seeing-nature has no such distinctions. This refined luminosity is truly your seeing-nature. ... The seeing-nature pervades everywhere — if not you, then who?"[^12]

**Seventh Demonstration: Seeing is unobstructed.** The Buddha systematically eliminates causal accounts: if seeing arises because of brightness, it should not see darkness; if because of darkness, it should not see brightness; likewise for space and obstruction. The conclusion:

> "Know that this refined awakening, wondrous and luminous, is neither cause nor condition, neither natural nor non-natural; neither non-non nor not-non; neither is nor is-not. It transcends all characteristics, and is identical with all dharmas."[^13]

These eighteen characters constitute the doctrinal heart of the entire Śūraṅgama Sūtra. The seeing-nature is not produced by causes and conditions (otherwise it would perish with them), nor is it a self-existent natural entity (otherwise it should manifest only one unchanging appearance). It transcends every conceptual framework that could be applied to it, yet is identical with the nature of all dharmas.

#### 2.3 The Three Transcendent Demonstrations: Non-Loss, Beyond Is-and-Is-Not, Beyond Size

**Fourth Demonstration: Seeing is not lost ("When seeing sees seeing").** After establishing that seeing is not equivalent to what is seen — seeing brightness is not brightness, seeing darkness is not darkness, seeing space is not space, seeing obstruction is not obstruction — the Buddha delivers the most enigmatic line among all ten demonstrations:

> "When seeing sees seeing, that seeing is not seeing. Seeing still transcends seeing; seeing cannot reach it."[^14]

When you attempt to turn seeing upon seeing itself, what you "see" is no longer the seeing-nature. The seeing-nature outruns your attempt to objectify it — you cannot use the eye to look at itself, you cannot use the blade to cut its own edge.

**Ninth Demonstration: Seeing transcends is-and-is-not.** Ānanda tries to point out which among the myriad objects is "seeing." He fails:

> "Everything I point to is an object; none is seeing. ... Even bodhisattvas cannot extract a pure seeing from the myriad objects as something with an independent self-nature."[^15]

Seeing cannot be isolated as an object among objects — yet it pervades all perception.

**Tenth Demonstration: Seeing transcends size.** Ānanda asks: when gazing at the sun, the seeing-nature encompasses the cosmos; upon returning indoors, it fills only the room — did the seeing-nature shrink, or did the walls cut it off? The Buddha replies with the analogy of space in square and round vessels — space itself has no shape; it is the vessel that imposes apparent shape. Then:

> "All sentient beings from beginningless time have mistaken themselves for objects, lost their original mind, and are turned by objects. Hence they perceive large and small. If you could turn objects rather than being turned by them, you would be the same as the Tathāgata — body and mind perfectly luminous, the unmoving sanctuary, able to contain the ten directions of lands upon a single hair-tip."[^16]

#### 2.4 Transition: Ānanda's Tears and the Path to the Four Categories

After the ten demonstrations, Ānanda's response is deeply affecting:

> "My mind is still not opened; and now, hearing that 'when seeing sees seeing, seeing is not seeing,' my confusion has only deepened."
>
> Having said this, **he wept and prostrated**, receiving the holy instruction.[^17]

Ānanda understood every word. Yet his mind remained "unopened." He weeps not from frustration but from the recognition that he *should* understand — and does not. This is the gap between knowing (*zhī* 知) and realization (*zhèng* 證) in its most poignant expression.

The Buddha then announces the transition to a broader scope:

> "Arising and perishing, coming and going — fundamentally the tathāgatagarbha, eternally abiding, wondrous, luminous, unmoving, perfectly complete, the true nature of wonderful suchness."[^18]

The five aggregates, six sense-entries, twelve sense-bases, and eighteen elements are all originally tathāgatagarbha. This is the entrance to S6-P04.

### B. Commentary Sources: Doctrinal Positioning by Two Major Commentators

#### 2.5 Zixuan's *Śūraṅgama Commentary*: Three Qualities That Distinguish the Real

Zixuan 子璿 does not employ the term "ten demonstrations," classifying the entire passage under "progressively illuminating the true seeing through demolishing inversion."[^19] His core insight distills the ten demonstrations into three qualities:

> "Three qualities distinguish the real across these passages: first, revealing the absence of arising and perishing; second, demonstrating superior function; third, showing non-differentiation."[^20]

Regarding the pivotal phrase "transcending all characteristics, identical with all dharmas," Zixuan's analysis is particularly incisive. He identifies eight propositions — causation, naturalness, is, is-not (these four are the "illness"), non-causation, non-naturalness, non-is, non-is-not (these four are the "medicine") — and then:

> "When both medicine and illness are forgotten, no trace remains to cling to; the path of mental activity ceases, the road of language is cut off — hence 'transcending all characteristics.'"[^21]

And the turn:

> "The refined awakening, wondrous and luminous, does not have a separate substance; it is simply that, among all dharmas, when the previous false pervading attachment is abandoned, this is itself the perfectly accomplished nature of wondrous awakened luminosity."[^22]

The seeing-nature is not a separate entity discovered behind phenomena. It is what phenomena already are once false projection (*parikalpita*) is released.

#### 2.6 Jiaokuang Zhenjian's *True Pulse Commentary*: The Empty Tathāgatagarbha and Revealing Truth While Bearing Delusion

Jiaokuang provides the clearest structural map for the ten demonstrations within the sūtra's overall architecture. He distributes the Śūraṅgama's revelatory method across four levels:

| Level | Object | Method | Corresponding Passage |
|-------|--------|--------|-----------------------|
| 1. Consciousness | False mind | Complete demolition | Seven locations inquiry |
| **2. Root** | **Seeing-nature** | **Mostly revealing truth, minor demolition** | **Ten demonstrations** |
| 3. Aggregates, entries, bases, elements | Four categories | Demolishing delusion, revealing truth one by one | Integrating the four categories |
| 4. Seven elements | Earth, water, fire, wind, space, seeing, consciousness | Complete revelation of truth | Fully manifesting the seven elements |

The ten demonstrations occupy the second level — "mostly revealing truth, minor demolition" at the level of the root (*gēn* 根).[^23] "Root" here does not mean the physical sense organ but the capacity of seeing-hearing-awareness-knowing (*jiàn wén jué zhī* 見聞覺知) — a stratum deeper than consciousness yet not yet the full tathāgatagarbha.

Jiaokuang further provides a comprehensive summary of each demonstration's specific revelatory function, culminating in the tenth: "its own characteristics are also transcended, turning into the purely real without delusion."[^24]

He emphasizes, however, that what the ten demonstrations reveal remains "truth bearing delusion" — because the practitioner is still in the state of confusion, with "two kinds of delusory seeing" not yet eliminated.[^25] This is not a deficiency of the seeing-nature but a feature of the pedagogical method: the Buddha teaches within the real conditions of ordinary beings.

Within his threefold classification of tathāgatagarbha, Jiaokuang unambiguously assigns the ten demonstrations to the **empty tathāgatagarbha** (*kōng rúlái zàng* 空如來藏):

> "Among the three [kinds of] tathāgatagarbha, this properly belongs to the empty tathāgatagarbha."[^26]

He also performs an important act of terminological correction. The earlier tradition paired "eight returns to discern seeing" with "seven locations to examine the mind." Jiaokuang objects that all ten demonstrations are about the seeing-nature, not just the eighth, and renames the entire section: **"Seven Locations to Demolish the [False] Mind, Ten Demonstrations to Reveal the Seeing-Nature"**:

> "I now correct this to: seven locations demolishing mind, ten demonstrations revealing seeing. Not only do the numerical designations correspond, but the principle — delusory mind versus true seeing — is thereby clarified."[^27]

### C. Supporting Treatise and Sūtra

#### 2.7 The Four-Aspect Model in the *Cheng Weishi Lun*: The Structure of Seeing-Aspect and Self-Witnessing Aspect

The four-aspect model (*sì fēn shuō* 四分說) in Fascicle Two of the *Cheng Weishi Lun* 成唯識論 provides an analytical instrument for understanding the ten demonstrations.[^28]

The model distinguishes four aspects within any act of cognition: the object-aspect (*xiàng fēn* 相分, what appears as if perceived), the seeing-aspect (*jiàn fēn* 見分, what appears as if perceiving), the self-witnessing aspect (*zì zhèng fēn* 自證分, which witnesses the seeing-aspect's own activity), and the witnessing-the-self-witnessing aspect (*zhèng zì zhèng fēn* 證自證分, which prevents infinite regress).

The critical passage for our purposes states:

> "The seeing-aspect is sometimes classified as non-veridical cognition (*fēi liàng* 非量). Therefore the seeing-aspect cannot verify the third [self-witnessing aspect]. What witnesses one's own substance must be direct perception (*xiàn liàng* 現量)."[^29]

The seeing-aspect can err; the self-witnessing aspect necessarily does not. This structural feature parallels the Śūraṅgama's seeing-nature: it is not the seeing-aspect that may misidentify (as Ānanda's seven attempts did), but something closer to the self-witnessing aspect's non-mediated self-luminosity. The limits of this analogy will be examined in §III.7 and §V.3.

#### 2.8 The Sixth Patriarch's "How Unexpected, This Self-Nature": Chan Corroboration

Huineng 惠能, upon hearing the *Diamond Sūtra*'s phrase "generate the mind that abides nowhere" (*yīng wú suǒ zhù ér shēng qí xīn*), expressed his awakening in five exclamations:[^30]

| Sixth Patriarch's Five Phrases | Corresponding Demonstration | Shared Revelation |
|------|------|------|
| "Originally pure of itself" 本自清淨 | 6th: Non-mixture | Seeing-nature is not contaminated by objects |
| "Originally without arising or perishing" 本不生滅 | 3rd: Imperishability | Seeing-nature does not change with time |
| "Originally self-sufficient" 本自具足 | 5th: Nothing to return | Seeing-nature is not borrowed from conditions |
| "Originally without wavering" 本無動搖 | 2nd: Immovability | Seeing-nature does not follow sense objects |
| "Able to generate myriad dharmas" 能生萬法 | 8th: Neither caused nor uncaused | Tathāgatagarbha's unchanging responsiveness to conditions |

The Sixth Patriarch's language of awakening and the Buddha's pedagogical language refer to the same reality — the former compressed into five direct utterances, the latter unfolded across ten systematic demonstrations. The Śūraṅgama teaches; the Platform Sūtra corroborates.

## III. Core Argument

### 3.1 Pedagogical Architecture: Ten Angles of Approach toward the Undesignable

The ten demonstrations do not enumerate ten attributes of the seeing-nature as though it possessed a checklist of properties. They approach the same *undesignable reality* from ten different angles — each demonstration probing deeper than the last, yet none capable of capturing the seeing-nature by itself.

This is why ten demonstrations are needed rather than one. If the seeing-nature could be stated in a single proposition, the Buddha would not require two fascicles of repeated demonstration. If it could be defined by any single term — "immovable," "imperishable," "without return" — it would be captured by language, and the moment language captures it, it becomes another name-form, returning us to "taking objects for the self."

The structure is a spiral, not a parallel enumeration. The first demonstration establishes the starting point: seeing is not the eye. The second: seeing does not follow the hand's opening and closing. The third: seeing does not follow the face's aging. Each says: you think seeing is X, but it is not X. By the fourth — "when seeing sees seeing, seeing is not seeing" — even the concept of "seeing" itself is transcended. By the eighth — "transcending all characteristics, identical with all dharmas" — every framework that could be used to describe it has been dismantled.

This pedagogical design resonates deeply with the tripartite formula (*jí fēi sān duàn shì* 即非三段式) examined in S5 on the Diamond Sūtra. The Diamond Sūtra says: "What the Buddha calls prajñā-pāramitā is not prajñā-pāramitā; it is called prajñā-pāramitā" — positing, negating, re-naming. The ten demonstrations perform the expanded version of the same operation: display an aspect of the seeing-nature (posit), show that this aspect cannot exhaust it (negate), and let the reader glimpse the unnameable between positing and negating.

### 3.2 The Seeing-Nature Is Not Eye-Consciousness: Śūraṅgama Upgrade of the S3 Analysis

The first demonstration's "seeing-nature is mind, not eye" directly engages a question requiring precise differentiation: what is the relationship between the seeing-nature and the Yogācāra category of eye-consciousness (*yǎn shí* 眼識)?

In S3-P06, we analyzed the first five consciousnesses as "true-object, direct-perception, morally neutral" (*xìng jìng xiàn liàng tōng sān xìng* 性境現量通三性).[^31] Eye-consciousness takes real objects directly, without conceptual mediation, and is ethically indeterminate. Its function: light enters the eye-root, producing visual cognition.

The Śūraṅgama's first demonstration addresses something different. The Buddha's argument is: the lamp illuminates objects but does not "see"; the eye manifests colors but does not "see" — what sees is *mind*, not eye. The "mind" here is not the sixth consciousness (*mano-vijñāna*) or the seventh (*manas*), but a more fundamental capacity of "being able to see."

Where is the upgrade? In the Yogācāra framework, eye-consciousness *arises and perishes* — it depends on the eye-root for its arising; when the root is damaged, the consciousness does not arise. In the Śūraṅgama's framework, the seeing-nature *neither arises nor perishes* — even when the eye-root is damaged (as in blindness), the seeing-nature has not disappeared; it merely manifests differently (the blind person "sees" darkness; the seeing-nature is still operative, only without light as a condition).[^32]

In S3 terminology: eye-consciousness is a dharma arising from causes and conditions, belonging to the dependent nature (*paratantra-svabhāva*). The seeing-nature transcends causation — "neither cause nor condition, neither natural nor non-natural." The former falls within the epistemological categories of the theory of valid cognition (*pramāṇa-vāda*: direct perception, inference, non-veridical cognition). The latter transcends these categories altogether.

### 3.3 "Your Face Wrinkles, but the Seeing-Essence Has Never Wrinkled": The Temporal Dimension

The third demonstration, through the dialogue with King Prasenajit, addresses a fundamental question: within the impermanent flow of time, is there anything that does not change with time?

The King's own account is vivid: at twenty, already declining from ten; at thirty, declining from twenty; until "moment by moment, thought by thought, never ceasing." He describes an irreversible experiential fact — the body changes, and it does not change back.

The Buddha does not deny this fact. He asks: you know the body changes — has the one that *knows it changes* also changed? You saw the Ganges at three and you see it at sixty-two — has the *seeing* aged along with your face?

"Your face wrinkles; the seeing-essence has never wrinkled."

The power of this statement lies not in its philosophical abstraction but in its immediate verifiability: recall a visual memory from childhood. The *content* of the memory differs from what you see now — but the *capacity of seeing* that operated then and operates now, has it fundamentally altered? Zixuan glosses: "Arising and perishing merely transit within the conditioned; the unconditioned does not undergo birth and death."[^33] The point is not that the seeing-nature "resists" time, but that it was never within time's jurisdiction.

### 3.4 The Eight Returns: Elimination Carried to Its Limit

The fifth demonstration deploys the most rigorous logical structure among the ten. The Buddha's method is systematic elimination: list eight phenomenal qualities, trace each to its source — brightness comes from the sun, darkness from the moonless night, openness from doors and windows, obstruction from walls — each can be "returned" to its origin.

Then: your seeing — to what do you return it?

"Whatever can be returned is naturally not you; what cannot be returned — if not you, then who?"

The power of this elimination lies in its thoroughness: the Buddha does not tell you where the seeing-nature is. He removes everything that is not the seeing-nature, and what remains — the un-removable — is what it is. But notice: he does not say "that is the seeing-nature." He says "if not you, then who?" — pointing through negation, not defining through affirmation.

This is because the moment he says "this is the seeing-nature," you would objectify it — transform it into another "object-aspect" (*xiàng fēn*), falling back into the trap of name-form. The Śūraṅgama's pedagogical method throughout the ten demonstrations is *apophatic* (*zhē quán* 遮詮, indicating through negation) rather than *cataphatic* (*biǎo quán* 表詮, defining through affirmation).

### 3.5 "When Seeing Sees Seeing, Seeing Is Not Seeing": The Apex of the Ten Demonstrations

The fourth demonstration's enigmatic phrase is both the most difficult and most essential among the ten.

Return to the question that opened this paper: close your eyes — what do you see? Your answer — whether "black" or "nothing" — is already a name-form. You used seeing to look, then named the result. But what happens when you attempt to turn seeing upon *seeing itself*?

"When seeing sees seeing, that seeing is not seeing. Seeing still transcends seeing; seeing cannot reach it."

The moment you try to "see" the seeing-nature, what you "see" is no longer it. Seeing outruns your attempt to objectify it — because the seeing-nature is precisely what is doing the attempting. You cannot use the eye to look at the eye itself; you cannot use the fingertip to touch the fingertip itself; you cannot use the blade to cut the blade's own edge.

This is why Ānanda, after hearing this demonstration, reports that his "confusion has only deepened." He is not confused because the teaching is unclear. He is confused because he is trying to use *understanding* to grasp the seeing-nature — and understanding itself is a name-form operation. You cannot "understand" the seeing-nature; you can only *be* it.

This is the tension between knowing (*zhī* 知) and realization (*zhèng* 證) that has been tracked throughout this series: one can perfectly comprehend the logic of "seeing sees seeing, seeing is not seeing" at the textual level, yet this comprehension does not constitute realization. Comprehension is a function of the sixth consciousness — it operates in the domain of name-form. The seeing-nature does not.

### 3.6 "Transcending All Characteristics, Identical with All Dharmas": The Śūraṅgama's Version of the Three Natures

Zixuan's "medicine and illness both forgotten" interpretation (§2.5) reveals the layered structure of this eighteen-character phrase:

| Layer | Content | Function |
|-------|---------|----------|
| 1 (Illness) | Causation, naturalness, is, is-not | Four frameworks used by ordinary beings and non-Buddhist traditions |
| 2 (Medicine) | Non-causation, non-naturalness, non-is, non-is-not | Four negations countering the first layer |
| 3 (Medicine and illness forgotten) | Transcending all characteristics | Even the negations are released |
| 4 (Revelation) | Identical with all dharmas | Within all dharmas, the perfectly accomplished nature manifests |

This structure resonates with the Diamond Sūtra's tripartite formula examined in S5, but goes further. The Diamond Sūtra operates with three layers: positing, negating, re-naming. The Śūraṅgama adds a fourth — negating the negation — before arriving at "identical with all dharmas" without falling into nihilism.

Read through the Yogācāra framework of the three natures (*trisvabhāva*): "causation, naturalness, is, is-not" = *parikalpita* (falsely projecting causation or self-existence as real). "Non-causation, non-naturalness" = stripping *parikalpita*, returning to the dependent nature (*paratantra*). "Transcending all characteristics" = going beyond the name-language framework of *paratantra* itself. "Identical with all dharmas" = the perfectly accomplished nature (*pariniṣpanna*) — not somewhere else, but in the "just-as-it-is" of all dharmas.

Zixuan's concluding gloss captures the turn precisely: "The refined awakening, wondrous and luminous, does not have a separate substance; when the previous false pervading attachment is abandoned among all dharmas, this is itself the perfectly accomplished nature of wondrous awakened luminosity." One does not leave dharmas to find something else; one releases the projections upon dharmas, and the seeing-nature is already there.

### 3.7 The Four-Aspect Model as Analytical Tool: Where the Analogy Reaches Its Limit

S6-P02 employed the four-aspect model to analyze the seven locations — Ānanda's seven attempts each projected the seeing-aspect's function into the object-aspect's space. S6-P03 requires the model to perform the inverse operation: not analyzing the false mind's errors, but positioning the seeing-nature's proximity to — and divergence from — the four aspects.

The seeing-nature most closely approximates the **self-witnessing aspect** (*zì zhèng fēn* 自證分).

The reasons:

1. **Self-luminosity**: The self-witnessing aspect's core function is non-mediated self-awareness — it does not require an external object to verify its own existence. The seeing-nature likewise does not depend on what is seen: the face wrinkles, but the seeing-essence does not; brightness returns to the sun, darkness to the moonless night, but seeing has nowhere to return.

2. **Necessarily direct perception**: The *Cheng Weishi Lun* states explicitly that "what witnesses one's own substance must be direct perception." The seeing-nature as displayed in the ten demonstrations is equally direct — it is not a conclusion reached by inference but a fact available in present experience.

3. **Resistance to objectification**: The self-witnessing aspect cannot become an object for the seeing-aspect (since the seeing-aspect sometimes involves non-veridical cognition and thus cannot guarantee correct apprehension of the third aspect). This mirrors "when seeing sees seeing, seeing is not seeing" — you cannot turn the seeing-nature into an object of observation.

However, this analogy reaches its **limit** at three points:

- **The four-aspect model describes conditioned dharmas**: The four aspects analyze the operations of defiled consciousness (*yǒu lòu shí* 有漏識). The seeing-nature in the Śūraṅgama is positioned as "neither cause nor condition, neither natural nor non-natural" — outside the domain of conditioned dharmas. Applying a conditioned-dharma analytical tool to what transcends conditioned dharmas encounters a methodological ceiling.

- **The self-witnessing aspect remains at the level of consciousness**: Jiaokuang's taxonomy explicitly places the ten demonstrations at the level of "root" (*gēn*), not "consciousness" (*shí*). The self-witnessing aspect is an internal dimension of consciousness; the seeing-nature is more fundamental than consciousness.

- **The four aspects ultimately collapse into one**: The *Cheng Weishi Lun* itself notes: "Or they may be subsumed into one, since there is no difference in substance." The four-fold distinction is an analytical convenience, not an ontological partition. By the same token, reading the ten demonstrations through the four-aspect lens is an analytical convenience — not a claim about what the seeing-nature ontologically is.

⚠️ Accordingly, the four-aspect model in this paper and throughout S6 is positioned as an **analytical instrument, not an ontological description**. It helps us discern the doctrinal level at which the ten demonstrations operate. It does not claim equivalence with the seeing-nature itself.

### 3.8 "Revealing Truth While Bearing Delusion": The Methodological Significance for Practice

Jiaokuang's phrase "revealing truth while bearing delusion" is not merely a taxonomic label but a declaration of method.

The ten demonstrations are not describing a state realized after awakening. They are performed before Ānanda — a practitioner still mired in confusion. The Buddha knows Ānanda still carries "two kinds of delusory seeing," yet he does not wait until they are eliminated. He reveals truth *under the conditions of delusion*.

What does this mean for the reader? It means **you do not need to become a sage before the seeing-nature can be pointed to.** Close your eyes — what do you see? You can ask yourself this question right now, without any meditative prerequisite. You forgot that you are seeing — you can notice this right now, without having attained any fruit.

But simultaneously — and this anticipates the Middle Way corrections of §V — **noticing is not realization.** You can intellectually grasp "the face wrinkles but the seeing-essence does not wrinkle" in this moment, yet this grasp does not constitute having directly realized the seeing-nature's freedom from arising and perishing. "Bearing delusion" in "revealing truth while bearing delusion" is not optional rhetoric — it means that while you look, your delusory projections are still operative. You can see the direction, but you are still on the road.

## IV. Interdisciplinary Resonance: Husserl's Phenomenological Epoché and Residuum

⚠️ **The following comparison is a heuristic analogy, not a theoretical equivalence. The soteriological aim of Buddhist teaching and the epistemological aim of phenomenology are fundamentally different. The analogy illuminates the methodological precision of the ten demonstrations; it does not establish a reductive relationship between the two systems.**

### 4.1 Structural Similarity: Epoché and the Eight Returns

Edmund Husserl (1859–1938), the founder of twentieth-century phenomenology, proposed the method of the "phenomenological epoché": the systematic bracketing of all presuppositions regarding the existence of the external world — suspending judgment not about whether the table exists, but about the *mode* of its existence, in order to attend to how it appears within consciousness.[^34]

What remains after the epoché, Husserl terms "pure consciousness" (*reines Bewußtsein*) or the "transcendental residuum" — when every existential presupposition about external objects is bracketed, consciousness itself persists.

This parallels the eight returns with striking precision: brightness returns to the sun, darkness to the moonless night, openness to doors and windows — eight phenomenal qualities, each traced to its external source and "bracketed." What the Buddha seeks to reveal is what remains after all eight are returned — the un-returnable seeing.

### 4.2 Fundamental Divergence: Intentionality

But the similarity ends here.

Husserl's "pure consciousness" retains the structure of **intentionality** (*Intentionalität*) — consciousness is always "consciousness *of* something." Even after the epoché brackets the existence of external objects, consciousness still points toward some object (now understood as a phenomenon, not an external reality). Intentionality, for Husserl, is irremovable.[^35]

The Śūraṅgama's seeing-nature **transcends intentionality.** "When seeing sees seeing, seeing is not seeing" — when the seeing-nature no longer points toward *any* object (not even "seeing" as an object), it is still there. It is not "seeing *of* something"; it is simply seeing. Husserl cannot reach this step — within his system, consciousness without an object is inconceivable. Within the Buddha's teaching, the seeing-nature is precisely what remains self-luminous after all objects — including the object "seeing" — are removed.

### 4.3 The Irreducible Boundary

⚠️ Therefore:

- One cannot say "the eight returns are the Buddhist version of the phenomenological epoché" — the purposes differ (the epoché establishes rigorous epistemological foundations; the eight returns reveal the seeing-nature to guide toward liberation).
- One cannot say "the seeing-nature is the transcendental ego" — the transcendental ego remains within the subject-object structure; the seeing-nature transcends it.
- One cannot use the phenomenological framework to "explain" the ten demonstrations — what the ten demonstrations point toward lies beyond what phenomenology can reach.

The value of this comparison is methodological: it helps readers familiar with Western philosophy appreciate the logical rigor of the eight returns — the Buddha is not making a mystical proclamation; he is performing a strict eliminative argument. But the result of the argument transcends what argumentative reason itself can access.

## V. Middle Way Corrections

### ❌ 5.1 The Seeing-Nature Is Not a Soul or Self-Entity

The ten demonstrations' portrayal of something "immovable, imperishable, unchanging" is most commonly misread as an eternal soul-substance (*ātman*). If the seeing-nature never changes and is not subject to birth and death — is this not precisely what the Indian non-Buddhist traditions call the "divine self" (*shén wǒ* 神我)?

It is not, for two reasons.

First, **the seeing-nature is defined as "neither cause-and-condition nor natural"** — it is not produced by causes (or it would perish), but it is also not a self-existent independent entity (or it would manifest only one unchanging appearance and could not "see" the infinitely varied phenomena). The *ātman* is precisely the presupposition of a "naturally self-existent" substance. The Śūraṅgama explicitly negates this.

Second, **Jiaokuang classifies the ten demonstrations as "empty tathāgatagarbha"** — emphasis on "empty." The empty tathāgatagarbha is not "a thing called tathāgatagarbha that exists"; it is the suchness (*tathatā*) that appears when all false characteristics are removed. You cannot say "the seeing-nature exists" (that objectifies it), nor can you say "the seeing-nature does not exist" (that contradicts everything the ten demonstrations have shown).

### ❌ 5.2 Knowing the Ten Demonstrations Does Not Equal Realizing the Seeing-Nature

Ānanda wept after hearing the ten demonstrations. He was the Buddha's attendant, foremost in learning, possessed of extraordinary memory — he understood every word of the ten demonstrations. Yet the Buddha addressed him directly:

> "Though you have powerful memory, you merely benefit your extensive learning. Regarding the subtle, intimate observation of śamatha, your mind is still not clear."[^36]

This is the knowing-versus-realization distinction emphasized throughout S6. The reader who has now finished §II, who understands the logic of "the face wrinkles, the seeing-essence does not," and who finds it "reasonable" — this is *knowing*. But has the reader, the next time they look in a mirror and notice a wrinkle, truly perceived that the *seeing of the wrinkle* does not arise or perish along with the wrinkle? That would be the beginning of *realization*.

This paper can provide the path of knowing. The path of realization lies in one's own practice — the Śūraṅgama is the practitioner's diagnostic manual, not a substitute for practice.

### ⚠️ 5.3 The Instrumental Status of the Four-Aspect Model (Continued from P02 §V.3)

Continuing the correction established in P02: the four-aspect model is an internal structural analysis of consciousness within Yogācāra, and its role in S6 is that of an **analytical instrument**, not an ontological description of the seeing-nature.

Specifically:

- Saying "the seeing-nature approximates the self-witnessing aspect" leverages the four-aspect model's precision to position the doctrinal level of the ten demonstrations — it does not assert that "the seeing-nature *is* the self-witnessing aspect."
- The Śūraṅgama Sūtra does not employ four-aspect terminology. The Buddha never mentions "self-witnessing aspect." Reading the ten demonstrations through the four-aspect lens is a later interpretive instrument, not the sūtra's own language.
- Jiaokuang's taxonomy explicitly places the ten demonstrations at the "root" level — deeper than consciousness. The four-aspect model analyzes the interior of consciousness; applying it to what lies deeper than consciousness necessarily encounters a limit.

### ❌ 5.4 "Transcending All Characteristics" Is Not Nihilism

"Transcending all characteristics" (*lí yī qiè xiàng* 離一切相) risks being read as "nothing at all" — once every conceptual framework has been dismantled, is there not simply a void?

No. Zixuan is explicit: "The refined awakening, wondrous and luminous, does not have a separate substance; when the previous false pervading attachment is abandoned among all dharmas, this is itself the perfectly accomplished nature." To transcend all characteristics is not to arrive at blankness but at "identical with all dharmas." The perfectly accomplished nature (*pariniṣpanna*) is not outside phenomena — it is phenomena as they are once false projection is released.

An imperfect but perhaps useful analogy: you view the world through tinted glasses, and everything appears that color. "Transcending all characteristics" means removing the glasses. What you see afterward is not "nothing" — it is the world as it has always been. The world has not changed; your filter has simply been removed.

## VI. Conclusion

### Essential Recapitulation

The ten demonstrations of seeing constitute the Śūraṅgama Sūtra's pivotal transition from demolishing the false to revealing the true. From ten angles — immovability, imperishability, non-loss, nothing to return, non-mixture, non-obstruction, beyond size, beyond is-and-is-not, neither caused nor uncaused — the Buddha demonstrates the same fact repeatedly: the seeing-nature has always been functioning, but you fabricate names and forms so quickly that you leap past the seeing itself. You close your eyes and see "black" — but you forgot that you are seeing.

Jiaokuang classifies the ten demonstrations as "empty tathāgatagarbha," methodologically characterized as "revealing truth while bearing delusion" — not exhibiting true suchness in a purified saintly state, but pointing to truth under the ordinary conditions of beings who still carry delusion. This means the teaching is effective for every reader — you do not need to attain realization first in order to begin observing.

### Guidance for Practice

What, then, can you do?

The next time you close your eyes, do not rush to name your experience. Do not leap to "black." Do not leap to "nothing." In the instant before the name arises — notice whether **the one that is seeing** disappeared when your eyes closed.

It did not disappear. It has never disappeared. You simply forgot.

This is not awakening. It is not even a meditative state. It is merely a direction — the direction of "revealing truth while bearing delusion." You carry all your delusions, afflictions, and distractions, and you can still notice, in this very moment, the direction the seeing-nature points toward. Then you will forget, pulled away by the next name-form. That is fine. When you forget, come back.

The ten demonstrations do not instruct you to "grasp" the seeing-nature. The seeing-nature cannot be grasped — "when seeing sees seeing, seeing is not seeing." The ten demonstrations instruct you to notice: **you already are the seeing-nature; you have only been using it to look at other things, forgetting to notice the looking itself.** And even "noticing the looking itself" is a trap — because "itself" implies an object. So: not to look *at* the seeing-nature, but **to know, in the act of looking, that you are looking.**

### Open Question

The seeing-nature revealed by the ten demonstrations, in Jiaokuang's taxonomy, still resides at the level of the "root" — it is the fundamental capacity of seeing-hearing-awareness-knowing, but not yet the full tathāgatagarbha. Jiaokuang notes: "Hereafter, this is renamed 'tathāgatagarbha-nature,' and the partial name 'seeing-nature' is no longer used."[^37]

Then what more is needed to move from "seeing-nature" to "tathāgatagarbha"?

The next paper (S6-P04) enters the Śūraṅgama's four categories and seven elements — the Buddha no longer demonstrates only the unchanging nature of *seeing*, but integrates the five aggregates, twelve sense-bases, eighteen elements, and seven great elements entirely into "tathāgatagarbha, eternally abiding, wondrous suchness." From the single point of seeing, to the totality of all dharmas.

Close your eyes now.

What do you see?

If your answer has changed from before you read this paper — the paper has served its purpose.
