## Abstract

The Yogācāra tradition categorizes all mental activity into eight consciousnesses (*vijñāna*), yet these are not eight independent "soul fragments" but an organic totality constituted through the Three Transformations (*tri-pariṇāma*): the maturation transformation (ālaya-vijñāna, the eighth consciousness), the deliberative transformation (manas, the seventh consciousness), and the object-discerning transformation (the first six consciousnesses). Drawing on the core framework of the *Cheng Weishi Lun* (Vijñaptimātratāsiddhi) and the distilled formulas of the *Bashi Guiju Song* (Verses on the Patterns and Features of the Eight Consciousnesses), this paper presents a panoramic survey of the eight consciousnesses — their respective natures, functional distinctions, and internal interconnections. The Four Aspects theory (*catuḥ-prativibhāga*: the image aspect, the perceiving aspect, the self-witnessing aspect, and the witnessing-of-self-witnessing aspect) is introduced as the micro-structure operating within each consciousness. The *Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra*'s "ocean of store-consciousness" metaphor and Yongming Yanshou's vision in the *Zongjing Lu* — "consciousness and wisdom self-differentiate; the substance does not turn, only the name turns" — frame the transformative horizon. Cross-disciplinary dialogue engages the sensory cortex–association cortex architecture in modern cognitive neuroscience as a heuristic analogy. Middle Way corrections guard against two persistent dangers: reifying the eight consciousnesses into independent entities, and the fallacy of instant awakening through intellectual understanding alone.

本文以《成唯識論》三能變架構為核心，配合《八識規矩頌》四組體性頌，全景式呈現八識的體性、功能差異與內在關聯。引入四分說作為每一識的微觀結構，並以楞伽經「藏識海」比喻與宗鏡錄「識智自分」的圓融視角展示轉化可能。中道校正警示「八識實體化」與「轉識成智速成論」兩大陷阱。

## I. The Problem: Eight Consciousnesses Are Not Eight Rooms

S3-P01 laid out the master map of the Hundred Dharmas and established "all dharmas are without self" as the Yogācāra methodology. In the five-category ordering of the Hundred Dharmas, mind dharmas (*citta*) occupy the first position — because all conditioned phenomena are either activities of consciousness or projections by it. The *Treatise on the Gate of the Hundred Dharmas* opens with: "The first category, mind dharmas, consists in brief of eight."[^1] All mental activity is thereby reduced to eight consciousnesses.

But "eight consciousnesses" is easily misunderstood as eight parallel, independent "rooms of awareness" — as if each consciousness operates on its own, occasionally passing information to the others. This understanding is fundamentally wrong. The relationship among the eight consciousnesses is not parallel juxtaposition but layered dependence and dynamic transformation — an organic totality. The *Cheng Weishi Lun* describes this totality with a precise framework: the Three Transformations (*tri-pariṇāma*).

The Three Transformations are not three separate things but three functional modes of transformation within a single consciousness-body. The first transformation is the maturation transformation (the eighth consciousness), which serves as the foundational support of the entire system. The second is the deliberative transformation (the seventh consciousness), which continuously grasps the perceiving aspect of the eighth consciousness as "self." The third is the object-discerning transformation (the first six consciousnesses), which, on the basis of the first two transformations, engages in the discrimination and cognition of objects.

This paper's task is to draw the panoramic map of the eight consciousnesses — not to delve into any single consciousness's internal mechanisms (that is the task of P03–P06), but to let the reader see the entire system's structure, each consciousness's functional position, and the trajectory of transformation from consciousness to wisdom.

## II. Scriptural and Treatise Foundations

The eight-consciousness theory reaches its most precise systematization in the *Cheng Weishi Lun*, but the distinction among "mind" (citta), "thought" (manas), and "consciousness" (vijñāna), and the relationship between the six consciousnesses and deeper levels of mind, were already clearly taught in early Buddhist and Mahāyāna scriptures. This section first establishes sūtra authority (Part A), then unfolds the systematic doctrinal analysis through the treatise tradition (Part B).

### Part A: Sūtra Foundations

#### 2.1 The Āgamas — The Original Teaching of the Six Consciousness-Bodies

The eight-consciousness system was not constructed from nothing. The Āgamas' six-consciousness model — eye-consciousness through mind-consciousness — is Yogācāra's starting point. The *Saṃyuktāgama*'s "Sūtra on the Six Sixes" organizes all cognitive activity under twelve bases and six consciousness-bodies: "What are the six internal bases? The eye-base, ear-base, nose-base, tongue-base, body-base, and mind-base"; "Conditioned by eye and form, eye-consciousness arises; the meeting of the three is contact."[^41]

The six-consciousness model precisely describes the basic structure of sensory cognition, but encounters difficulties in explaining certain phenomena: when a practitioner enters the attainment of cessation and all six consciousnesses cease, how does the sentient being differ from a corpse? How do karmic seeds maintain their continuity during the gap between conscious moments? Why do beings persistently grasp at self, even when the sixth consciousness's self-view has been temporarily suppressed? These difficulties provided the doctrinal impetus for Yogācāra's establishment of the seventh and eighth consciousnesses. The Āgamas' six-consciousness model was not rejected by Yogācāra but expanded: the six-consciousness observations are entirely valid; Yogācāra simply asks what lies behind them.[^42]

#### 2.2 The Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra — From Six to Eight: The Canonical Transition

The *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra*'s chapter on "The Characteristics of Mind, Thought, and Consciousness" is the Buddha's own canonical transition from six consciousnesses to eight. The sūtra establishes the eighth consciousness under three names — ādāna-vijñāna, ālaya-vijñāna, and citta — and implies the seventh through the special status of "thought" (manas):

> "The ādāna-vijñāna serves as the basis and foundation, and thereby the six consciousness-bodies turn."[^43]

The "six consciousness-bodies" turn dependent on the "ādāna-vijñāna" — this architecture already exceeds the Āgama six-consciousness model, explicitly establishing a deeper "basis" beneath the six. The sūtra further illustrates this consciousness's perpetual continuity through the "torrent" verse: "The ādāna-vijñāna is extremely deep and subtle; all seeds flow like a torrent. I do not teach this to the ordinary and foolish, lest they discriminate and grasp it as self."[^44] The Buddha's silence about this consciousness during the Āgama period was precisely because of "lest they grasp it as self" — and this psychological mechanism of "grasping as self" would later be precisely identified by Yogācāra as the function of manas.

The *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra* provides canonical authority for the eight-consciousness panorama by establishing, through the Buddha's own teaching, the foundational three-tier architecture of citta—manas—vijñāna — the critical transitional text from six to eight.

#### 2.3 The Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra — The Ocean of Store-Consciousness and Five Dharmas, Three Natures, Eight Consciousnesses, Two No-Selfs

The *Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra* provides the most classic image of the eight consciousnesses as an organic totality through its ocean-and-wave metaphor:

> "The ocean of store-consciousness abides constantly; stirred by the wind of object-realms, various consciousness-waves surge forth and arise."[^45]

The ocean (store-consciousness) is the foundational support; the wind (object-realms) is the triggering condition; the waves (seven active consciousnesses) are the specific mental activities. Ocean does not separate from waves, waves do not separate from ocean — the eight consciousnesses are not eight separate things but different facets of a single mind-ocean. The verses continue: "The seven consciousnesses beginning with manas — know that they are likewise: like ocean and waves together, mind arises in union."[^46] And the final distillation: "The eight consciousnesses have no distinct characteristics; there is no characteristic-bearer and no characteristic-borne."[^47]

The *Laṅkāvatāra* further subsumes all Yogācāra doctrine under a single formula — "five dharmas, three self-natures, eight consciousnesses, two no-selfs":

> "The five dharmas, self-natures, consciousnesses, and two no-selfs are distinguished as follows: name, appearance, conceptual projection, correct wisdom, and suchness... The three self-natures and eight consciousnesses, together with the two kinds of no-self, are all subsumed within the five dharmas."[^48]

This passage explicitly places the eight consciousnesses within the larger framework of the five dharmas — name and appearance belong to the imagined nature (parikalpita); conditioned arising belongs to the dependent nature (paratantra); correct wisdom and suchness belong to the consummated nature (pariniṣpanna). The eight consciousnesses are dependent-nature functional presentations, not imagined-nature entities — providing canonical support for the Middle Way correction against "reifying the eight consciousnesses."

#### 2.4 The Avataṃsaka Sūtra — "All Three Realms Are Mind Alone" and "Mind Like a Painter"

The *Avataṃsaka Sūtra*, from the height of definitive Mahāyāna teaching, provides the most panoramic canonical declaration for "all is consciousness-only." The Ten Grounds chapter, sixth ground (Direct Presence):

> "All that exists in the three realms is mind alone. The Tathāgata analyzes and teaches the twelve links of dependent origination — all are established dependent on this one mind."[^49]

"All three realms are mind alone" — in Yogācāra exegesis, this "mind" is interpreted as the comprehensive function of the ālaya-vijñāna. The twelve links (dependent origination) are all established dependent on one mind — providing the most fundamental canonical support for the Three Transformations framework: the arising, continuation, and cessation of all conditioned phenomena pivot on consciousness.

The Bodhisattva Juelin's verse in the Night of Yāma Palace chapter further unfolds this through the "mind like a painter" simile:

> "Mind is like a painter, painting all kinds of five aggregates; in all worlds, there is nothing it does not create. As is the mind, so is the Buddha; as is the Buddha, so are sentient beings. Mind, Buddha, and sentient beings — these three are without difference."[^50]

> "If one wishes to understand all Buddhas of the three times, one should contemplate the nature of the dharma-realm: all is created by mind alone."[^51]

"All is created by mind alone" — this verse is the canonical echo of the *Cheng Weishi Lun*'s "therefore, all is consciousness-only." Mind like a painter, painting the five-aggregate world — the Three Transformations are precisely the mechanism of this "painting": the eighth consciousness holds seeds (canvas and pigments), the seventh grasps self (the painter's bias), the first six discern objects (the actual brushstrokes).

#### 2.5 The Śūraṅgama Sūtra — Seven Inquiries into the Location of Mind

The *Śūraṅgama Sūtra*, through the Buddha's seven exchanges with Ānanda, fundamentally interrogates "where is the mind?" — an interrogation that serves as the ideal entry-point for the eight-consciousness panorama.

Ānanda successively proposes that the mind is inside the body, outside the body, hidden within the sense-faculties, that closed-eyed darkness counts as seeing the inside, that mind is wherever it encounters [objects], that mind is between [inside and outside], and that total non-attachment constitutes mind — all seven proposals are refuted: "There is no such place."[^52]

After the seven inquiries, the Buddha reveals:

> "Stop! Ānanda! This is not your mind!... This is the illusory image of the preceding sense-dust, confusing your true nature! From beginningless time until this life, you have mistaken the thief for your son, lost your original constancy, and therefore undergo the cycle of birth and death."[^53]

"Mistaking the thief for your son" — taking the illusory discrimination of sense-dust for one's true "mind" is the most fundamental error of ordinary beings. The significance of the seven inquiries: they directly dismantle the illusion that "the eight consciousnesses are eight spatially located entities" — the mind is neither inside the body nor outside it, not in any "place." This provides the Buddha's own canonical argument for this paper's opening thesis: "eight consciousnesses are not eight rooms."[^54]

### Part B: Treatise Foundations

#### 2.6 The Summary Verse of the Three Transformations

After completing the individual analysis of the Three Transformations, the *Cheng Weishi Lun* in fascicle 7 offers a summary verse:

> "These consciousnesses transform; what discriminates and what is discriminated — by this, those [self and dharmas] are all non-existent. Therefore, all is consciousness-only."[^2]

The treatise immediately explains: "These consciousnesses" refers to the three transforming consciousnesses and their associated mental factors, all of which can manifest apparent perceiving and image aspects. The manifested perceiving aspect is called "discrimination" because it apprehends images; the manifested image aspect is called "what is discriminated" because it is apprehended by the perceiving aspect. By this correct reasoning, any real self or real dharmas apart from what consciousness transforms are definitively non-existent.[^3]

This passage reveals the core import of the Three Transformations: all mental activity is the self-transformation of the consciousness-body, projecting the perceiving and image aspects. What we take to be "self" and "dharmas" are merely the products of these transformations; apart from consciousness's transformations, no independent entities can be found. The *Avataṃsaka Sūtra*'s "all is created by mind alone" is here precision-rendered by the *Cheng Weishi Lun* as "therefore, all is consciousness-only" — the mechanism of "mind painting the world" is precisely the Three Transformations.[^55]

#### 2.7 The First Transformation — Maturation: Ālaya-vijñāna

The *Cheng Weishi Lun*, fascicle 2, defines the threefold meaning of the first transformation:

> "The first transforming consciousness, named ālaya in both Mahāyāna and Hīnayāna teachings, possesses the meanings of actively storing, being stored in, and being grasped as [self]."[^4]

These three meanings correspond to three aspects: as causal aspect, it is the "holder of all seeds"; as resultant aspect, it is "maturation" (*vipāka*); as intrinsic aspect, it is "ālaya."[^5] The *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra*'s "the ādāna-vijñāna is extremely deep and subtle; all seeds flow like a torrent" is the canonical proclamation of the first transformation's "all-seeds" meaning.[^56]

The *Verses* summarize the eighth consciousness's nature:

> "Its nature is solely unobscured [non-defiled neutral]; its associated mental factors are only the five universals."[^6]

The commentary explains: this consciousness is exclusively of unobscured-indeterminate nature — because it does not accompany afflictions, because it is impartially without resistance, and because it qualifies as the receptor of perfuming.[^7]

#### 2.8 The Second Transformation — Deliberation: Manas

The *Cheng Weishi Lun*, fascicle 4, introduces manas:

> "Next, the second transformation: this consciousness is named manas. It turns dependent on that [the eighth] and takes that as its object. Deliberation is both its nature and its activity. Four afflictions constantly accompany it: self-delusion, self-view, self-conceit, and self-love."[^8]

"Deliberation as both nature and activity" reveals a double function.[^9] Kuiji further clarifies: "Deliberation is called 'manas'; among the consciousnesses, manas excels — it surpasses the others in constant and continuous deliberation."[^40]

The fourfold distinction of "constant" and "deliberating":

> "Constant but not deliberating: the eighth consciousness. Deliberating but not constant: the sixth consciousness. Both constant and deliberating: the seventh consciousness. Neither constant nor deliberating: the first five consciousnesses."[^10]

In everyday language: the first five are like windows; the sixth is like a commentator who takes breaks; the seventh is like a security guard who never rests; the eighth is like a warehouse that never closes.

Dharmapāla's couplet: "The five and the eighth neither grasp dharma-self nor person-self; the sixth and seventh are perfectly matched in both."[^11]

#### 2.9 The Third Transformation — Object-Discernment: The First Six

The *Cheng Weishi Lun*, fascicle 5, on the three natures of the six active consciousnesses:

> "Under which natures are these six active consciousnesses subsumed? Under wholesome, unwholesome, and neutral natures."[^12]

The three natures can operate simultaneously.[^13] The *Verses* contrast the first five and the sixth: the first five cognize "real objects only, direct perception, pervading the three natures"[^14]; the sixth pervades "three natures, three modes of knowing, three object-types."[^15] The sixth consciousness can engage with all fifty-one mental factors.[^16]

#### 2.10 From Six to Eight: Why the Seventh and Eighth Are Necessary

The *Cheng Weishi Lun*, fascicle 4, presents the core argument: in cessation-attainment, all six consciousnesses have ceased, yet "lifespan does not cease, bodily warmth does not depart." Without a subtle consciousness, the practitioner "would be like tile or gravel."[^17][^18]

This argument demonstrates why ālaya-vijñāna is indispensable: it maintains life-continuity when all coarse mental activity has ceased. The Āgamas' six-consciousness model already observed that the six consciousnesses are intermittent; Yogācāra's contribution is asking what sustains life during these interruptions — the answer is the eighth consciousness.[^57] The seventh manas explains why beings persistently cling to self through a mechanism independent of the sixth consciousness's intermittent operations.

#### 2.11 The Four Aspects — The Micro-Structure Within Each Consciousness

Each consciousness possesses an internal differentiated structure. The *Cheng Weishi Lun*, fascicle 1: "Transformation means the consciousness-body turns to manifest apparent two aspects."[^19] Fascicle 2 unfolds this into four aspects: image, perceiving, self-witnessing, and witnessing-of-self-witnessing.[^20][^21]

The structural relations: the first two are external, the latter two internal. The third and fourth mutually verify each other, forming a self-enclosed loop without infinite regress.[^22] The four can be condensed to three, two, or one — "since their substance is not distinct."[^23]

#### 2.12 Consciousness and Wisdom Self-Differentiate — The Zongjing Lu's Integrative View

Chan Master Yongming Yanshou, in fascicle 88 of the *Zongjing Lu* (Records of the Source Mirror), describes the transformation from consciousness to wisdom with characteristic directness:

> "When one is deluded: the eighth consciousness takes on the title of 'grasping and storing,' the seventh receives the name 'defiled,' the sixth engages in 'universal construction,' and the five transform into 'roots and dusts.' When one understands: the ālaya becomes the substance of the Great Mirror, holding the gate of merits; manas becomes the source of Equality, unifying self and other; the sixth gives rise to the wonder of Observation, turning the wheel of true Dharma; the five perform the function of Accomplishment, manifesting responsive traces."[^27]

The conclusion is pivotal:

> 一心匪動，識智自分，不轉其體，但轉其名。
> "The one mind has never moved. Consciousness and wisdom self-differentiate. It is not the substance that turns — only the name turns."[^28]

"The one mind has never moved" — the fundamental mind-substance has never shifted. "Consciousness and wisdom self-differentiate" — the distinction arises naturally; nothing is destroyed, nothing added. "Not the substance that turns, only the name" — in delusion we call them eight consciousnesses; in awakening we call them four wisdoms. This is Chan's most direct statement on the transformation of consciousness into wisdom.

## III. Core Analysis

### 3.1 The Organic Hierarchical Structure of the Three Transformations

The Three Transformations are not three parallel systems but a hierarchy from deep to shallow, from foundational to surface:

**Layer one (First Transformation · Maturation):** Ālaya-vijñāna is the "operating system" of the entire structure. It makes no active judgments or discriminations; it faithfully receives perfuming, stores seeds, and manifests the body-faculties and material environment. Unobscured and morally neutral, it associates only with the five universals. Its "unobscured" nature is precisely what qualifies it as the receptor of perfuming — were it obscured by defilement, it could not impartially receive the perfuming of both wholesome and unwholesome seeds.

**Layer two (Second Transformation · Deliberation):** Manas turns dependent on the eighth consciousness, grasping its perceiving aspect as "self." The four afflictions (self-delusion, self-view, self-conceit, self-love) accompany it at all times — this is the root of the deep-level self-grasping that has persisted since beginningless time. The "obscured-indeterminate" nature of manas means that although it is defiled (obscured), it does not directly create wholesome or unwholesome karma (indeterminate). Its function is to continuously overlay a "this is mine" filter onto the entire cognitive system.

**Layer three (Third Transformation · Object-Discernment):** The first six consciousnesses, on the basis of the first two layers, engage in discriminating external objects (the five sense-consciousnesses) and dharma-objects (the sixth consciousness). This is the most obvious level of mental activity in our daily experience — seeing, hearing, thinking, judging. But these seemingly "first-hand" experiences have already passed through the eighth consciousness's seed-manifestation and the seventh consciousness's self-grasping filter.

A concrete illustration: you walk into a coffee shop. You smell the aroma of coffee (the nose-consciousness discerns the scent-object). You see the price board (the eye-consciousness discerns the form-object). The sixth consciousness immediately activates — "This place is more expensive than yesterday's" (inferential discrimination), "Should I order an Americano or a latte?" (deliberative discrimination). But throughout the entire process, the seventh manas is operating behind the scenes — "I want coffee," "I want the best deal," "I don't want to wait in line too long" — every judgment orbits around a center that is never questioned: "I." And the eighth consciousness? At a deeper level still, it silently stores this coffee experience as new seeds while providing the old seeds that allow you to identify the coffee aroma in the first place — the reason you find coffee "fragrant" rather than "foul" is because ālaya-vijñāna already holds the relevant seeds.

### 3.2 The Four Aspects as the Universal Micro-Structure of Consciousness

The Four Aspects theory does not apply only to one particular consciousness; it is the internal structure shared by all eight. Any act of consciousness includes:

— The image aspect: the object presented by consciousness (the cognized facet)
— The perceiving aspect: the cognitive activity itself (the cognizing facet)
— The self-witnessing aspect: awareness of one's own cognitive activity (knowing that one is cognizing)
— The witnessing-of-self-witnessing: confirmation of that self-awareness (verifying that the knowing is genuine)

The critical insight of the *Cheng Weishi Lun* is: "Neither identical nor separate — thus the principle of consciousness-only is established."[^29] The four aspects are neither completely the same nor completely different, and it is precisely this "neither identical nor separate" structure that avoids the problem of infinite regress. The third aspect (self-witnessing) and fourth aspect (witnessing-of-self-witnessing) mutually serve as each other's measure and fruit, forming a self-enclosed cognitive loop that does not require a fifth, sixth, or further aspects ad infinitum.

### 3.3 The Nature Verses — Genetic Maps of the Four Groups

The first verse (the "nature verse") of each group in the *Verses on the Eight Consciousnesses* can be read as that group's "genetic map," precisely marking its parameters across object-type, mode of knowing, moral nature, and associated mental factors:

| Parameter | First Five | Sixth | Seventh | Eighth |
|-----------|-----------|-------|---------|--------|
| **Object-type** | Real objects only | All three types | Hybrid objects | Real objects |
| **Mode of knowing** | Direct perception only | All three modes | Non-valid cognition | Direct perception |
| **Moral nature** | All three | All three | Obscured-indeterminate | Unobscured-indeterminate |
| **Mental factors** | 34 | 51 (all) | 18 | 5 (universals only) |
| **Character** | Direct, passive, root-dependent | Widest range, active | Constant deliberative self-grasping | Constant but non-deliberating, unobscured |

This table reveals the functional gradient across the eight consciousnesses: from the most minimal eighth consciousness (only five universals) to the most comprehensive sixth (all fifty-one mental factors), functional richness increases layer by layer. But richer functionality does not mean more fundamental — on the contrary, it is the most minimal eighth consciousness that serves as the foundational support of the entire system.

### 3.4 Nine Conditions for Consciousness to Arise

The eight consciousnesses do not operate unconditionally; each requires specific conditions (*pratyaya*) for its arising. The *Verses* summarize:

> 眼識九緣生，耳識唯從八，鼻舌身三七，後三五三四。
> "Eye-consciousness arises from nine conditions; ear-consciousness from only eight. Nose, tongue, and body — these three from seven. The latter three: five, three, and four."[^30]

The nine conditions are: space, illumination, faculty (sense-organ), object, attention (*manaskāra*), discriminative support (sixth consciousness), defiled-or-pure support (seventh consciousness), fundamental support (eighth consciousness), and seed.[^31] Eye-consciousness requires all nine (needing spatial distance and light). Ear-consciousness requires eight, omitting illumination (sound does not depend on light). Nose, tongue, and body require seven, omitting both space and illumination (they apprehend objects through direct contact, requiring neither distance nor light).

Critically: all five sense-consciousnesses require the "discriminative support" (sixth consciousness), "defiled-or-pure support" (seventh), and "fundamental support" (eighth) as conditions for arising. The *Cheng Weishi Lun* states explicitly: "The simultaneous supports of the five consciousnesses are definitely four: the five physical faculties, and the sixth, seventh, and eighth consciousnesses — lacking any one, they will not operate."[^32] This doctrinally confirms the hierarchical dependence structure of the Three Transformations: the operation of the first five consciousnesses necessarily depends on the simultaneous presence of the sixth, seventh, and eighth.

### 3.5 Transformation of Consciousness into Wisdom — The Four Wisdoms

The *Cheng Weishi Lun*, fascicle 10, defines the four wisdoms and their correspondences:

The Great Mirror Wisdom — transformed from the eighth consciousness: "Free from all discrimination... like a great round mirror reflecting all color-images."[^33]

The Equality Wisdom — transformed from the seventh consciousness: "Observing all dharmas, self and other beings as entirely equal; great compassion and the rest constantly accompanying it."[^34]

The Profound Observation Wisdom — transformed from the sixth consciousness: "Skillfully observing the specific and general characteristics of all dharmas, turning without obstruction."[^35]

The Accomplishment Wisdom — transformed from the first five consciousnesses: "Universally throughout the ten directions, manifesting various transformed three karmas, accomplishing what should be done by the power of original vows."[^36]

The treatise concludes: "Although wisdom is not consciousness, it turns dependent on consciousness; because consciousness is the principal, we say 'turning consciousness obtains [wisdom].' Moreover, in the defiled stage, wisdom is weak and consciousness is strong; in the undefiled stage, wisdom is strong and consciousness is weak. To encourage sentient beings to rely on wisdom and relinquish consciousness, we speak of 'turning the eight consciousnesses to obtain these four wisdoms.'"[^37]

This passage reveals a point of paramount importance: wisdom and consciousness are not two different things but different presentations of the same mind-body under defiled and undefiled conditions. In the defiled stage, consciousness dominates; in the undefiled stage, wisdom dominates. What turns is not the substance but the balance of forces. This precisely echoes the *Zongjing Lu*'s judgment: "Not the substance that turns, only the name."

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Dialogue

### 4.1 A Heuristic Analogy from Cognitive Neuroscience

⚠️ Heuristic analogy: The following comparison is offered for illumination only and does not constitute theoretical equivalence. The Yogācāra eight-consciousness model is a mind-analysis framework grounded in contemplative insight, while neuroscience describes the material structure of the brain — these belong to fundamentally different epistemological categories.

Modern cognitive neuroscience divides sensory processing into two major tiers: primary sensory cortex and higher-order association cortex. The primary sensory cortices (visual cortex, auditory cortex, somatosensory cortex, etc.) process direct sensory input. Their characteristics include modality-specificity (each cortex handles only one sense modality), rapid response, and absence of semantic judgment. The higher-order association cortices (prefrontal cortex, temporoparietal junction, etc.) perform cross-modal integration, semantic evaluation, planning, and reasoning.

This bears a structural resemblance to the Yogācāra division between the first five consciousnesses and the sixth: the five sense-consciousnesses each depend on a specific physical faculty to cognize a specific object-domain, operate only in direct perception, and do not engage in deliberative discrimination. The sixth consciousness pervades all three object-types and all three modes of knowing, capable of three levels of cognitive activity — intrinsic discrimination, recollective discrimination, and deliberative discrimination. Both frameworks observe that a functional distinction exists between "direct sensing" and "conceptual processing."

⚠️ However, the analogy stops here. The seventh manas (the deep-level, constantly operating self-grasping) and the eighth ālaya (the foundational support for all seeds) have no direct counterparts in neuroscience. What manas describes is not the function of a particular brain region but a "self-centering bias" structure pervading all cognitive activity. The seed-storing function described by ālaya is not equivalent to the brain's memory systems — seeds encompass the dimension of karmic causation, which lies entirely beyond neuroscience's descriptive reach.

### 4.2 The Hierarchical Structure Analogy from Systems Theory

⚠️ Heuristic analogy: The hierarchical structure of the Three Transformations resonates with the concept of "emergent hierarchy" in modern systems theory. In complex systems, the base layer provides foundational constraints, the middle layer provides regulatory mechanisms, and the upper layer executes specific functions — and the behavior of the upper layer cannot be fully reduced to the properties of the base layer.

The Three Transformations display the same structure: the eighth consciousness provides the foundational support (the basis of seeds, body-faculties, and material environment); the seventh provides the regulatory mechanism (the self-grasping filter continuously influences all cognition); and the first six execute the specific functions of discernment. Crucially, the activities of the first six consciousnesses cannot be reduced to the seed-mechanisms of the eighth — the seventh's self-grasping intervenes in between, and each consciousness possesses its own four-aspect structure.

But the boundary must again be marked: systems theory describes the organizational principles of material systems; Yogācāra describes the structure of consciousness. The concept of "hierarchy" in these two frameworks is ontologically entirely different.

## V. Middle Way Corrections

### 5.1 ❌ Reifying the Eight Consciousnesses: They Are Not Eight Independent "Mini-Souls"

The most common misunderstanding is imagining the eight consciousnesses as eight independently existing entities — as if eight different subjects of awareness inhabit the body. The *Verses* divide them into four groups as an analytical convenience, not as an ontological assertion. The *Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra* states plainly: "The eight consciousnesses have no distinct characteristics."[^38] The word "transformation" (*pariṇāma*) in the Three Transformations itself implies dynamism, fluidity, non-fixity. Each consciousness is a functional presentation arising from the conjunction of conditions, not an independent entity waiting to be discovered.

The antidote: when observing your own mental activity in daily life, do not try to identify "this is the sixth consciousness operating" or "that's the seventh consciousness acting" — doing so merely converts an analytical framework into a new object of attachment. The eight consciousnesses are tools, not ends.

### 5.2 ❌ The Instant-Awakening Fallacy: Reading This Paper Does Not Mean You Have Transformed

The *Zongjing Lu*'s "not the substance that turns, only the name" is easily misread as "since the substance doesn't need to turn, I'm already a buddha — no practice necessary." This is a serious distortion. "Not the substance that turns" means that the goal of transformation is not to destroy consciousness and create wisdom, but to remove obscurations and reveal what is inherently present. But the removal of obscurations itself demands rigorous practice — the *Cheng Weishi Lun* describes this process through five stages of cultivation (accumulation, preparation, insight, cultivation, and ultimate). From knowing that "consciousness is wisdom" to realizing it firsthand — between these lies the entire bodhisattva path.

What this paper accomplishes belongs to the level of "knowing." "Knowing" is the map for "practice" — but the map is not the journey.

### 5.3 ❌ Linearizing the Three Transformations: The Eighth Does Not "Produce" the Seventh, Which Does Not "Produce" the First Six

The "first, second, third" sequence of the Three Transformations is an analytical order, not a causal-temporal sequence. In any given instant, all eight consciousnesses operate simultaneously (provided the conditions for arising are met). Ālaya-vijñāna does not finish operating and then "hand off" to manas. The Three Transformations describe a dependence relation — who depends on whom for existence — not a sequential production line. The "simultaneous supports" of the five sense-consciousnesses must all be present at once: "lacking any one, they will not operate."

### 5.4 ❌ Reifying the Four Aspects: They Are Not Four Puzzle Pieces

The *Cheng Weishi Lun* explicitly states that the four aspects "can be condensed to three, to two, or even to one — since their substance is not distinct."[^39] The four aspects are four functional facets of a single consciousness-body, not four separable components. To reify the four aspects is like forcibly splitting the activity "I am looking at a flower" into four independent events: "the image of the flower," "the act of looking," "knowing I'm looking," and "confirming the knowing is genuine" — in reality, these are different descriptive angles of one and the same cognitive activity.

---

## VI. Conclusion

The eight consciousnesses are not eight rooms but different facets of a single ocean. The first transformation (the eighth consciousness) is like the ocean water itself — deep, vast, and continuous. The second transformation (the seventh consciousness) is like the undercurrent at the ocean floor — invisible yet constantly churning. The third transformation (the first six consciousnesses) is like the waves on the surface — endlessly varying yet never apart from the water. The Four Aspects reveal that within each wave there are also layers — it is not a homogeneous mass of water but a precise structure of cognizer and cognized, self-awareness and confirmation.

The *Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra*'s "the ocean of store-consciousness abides constantly; stirred by the wind of object-realms" and the *Zongjing Lu*'s "the one mind has never moved; consciousness and wisdom self-differentiate" complete the final positioning of the eight-consciousness panorama from two dimensions — the temporal (the ocean's continuity versus the waves' arising and ceasing) and the essential (in delusion they are consciousnesses; in awakening they are wisdoms).

The significance of this panoramic map lies not in memorizing technical terms but in seeing a crucial structural fact: what we take to be a genuinely existing "self" and "external world" are merely the products of the Three Transformations — "these consciousnesses transform; what discriminates and what is discriminated — by this, those are all non-existent. Therefore, all is consciousness-only."

This paper is the bird's-eye view of the S3 series. From the next paper onward, we descend into the internal world of each consciousness: P03 enters the seed-warehouse of ālaya-vijñāna, P04 confronts the self-grasping mechanism of manas, P05 explores the vast functional range of the sixth consciousness, and P06 returns to the directness of the first five. The map is now unfolded; what follows is walking each street.
