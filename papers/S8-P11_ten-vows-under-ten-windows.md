## Abstract

The Ten Profound Gates (*shíxuán mén* 十玄門) formulated by the Huayan patriarch Fazang constitute the central doctrinal structure by which the Huayan tradition articulates *dharmadhātu* dependent origination (*fǎjiè yuánqǐ* 法界緣起). Successive patriarchs—Zhiyan, Fazang himself, Chengguan, and Zongmi—adjusted the order, the gate names, and the totality-differentiation relations among the ten gates. This paper does not rehearse that hermeneutic history. Instead, it proposes an original soteriological thesis: **the Ten Profound Gates and the Ten Great Vows of Samantabhadra (*Pǔxián shí dà yuàn wáng* 普賢十大願王) constitute two coordinated unfoldings of a single Perfect Teaching (*yuánjiào* 圓教) structure—the former as discursive face, the latter as practical face**.

The Ten Gates, on this reading, are not ten independent doctrinal channels but rather a "one-totality, nine-differentiations" stereoscopic structure—a reading made explicit by Chengguan's dictum that "the Gate of Simultaneous Mutual Correspondence contains the lower nine gates, while the lower nine do not contain Simultaneous Mutual Correspondence." The Ten Vows are likewise not ten independent daily practices but a single bodhisattva mind-conduct deployed across ten facets. Building on Chengguan's asymmetric formulation, this paper installs each gate onto its corresponding vow and then performs two "pivot demonstrations" (the Gate of Simultaneous Mutual Correspondence containing all ten vows; Homage to All Buddhas containing all ten gates) to display how *one-is-all* operates in actual practice.

The paper's own structure is itself a demonstration of the Ten Gates—reading it constitutes a single complete passage through the Gates' structure. This paper is the culminating gathering of the ten cultivation correspondences across the S8 series, and it also lays preparatory ground for the formal argument of the original thesis in S8-P10 (Samantabhadra's Vows of Practice).

## I. Introduction: What Is Actually Happening When You Prostrate?

When you prostrate each morning, are you paying homage to one buddha or to ten directions? When you praise the Tathāgata, are you praising one voice or resonating through an entire dharma-realm? When you dedicate merit, are you dedicating it to "the future" or to "the ten worlds"?

These three questions are not rhetorical. They point to a fact that most readers of the *Avataṃsaka Sūtra* have never stopped to examine: **before the Ten Profound Gates illuminate them, the Ten Great Vows of Samantabhadra appear as ten independent daily actions; after the Ten Gates illuminate them, each vow rises from the behavioral layer to the dharma-realm layer**. The physical action of prostration does not change; what changes is the internal grammar by which that single physical moment is understood. The inner structure of time, space, and agency transforms from linear to interfusing, from individual to dharma-realm.

The task of this paper is simple to state: to install each of Fazang's ten windows (the Ten Profound Gates) onto each of the ten actions of Samantabhadra's Ten Great Vows, and to demonstrate how that installation operates in actual practice.

### 1.1 Why This Paper Must Be a Meta-Level Exception

The methodological manifesto of the S8 series (set out in S8-P01) stipulates that each paper in the series adopts one of the Ten Gates as its writing posture, explicitly declaring that gate at the beginning of the paper. This paper (S8-P11), however, is one of the few in the series that does not choose a single gate-posture. The other such exception is S8-P12.

The reason is clear: this paper is itself a synoptic display of all ten gates in their totality. To select any one gate as posture would be to assert that "the other nine gates are secondary in this paper"—a claim that would violate the asymmetric "one-totality, nine-differentiations" structure (Chengguan's explicit reading: every gate contains every other gate). This paper must take "flat review plus stereoscopic self-demonstration" as its meta-level posture in order for its form to align with the object it discusses.

But there is a deeper consideration. The chapter structure of this paper should itself be a demonstration of the Ten Gates. The aim is not to *describe* the Ten Gates but to *argue in a manner that is itself the Ten Gates*. After finishing this paper, the reader should not merely *know what* the Ten Gates are; she should have *passed through* the structure of the Ten Gates once. Reading becomes cultivation in its own modest register—this is what the S8 series pursues at the meta level: form as content.

### 1.2 A One-Paragraph Historical Background

Zhiyan, in his *One-Vehicle Ten Profound Gates of the Avataṃsaka* (*Huáyán yīchéng shíxuán mén* 華嚴一乘十玄門, T1868), gave the first systematic formulation of the Ten Gates, building on the teachings of Du Shun. Fazang, in his *Treatise on the Five Doctrines of the One-Vehicle of the Avataṃsaka* (*Huáyán yīchéng jiàoyì fēnqí zhāng* 華嚴一乘教義分齊章, T1866, fascicle four), retained Zhiyan's gate-names but rearranged the sequence. In his later *Record of Investigations into the Mysteries of the Avataṃsaka* (*Huáyán jīng tànxuán jì* 華嚴經探玄記, T1733, fascicle one), Fazang rearranged them a second time and substituted the "Gate of Unhindered Freedom of Broad and Narrow" (*guǎngxiá zìzài wúài mén* 廣狹自在無礙門) for Zhiyan's "Gate of Pure-and-Mixed Adornments" (*zhūzàng chúnzá jùdé mén* 諸藏純雜具德門), and substituted the "Gate of Host-and-Guest Perfect Clarity and Complete Virtue" (*zhǔbàn yuánmíng jùdé mén* 主伴圓明具德門) for the "Gate of Mind-Only Perfect Turning" (*wéixīn huízhuǎn shànchéng mén* 唯心迴轉善成門)[^1]. Chengguan, in his *Commentary on the Avataṃsaka Sūtra* (*Dà fāng guǎng fó huáyán jīng shū* 大方廣佛華嚴經疏, T1735) and in the *Elaborated Exegesis* (*Dà fāng guǎng fó huáyán jīng suíshū yǎnyì chāo* 大方廣佛華嚴經隨疏演義鈔, T1736), made explicit that the ten-gate structure is "one-totality and nine-differentiations"—the Gate of Simultaneous Mutual Correspondence is the totality, the remaining nine are differentiations[^2]. **All three patriarchs disagreed on the sequence, and Chengguan explicitly stated that the ten gates are not a linear progression**.

The significance of this history for the present paper lies not in textual criticism but in a principle it demonstrates: **the order of the Ten Profound Gates is not fixed**. Order can be rearranged for pedagogical purpose; gate-names can be substituted for doctrinal precision. This is precisely the principle that "patriarchs may rearrange, but what the Buddha said may not be rearranged"—an internal warrant from within the Huayan tradition itself. What this paper does—taking the sequence of Samantabhadra's Ten Great Vows as its organizing axis and pairing it with the Ten Profound Gates—continues the same tradition. What Fazang did in the seventh century, this paper does in the twenty-first, in the same spirit.

### 1.3 The Division of Labor between This Paper and S8-P10

A clarification is necessary: **this paper does not argue for the legitimacy of the correspondence "Ten Gates with Ten Vows."** That argument belongs to S8-P10 §VI, where a historical, doctrinal, and text-critical argument for why this correspondence holds will be formally advanced. This paper assumes the correspondence has been established and demonstrates how it operates in practice.

In short: **P10 proves it; P11 shows you how to use it**. The division is clear, there is no overlap, and neither paper steals the other's argument. The value of this paper lies not in *why* but in *how*—after reading it, the reader should be able to see, in the daily actions of homage, praise, offering, confession, rejoicing, requesting the turning of the Dharma wheel, requesting buddhas to remain, constant study of the Buddha, accordance with beings, and universal dedication, how the ten windows lift each action from the behavioral layer to the dharma-realm layer.

### 1.4 Notice of Failure Points

This paper carries a distinctive risk. It looks, more than any other paper in the S8 series, like a "manual of cultivation." But it remains an academic paper—its conclusion is not "you should do it this way," but rather "the Ten-Profound-Gates structure established by the patriarchs can serve as a hermeneutic lens through which the daily practice of the Ten Vows is deepened at the soteriological level." The Middle-Way corrections in §V will address this boundary, paying particular attention to the fundamental difference between "a scholarly discussion of the soteriological face" and "a practice manual." The reader is asked to hold this distinction throughout.

As a meta-level paper, moreover, this text carries a second danger: after reading it, the reader may mistakenly feel, "I understand it now; I have leveled up"—which is a mistake. Understanding a structure is not the same as walking through that structure. This paper leads you to the ten windows; the work of stepping through each window must be done, each day, by you.

> The three-dimensional pen cannot write to a ten-dimensional object. What the pen can do is mark clearly the place where the pen is set down.

This is the methodological anchor set by S8-P01 for the whole S8 series, and it is also the fundamental posture of this paper. This paper will mark the place where the pen is set down three times: at the conclusion of the pivot demonstrations in §3.11, at the final item of the Middle-Way corrections in §V, and at the final paragraph of the conclusion in §VI.

## II. Scriptural Foundations

Following the "sūtras-first, treatises-second" principle of the S8 series, this section is divided into three sub-sections: (A) *Āgama* leakage—the seed of the method of multi-angled observation; (B) scriptural grounding within the *Avataṃsaka*; (C) patriarchal formulations, from Zhiyan to Zongmi, in their minimal installation. Since this paper is a meta-level work, the *Āgama* leakage does not point to any particular dharma-realm phenomenon, but rather to the method of multi-angled observation itself in its *Āgama* prototype.

### 2.1 A. Āgama Leakage: The Seed of Multi-Angled Observation

The methodological manifesto of the S8 series (S8-P01) stipulates that *Āgama* leakage is not a "seed" in the sense of an anticipation of later developments; it is evidence that "what the later tradition displays was always there—simply not broadcast at full power on that channel." What this paper seeks in the *Āgamas* is therefore the early form of the method of "multi-angled observation of a single object"—evidence that Fazang's innovation was not the method itself but rather the extension of that method's object from the "five aggregates" to the "dharma-realm."

#### The Householder Citta: Dharma of Multiple Meanings versus Dharma of a Single Meaning

In the *Saṃyuktāgama* (*Zá āhán jīng* 雜阿含經), fascicle 21, sūtra 567, the householder Citta engages in dialogue with the venerable Nāgadatta. Nāgadatta asks: concentration of immeasurables, concentration of signlessness, concentration of nothingness, concentration of emptiness—are these "different meanings with different names" or "a single meaning with different names"? After reflection, Citta replies:

> "There is a dharma with multiple meanings, multiple phrases, multiple flavors; and there is a dharma with a single meaning and multiple flavors."[^3]

Citta then unfolds two directions. The first direction is "multiple meanings, multiple flavors": each of the four concentrations has its own distinct doctrinal context—immeasurables are compassion pervading all objects, signlessness is the non-grasping of any mark, nothingness is the fourth formless absorption, emptiness is the emptiness of the world, neither self nor belonging to self. The second direction, "one meaning, multiple flavors," runs deeper. When the four concentrations are gathered under the single meaning of "the absence of greed, hatred, and delusion," they are no longer four independent practices but four angles of approach to the same liberation:

> "Greed has measure; the one without dispute is the highest immeasurable. Greed is a mark, hatred and delusion are marks; the one without dispute is signless. Greed is possessing, hatred and delusion are possessing; the one without dispute is nothing possessed. The one without dispute is empty: empty of greed, empty of hatred, empty of delusion."[^4]

The structural significance of this dialogue is considerable. Already in the *Āgama* period, a clear distinction is drawn between two layers of observation: the same object (the state of liberation) can be approached through multiple independent angles ("multiple meanings"), *and* it can be recognized as the multiple facets of a single essence ("one meaning, multiple flavors"). **This is the earliest structural prototype of the Ten Gates' "one-is-all, all-is-one."** What Fazang did seven centuries later was to take the structure Citta had already discovered and extend it from "the four concentrations" to "the entire dharma-realm." The method is continuous; the object differs.

#### The Five Aggregates and the Five-Fold True-Knowing: A 5×5 Observational Matrix

In the *Saṃyuktāgama*, fascicle 2, sūtra 41, the most systematic "multi-angled observation of the same object" in the *Āgamas* is given:

> "There are five aggregates of clinging—the aggregate of form, feeling, perception, formations, consciousness. Concerning these five aggregates of clinging, I know them truly in five ways—I know form truly, and I know form's arising, form's gratification, form's danger, form's escape truly."[^5]

For each aggregate, observation proceeds along five dimensions: nature, arising, gratification, danger, escape—five aggregates multiplied by five observations, yielding a 5×5=25-dimensional analytical matrix. This matrix is itself a "systematic multi-dimensional observation of a single object": each dimension gives one facet of the object; the five dimensions together present its complete structure. **This method was already fully established in the *Āgama* period. Fazang merely extended it from the focused object of "the five aggregates" to the total object of "the dharma-realm."**

#### Ānāpānasati: One Dharma Subsuming Many

The *Saṃyuktāgama*, fascicle 29, sūtra 810, displays the early structure of "one-is-all" even more directly. Ānanda asks the Buddha: is there a single practice that, when cultivated, brings four practices to fulfillment; four practices that bring seven to fulfillment; seven that bring two to fulfillment? The Buddha replies:

> "There is one practice which, much cultivated, brings about the fulfillment of two dharmas. What is this one practice? It is mindfulness of in-and-out breathing (*ānāpānasati*). Much cultivated, it brings the four foundations of mindfulness to fulfillment; when the four foundations of mindfulness are fulfilled, the seven factors of awakening are fulfilled; when the seven factors of awakening are fulfilled, clear knowing and liberation are fulfilled."[^6]

One practice (mindfulness of breathing) → four practices (four foundations of mindfulness) → seven practices (seven factors of awakening) → two practices (clear knowing and liberation). This structure of progressive unfolding is structurally identical to the Ten Gates' "one contains many, many contains one." A simple observation of the breath itself subsumes the full soteriological progression from the four foundations of mindfulness to liberating knowledge. **This is the *Āgamas*' most direct statement of "one dharma is all dharmas."**

#### The Blind Men and the Elephant: Counter-Evidence

The parable of the blind men and the elephant in the *Dīrghāgama* (*Cháng āhán jīng* 長阿含經, fascicle 19) states the same methodological point in its negative form. King Mirror-Face assembles those born blind and has each touch a different part of an elephant. The one who touches the trunk says the elephant is like a curved shaft; the one who touches the tusk says it is like a pestle; the one who touches the ear says it is like a winnowing basket; the one who touches the back says it is like a hill; the one who touches the belly says it is like a wall. The king speaks a verse:

> "These blind men gathered here argue and dispute. The elephant's body is truly one, but differing marks give rise to contention."[^7]

The object is one; the angles of observation are many; partiality generates dispute, interfusion generates harmony. The Buddha, immediately following this parable, extends it to "non-Buddhist teachers, not knowing the truth of suffering, the truth of origination, the truth of cessation, the truth of the path, give rise to divergent views and contend with one another." **This is the earliest negative argument in the *Āgamas* for why multi-angled observation of a single object is necessary**—partiality in one gate breeds dispute, interfusion through ten gates breeds harmony. The structural response of the Ten Profound Gates exists precisely for this reason.

#### Summary: Continuous Method, Expanded Object

The four *Āgama* passages together establish that the method of "systematic multi-angled observation of a single object" was already fully articulated in the *Āgama* period. The householder Citta gives the earliest explicit formulation of "one meaning, multiple flavors"; the five-fold true-knowing of the aggregates gives the most systematic multi-dimensional matrix; ānāpānasati gives the specific model of "one dharma is all dharmas"; the parable of the blind men gives the negative case for why interfusion is necessary.

What Fazang did was **not to invent this method, but to extend the object of this method from the specific dharma-numbers (the five aggregates, the four noble truths, liberation) that early Buddhism focused on, to the dharma-realm in its totality**. The object expanded; the methodology did not rupture. This is the paradigm case of "leakage is not seed": the multi-angled observation of the dharma-realm displayed by the Huayan Ten Gates was not a later invention but a method always present in the Buddha's teaching—merely focused, in the *Āgama* discourses, on objects that were operationally accessible to the Buddha's immediate audience. When Fazang expanded the object of this method to the entire dharma-realm, he was not creating something new; he was releasing the Buddha's methodological restriction for the specific audience of the *Āgamas*.

### 2.2 B. Scriptural Grounding within the Avataṃsaka

#### "Ten" as the Avataṃsaka's Own Structural Principle

Fazang, in his *Treatise on the Five Doctrines*, fascicle four, explicitly poses and answers the question:

> "Question: How do we know that the number ten manifests the inexhaustible? Answer: In the *Avataṃsaka Sūtra* the number ten is established as a rule in order to manifest the meaning of the inexhaustible."[^8]

The critical implication of this self-posed question is that "ten" is not a pedagogical device Fazang chose for convenience; it is the *Avataṃsaka*'s own structural principle. The sūtra repeatedly unfolds in units of ten—ten faiths, ten abodes, ten practices, ten dedications, ten grounds, ten concentrations, ten spiritual powers, ten patiences, ten bodies, ten eyes, ten ears, ten treasuries, ten perfections, ten great vows. "Ten" is not a literal counting of dharma-numbers; it is the sūtra's indexing-scheme for "completeness" and "inexhaustibility." Fazang's Ten Profound Gates build on this principle of the text itself; he is echoing the *Avataṃsaka*'s own language.

In his *Record of Investigations into the Mysteries*, fascicle 15, Fazang refines this further:

> "Ten is the round number, inexhaustible."[^9]

The concept "round number" (*yuánshù* 圓數) is remarkably precise. It does not mean simply "ten items"; it means "the completion of one cycle"—ten signifies the fullness of a structure from beginning to end, and because of its "round" (cyclical) character it naturally points toward the inexhaustible (the end of each cycle being the beginning of the next). The "ten" of the Ten Profound Gates is this "round number"; it is neither a random pedagogical choice nor a Western-style "sacred number" in the sense of numerological mysticism.

#### Samantabhadra as the "Person of the Cause" of the Ten Gates

Zhiyan, in the *One-Vehicle Ten Profound Gates*, explicitly positions Samantabhadra as the "person of the cause" (*yīn rén* 因人) of the Ten Gates:

> "By 'cause' is meant skillful expedient-cultivation perfecting both essence and position—that is, Samantabhadra. By 'fruit' is meant the ultimate quiescence, the perfect fruit, the state of the ten buddhas where one is all."[^10]

When someone asks Zhiyan why, since Mañjuśrī is also a cause, Samantabhadra alone is named as the person of the cause, Zhiyan replies: "Although the initial arising is from marvelous wisdom [Mañjuśrī], the perfection lies in completeness; therefore Mañjuśrī is concealed and only Samantabhadra is named."[^11] Zhiyan is explicit: the "power and function of the dependent origination of dharmadhātu" that the Ten Gates describe *is* "what corresponds to Samantabhadra's state." From the day the Ten Gates were established, they have been Samantabhadra's business, not the business of any other bodhisattva.

This positioning is decisive for the present paper. **It establishes, from within the Huayan tradition itself, the basic verdict that "the Ten Profound Gates = the work of Samantabhadra."** When the Zhiyue project, in the twenty-first century, proposes that "the Ten Gates correspond to the Ten Great Vows," it is not imposing an external correspondence; it is completing the other half of the verdict Zhiyan had already established—Zhiyan said the Ten Gates are the causal gateway of Samantabhadra; the present paper adds that the soteriological face corresponding to the Ten Gates is precisely Samantabhadra's Ten Great Vows.

A passage by Chengguan, in his *Commentary on the Avataṃsaka Sūtra*, fascicle 28, is even more direct:

> "In truth, all of these are the great dependent-origination gate of dharmadhātu—the unhindered and sovereign conduct of Samantabhadra."[^12]

"The great dependent-origination gate of dharmadhātu" is the object the Ten Gates describe (the Ten Gates are its ten angles of observation); "the unhindered and sovereign conduct of Samantabhadra" is the object the Ten Great Vows describe (the Ten Vows are its ten enacted practices). In a single sentence, Chengguan identifies the two. The four words "in truth, all of these" collapse the discursive face (the great dependent-origination gate of dharmadhātu) and the soteriological face (the unhindered and sovereign conduct of Samantabhadra) into one. **This is the most direct textual basis, within the Huayan patriarchal corpus, for the central thesis of the present paper.**

A qualification is in order: Chengguan did not unfold this identification into an explicit term-by-term correspondence between "the Ten Gates" and "the Ten Great Vows." The term-by-term correspondence is the original contribution of the Zhiyue project, to be formally argued in S8-P10 §VI. But the basic verdict—"the two are, in truth, the same matter"—Chengguan has already made. The present paper only takes one step forward from that basic verdict.

#### The "Sea-Seal" Verses of the Xianshou Chapter: The Dharma-Realm Basis of Daily Practice

One final passage must be cited as the dharma-realm basis for daily practice, from the "Xianshou" chapter, verses of the Sea-Seal Samādhi:

> "The forms of sentient beings are each not the same;
> their deeds and sounds are also immeasurable.
> All of these can be manifested—
> by the majestic power of the Sea-Seal Samādhi."[^13]

These four lines are the core scriptural citation for S8-P01 and S8-P07, and this paper does not rehearse them. But they must be invoked here, because they establish a critical link: the "forms, deeds, sounds" of sentient beings—that is, the posture of prostration, the voice of praise, the movements of offering, the tears of confession—are "all manifested" in the Sea-Seal Samādhi. The majestic power of the Sea-Seal Samādhi consists precisely in taking the daily actions of sentient beings and reflecting them as events of the dharma-realm. **The dynamism of this reflection is precisely the dharma-realm dependent-origination structure that the Ten Profound Gates describe.**

### 2.3 C. Patriarchal Formulations (Minimal Installation)

This sub-section lists only the necessary anchors; no expansion of historical critique is offered (that critique having been compressed into a single paragraph in §1.2). The patriarchal authorities relied upon in this paper, in chronological order:

**1. Zhiyan, *One-Vehicle Ten Profound Gates of the Avataṃsaka* (T1868).** The earliest systematic formulation, transmitting Du Shun's teaching. The ten gates, in Zhiyan's order: (1) Simultaneous Mutual Correspondence, (2) Indra's Net State, (3) Hidden-and-Manifest Mutual Completion, (4) Subtle Containing and Establishment, (5) Ten Worlds Differentiated-and-Mutually-Accomplished, (6) Pure-and-Mixed Adornments, (7) One-and-Many Mutual Containing without Identity, (8) Mutual Identity and Sovereign Freedom of All Dharmas, (9) Mind-Only Perfect Turning, (10) Entrusted-Matter Revealing the Dharma for Generating Understanding. Zhiyan's version also contains an important preliminary structure of "ten numbers of distinct and same essences," displaying a deeper dual-axis of the Ten Gates: "one-in-many, many-in-one" (spoken in terms of marks) and "one-is-many, many-is-one" (spoken in terms of principle). §3.0 of this paper returns to this dual-axis.

**2. Fazang, *Record of Investigations into the Mysteries of the Avataṃsaka* (T1733), fascicle one.** The definitive "new ten gates" version. The ten gate-names, in Fazang's mature order: (1) Simultaneous Mutual Correspondence, (2) Unhindered Freedom of Broad and Narrow, (3) One-and-Many Mutual Containing without Identity, (4) Mutual Identity and Sovereign Freedom of All Dharmas, (5) Hidden-and-Manifest Mutual Completion, (6) Subtle Containing and Establishment, (7) Indra's Net Dharmadhātu, (8) Entrusted-Matter Revealing the Dharma, (9) Ten Worlds Differentiated-and-Mutually-Accomplished, (10) Host-and-Guest Perfect Clarity and Complete Virtue. **All Ten Gate names and ordering in this paper follow Fazang's new ten gates.**

**3. Fazang, *Essay on the Golden Lion of the Avataṃsaka* (*Huáyán jīnshīzi zhāng* 華嚴金師子章, T1880 / T1881).** Fazang's most succinct demonstration, delivered as a teaching to Empress Wu Zetian. Here Fazang uses "gold" (metaphor for dharma-realm essence) and "lion" (metaphor for dependently-arisen phenomena) to install the Ten Gates, one by one, onto the single concrete image of a "golden lion." **This is the most important patriarchal precedent for the present paper**: Fazang has already demonstrated the installation of the Ten Gates onto a simple concrete object. What this paper does is identical in spirit—only the object is exchanged, from the golden lion to the actions of daily practice.

Fazang's handling of the Gate of Simultaneous Mutual Correspondence in the *Golden Lion* can serve as a methodological template for §III of this paper: "Gold and lion arise simultaneously; complete, perfect, mutually fulfilling." In nine characters the abstract doctrine is collapsed onto a single concrete visual image. §3.1 of this paper ("Homage to All Buddhas × Simultaneous Mutual Correspondence") adopts the same method—collapsing the abstract doctrine onto a single concrete bodily action.

**4. Chengguan, *Commentary on the Avataṃsaka Sūtra* (T1735) and *Elaborated Exegesis* (T1736).** The proponent of the "one-totality, nine-differentiations" reading. Chengguan makes this reading explicit in three places:

- *Commentary*, fascicle 2: "It also contains the subsequent nine gates, and the teaching-and-meaning that those gates themselves contain, because it is the totality."[^14]
- *Elaborated Exegesis*, fascicle 10: "The Gate of Simultaneous Mutual Correspondence, because it is the totality, crowns the beginning of the nine."[^15]
- *Elaborated Exegesis*, fascicle 11: "Within the gate of simultaneity, the lower nine gates are contained; the lower nine gates do not contain simultaneity."[^16]

The third statement is of particular importance—it identifies the asymmetry of the totality-differentiation relation. The gate of simultaneity contains the lower nine gates, but the lower nine do not contain the gate of simultaneity. This asymmetry is the structural basis for both §3.0 and §3.11 of this paper.

**5. Zongmi, *Commentary on the Huayan Contemplation of Dharmadhātu* (*Zhù huáyán fǎjiè guānmén* 註華嚴法界觀門, T1884).** The post-Fazang hermeneutic adjustment. Zongmi does not directly list the Ten Profound Gates in this work; instead, starting from Du Shun's "contemplation of universal containment" in the *Contemplation of Dharmadhātu*, he unfolds ten gates (principle-as-matter, matter-as-principle, matter-containing-principle-and-matter, pervasive-and-local-without-hindrance, broad-and-narrow-without-hindrance, pervasive-containment-without-hindrance, gathering-and-entering-without-hindrance, interrelation-without-hindrance, mutual-presence-without-hindrance, thoroughly-interfusing-without-hindrance). He notes that "if we distribute these ten gates across all dharmas and meanings, only then do they become the meaning of the Ten Profound"[^17]. Zongmi's treatment shows that the interpretation of the Ten Gates continued to move in the post-Fazang era, but the direction of that movement was "toward more precise alignment with the structure of dharmadhātu," not "toward a better ordering." Zongmi is minimally cited in this paper because his ten gates constitute an independent hermeneutic system not on the main axis of this paper's argument.

**The patriarchal installation is now complete.** From Zhiyan to Zongmi, all the authorities this paper requires have been listed. The next section (§III) proceeds directly to the cultivation correspondences.

## III. Ten Vows under Ten Windows: Core Argument

### 3.0 The Totality-Differentiation Pivot: Why Chengguan's "One-Totality, Nine-Differentiations" Determines the Writing of This Paper

Chengguan's "one-totality, nine-differentiations" reading is not merely one more interpretive viewpoint within the hermeneutic history of the Ten Gates; it directly determines the writing of this paper. This subsection spells out the logic of that determination in roughly seven hundred words.

Chengguan, in the *Elaborated Exegesis*, fascicle 11, writes:

> "Within the gate of simultaneity, the lower nine gates are contained; the lower nine gates do not contain simultaneity."

The literal meaning is: the Gate of Simultaneous Mutual Correspondence contains the subsequent nine gates (because it is the totality), but the subsequent nine gates do not contain the Gate of Simultaneous Mutual Correspondence (because they are differentiations). Taken at face value, the totality-differentiation relation is a unidirectional "totality contains differentiations, differentiations do not contain totality."

But Chengguan does not stop there. Slightly later in the same passage, he adds:

> "Now, speaking in terms of non-separation, the nine gates likewise, following the example of simultaneity, each contain nine gates."[^18]

This is a critical turn. "Speaking in terms of non-separation" means: when we shift focus from the "totality-differentiation relation" to the "non-separation (mutual containment) relation," each differentiation-gate also contains the other nine. Chengguan thus gives two grammars for "one-contains-ten":

- **Totality-grammar**: the Gate of Simultaneous Mutual Correspondence, as "totality," contains the subsequent nine gates—this is the position already held by S8-P01 (the Gate of Simultaneous Mutual Correspondence × Vow 1, Homage to All Buddhas).
- **Differentiation-grammar**: each differentiation-gate also contains the other nine—this is what §III of this paper demonstrates in each pair.

In other words: "one-is-all" in the structure of the Ten Gates operates at two scales. The first scale is "totality over totality"—the Gate of Simultaneous Mutual Correspondence, as totality, naturally contains the subsequent nine gates. The second scale is "differentiation over totality"—each differentiation-gate, when you enter into its internal structure, also contains the other nine. **The first scale is "structural containment"; the second is "performative containment."**

This distinction has two direct consequences for how this paper is written.

**Consequence One: §3.1–§3.10 of this paper cannot be written as isolated pair-by-pair correspondences.** If each pair were written only as a single-line correspondence ("Vow 1 ↔ Simultaneous Mutual Correspondence; Vow 2 ↔ Broad-and-Narrow..."), the writing would remain at the level of "differentiation against differentiation" and would not touch the "differentiation-against-totality" scale. Each pair in this paper must display how "this window also contains the shadows of the other nine windows"—this is the necessity of the "horizontal leakage" block in each pair.

**Consequence Two: This paper must, in §3.11 (Pivot Demonstrations), push "one-contains-ten" to its logical limit.** It is not enough to offer "horizontal hints" in each pair; at some point (§3.11), the full unfolding of "one-contains-ten" must be displayed. This paper selects a totality-gate (Simultaneous Mutual Correspondence) and shows how it contains all ten vows; it selects a totality-vow (Homage to All Buddhas) and shows how it contains all ten gates. These pivot demonstrations are not "another iteration"; they are the explicit completion of what the horizontal leakages only hint at, so the reader may see with her own eyes the full shape of "one-is-ten."

**The Ten Great Vows likewise have a totality-differentiation structure.** Which vow is the "totality"? This is the question that S8-P10 §VI will formally argue. The present paper offers a pointing answer: **Homage to All Buddhas is the totality of the ten vows, because homage itself already contains the other nine vows**—and the gate it corresponds to, the Gate of Simultaneous Mutual Correspondence, is likewise the totality of the Ten Gates. **The correspondence totality-vow-to-totality-gate is not coincidence; it is the same Perfect Teaching structure unfolding its two faces in parallel on the soteriological and the discursive sides.** §3.11.B below will display how this one action of Homage to All Buddhas contains the shadows of the other nine vows.

Having articulated this pivot, this section proceeds directly to the ten-pair unfolding.

### 3.1 Vow 1: Homage to All Buddhas × Gate of Simultaneous Mutual Correspondence

**The action.** A standard prostration breaks into seven phases: palms joined, head lowered, knees bent, kneeling, forehead to ground, palms turned up, rising. From the outside, these seven phases are linear—palms join before the knees bend; kneeling precedes the forehead touching the ground. Each physical phase has temporal priority.

But ask yourself: when I prostrate a thousand times each day, am I paying homage to one buddha or to ten directions? If to one, then the thousand prostrations are a thousand independent actions, each bowing to one specific buddha. If to ten directions, then each of the thousand prostrations is simultaneously paying homage to all buddhas of the ten directions and three times—but how is that possible? My physical body can only perform one action in any given moment. How can a single moment "simultaneously" render homage to immeasurable buddhas?

**The window opens.** The central doctrine of the Gate of Simultaneous Mutual Correspondence is that the ten paired dharmas (teaching and meaning, principle and matter, object and wisdom, practice and position, cause and result, etc.) "mutually correspond in simultaneity as a single dependent origination" on this one event (Fazang's phrasing in the *Record of Investigations*, fascicle 1)[^19]. Fazang immediately asks, "How is this possible?" and answers: "It is because of the true virtue of dependent origination and the power of the dharma-nature Sea-Seal Samādhi that it is so; it is not accomplished by expedient-cultivation, and therefore the ten are simultaneous."[^20] Simultaneity is not a simultaneity cultivated into being; it is the originally simultaneous structure of dharmadhātu itself.

When this doctrine is installed onto the action of prostration: at the physical instant when your forehead touches the ground, this one prostration is itself the simultaneous correspondence of the ten paired dharmas. "Teaching" is the dharma toward which the act points (reverence for the Buddha); "meaning" is the principle the act embodies (subject and object are not two); "principle" is the original quiescence of the prostration; "matter" is its concrete posture; "object" is the buddhas being paid homage; "wisdom" is the awareness paying homage; "practice" is the movement of prostrating; "position" is the stage of the practitioner in that moment; "cause" is the wholesome root this prostration plants; "result" is the merit this prostration ripens. All ten pairs arise in the same physical instant of this single prostration—not "first teaching, then meaning, then principle-and-matter," but ten pairs simultaneously arising in the same moment.

**The reversal of time and space.** The linear "first this buddha, then that buddha" inverts into the perfectly interfusing "one prostration gathers the ten directions and three times." This inversion is not rhetorical; it is the literal statement of the Ten Gates' structure.

Fazang, in the *Contemplation of Exhausting Delusion and Returning to the Source, the Profound Meaning of the Cultivation of the Avataṃsaka* (*Xiū huáyán àozhǐ wàngjìn huányuán guān* 修華嚴奧旨妄盡還源觀), defines the Sea-Seal Samādhi in sixteen characters: "Suchness is the original awareness. When deluded thoughts are exhausted and the mind is clear, the myriad images arise all together."[^21] Installed onto prostration: at the physical instant of the prostration, deluded thoughts come momentarily to rest—this is "deluded thoughts exhausted and the mind clear"; at that same moment, all the buddhas of the ten directions and three times arise together in the structure of this one prostration—this is "the myriad images arise all together." The Sea-Seal Samādhi is not some advanced meditative attainment; it is the present accomplishment of this one prostration in the simplest daily action.

**Realizing this in practice.** The reader can perform a simple inner experiment during prostration. Do not think, "which buddha am I paying homage to now?" Do not think, "I am paying homage to all buddhas of the ten directions and three times." **Let the moment when the forehead touches the ground be nothing but the touching of the ground**—no calculation of object, no calculation of merit, no calculation of time.

When the inner grammar of this prostration ceases to be the dualistic structure of "I" and "buddha," the prostration itself becomes an event in which buddhas of the ten directions and three times receive homage simultaneously. It is not "I performed an action of paying homage to the ten directions and three times"; it is "the event of paying homage to the ten directions and three times is self-manifesting in this one prostration." The distinction between one-who-pays-homage and one-to-whom-homage-is-paid fails, for the duration of this one prostration—this is the soteriological face of the Gate of Simultaneous Mutual Correspondence, "as a single dependent origination, without prior or posterior, beginning or end, or any such distinction."

**Horizontal leakage.** The Gate of Simultaneous Mutual Correspondence is not an isolated temporal statement. When the posture of prostration is established, this one prostration itself is also **broad-and-narrow sovereign** (one prostration covers the ten directions without expansion), **host-and-guest perfectly clear** (you are host and the buddhas are guests; simultaneously the buddhas are host and you are guest), **Indra's net** (each prostration reflects every other prostration as the jewels of the king's net), **ten-worlds differentiated-and-mutually-accomplished** (this one prostration gathers all prostrations of past and future). Simultaneous Mutual Correspondence is the "totality-gate" of this structure; the other nine gates are the "differentiation-gates"—and the differentiation-gates are not located elsewhere. They are here, in the same physical instant of this one prostration, each seen from a different angle.

**Pointing-at-the-moon phrase.**

> **One prostration is already the ten directions and three times entire. There is no counting to be done.**

### 3.2 Vow 2: Praise of the Tathāgata × Gate of Unhindered Freedom of Broad and Narrow

**The action.** The six-character formula "Homage to the Original Teacher Śākyamuni Buddha" (*Nánwú běnshī Shìjiāmóuní Fó* 南無本師釋迦牟尼佛). Or the recitation of a verse of praise: "In heaven and on earth there is none like the Buddha; in the worlds of the ten directions there is also none to compare; of all that the world contains I have seen, none is like the Buddha." A single word, a single phrase, a single verse—this is the physical action of praise.

**The window opens.** The Gate of Unhindered Freedom of Broad and Narrow states that "the divisible is the indivisible, the indivisible is the divisible; broad and narrow are sovereign and without obstruction" (Fazang's phrasing in the *Record of Investigations*)[^22]. A limited material unit—say, a single lotus leaf, or the six-character buddha-name—can "universally pervade the dharma-realm without losing its own position"—remaining both finite and pervasive of the infinite dharma-realm.

Installed onto the action of praise: the six characters of "Homage to the Original Teacher Śākyamuni Buddha" are "narrow"—a finite sound in six syllables. But if "Original Teacher" (*běnshī* 本師) is not restricted to the historical person of Śākyamuni alone, but points to "the original ground from which all buddhas issue," then these six characters become the event of universal praise of all buddhas of the ten directions and three times. Narrow does not lose its narrowness; broad does not lose its broadness. The six characters remain six characters (they do not become more because of "broad"), but their function is already the broad covering of the dharma-realm.

**The reversal of time and space.** Praise is not a quantitative relation of "short phrases are inferior to long verses" (it is not that reciting a verse a thousand times creates more merit than reciting a short phrase); it is the qualitative structure of "the sincerity of the narrow is the dharma-realm resonance of the broad." When the six characters are sincere inner-evidential praise, their function is broad; when they are performative outer-directed praise, even a thousand recitations remain narrow. **The difference between broad and narrow is not in the number of recitations but in the inner structure of this one recitation.**

S8-P02 §3.5 proposed a critical reversal in practice: after a reader has worked through S1–S7, the inner grammar of the action "Praise of the Tathāgata" has quietly shifted—from "I praise an external object" to "I recognize in myself the same nature as that object." The recognition is itself praise. And when the recognition is genuine, the dharma-realm praises back. §3.2 of the present paper connects directly to S8-P02 §3.5 and does not rehearse its argument.

**Realizing this in practice.** The reader may perform another inner experiment during praise. When you recite "Homage to the Original Teacher Śākyamuni Buddha," ask: to whom do these two characters "Original Teacher" point?

If they point only to the historical Śākyamuni, then the six characters are narrow (one object). If they point to "the original ground in me that is identical in nature with Śākyamuni," then the six characters are broad (because "the original ground identical in nature with Śākyamuni" pervades all sentient beings and the ten directions). The same six characters, according to the inner grammar of those two characters "Original Teacher," can be either narrow or broad. **True broad-and-narrow sovereignty is not in the mouth but in the inner object to which "Original Teacher" refers.**

**Horizontal leakage.** When these six characters are sincere inner-evidential praise, this one recitation is also **simultaneously mutually corresponding** (the physical instant of the six characters gathers all praises to all buddhas of the ten directions and three times), **host-and-guest perfectly clear** (your mouth speaks, the dharma-realm resonates; when you are host the dharma-realm is guest, when the dharma-realm is host you are guest), **mutually identical and sovereign** (the one who praises and the buddha who is praised are mutually identical in this one recitation), **entrusted-matter revealing the dharma** (the six characters as concrete events reveal the dharma-realm's own structure of mutual praise). The Gate of Broad-and-Narrow Sovereignty is one angle of this event; the other nine gates are nine other angles on the same event.

**Pointing-at-the-moon phrase.**

> **The sincerity of the narrow is the resonance of the broad.**

### 3.3 Vow 3: Extensive Offering × Gate of Mutual Identity and Sovereign Freedom of All Dharmas

**The action.** Offer flowers, incense, lamps, water, fruit. Each is arranged before the buddha. A hand passes over the petals; a stick of incense is lit; water is poured into the cup; fruit is placed on the dish. This is the physical action of offering.

**The window opens.** The central doctrine of the Gate of Mutual Identity and Sovereign Freedom of All Dharmas is that "one contains all, fulfilling its inexhaustibility, and again inexhaustible" (Zhiyan's phrasing in *One-Vehicle Ten Profound Gates*)[^23]. Fazang in the *Record of Investigations* further explains: "The Gate of Mutual Identity and Sovereign Freedom of All Dharmas... speaks in terms of function. Returning to the ten gates of teaching, meaning, principle, and matter, we take the three-worlds' perfect interfusion, unhindered and sovereign; thus one contains all."[^24] "Mutual identity" is not "similarity"; it is "being the two faces of the same thing."

Installed onto the action of offering: **one stick of incense is all offering instruments**. This is not a rhetorical flourish—it is the literal statement of "mutual identity." When you light this one stick of incense with the correct inner grammar, the one stick of incense itself is already flower, lamp, water, fruit, immeasurable offering instruments. Why? Because "incense" and "all offering instruments" are mutually identical in the dharma-realm—they are not ten different things but ten expressive faces of the same "offering mind."

Fazang, in the *Essay on the Golden Lion*, uses gold and lion to illustrate mutual identity: "Every sense-faculty and every hair of the lion, all are gathered into the gold of the lion exhaustively. Each one pervades the lion's eye. Eye is ear; ear is nose; nose is tongue; tongue is body. Sovereignly established, without obstruction."[^25] "Eye is ear"—this does not say that eye and ear are similar; it says that eye and ear are originally the same thing in the gold (in the dharma-realm essence). Incense is flower, flower is lamp, lamp is water—the same structure.

**The reversal of time and space.** Offering is not the unidirectional giving structure of "I give the buddha objects"; it is the mutually-identical structure of "offering instrument, offerer, and recipient are empty of the three wheels." Emptiness of the three wheels is not a rhetorical addition; it is the literal realization of the Gate of Mutual Identity—when offering instrument, offerer, and recipient are mutually identical, the dualistic structure of "giving" dissolves. There is no "I" who is giving; there is no "buddha" who is receiving; there is no "object" independent of the two. These three are three aspects of a single event in the moment of this one offering.

**Realizing this in practice.** The Dharma-offering is supreme. Samantabhadra's unfolding of "Extensive Offering" in the Ten Great Vows explicitly states: "Among all offerings, the Dharma-offering is the highest"—the Dharma-offering includes "offering by practicing according to the teaching, offering by benefiting beings, offering by gathering beings in, offering by bearing the suffering of beings, offering by diligently cultivating wholesome roots, offering by not abandoning the work of a bodhisattva, offering by not leaving the awakening-mind." Why is the Dharma-offering supreme? Because the Dharma-offering most thoroughly realizes "mutual identity"—the act of "practicing according to the teaching" is itself the object of the offering (the Dharma), the offerer and the object of offering being mutually identical in this one action.

The progression from matter-offering (flower, incense) → principle-offering (understanding the principle of offering) → Dharma-offering (offering by practicing according to the teaching) is the unfolding of the Four Dharma-Realms (matter → principle → matter-and-principle unhindered → matter-and-matter unhindered) on the action of offering. S8-P03 will develop this in full; the present paper does not repeat it. What the present paper emphasizes is: **the simplest single stick of incense, when its inner grammar is "mutual identity," is already the deepest Dharma-offering**.

And there is a more radical observation to state: in the structure of "mutual identity," **the offerer is also the one who is offered to**. Why? Because offerer and recipient are originally not-two. When you offer sincerely to the buddha, you too are the recipient of this offering—not because you "deserve" it, but because under "mutual identity" the structure of "offering" has no unidirectional arrow. The dharma-realm's offering to itself does not distinguish inner from outer.

**Horizontal leakage.** When this one stick of incense is a sincere Dharma-offering, it is also **simultaneously mutually corresponding** (the physical instant of one stick gathers immeasurable offering instruments), **broad-and-narrow sovereign** (the narrow one stick is broadly the offering merit of the whole dharma-realm), **Indra's net** (each stick reflects immeasurable net-jewels of offering), **host-and-guest perfectly clear** (when you are host the buddha is guest; when the buddha is host, you are guest). Mutual identity is the central structure of this event; the other nine gates are nine other angles on the same event.

**Pointing-at-the-moon phrase.**

> **Instrument and dharma-body are mutually identical; the offerer is also the one offered to.**

### 3.4 Vow 4: Confession of Karmic Obstructions × Gate of Hidden-and-Manifest Mutual Completion

**The action.** Kneel, palms joined, recite the verse of confession: "Whatever evil karma I have created, / Born of beginningless greed, hatred, and delusion, / Arising from body, speech, and mind, / I now confess it all." Give rise to the mind of shame and regret. Weep or do not weep. Rise. This is the physical action of confession.

**The window opens.** The central doctrine of the Gate of Hidden-and-Manifest Mutual Completion states: "When the flower can gather them, then the one is manifest and the many are hidden; when the many gather the flower, the one is hidden and the many are manifest. 'Manifest' does not stand together with 'manifest,' and 'hidden' does not stand together with 'hidden'; hidden and manifest, manifest and hidden, are simultaneously without obstruction" (Fazang in the *Record of Investigations*)[^26]. Hidden and manifest are not temporal (first hidden, then manifest); they are simultaneous—the same object, in the same instant, seen from one angle is manifest, seen from another angle is hidden; the two angles hold simultaneously.

Installed onto the action of confession: at the moment of confession, the "original emptiness" of karmic obstructions (hidden) and the "vivid presence of their phenomenal marks" (manifest) hold simultaneously. From the angle of original emptiness, there is "no karma to be confessed"—karmic obstructions are ultimately empty; there is no independently-existing "karma." From the angle of phenomenal marks, "karma-marks are vivid"—the cause-and-effect of this particular wholesome or unwholesome deed has truly left a trace in the continuum of body and mind. **These two angles are not contradictory but simultaneous.** If you see only the emptiness without the phenomenal marks, confession becomes frivolous ("after all, karma is empty; why confess?"). If you see only the phenomenal marks without the emptiness, confession becomes oppressive ("I can never wash away this karma"). **In the moment of true confession, emptiness and phenomenal marks arise together—this is precisely the literal realization of the Gate of Hidden-and-Manifest Mutual Completion.**

**The reversal of time and space.** Confession is not the sequential structure of "first confirm the karma, then seek emptiness" (first feel the weight of karma, then use confession to remove it); it is the interfusing structure of "the existence of karma and the non-existence of karma arise together." The moment of confession is itself the simultaneous arising of two layers—you see the karma (manifest), you also see the emptiness of the karma (hidden), both in the same moment of weeping.

S8-P04 will fully develop this doctrinal structure from the perspective of nature-origination and dependent-origination. The present paper does not repeat that argument. What the present paper states is the concrete instantiation at the body-mind level: at the moment when your tears fall, the falling itself is both "karma is vividly present" (if karma is empty, where do the tears come from?) and "karma is originally empty" (if karma is solid, how can tears wash it clean?). Weeping itself is hidden-and-manifest mutual completion—it is both karma and the emptiness of karma.

**Realizing this in practice.** Many practitioners fall into one of two extremes during confession. The first extreme is "cheap confession"—treating confession as a verbal ritual, reciting the confession verse and moving on, never truly seeing the karmic obstructions. This is the failure-mode of seeing only "hidden" (emptiness) and missing "manifest" (phenomenal marks). The second extreme is "oppressive confession"—falling into endless self-reproach, treating karmic obstructions as permanent stains, feeling oneself beyond redemption. This is the failure-mode of seeing only "manifest" and missing "hidden."

In the moment of true confession, both extremes are simultaneously transcended. You see the karma, so it is not cheap confession. You also see the emptiness of the karma, so it is not oppressive confession. **This dual seeing is not a sequence of two actions; it is two facets of a single seeing**—this is the most precise soteriological correspondence for the Gate of Hidden-and-Manifest Mutual Completion, without exception.

**Horizontal leakage.** When this one confession is a true confession, this one thought is itself **simultaneously mutually corresponding** (the physical instant of confession gathers the simultaneous purification of immeasurable past karma), **mutually identical** (the one confessing and the karma confessed are mutually identical), **ten-worlds differentiated-and-mutually-accomplished** (this one thought of confession pervades all karma of past, present, and future), **entrusted-matter revealing the dharma** (the phenomenal mark of weeping directly reveals the dharma of the mutual completion of emptiness and phenomenality). Hidden-and-manifest mutual completion is the central structure of this event; the other nine gates are nine other angles on the same event.

**Pointing-at-the-moon phrase.**

> **Confession is emptiness; emptiness is confession.**

### 3.5 Vow 5: Rejoicing in Others' Merit × Gate of Host-and-Guest Perfect Clarity and Complete Virtue

**The action.** Hearing that another has done good, practiced, or attained fruition—a friend begins to keep precepts, an elder funds the building of a stūpa, a teacher enters a ten-year retreat, a stranger rescues others in an earthquake. At the moment of hearing, joy arises in the heart. This is the "action" of rejoicing—it does not even require an external bodily movement, only the single inner thought of joy.

**The window opens.** The central doctrine of the Gate of Host-and-Guest Perfect Clarity and Complete Virtue states: "Because every dharma is so, whichever one is brought forward becomes the host; and, by the connected dependent origination, there is immediately host and guest" (Chengguan in his *Commentary*)[^27]. In the dharma-realm there is no fixed "host"; each dharma, when you take it as your focus, becomes the host, and the other dharmas become its guests; when focus shifts, host and guest exchange roles. **And more: the guest possesses the host's virtue**—when some dharma is guest, it is not "secondary, incomplete"; it still possesses the full virtue of the host.

Fazang in the *Record of Investigations* emphasizes that the heart of this gate is "host-and-guest connected dependent origination": host and guest are not two independent things but two positions in the same dependent-origination structure. The positions can exchange; the structure does not change. Fazang, in his later *new ten gates*, replaced Zhiyan's old "Gate of Mind-Only Perfect Turning" with this "Gate of Host-and-Guest Perfect Clarity and Complete Virtue." The doctrinal motivation for the substitution is profound—"mind-only" emphasizes the central position of the cognizing subject (mind); "host-and-guest" explicitly states that there is no fixed center, and every dharma can be the center. **This is a critical move within Huayan from "mind-centered" toward "dharmadhātu-centered."**

**The reversal of time and space.** Rejoicing is not the envy-structure of "he is high and I am low" (he did something I could not do, so I share his joy with shame); it is the interfusing structure of "host and guest mutually embrace." When you rejoice in another's merit, he is host and you are guest; but the guest possesses the host's virtue—you do not merely "share his joy," your one thought of rejoicing itself already possesses the full virtue of that merit.

Fazang, in the *Treatise on the Five Doctrines*, fascicle 2, discussing "faith-fulfillment attaining buddhahood," explicitly states: "Because of the attainment through faith, this is the practice-buddha, not the position-buddha."[^28] The practice-buddha is not the attainment of buddhahood in terms of stages; it is the practice that already possesses the buddha's virtue. The single thought of rejoicing is the practice-buddha in its affective manifestation—your one thought of rejoicing is already the buddha's rejoicing (because the buddha universally rejoices in the merit of all beings). Though your position is that of an ordinary being, your practice is already that of a buddha. **In the moment of rejoicing, the practice-buddha has been accomplished.**

**Realizing this in practice.** The reader may perform a simple inner experiment. The next time you hear of another's good deed, watch your first inner reaction. Is it jealousy? Indifference? Genuine joy?

Jealousy springs from "host-and-guest opposition"—you treat the other as a competitor, and his merit seems to diminish your position. Indifference springs from "host-and-guest isolation"—you treat the other as someone unrelated, and his merit is nothing to you. Genuine rejoicing springs from "host-and-guest mutual embrace"—you treat the other as another you within the same dharma-realm, and his merit is your merit.

**These three reactions correspond to three entirely different inner structures**. Jealousy corresponds to "separated individualism," indifference to "disengaged pluralism," rejoicing to "perfect-teaching host-and-guest mutuality." Moving from jealousy to indifference is easy (you only have to move the other out of your circle of concern); moving from indifference to rejoicing is hard (you have to take the other into "another self within the same dharma-realm"). Rejoicing is not an emotional training; it is an inner structural shift—from an individualist structure to a host-and-guest-mutual structure.

A deeper observation: **in the moment of genuine rejoicing, the duality of "I" and "other" momentarily dissolves in this one thought**. You are both him (rejoicing in his merit = you already possess his merit) and still you (this one thought occurs in your own continuum of body and mind). This simultaneously-both-and-still structure is the literal realization of host-and-guest perfect clarity.

**Horizontal leakage.** When this one act of rejoicing is true rejoicing, this one thought is itself **simultaneously mutually corresponding** (the physical instant of rejoicing gathers all the merit the other has practiced), **broad-and-narrow sovereign** (your one narrow thought broadly covers his immeasurable merit), **mutually identical** (the one rejoicing and the merit rejoiced in are mutually identical), **Indra's net** (your one thought of rejoicing reflects all jewels of the dharma-realm's rejoicing). Host-and-guest perfect clarity is the central structure of this event; the other nine gates are nine other angles on the same event.

**Pointing-at-the-moon phrase.**

> **In the moment of rejoicing, the practice-buddha has been accomplished.**
### 3.6 Vow 6 Requesting the Turning of the Dharma-Wheel × The Gate of Indra's Net Realm

**Practice layer.**

Kneel and request the buddhas, the saṅgha, and good spiritual friends to expound the Dharma. Or in your mind raise gratitude toward all good spiritual friends who have ever taught you, and request that they continue to turn the Dharma-wheel. Or while reading sūtras and treatises, raise the inner gesture of "requesting the turning of the wheel." Requesting the Dharma is not limited to outward verbal request; the deeper layer is the inner posture of requesting.

**The window opens.**

The core teaching of the Indra's Net Realm Gate is the metaphor of Indra's palace-net: every knot in the net of his palace bears a jewel, and each jewel reflects the images of all other jewels; and within the "image of all other jewels" reflected in any one jewel, the image of the entire net is again reflected. "The reflections multiply layer upon layer, accomplishing endless endlessness" (Zhiyan, *One-Vehicle Ten Profound Gates of Huayan*)[^29]. Fazang reaffirms this structure in *Elaborated Exegesis* and renames it "Indra's Net Dharma-Realm Gate"[^30].

Install this teaching onto requesting the Dharma: when you request the Buddha to expound the Dharma at your seat, this request is not isolated—it is simultaneously reflected in every jewel of Indra's net. Every jewel is at the same time requesting, listening, and turning the Dharma. And within the image of "other jewels requesting the Dharma" reflected in each jewel, the entire net's requesting is again reflected. Endless layers without end.

A clarification of particular importance is needed here: Indra's net is not animism (each jewel possesses an independent consciousness that is requesting); it is not the holographic-universe theory (each jewel is a miniature of the whole); it is not quantum entanglement (instantaneous correlation between two particles). S8-P06 will address these cross-disciplinary failure modes in full; this paper §IV gives the structural statement, and we do not unfold it here. **Indra's net is a description of a relational structure: it says "every position in the net is non-isolated," not "every position possesses independent consciousness."**

**Spatiotemporal inversion.**

Requesting the Dharma is not the isolated action of "I myself"; it is one node in the dharma-realm's resonance. When your one thought of requesting the Dharma is established, this very thought is the dharma-realm requesting itself—not "the individual requesting the Buddha," but "the dharma-realm requesting one of its own facets through your position."

This inversion has very significant meaning. It dissolves a common cultivation misunderstanding: "I request the Dharma because I have need." Under the structure of Indra's net, requesting the Dharma is not because "you have need," but because "the dharma-realm is requesting one of its facets through your position." Your "need" is a node in the dharma-realm's resonance, not an individual hunger.

**Cultivation realization.**

The reader can perform this introspective experiment. Next time you request the Dharma (whether inward request or outward verbal request), try to shift the focus from "I am requesting" to "the event of requesting the Dharma is occurring."

The difference between the two foci is very subtle but fundamental. "I am requesting" is an individual request, with focus on the position "I." "The event of requesting the Dharma is occurring" is an event-centered request, with focus on the requesting itself—and the requesting is an event simultaneously occurring on every jewel of Indra's net; you are merely one of innumerable jewels.

When the focus shifts from "I" to "event," the inner grammar of requesting changes from "hunger" to "resonance." Your one thought of requesting is no longer a solitary cry, but the event of, when one place in Indra's net begins to glow, all other jewels simultaneously glowing. Every spiritual friend's response is not because they "heard your individual request," but because the dharma-realm is structurally already responding to itself in this configuration.

**Horizontal leakage.**

When this one thought of requesting is an Indra's-net request, this one thought is itself **simultaneously mutually corresponding** (the physical instant of "one place requests, all directions co-request" is established at once), **broad-and-narrow sovereign** (your one narrow thought broadly covers the dharma-realm's request), **mutually identical** (the requester and the requested are mutually identical), **host-and-guest perfect clarity** (when you are host the buddhas and friends are guests, when they are host you are guest). Indra's net is the central structure of this event; the other nine gates are nine other angles on the same event.

**Pointing-at-the-moon phrase.**

> **Requesting the Dharma is the dharma-realm's resonance, not the individual's plea.**

### 3.7 Vow 7 Requesting the Buddhas to Remain in the World × The Gate of Subtle Containment and Establishment

**Practice layer.**

Request the buddhas, bodhisattvas, śrāvakas, pratyekabuddhas, and all spiritual friends not to enter parinirvāṇa, but to remain in the world for the sake of sentient beings. Or in your mind ask all spiritual friends who have ever guided you to "please continue to be present for me." Or in moments of reading the sūtras and treatises, raise the gesture of "requesting the buddhas to remain in the world."

**The window opens.**

The core teaching of the Subtle Containment and Establishment Gate is: in any one fine, subtle position, all the meanings of the dharma-realm are completely contained, and these meanings are mutually established without obstruction. The metaphor in Chengguan's *Huayan jīn shīzi zhāng yún jiān lèi jiě* is: "Gold and the lion: now hidden, now manifest; now one, now many. Determinately pure, determinately mixed; with power, without power. Identical here, identical there; host and guest mutually radiant. Principle and matter simultaneously appearing, all completely contained, without obstructing the establishment of the subtle"[^31]. The point of this gate is "in the most fine and subtle, the most complete is contained."

Install this teaching onto requesting the buddhas to remain in the world: when you request the buddhas to remain, this request is not a request for "physical longevity"; it is a request that "the buddhas' presence be subtly contained in every fine and subtle position." Every breath of yours, every glance, every thought—if it can be a position where the buddhas' presence is contained, then the buddhas have already remained in the world, in the most subtle way.

**Spatiotemporal inversion.**

The conventional understanding of "requesting the buddhas to remain in the world" is: I request the buddhas not to leave, so that they continue to exist in the visible world. The inversion under the gate of subtle containment is: the buddhas' "remaining in the world" is not a function of physical presence; it is a function of being subtly contained in every fine and subtle position of the practitioner. **As long as one practitioner can have the buddhas' presence subtly contained in their breath and glance, the buddhas have not entered parinirvāṇa.**

This inversion solves a very stubborn problem: the buddhas have already entered parinirvāṇa more than two thousand years ago; how do we still "request them to remain"? Under the gate of subtle containment, the buddhas' "remaining" never depended on physical longevity. The buddhas' remaining is a function of "subtle containment in the practitioner's every position."

**Cultivation realization.**

The reader can perform this experiment. Choose one moment of daily life that you usually consider "fine and trivial"—the moment of taking a sip of water, the moment of opening a door, the moment of looking at a stranger across the street. At this moment, ask: "Is the buddhas' presence subtly contained in this position?"

If your answer is yes, then in this moment the buddhas have remained in the world. If your answer is no, that is fine—the next sip of water, the next door, the next glance is another opportunity. The gate of subtle containment teaches you: "remaining in the world" is not a once-and-for-all event; it is a continuous, fine-grained, never-ending containment. The buddhas remain in the world precisely in the trivial moments you would never have considered cultivation.

**Horizontal leakage.**

When this one act of requesting the buddhas to remain is a subtle-containment request, it is itself **simultaneously mutually corresponding** (the buddhas' remaining contained in every fine subtle position is the simultaneity of one and all), **Indra's net** (each subtle position reflects the buddhas' remaining in all other subtle positions), **mutually identical** (the practitioner and the buddhas remain mutually identical in this subtle position), **host-and-guest perfect clarity** (in this subtle position, the practitioner is host with the buddhas as guests, the buddhas as host with the practitioner as guest). Subtle containment is the central structure of this event; the other nine gates are the same event observed from nine other angles.

**Pointing-at-the-moon phrase.**

> **The buddhas remain in the world in the fine subtle position; not in physical longevity.**

### 3.8 Vow 8 Constant Following and Learning from the Buddha × The Gate of One-and-Many Mutual Containment Without Identity

**Practice layer.**

Constantly take Śākyamuni Buddha as a model: from his practice in past lives as a bodhisattva, to his attainment of buddhahood under the Bodhi tree, to his forty-nine years of teaching, to his entering parinirvāṇa—every step of his is your model. Constantly follow and learn. Or in your mind, in any decision of daily life, ask: "What would the Buddha do?" and use that as the guide.

**The window opens.**

The core teaching of the One-and-Many Mutual Containment Without Identity Gate is: one and many mutually contain, but neither dissolves into the other. Zhiyan's *One-Vehicle Ten Profound Gates*: "Gold and lion mutually contain, accomplishing the establishment of one and many without obstruction. Within this, principle and matter each are different. Either one or many, each remains in its own position"[^32]. The point of this gate is "mutual containment without dissolving identity."

Install this teaching onto constant learning from the Buddha: when you take the Buddha as your model, the relationship of "you" and "the Buddha" is not a relationship of dissolution (you become the Buddha and lose yourself), it is also not a relationship of separation (you and the Buddha forever remain two unrelated individuals). It is a relationship of mutual containment without identity—**the Buddha is contained within you (so you can learn from him), and you are contained within the Buddha (so your learning is not solitary), but neither becomes the other**.

**Spatiotemporal inversion.**

The conventional understanding of "constant learning from the Buddha" is: I take the Buddha as a distant standard, and progressively approach this standard through cultivation. The inversion under the gate of one-and-many mutual containment is: the Buddha and you are not two beings on a "distance-and-approach" axis. **The Buddha is already contained within you, in this very instant; your every learning is the Buddha already in you operating. And you are also already contained within the Buddha; your every learning is also you in the Buddha being shaped.**

This inversion solves a common cultivation discouragement: "the Buddha is so far, I am so close to where I am, when can I ever catch up?" Under the gate of one-and-many mutual containment, the question of "catching up" does not arise. There is no one-dimensional axis on which to measure distance. There is only the structure of "the Buddha containing you and you containing the Buddha," and within this structure your every learning is at once the Buddha's accomplishment and your accomplishment.

**Cultivation realization.**

The reader can perform this experiment. Next time you face a decision in daily life and ask "what would the Buddha do," observe two layers carefully.

Layer one: the Buddha's wisdom is providing the answer (the Buddha contained within you is operating).

Layer two: your asking this question is itself the Buddha's wisdom in operation (you contained within the Buddha is being shaped).

The two layers are simultaneously present, and neither dissolves the other. The Buddha is operating within you, but you remain you. You are being shaped within the Buddha, but the Buddha remains the Buddha. This simultaneous double layering is the literal realization of one-and-many mutual containment without identity.

**Horizontal leakage.**

When this one act of constant learning is a mutual-containment-without-identity learning, it is itself **simultaneously mutually corresponding** (the Buddha's operation within you and your being shaped within the Buddha establish at the same instant), **mutually identical** (the practitioner and the Buddha are mutually identical in this learning), **host-and-guest perfect clarity** (in this learning the Buddha is host with you as guest, you are host with the Buddha as guest), **Indra's net** (this learning reflects in all other practitioners simultaneously learning from the Buddha, and the Buddha simultaneously containing all practitioners). One-and-many mutual containment is the central structure of this event; the other nine gates are nine other angles on the same event.

**Pointing-at-the-moon phrase.**

> **You learn from the Buddha within yourself, and you are being shaped within the Buddha—both are simultaneous, and neither dissolves the other.**

### 3.9 Vow 9 Constantly Accommodating Sentient Beings × The Gate of Dependence on Phenomena to Reveal the Dharma

**Practice layer.**

Constantly accommodate sentient beings—not as the parent accommodates the child (with the parent always knowing better), but as Samantabhadra accommodates: serving sentient beings "as one serves one's own father and mother, as one serves one's teacher, the arhats, and the Tathāgata, all equally without distinction." Heal the sick, guide the lost, accompany the lonely, comfort the suffering. Or in your mind raise the inner gesture of "accommodating all sentient beings without distinction."

**The window opens.**

The core teaching of the Dependence on Phenomena to Reveal the Dharma Gate is: the Dharma is not revealed apart from phenomena; the Dharma is revealed through specific phenomena. Zhiyan's *One-Vehicle Ten Profound Gates*: "Speaking of dependence on phenomena: as in the sūtra raising the matter of the gold-colored realm, this immediately reveals the Dharma of original arising in the actual edge"[^33]. The point of this gate is "no specific phenomenon, no Dharma."

Install this teaching onto accommodating sentient beings: when you accommodate sentient beings, you are not accommodating an abstract category of "sentient beings"; you are accommodating this particular person in front of you, in their particular suffering, in their particular need. **The Dharma is revealed through this particular accommodation, not despite it. If you bypass the particular and try to access the universal directly, you have lost the Dharma.**

**Spatiotemporal inversion.**

The conventional understanding of "accommodating sentient beings" is: I have wisdom and compassion, and I distribute them to sentient beings. The inversion under the gate of dependence on phenomena is: **sentient beings are the phenomena through which the Dharma reveals itself to me**. Without this particular sentient being in front of me, the Dharma has no occasion to reveal one of its facets. Sentient beings are not the recipients of my compassion; they are the conditions for the Dharma's self-revelation.

This inversion is structurally crucial. It dissolves a subtle pride that haunts even sincere bodhisattva practice: "I am the giver, they are the receivers." Under the gate of dependence on phenomena, the relationship is reversed—**they are the conditions under which the Dharma reveals itself, and I am the one being revealed to**. My accommodation is not a gift to them; it is the Dharma using their particular existence to teach me one of its facets.

**Cultivation realization.**

The reader can perform this experiment. Next time you encounter a sentient being you would normally consider "in need of help"—a beggar on the street, a confused child, an angry colleague—try to shift the framing from "this person needs my help" to "this person is the phenomenon through which the Dharma is revealing one of its facets to me."

Under the second framing, your accommodation does not vanish—you still help, still listen, still offer. But the inner posture changes radically. You are no longer the donor; you are the student. The beggar is your teacher in giving without attachment. The confused child is your teacher in patience. The angry colleague is your teacher in equanimity. **Every accommodation of sentient beings is also a Dharma-teaching delivered to you by sentient beings**—and this is exactly what the gate of dependence on phenomena teaches.

**Horizontal leakage.**

When this one act of accommodation is a dependence-on-phenomena accommodation, it is itself **simultaneously mutually corresponding** (the giving and the receiving are simultaneously accomplished), **broad-and-narrow sovereign** (this one specific person broadly contains the entire dharma-realm of suffering beings), **mutually identical** (giver and receiver are mutually identical), **host-and-guest perfect clarity** (when you are host the sentient being is guest, when the sentient being is host you are guest, both are simultaneously accomplished). Dependence on phenomena is the central structure of this event; the other nine gates are nine other angles on the same event.

**Pointing-at-the-moon phrase.**

> **Sentient beings are not the recipients of compassion; they are the phenomena through which the Dharma reveals itself.**

### 3.10 Vow 10 Universal Dedication × The Gate of Ten-Time Distinct Establishment

**Practice layer.**

Take all the merit you have accumulated through all the previous nine vows and dedicate it universally—to all sentient beings, to the supreme bodhi, to the dharma-realm. The dedication is "to the limit of empty space, to the exhaustion of the realm of beings, to the exhaustion of the karma of beings, to the exhaustion of the afflictions of beings; my dedication is without limit. Thought after thought succeeding without interruption, in body, speech, and mind, without weariness"[^36].

**The window opens.**

The core teaching of the Ten-Time Distinct Establishment Gate is: the three times (past, present, future) each contain the three times, accomplishing the nine times; the nine times become one, accomplishing the ten times. Each time is distinct (not collapsed into one another), and yet each contains the totality. Zhiyan: "The past speaks of past… The three times become one thought; combined with the previous nine, they make ten times"[^34]. And: "By the power of dependent arising, they are mutually identical and mutually entering, yet do not lose the three times. Like the five fingers becoming a fist without losing the fingers. The ten times though simultaneous do not lose the ten times"[^35].

Install this teaching onto universal dedication: when you dedicate the merit, the dedication is not occurring "now and only now"; it is occurring across the ten times simultaneously. The dedication you make in this present thought is also being made in all your past lives' past, present, and future, in this present life's past, present, and future, and in all your future lives' past, present, and future. **One thought of dedication = ten thoughts of dedication across the ten times.**

**Spatiotemporal inversion.**

The conventional understanding of dedication is: I take the merit accumulated up to this point and assign it forward. The inversion under the gate of ten-time distinct establishment is: **dedication is not forward-directed; it is omnidirectional across the ten times**. Your present dedication retroactively dedicates all merit you have accumulated in past lives. It also pre-dedicates all merit you will accumulate in future lives. And it dedicates the merit being accumulated in the parallel "past, present, future" of every other time-position.

This inversion solves a deep problem in dedication practice: "but I have only accumulated a tiny amount of merit; what good does it do to dedicate so little?" Under the gate of ten-time distinct establishment, "the amount you have accumulated up to this point" is a misframing. **Your one thought of dedication activates the merit of all ten times simultaneously**—and this merit is not "yours" in the individualist sense, it is the merit of the dharma-realm operating through your position.

**Cultivation realization.**

The reader can perform this experiment. At the end of any cultivation session—reading sūtras, meditation, mantra recitation, even something as simple as helping another person—pause and make a deliberate dedication. As you make the dedication, do not think "I dedicate this small merit"; think "this dedication is occurring in the past, present, and future of past lives, this life, and future lives, all simultaneously."

You do not need to "make this true" through visualization. Under the structure of the ten-time distinct establishment gate, this is already the case; your visualization is merely the recognition of what is already structurally true. The dedication of one thought is the dedication of ten times. This is not a magnification; it is the literal structure of the dedication event.

**Horizontal leakage.**

When this one act of dedication is a ten-time dedication, it is itself **simultaneously mutually corresponding** (ten times accomplish at the same instant), **mutually identical** (each time is mutually identical with the others), **mutually contained** (one time contains the other nine, and is contained by them), **Indra's net** (this dedication reflects in all dedications across all times of all practitioners). Ten-time distinct establishment is the central structure of this event; the other nine gates are nine other angles on the same event.

**Pointing-at-the-moon phrase.**

> **One thought of dedication is the dedication of ten times.**

### 3.11 Pivot Demonstrations: Two Directions of Deeper Performance

We have now completed the ten vow-gate pairs. Each pair has primary correspondence with its assigned gate and horizontal leakage with the other nine gates. The argument so far has demonstrated that **each vow contains all ten gates** (because each pair's "horizontal leakage" block names the other nine gates' presence in the act). What §III.0 promised was the symmetric demonstration: each gate contains all ten vows. This subsection performs both demonstrations explicitly, on the strength of Chengguan's totality-differentiation pivot.

Two demonstrations follow. The first takes one gate—the gate of simultaneous mutual correspondence, which is the totality among the ten gates—and shows how it contains all ten vows. The second takes one vow—Vow 1, "homage to all buddhas," which is the totality among the ten vows—and shows how it contains all ten gates. The two demonstrations are not arbitrary; they are determined by the totality-differentiation pivot. The two totalities (totality-gate and totality-vow) are the natural starting points for the symmetric exhibition.

#### Demonstration A: The Gate of Simultaneous Mutual Correspondence Contains All Ten Vows

The gate of simultaneous mutual correspondence is the totality among the ten gates. As Chengguan explicitly states, "within the gate of simultaneity, the lower nine gates are contained." We now ask: within the gate of simultaneity, are the ten vows also contained? The answer must be yes—because if simultaneity is the totality and the ten vows are practical actualizations of the dharma-realm, then by structural symmetry the ten vows must also be contained within simultaneity.

Concretely: the gate of simultaneous mutual correspondence says that all dharmas are simultaneously mutually corresponding, without before and after, without obstruction. When this teaching is installed onto the totality of the ten vows, the result is: **all ten vows are simultaneously mutually corresponding in any one act of cultivation**. When the practitioner makes one prostration to the buddhas, this one prostration is simultaneously: (1) homage to all buddhas, (2) praise of the Tathāgata (the body's prostration is itself a praise), (3) abundant offering (the body's prostration offers the practitioner's whole being), (4) repentance of karmic obstacles (the body's prostration humbles the karmic obstacles), (5) rejoicing in merit (the body's prostration rejoices in the buddhas' awakening), (6) requesting the Dharma-wheel (the body's prostration is itself a request that the Dharma continue to turn), (7) requesting the buddhas to remain (the body's prostration asks the buddhas not to leave), (8) constant learning from the Buddha (the body's prostration follows the model of all bodhisattvas who have prostrated), (9) accommodating sentient beings (the body's prostration includes all beings in this single act), (10) universal dedication (the body's prostration is dedicated to all beings).

**These ten are not "ten meanings sequentially read into one prostration"; they are simultaneously present in the one prostration.** This simultaneous presence is the literal application of the gate of simultaneous mutual correspondence to the ten vows.

The reader may object: but is this not just rhetorical multiplication? Are these ten "really" present, or am I just decorating one act with ten labels? The answer is: under the gate of simultaneous mutual correspondence, the question "really present or just labeled" is itself misframed. **In the perfect-teaching reading, "present" means "structurally instantiated by the act," and the prostration structurally instantiates all ten vows in their relational totality.** If you read "present" in the separative sense (each vow as a distinct thing that must be located in the act), you have already left the perfect-teaching framework.

#### Demonstration B: Homage to All Buddhas Contains All Ten Gates

Homage to all buddhas is the totality among the ten vows. (The full argument for why is reserved for S8-P10 §VI; here we accept the structural fact.) We now ask: within homage to all buddhas, are the ten gates also contained? The answer must be yes—by the same symmetry as Demonstration A.

Concretely: homage to all buddhas means making prostrations and offerings to "all buddhas of past, present, and future, throughout the ten directions, exhausting the limits of empty space, of the dharma-realm." When this one act is performed in the perfect-teaching framework, the ten gates are simultaneously instantiated:

(1) **Simultaneous mutual correspondence**: the homage and the buddhas correspond at the same instant; there is no temporal gap between "I make homage" and "the buddhas receive homage."

(2) **Broad-and-narrow sovereign**: the practitioner's narrow body is broad enough to make homage to all buddhas of the ten directions and three times.

(3) **Mutually identical**: the act of making homage and the buddhas being homaged are mutually identical—the homage is the buddhas' presence operating through the practitioner.

(4) **Hidden-and-manifest mutually accomplished**: the homage is manifest (the body prostrates) and hidden (the inner gesture is invisible); both are mutually accomplished.

(5) **Host-and-guest perfect clarity**: in this homage, the practitioner is host with the buddhas as guests, the buddhas are host with the practitioner as guest; both simultaneously.

(6) **Indra's net**: this one homage reflects in all homages being made by all practitioners across all dharma-realms.

(7) **Subtle containment and establishment**: in the most subtle position of the homage (the slight bow of the head, the touch of the forehead to the ground), the entire dharma-realm is subtly contained.

(8) **One-and-many mutual containment without identity**: the one homage and the many buddhas mutually contain, neither dissolving into the other.

(9) **Dependence on phenomena to reveal the Dharma**: this one specific phenomenal act of homage reveals the Dharma of "the practitioner and the buddhas being mutually present."

(10) **Ten-time distinct establishment**: this one homage in the present moment is also a homage in past lives' past-present-future, this life's past-present-future, and future lives' past-present-future.

**All ten gates are simultaneously instantiated in the one act of homage to all buddhas.** This is not metaphor; it is the literal structure of the act when performed in the perfect-teaching framework.

#### Closing of §III: The Two-Way Containment Is Now Demonstrated

We have now shown both directions: each vow contains all ten gates (via §3.1–§3.10's horizontal leakage blocks), and each totality (gate or vow) contains all ten of the other side (via §3.11's two demonstrations). The full two-way containment is structurally complete.

**This is what it means to call P11 a meta-self-demonstration paper.** P11's content (the ten vow-gate correspondences and their two-way containment) is itself an instantiation of the simultaneous-mutual-correspondence gate—because P11's structure is one in which each section contains all other sections, and all sections are simultaneously corresponding to one another. The paper performs what it teaches.

**A failure point must be marked here.** If the reader finishes §III and feels "I now understand the two-way containment intellectually, but I cannot perform it in practice," that is a normal and expected response. Two-way containment is not an intellectual achievement; it is a structural fact about the perfect-teaching reading. The intellectual recognition is the first step; the cultivation operationalization (which §3.6.4 in each pair has been pointing to) is the second step. The two steps are different in kind; the first does not automatically produce the second.

The reader should not be discouraged by this. The full operational unfolding of each pair is the work of the corresponding S8-P0X paper (P01–P10), where each pair receives its own complete treatment. P11 is the meta-paper; it provides the structural map. The cultivation work is done one pair at a time, in the corresponding individual papers and—more importantly—in the daily practice they direct.

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Convergence: The Fundamental Opposition of Two Readings

### 4.1 From CT Scan to Entanglement: The Difference in Layers Between Two Metaphors

The simplest cross-disciplinary metaphor is the CT scan: the ten windows function like ten axial CT slices, taking multi-axis cuts of a single object and reconstructing a three-dimensional image in the computer. This metaphor has three virtues: it is intuitive (everyone understands the concept of scanning), it is structurally correct (multi-axis observation), and the irreducibility statement is easy to make (the CT slice is obviously not the dharma-realm).

But the CT scan metaphor has one fundamental failure point: it presupposes that "object" and "scanner" are separated. The CT scanner is outside the patient, the patient is inside the CT scanner—the scanner and the scanned are two separate positions, and the scanning act establishes a relation between them. But the object that the Ten Profound Gates describe (the dharma-realm) **contains the scanner itself**. You do not stand outside the dharma-realm to observe it; you are one position within the dharma-realm observing the dharma-realm itself. **The CT scan metaphor cannot deliver this layer.**

Therefore this section needs a deeper metaphor—one that can let the structure of "separation is illusion" come to view. That metaphor is quantum entanglement. But quantum entanglement has two readings, and the difference between them is fundamental. The core work of this section is to mark this distinction precisely.

### 4.2 The Two Readings of Quantum Entanglement

In quantum physics there is a famous phenomenon: two particles that have been entangled, regardless of how far apart they are physically (in theory, on opposite ends of the universe), will exhibit a correlation in measurement that appears to be "instantaneous." This phenomenon was called "spooky action at a distance" (*spukhafte Fernwirkung*) in the Einstein-Podolsky-Rosen thought experiment, and has been verified repeatedly in subsequent Bell-inequality experiments.

For this phenomenon there are two utterly different readings.

**Reading One (the popular-science reading).** "Physics has discovered nonlocal phenomena, so the Indra's net that Buddhism spoke of two thousand years ago is correct." The hidden structure of this reading is that "Buddhism needs scientific verification"—Huayan teachings are treated as "ancient intuitions waiting for science to catch up." The implicit message is: now that we have experimental evidence of quantum entanglement, we can confidently say that Indra's net is not superstition, it is science.

**Pointing-at-the-Moon explicitly rejects this reading.** The reason is extremely simple: physics models will be revised, Indra's net will not. If today we use quantum entanglement to prove Indra's net, and tomorrow physics discovers that quantum mechanics needs revision (which has happened innumerable times in the history of physics), should Indra's net also be revised? Of course not—because Indra's net has never depended on physics models for its validity. Its legitimacy comes from the Huayan sūtra and the patriarchs' commentaries, not from any physics discovery.

Furthermore, this reading has a deeper problem: it places Buddhism in the position of "needing to be verified." But Buddhism's legitimacy has never been "experimental verification"—it is the mutual calibration of three sources: cultivation experience, scriptural authority, and patriarchal exposition. Quantum entanglement experimental results have no direct relationship with any of these three.

**Reading Two (the Pointing-at-the-Moon reading).** "Quantum entanglement startles physicists precisely because they have **not yet relinquished the premise of 'separation.'** Once that premise is released, entanglement is no longer a miracle. Buddhism relinquished this premise two thousand years ago, so for Buddhism nothing needs to be verified—it is physics that is now catching up, discovering the place where Buddhism has long been standing."

**The key difference between the two readings**: Reading One says "physics proves Buddhism"; Reading Two says "Buddhism observes why physics is startled." Pointing-at-the-Moon takes the second.

The internal logic of Reading Two is as follows. Classical physics is built on the premise of *locality*—any interaction between two objects must be transmitted via some physical carrier (particle, field, wave) at a finite speed (at most the speed of light). This premise is itself the concretization of "separation"—it presupposes that the world is composed of independent separate individuals, and the relations among individuals must be established by "transmission across distance."

Quantum entanglement breaks this premise. It shows that two entangled particles are not "first existing separately, then establishing a correlation through some transmission," but rather "originally one quantum system whose two positions we have mistakenly identified as two independent objects." On this deeper level, "spooky action at a distance" is not "at a distance" at all—because there is no "distance" between "two things" that needs to be "crossed." The so-called "two things" are originally one thing showing itself in two positions.

Physicists are startled because their entire theoretical framework is built on the premise of "separation," and that premise is now being broken by the phenomenon itself. **Buddhism is not startled, because Buddhism never built this premise to begin with.** The Indra's net of Huayan has never been "the strange correlation among many independent jewels"; it is "the simultaneous self-display of one net at different positions." There is no "distance" between "jewels" needing to be "crossed"—because there are no truly separate "jewels."

**This structural statement: "separation is illusion, therefore no transmission is needed"**—is precisely the core of Indra's net. It is incidentally touched at the physical level in the form of quantum entanglement, but its true domain is the relational structure of the dharma-realm in totality, far exceeding the physical level.

### 4.3 Folding Back to Yogācāra: The Seer-Portion and the Seen-Portion Were Never Two

There is a deeper closure on this structure: folding it back from the physics layer into the Yogācāra seer-and-seen-portion structure.

Yogācāra has an extremely precise description of "perception." When you see a thing, it is not that "the thing outside enters your eye-consciousness." It is that your eye-consciousness internally manifests two parts—the *seen-portion* (*nimitta-bhāga* 相分, the side that is seen) and the *seer-portion* (*darśana-bhāga* 見分, the side that sees). Both seen-portion and seer-portion are internal structures of the same consciousness. **External objects never truly enter perception, because external objects are originally the seen-portion of consciousness itself.**

Place this Yogācāra structure side by side with quantum entanglement:

| Quantum entanglement layer | Yogācāra seer-seen layer |
|---|---|
| The "distance" between two particles is illusion | The "distance" between you and external objects is illusion |
| Because they are originally one | Because seer- and seen-portions are originally one consciousness |
| So "instantaneous response" is not response | So "seeing" is not reception |
| It is the simultaneous self-display of one thing | It is the simultaneous self-display of one consciousness |

**Both layers close on the same structural statement: "separation is illusion, therefore no transmission is needed."**

There is an extremely precise illustration: "you see only what you want to see, you hear only what you want to hear." This is not a rhetorical observation (people have biases, so they pay attention only to interesting things). It is the literal statement of Yogācāra on the structure of perception. **What you see is originally the seen-portion of your own consciousness—it has never been "the thing outside," it is your own consciousness's internal structure.** So between "what you want to see" and "what you see" there is no real separation—the former is the inner premise of the latter, the latter is the outward self-display of the former.

And this Yogācāra structure is precisely the core structure of Indra's net: **there is no real separation between "outside" and "inside."** Every position in the dharma-realm is the dharma-realm's own internal structure, not something "outside the dharma-realm."

**The final closing statement.** "Separation is illusion, therefore no transmission is needed"—this single sentence is the true reading of quantum entanglement, the literal statement of Yogācāra's seer-and-seen-portion structure, and the core structure of Indra's net. **The three are not "analogies"; they are the same structure displaying itself simultaneously at three layers.**

### 4.4 Three-Layer Failure Points

Per the series convention, cross-disciplinary convergence must explicitly mark three layers of failure points.

**Similarity.** Two independent cognitive paths to the structure of "separation is illusion." Physics has incidentally touched this structure at the subatomic scale through the form of quantum entanglement; Buddhism has systematically unfolded this structure at the levels of cultivation and doctrine through the form of Indra's net. The similarity between the two paths is the structural statement itself: "separation is illusion, therefore no transmission is needed."

**Fundamental difference.** Physics's object is material entities (particles, fields, energy); Buddhism's object is the dharma-realm in totality, including the cognizer itself. Physicists observe phenomena from the position of a separate observer; Buddhist practitioners themselves are positions within the dharma-realm observing the dharma-realm itself. **This difference is fundamental at the epistemological level.** Physics's "separation is illusion" is "separation between material entities is illusion"; Buddhism's "separation is illusion" is "separation across the entire seer-seen structure is illusion, including the separation between observer and observed." The latter is one layer deeper than the former.

**Irreducibility statement.** This section's use of quantum entanglement as a metaphor is not for the purpose of "using physics to prove Buddhism." Physics models will be updated (quantum mechanics itself has been revised many times in the past century); Indra's net will not. If one day physicists discover that quantum entanglement needs to be replaced by a deeper theory, Indra's net will not be shaken—because Indra's net's legitimacy has never depended on quantum entanglement. **This section's purpose in using quantum entanglement is the opposite—to let the reader see a phenomenon that startles physicists themselves, and through that startle, to display the artificiality of the "separation premise."** Physicists are startled because their premise needs to be broken; Buddhism is not startled, because Buddhism never built this premise.

## V. Middle-Way Corrections

This section lists five correction items. The first four address potential misreadings of the paper's core argument; the fifth addresses a structural danger specific to meta-level papers.

### 5.1 ❌1 The Mechanized Reading of Correspondence (Pedagogical Danger #13, Main Battlefield)

**Potential misreading.** Reading "ten gates corresponding to ten vows" as an "vow = gate" equation. For example: "Homage to the buddhas *is* the gate of simultaneous mutual correspondence; abundant offerings *is* the gate of mutual identity…" This reading degrades correspondence into identity.

**Correction.** Correspondence is not "vow = gate" as an equation; it is the intermeshing of the cultivation face and the discursive face. "Intermeshing" is not "merging"—the vow remains an action (concrete movements the bodhisattva makes at the level of body and consciousness), the gate remains a doctrine (the angle from which the Huayan patriarchs observe the dharma-realm structure). The position of the correspondence is: the inner structure of each vow on the cultivation face is precisely what some gate describes as the dharma-realm structure on the discursive face. The two are two self-displays of the same perfect-teaching structure, not two names for the same thing.

More precisely: vow is "action," gate is "angle of observation," and correspondence is "the inner structure of the action is precisely captured by some angle of observation." The action does not become the angle of observation; the angle of observation does not become the action—they work at different levels but point to the same object.

### 5.2 ❌2 Reading the Ten Gates as "Cultivation Buttons"

**Potential misreading.** Once you understand the doctrine of a particular gate, the corresponding vow will automatically "upgrade." For example: "I have read the explanation of the gate of simultaneous mutual correspondence, so my homage to the buddhas has now been upgraded to the dharma-realm level."

**Correction.** The Profound Gates are *lenses*, not *buttons*. Understanding the doctrine of a particular gate will not automatically upgrade the corresponding vow; understanding only lets you see a structure you previously could not see. **Upgrade happens in the body's repeated practice, not in the mind's conceptual understanding.**

This is an extremely important distinction. In the Western academic tradition, "understanding" is often treated as a kind of endpoint—once you understand a concept, you can begin to apply it. But in Buddhist cultivation, "understanding" is only the entry point, not the endpoint. After you understand a gate's doctrine, you need to **practice the inner structural transformation repeatedly**—from linear structure to perfect-fusion structure—in your daily acts of homage, praise, offering, repentance, rejoicing, requesting, requesting-to-remain, learning, accommodation, and dedication. This practice may continue for many years, and is not linear "ever-improving"; it advances and recedes, until at certain instants the structure flashes forth.

**This is the meta-level paper's greatest danger.** After reading, the reader may think "I get it, I have upgraded"—this is illusion. After reading this paper you have seen the *shape* of the ten windows, not their *substance*. Shape belongs to the textual layer; substance belongs to the cultivation layer. **Seeing the shape is not the same as entering the substance.** This paper takes you to the door; what is inside the door must be walked into by yourself in daily practice.

### 5.3 ❌3 Reading This Paper as a "Practice Manual" Rather Than an Academic Paper

**Potential misreading.** This paper looks like a practice manual—it has ten pairs of "action × window × cultivation realization" concretely unfolded, each pair making the reader feel "this is something I can do step by step." Therefore this paper is an operating guide.

**Correction.** This paper remains an academic paper. Its conclusion is not "you should do this," but "the Ten Profound Gates structure built by the patriarchs can serve as a hermeneutic lens for the cultivation-face deepening of the Ten Great Vows daily practice."

The difference between "academic argument on the cultivation face" and "practice guide" is very subtle but fundamental:

- A **practice guide** says: "When prostrating, please think this: 'one prostration = prostration to all directions and three times.'" This is *normative*; it tells you how you should think.
- An **academic argument on the cultivation face** says: "When the inner grammar of prostration is 'one prostration = prostration to all directions and three times,' this prostration realizes the perfect-teaching structure described by the gate of simultaneous mutual correspondence on the cultivation face." This is *descriptive*; it describes a correspondence between an inner structure and a doctrinal structure, but it does not normatively prescribe how you should do it.

**Pointing-at-the-Moon does the latter, not the former.** All "cultivation realization" passages in this paper are descriptive—they describe "when your inner grammar is X, Y will occur," but do not normatively prescribe which inner grammar you should adopt. Readers may choose to adopt them, or choose not to. The paper's academic value does not depend on whether readers adopt them—its value lies in describing a structure, not in persuading anyone to do anything.

**This is the fundamental reason P11 is still classified as a meta-level paper rather than a practice manual.**

### 5.4 ❌4 Two Failure Modes of Cross-Disciplinary Analogy

**Potential misreading.** §IV uses quantum entanglement as a cross-disciplinary analogy; readers may misread it in two ways.

**Failure Mode A.** Reading quantum entanglement as evidence that "physics proves Buddhism." "Quantum physics has now verified Huayan's Indra's net, so Huayan is scientific." This builds Buddhist doctrine on physics models, violating S8_PLAN pedagogical danger #10. **Correction**: physics models will become outdated, Indra's net will not. Pointing-at-the-Moon takes the reverse reading of "Buddhism observes why physics is startled"—§4.2 has explicitly distinguished this.

**Failure Mode B.** Reading quantum entanglement as the "physical basis of cultivation experience." "The all-things-are-one feeling I had in meditation is the manifestation of quantum entanglement in my body." This is a serious scale error. **Correction**: quantum entanglement occurs at the subatomic scale and requires strict experimental conditions; it is at a completely different layer from the macroscopic "all-things-are-one" feeling. The legitimacy of cultivation experience does not depend on physics providing a basis—**its legitimacy comes from its repeatability across thirty years of daily practice and its correspondence with the patriarchs' exposition**.

Regarding the formulation "the startle is a reaction of the desire-realm consciousness, not an event in the dharma-realm itself," this paper deliberately uses a softer version ("physics's startle comes from physics's own premise structure"). S8-P06 will unfold this judgment with greater force. This paper has given enough of the simplified version in §IV; we do not repeat it here.

### 5.5 ❌5 Numerological Mysticism (Pedagogical Danger #4)

**Potential misreading.** What is special about ten windows? Why not nine or eleven? Does the choice of "ten" carry some kind of numerological mysticism?

**Correction.** Two layers of response.

**First layer (Zhiyan's "without ten, one cannot be established" principle).** Zhiyan in *One-Vehicle Ten Profound Gates of Huayan* explicitly says: "If there is no ten, one cannot be established"[^37]—meaning, for the "one" of dependent arising to be established, it must depend on the perfect structure of "ten" for support. Ten is not arbitrary; it is the minimum necessary set for the completeness of dependent arising. With only nine, some necessary face of dependent arising is missing; with eleven, there is redundancy. Ten is the perfect number that is "neither too much nor too little, just enough."

**Second layer (Fazang's "perfect number, endlessly extending" concept).** Fazang in *Elaborated Exegesis of the Huayan Sūtra* says "ten is the perfect number, endlessly extending"[^38]—"perfect number" means "a structure complete from beginning to end (cycle complete)," and because of the cyclical nature of the perfect number, it naturally points to the endless (the end of each cycle is the beginning of the next). Ten is not a closed system; it is an indexing method for the endless. Ten gates × ten meanings = one hundred gates; each of the one hundred gates contains another ten = one thousand gates; multiplying like this, layer upon layer without end. **Ten is an indicator of the endless, not an exhaustive enumeration.**

The two layers together: ten is both "the minimum necessary set" (cannot be less) and "the indexing method for the endless" (cannot be more, lest it become a closed system). Ten is neither nine nor eleven, because ten is the unique choice that simultaneously satisfies both conditions.

**This correction is treated minimally in P11**—because P11's theme is the cultivation face, not numerical ontology. Readers interested in the deeper meaning of "ten" may consult the complete exposition in fascicle one of Fazang's *Elaborated Exegesis of the Huayan Sūtra* on the "ten meanings" and "ten gates."

### 5.6 Marking the Failure Points

The middle-way corrections of this section cannot exhaust all possible misreadings. Readers may misread this paper in ways not listed here—this is unavoidable, because understanding always belongs to the reader, and the author can only provide structure, not guarantee the reader's inner transformation.

Furthermore, the five corrections in this section are themselves "outward-pointing"—they identify which readings are wrong, but do not guarantee that readers will adopt the correct reading. **The true middle way is not in these corrections, but in each reader's repeated practice in daily cultivation.** Corrections only help you avoid the most easily fallen pits; the path beyond the pits must be walked by yourself.

> Three-dimensional pens cannot write to ten-dimensional objects. What corrections can do is mark the locations of the pits clearly—letting the reader know, do not walk in these few places; the remaining direction must rely on your own dharma-eye.

This is the second time this paper has marked the position of putting down the pen (the first time was at the close of §3.11; the third will be at the final paragraph of §VI).

## VI. Conclusion

### 6.1 The Ten Windows Are Now Installed

This paper has done one concrete thing: install Fazang's Ten Profound Gates one by one onto the ten actions of Samantabhadra's Great Vows. Beginning with the gate of simultaneous mutual correspondence × homage to all buddhas, ending with the gate of ten-time distinct establishment × universal dedication. After the ten pairs are unfolded, two pivot demonstrations (the gate of simultaneous correspondence containing all ten vows; homage to all buddhas containing all ten gates) demonstrate the concrete operation of "one is ten" on the cultivation face.

The purpose of this installation is not to establish an external correspondence table, but to let the reader see: **each action of Samantabhadra's Great Vows has, in its inner structure, precisely the dharma-realm structure described by some Profound Gate**. Ten gates and ten vows are not the accidental coincidence of two independent systems; they are two self-displays of the same perfect-teaching structure on the discursive face and the cultivation face.

Looking back over the previous ten papers of S8 from this paper, one can see a larger picture: the work of §VI cultivation correspondence in each paper (P01 through P10, with P11 and P12 as meta-level exceptions) gathers and rises in this paper's §III ten correspondences. **P11 is the sum of ten §VI's**—it gathers the cultivation-face work that was distributed across ten papers into one place, letting the reader see the whole picture at once.

### 6.2 Folding Back the Foreshadowing for S8-P10 §VI

This paper has hinted at a small thesis: homage to all buddhas is the totality of the ten vows, because homage already contains the other nine vows—and the corresponding gate of simultaneous mutual correspondence is also the totality of the ten gates. **Totality-vow corresponds to totality-gate; this structural symmetry is not accidental, but the two self-displays of the same perfect-teaching structure on the cultivation face and the discursive face.**

The formal argument for this small thesis belongs to the work of S8-P10 §VI. S8-P10 will use historical investigation, doctrinal intermeshing, and textual history to formally argue why this original thesis—"the Ten Profound Gates correspond to the Ten Great Vows"—holds. This paper does not repeat P10's work, but in §3.11.B's homage-to-buddhas × ten-gates unfolding has already laid out the cultivation-face evidence for the small thesis—P10 only needs to bring up the academic argument in §VI.

**P10 proves it; P11 teaches you how to use it.** The division of labor is clear.

### 6.3 Practice Guidance: The Concrete Daily Operation of the Ten Windows

Per PAPER_STYLE convention, the conclusion must include "practice guidance" (so what for practice). This paper's practice guidance is extremely simple:

Starting tomorrow, in your daily practice, after completing each action, pause for one second and ask yourself one question—**"What is the inner structure of this action?"**

- After prostration: "Was this prostration to one buddha or to the buddhas of the ten directions?" (Gate of simultaneous mutual correspondence)
- After praise: "Were these six words narrow or broad?" (Gate of broad-and-narrow sovereign without obstruction)
- After offering: "Was this stick of incense isolated or mutually identical?" (Gate of mutual identity sovereign)
- After repentance: "Where is the karma? Where is the emptiness of karma?" (Gate of hidden-and-manifest mutually accomplished)
- After rejoicing: "Was he the host or was I the host?" (Gate of host-and-guest perfect clarity)
- After requesting the Dharma: "Was this individual request or dharma-realm resonance?" (Gate of Indra's net realm)
- After requesting the buddhas to remain: "Did I request physical time or the limit of the future?" (Gate of subtle containment and establishment)
- After learning from the Buddha: "Did I learn one or learn all?" (Gate of one-and-many mutual containment)
- After accommodating sentient beings: "What Dharma is this sentient being revealing?" (Gate of dependence on phenomena to reveal the Dharma)
- After dedication: "Was the dedication a transmission or a folding?" (Gate of ten-time distinct establishment)

Ten questions, one round per day. You do not need an answer to every question—the question itself is the cultivation. After asking, the inner structure of the action will slowly transform from linear to perfect-fusion. This transformation will not be completed in a day, and may take many years. But one round per day, and after thirty years looking back, you will see a completely different practitioner.

**This is the concrete daily operation of the ten windows.** What this paper can do is only draw the shape of the ten windows clearly; the work of walking into each window must be done by yourself.

### 6.4 Open Questions: Left for the Next Paper and the Reader

S8-P12, "The Single Mark of the Dharma-Realm in the Eight Movements," will use the "ten gates × ten vows" structure built in this paper as a basis to install the entire eight-movement series (faith / understanding / practice / inquiry / cutting / illumination / gathering / display) into a larger perfect-teaching structure. This paper deals only with one meta-level question internal to S8 (ten gates against ten vows); P12 will deal with the meta-level question of the eight movements in totality (how the eight series compose a single mark in the dharma-realm). The open question of this paper is: **after reading this paper, can you, in your own daily practice, personally verify "one is ten"?**

You do not need complete verification. Once is enough—on some day, in the instant of some prostration, you suddenly see in this one prostration the shadow of the ten directions and three times; or in some instant of praise, you suddenly hear the resonance of the dharma-realm; or in some instant of dedication, you suddenly find time folding in this one thought. Once is enough. After one verification, you will know that what this paper has done is not a word game, but a description of a structure that was always already there.

### 6.5 The Final Marking of Putting Down the Pen

This paper is a meta-level paper. It has drawn a "guide map" of the ten windows. But a guide map is more easily mistaken for the moon than a window is—this is the meta-level paper's greatest danger.

After reading this paper, please put it down and continue your daily practice—**this is the true window**.

**Closing verse:**

> **Ten gates are ten vows,**
> **Ten vows are ten gates.**
> **Where vow and gate become one,**
> **The perfect teaching is in body and mind.**
