## Abstract

This paper establishes repentance (懺悔) of karmic obstructions as the operative gate of practice (行門) into the Huayan Perfect Teaching (圓教), demonstrating how the Āgama's three-limb program—seeing the offense, disclosing it, and guarding against recurrence—provides the practical method for uprooting even the gravest karma, such as King Ajātaśatru's patricide. However, the Āgamas supply the method without explaining its metaphysical ground. The Śūraṅgama Sūtra bridges this gap through the tathāgatagarbha doctrine, and Huayan completes it through the doctrine of nature-arising (性起): the principle that all dharmas are "originally thus" (法爾如是), operating through the non-arising arising (即無起以為起) that distinguishes nature-arising from dependent co-arising (緣起). The paper demonstrates that Samantabhadra's fourth great vow—Repentance of Karmic Obstructions—when read through the Gate of Simultaneous Completion of Hidden and Manifest (隱密顯了俱成門) of the Ten Mysterious Gates (十玄門), reveals that the bodily act of prostration before the Buddha is the direct, immediate perception of this gate: in the instant concealment (覆) is lifted through voiced disclosure, both the empty nature of karmic obstructions and their phenomenal vividness are simultaneously established, neither denying the karma nor being crushed by it. The paper concludes by bringing the entire doctrinal apparatus down to a concrete action performable this afternoon: walk to the Buddha, join your palms, and let the twenty-year concealment leave your throat as sound.

**Keywords:** nature-arising (性起), dependent co-arising (緣起), concealment (覆), repentance of karmic obstructions (懺悔業障), Huayan Perfect Teaching (華嚴圓教), tathāgatagarbha (如來藏), Gate of Simultaneous Completion of Hidden and Manifest (隱密顯了俱成門), Ten Mysterious Gates (十玄門), Āgama repentance, Samantabhadra's vows (普賢願), fourfold dharmadhātu (四法界), nature-arising arising (即無起以為起)

## I. One Word, "Concealment" (覆), Seals the Entire Path

There is something you have not told anyone for twenty years. Sometimes it is not even large — sometimes it is small enough that you cannot say why you still remember it. But every time it surfaces from some corner of your mind, you push it back down at once, switch to another thought, open your phone, or do something else.

That act of "pushing down" is what Buddhism calls *fu* 覆 — concealment. It is not lying, it is not hiding. When you lie, you still know you are lying. Concealment covers up the very fact that a cover-up is happening. You think that thing is over, but in truth it never ended; it was pressed beneath the waterline and has grown older alongside the version of you living above the water.

The *Treatise on the Hundred Dharmas of the Mahāyāna* (百法明門論) lists concealment among the minor derivative afflictions, and the *Cheng Weishi Lun* (成唯識論), Fascicle Six, traces its root in the mental factors: "Concealment: with regard to offenses one has committed, fearing the loss of gain and reputation, its nature is to hide; it obstructs non-remorse, and its function is vexing heat… This concealment is a portion of greed and delusion."[^1] Concealment is not merely a moral evil. Beneath it lie two conjoined roots: attachment to one's own image (a portion of greed), and avoidance of reality (a portion of delusion). To conceal one thing is necessarily to conceal these two as well.

The claim of this paper is simple, but it requires the Āgamas, the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra*, and the *Avataṃsaka Sūtra* together to state it clearly: **so long as there remains even one concealment in you that has not been opened, the Huayan doctrine of nature-arising (*xingqi* 性起) is, for you, a beautiful and empty phrase.** Not because it is untrue — it is profoundly true — but because the handle of that door is in your own hand, and you have been holding it shut.

The paper proceeds as follows. Section II unfolds the Āgama's three-limb program of repentance: "seeing the offense and disclosing it, guarding against recurrence" — nine Chinese characters that constitute an operational sequence, not a moral exhortation. The case of King Ajātaśatru demonstrates that this program can uproot karma even of the magnitude of patricide. But the Āgama's soteriological verbs are verbs of *attainment* — attaining the extinction of the fluxes, attaining liberation — not verbs of *disclosure*, and this exposes a boundary: the Āgamas provide the method but not its ground. Section III is the hinge of the whole paper, and answers the most dangerous question: why this program cannot be performed silently in the mind — why the body must be present before the Buddha. Sections IV through VI turn to Huayan: why nature-arising can say "originally thus"; how the *Śūraṅgama*'s three-fold *tathāgatagarbha* is re-positioned within Huayan's fourfold *dharmadhātu*; and how the single phrase "all the same nature, namely, no nature" immunizes the doctrine against any Self-view. Section VII gathers the whole: Samantabhadra's fourth vow, Repentance of Karmic Obstructions, read through the Gate of Simultaneous Completion of Hidden and Manifest (*yinmi xianliao jucheng men*), re-reads the bodily action of Section III as the direct, immediate perception of the Perfect Teaching.

The most direct meaning for you, the reader, is this: **the thing you have been carrying for twenty years — the thing you believe you have already dealt with — very likely has not been dealt with at all.** You have merely become expert at walking around it. After enough years of walking around it, the whole center of gravity of your life has tilted slightly — in a direction you yourself cannot see, because the eyes you are using to observe are the eyes of the person who has been walking around it. What this paper hopes to do is let you see that tilt, and then tell you that the gesture which three great scriptural traditions have spent fifteen hundred years teaching can be performed, by you, this afternoon.

## II. The Three-Limb Program of Āgama Repentance

### A. Seeing the Offense, Disclosing It, Guarding Against Recurrence

The most complete articulation of the repentance program in the Āgamas appears in the *Madhyama Āgama*, Fascicle Four, in the *Pāṭaliya Sutta*. The headman Pāṭaliya says to the Buddha what can almost be taken as the canonical formula of Āgama repentance:

> "I repent, Gautama! In the past, O Well-Gone One! I was as a fool, as one deluded, as one unsteady, as one unwholesome… May the Blessed One accept my repentance. **Seeing the offense and disclosing it, having repented, I shall guard against recurrence**."[^2]

The Buddha's reply ratifies this as the standard structure of repentance within the Saṅgha: "So it is, Pāṭaliya… If one can repent, seeing the offense and disclosing it, guarding against recurrence — then the noble Dharma grows without loss."[^3]

Three linguistic details in this exchange deserve to be unpacked.

**The first limb: *seeing the offense*.** The text does not use emotional vocabulary — not "admitting fault," not "feeling remorse" — it uses the verb *to see* (見). Seeing is an epistemic act: seeing a thing as it is. To see an offense as an offense — not as "an understandable constraint," not as "I too was a victim," not as "I was young then." Seeing the offense *as offense* is the first step of the program, and the hardest. Concealment operates precisely by making you unable to see it as it is; concealment always smuggles a substitute version onto the stage. Concealment never says to you "you did not do it"; it says "you did it, but actually…" — and everything that follows that "but actually" is a disguise of concealment.

**The second limb: *disclosing it* (發露).** Seeing is still inside your head; disclosure is what makes it come out. The Vinaya preserves the technical term *paṭidesanīya*[^4] — the act of confessing an infraction face-to-face to another monk or to the Saṅgha. The point of disclosure is not ritual; the point is *giving it voice*. It has to leave the heart and enter common space. Why must it be given voice? Because an acknowledgment that remains in the head always keeps a back door open: "I have not really said it, so I can still take it back." Voicing closes that door. Seeing without disclosure fades within days, and concealment quietly re-covers the ground.

**The third limb: *guarding against recurrence* (護不更作).** The first two limbs deal with the past; this one deals with the future. *Saṃvara* — often rendered as "restraint" or "moral discipline" — is an active guarding of future conduct. If repentance consists only of seeing and disclosing without guarding, it becomes the most tragic figure in the *Water-Simile Sutta*: "having risen, he sinks again"[^5] — the one who has just lifted his head above the water and pushes it back down himself. Repenting and re-offending, re-offending and repenting, becomes a self-consoling loop in which concealment grows denser (because each new offense now has "but I already repented last time" as its alibi). Guarding is not a vow never to err again — the Āgamas are not that naïve — it is the construction of an active warning system, so that the first stirrings of the next occurrence are recognized as such.

Nine characters, three actions, one sequence. This is the algorithm of Āgama repentance.

### B. King Ajātaśatru: Even the Gravest Offense Can Be Uprooted

The program sounds modest, but how grave an offense can it address? The Āgamas' answer is startlingly direct: *patricide*.

The *Dīrgha Āgama*, Fascicle Seventeen, *Śrāmaṇyaphala Sutta*, recounts that King Ajātaśatru of Magadha killed his father King Bimbisāra to usurp the throne. Patricide is one of the five *ānantarya* — the gravest category of evil karma the Āgamas acknowledge. Ajātaśatru later went to the Buddha and repented on the spot. The Buddha accepted his repentance and then said to Ānanda something of singular weight:

> "This King Ajātaśatru's offenses have diminished; **he has uprooted the grave offense** (*ba zhong jiu* 拔重咎). Had King Ajātaśatru not killed his father, he would have attained the pure Dharma-eye on this very seat. But now, since he has repented, his offense has diminished; he has uprooted the grave offense."[^6]

*Uprooted the grave offense* — four characters, and the highest verdict Āgama repentance-doctrine is capable of issuing. *Ba* 拔 is a horticultural verb: to pull up by the roots. Not to cover, not to offset, not to transfer, but to **tear the karma out from its growing point**. If the Āgamas consider even patricide reachable by this verb, then nothing in your twenty-year burden is beyond its reach.

But the Buddha's statement also contains an honest tension which must be faced: "had King Ajātaśatru not killed his father, he would have attained the pure Dharma-eye on this very seat." Repentance uprooted the grave offense, but it did not make what happened into something that had not happened. The king could not attain the fruit in that sitting; the threshold had been raised slightly by his karma, even though its root was pulled out. This is the Āgamas' sober posture on the question: **repentance is really effective, but it is not magic.** What it uproots is the continuing momentum of the karmic stream, not the historical fact of the act.

For you, this means: do not expect that after repenting, that thing will become as though it had never occurred. It did occur. You will remember. But the *way* you remember will change — from a living thing still growing, still influencing every decision, into a completed past you can look back upon calmly. That is what "uprooting" means.

### C. The Waters of Dharma Wash the Dust

The Āgamas also offer a second, more lyrical register. The *Partial Translation of the Saṃyukta Āgama*, Fascicle Five, preserves a verse spoken in the context of a bathing ritual on the banks of the Ganges, where bathers believed the river water could wash away offenses. A disciple of the Buddha replied:

> "If one's mind is truly pure, keeping the precepts, always observing the *uposatha*, cultivating pure action — such a one always has the complete precepts. Not killing and not stealing, not committing sexual misconduct, not speaking falsely, trusting in karma and its fruits, never envying others — **the waters of Dharma wash the dust of defilement; here, in this place, one should bathe**."[^7]

The waters of the Ganges cannot wash karma. What washes karma is the water of Dharma (*dhamma-udaka*), and "in this place" means the mind — the mind that keeps the precepts, that observes the *uposatha*, that trusts in karma and its fruits.

The contribution of this verse to our argument is a crucial linguistic bridge. The main grammar of Āgama repentance — "seeing," "disclosing," "guarding" — is operational and rather dry. But *the waters of Dharma wash the dust* supplies a sensory image: **concealment is a layer that can be rinsed away**. This image reappears throughout later Mahāyāna repentance liturgies — the *Repentance of Emperor Liang* (慈悲梁皇寶懺) and the *Water Repentance of the Compassionate Samādhi* (慈悲三昧水懺) both take the image of water into their very titles. That is no accident; they inherit the vocabulary of this Āgama verse.

### D. Raw Gold and Dust: The Boundary of the Āgamic "Attaining" Model

The previous three subsections establish the operational program. But an honest question must be faced: do the Āgamas contain the idea that the mind is *originally* pure?

The answer: not explicitly. The phrases *the nature of mind is originally pure*, *self-nature is pure*, *buddha-nature* — none of these appears in the four Āgamas in their explicit doctrinal sense.[^8] The famous passage later cited by Mahāyāna — "the nature of mind is originally pure, defiled by adventitious dust" — occurs in the *Ekottara Āgama*, but is, in the history of the literature, a late Abhidharma-era redactional layer and cannot be taken as representative of the earliest Āgamic stance.

Nevertheless, the Āgamas do contain several passages that *approach* the idea of a mind naturally pure — except with a different verb. The best known is the raw-gold simile in the *Saṃyukta Āgama*, Fascicle Forty-Seven: "A bhikṣu of pure mind, bound up by coarse afflictions, gradually severs and extinguishes them — like raw gold, from which the hard rocks and stone lumps are washed away… until the mind is pure, like raw gold with the colored dust removed, brought to its purity."[^9]

The verbs here are decisive. *Gradually sever*, *extinguish*, *remove*, *bring to purity* — all of them are verbs of *severing*, *cutting*, *extinguishing*. Not a single verb of *disclosing*, *manifesting*, *revealing*. This verbal difference is the deepest watershed between the Āgama soteriology and the Huayan doctrine of nature-arising. The Āgama model is: the mind has impurities; remove the impurities, and what remains is pure. You are not revealing something already there, you are *producing* purity by means of one act of removal after another. The *Seven Chariots Sutta*'s seven purifications (purification of morality, of mind, of view, of the overcoming of doubt, of knowledge and vision of path and non-path, of knowledge and vision of the course, of knowledge and vision leading to the severance of defilement)[^10] share the same structure exactly: purity is a state *attained* at each successive stage, not the *manifestation* of an already-present nature.

The point is not to demote the Āgamas, but to locate them with precision: **the Āgamas give us the operational method of repentance, but they do not give us the metaphysical ground of why repentance works.** Why should so simple an act as seeing and disclosing be capable of uprooting the gravest karma? Why should the gesture of "having risen, not sinking again" be capable of redirecting a whole life? The Āgama answer is: "Because it works. Do it and you will see." This is an empiricist and pragmatic answer, fully sufficient to sustain practice; but the reader who continues will ask: *on what grounds?*

That answer belongs to Huayan. But before entering Huayan, one more problem must be addressed, because if it is not, the entire Āgama program silently collapses into a quiet inner monologue.

### E. Summary: An Unanswered Question

The Āgama doctrine of repentance can be summarized in four points. First, it is a **three-limb closed-loop program**: seeing the offense, disclosing it, guarding against recurrence — the past seen clearly, released, and the future blocked. Second, it is **effective but not magical**: Ajātaśatru could uproot one of the five gravest offenses, but the historical fact of the act does not vanish; only the continuing momentum of its stream is cut. Third, linguistically it prefers **dry operational verbs**, but preserves the lyrical image "the waters of Dharma wash the dust," which later becomes the linguistic ancestor of all Mahāyāna repentance liturgies. Fourth, its lineage of "the mind can be made pure" is **indirect and subtractive**; its verbs are "sever, cut, extinguish, attain" — never "reveal, manifest, disclose."

Together these give the reader a clear practical instruction: **you can begin repenting today, without waiting for any metaphysical background**. But they leave an unanswered question: why does the act work? That question will be answered in Section IV.

Before reaching Section IV, one more thing must be said, or else everything above is liable to be misused.

## III. Prostration Before the Buddha: Why the Body Must Be Present

The preceding sections are dangerous. Having read this far, you are likely to draw the following conclusion: "If Āgama repentance is this simple, I can sit quietly for three minutes, see the thing once in my heart, disclose it once in my heart, resolve once in my heart not to do it again — and that will suffice."

**This conclusion is wrong.** And it is, precisely, the most sophisticated form of that concealment named at the beginning of this paper.

Why? Because **the inner monologue is concealment's most comfortable hiding place**. In the moment you say to yourself "I admit I did that thing," the one speaking and the one listening are the same person — which is to say, the very one who was doing the concealing is the one chairing this act of confession. The chair can decide at any moment "all right, the confession is finished"; the chair can adjust the wording; the chair can press pause whenever it gets uncomfortable. This is not seeing the offense and disclosing it. This is **a performance with a presiding officer** — in which the officer is you, the audience is you, and the concealment that needs to be opened is the very identity of the officer.

In the Āgamas, Pāṭaliya, Ajātaśatru, Aṅgulimāla — their repentances all share a feature we have so far intentionally left un-stressed: **every one of them was performed in the presence of the Buddha, face to face.** The Buddha is not literary background to the scene; the Buddha is the *field* in which the "seeing" can really take place. There is an eye that is not your eye, watching, and so you cannot quietly swap in a cleaner version. There is an ear that is not your ear, listening, and so you cannot press down on your own voice halfway through the disclosure. "Guarding against recurrence" is no longer a private promise you can revise tomorrow, but something set down before a presence you dare not deceive.

After the Buddha's *parinirvāṇa*, the structure of being-addressed is carried forward by images of the Buddha and by the liturgies of repentance. This is not cultural ornament. This is the necessary condition for turning an epistemic act back into a **bodily act**. If, having read this paper, you decide to repent of one thing today, the following is my earnest counsel. Please do not treat any of it as optional.

**Walk to the Buddha.** Not imagine the Buddha in your heart — the imagined Buddha is a product of the same presiding officer. Let the body physically go. If there is a home shrine, go to the home shrine; if there is a temple nearby, go to the temple; if all you have is an image on your phone, turn to it in earnest. The point is not the elegance of the object but the physical turning-toward.

**Join your palms.** Not as politeness, but as entering a posture in which the body cannot slide into its phone, cannot drift, cannot escape. The gesture itself draws two outward-reaching hands back to the centerline; it is a **bodily arresting of concealment**. Before you have even opened your mouth, concealment has already been blocked in half by the posture itself.

**Read a long repentance text.** *Repentance of Emperor Liang*, *Water Repentance of the Compassionate Samādhi*, the *Great Compassion Repentance* — these long liturgies are not for skimming. They are long because the reader needs that length. A short self-acknowledgment can be dispatched by concealment with a mere "all right, enough." A long text is a corridor you must walk through, and at every section it closes another escape route. As you read, a sentence will at some point strike you directly — a sentence that points precisely at the thing you have been covering. Let that strike land. Do not move around it.

What follows is heavy; please sit with it.

**The thing you are going to repent of today may be a tumor that has been quietly growing inside you.** This is not appropriating a real disease as a metaphor. It is borrowing a medical fact to describe the dynamics of concealment: **a thing that has never been brought into the light does not pause in the dark; it grows.** You believed that something twenty years ago was over. In truth it has continued dividing in the places you could not see. It has shaped the way you choose a partner, the degree to which you are severe with your children, the shape of your anxiety about money, the voice that rises in you when you wake at 3 a.m. You think these are parts of your personality. Some of them are branches that grew from the thing that never saw light.

When you really walk to the Buddha, join your palms, and bring it out of your throat — not silently inside, but allowing the sound to leave the body — three things will happen. These three will be re-read in Section VII as the direct perception of the Gate of Simultaneous Completion of Hidden and Manifest. Here, let us first record them as experience.

**First, the Buddha will hear you.** This is not mythology. It is a description of the deepest structure of "seeing" in your body-mind being activated. In ordinary states, that structure is blocked by your own concealment; you cannot feel it, you cannot hear it. In the moment of voiced disclosure before the Buddha, the obstruction between you and that structure temporarily vanishes, and you experience *being heard*. The experience is real, whatever vocabulary you use to describe it.

**Second, the gods and Dharma-protectors rejoice and praise.** To a reader familiar with Buddhist culture this phrasing is natural; to one less familiar, a note is required. There are countless operating threads of wholesome force in the web of dependent co-arising; in ordinary states they cannot reach you — your concealment works as a shield, sealing you off from the surrounding field. In the moment you open that concealment before the Buddha, the shielding drops, and those forces flow in. Tradition describes this in anthropomorphic language — "the gods rejoice" — but what it points to is a real structural event: **you have reconnected to the field that was running all along.**

**Third, the body will grow light.** Not metaphorically. The thing that had been growing in the dark begins, in the moment of being seen, to dissolve — as water rinses away mud. What the Āgama verse "the waters of Dharma wash the dust" was describing is exactly this. Some readers weep in this moment. That is not emotional collapse. It is the body expelling a weight it has carried for a long time. Let the weeping happen. Do not rush to control it.

After all of this, sit a while. Attend to your own breath. You may discover that you have not breathed this way in a long time — a breathing that does not need to pause halfway, that does not need to grow shallow to avoid feeling something. That is what "lightness" means.

This is not a lyrical aside. This is the action the paper is asking you to take today. All the Āgama passages of the previous sections, and all the Huayan architecture of the sections that follow, exist to ensure that you understand, when you do this action, why it works, how far it works, and on what grounds it works. But however clear the grounds may be, if you do not actually perform the action, the paper has failed.

The next section takes up a conceptual problem: if the Āgamas speak of "severing, cutting, attaining," and Huayan speaks of "disclosing, revealing, originally present," how can these two apparently opposed soteriological grammars co-obtain in one and the same act of repentance? The answer to that question is the doctrinal core of the paper.

## IV. Nature-Arising vs. Dependent Co-Arising: Huayan Gives the "On What Grounds"

### A. Sūtra Side: The Declaration of Nature-Arising in the *Tathāgata-Manifestation* Chapter

By the principle *sūtras first, treatises second*, we return to the scripture. The eighty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka*'s *Chapter on the Manifestation of the Tathāgata* (T0279, Fascicle 52) opens the true Dharma of nature-arising, and its core declaration is:

> "Sons of the Buddha! When the Tathāgata, Worthy, Perfectly Enlightened One, attains Perfect Enlightenment, he sees within his own body all sentient beings attaining Perfect Enlightenment, and further sees all sentient beings entering *nirvāṇa* — **all the same nature, namely: no nature**. No what sort of nature? No characteristic-nature, no exhausting-nature, no arising-nature, no cessation-nature, no self-nature, no not-self-nature, no sentient-being-nature, no not-sentient-being-nature… Knowing that all dharmas are without nature, he attains omniscience, and his great compassion continues unbroken, saving and delivering sentient beings."[^11]

This passage has three structural layers. **First**, at the moment of the Tathāgata's Perfect Enlightenment "he sees within his own body all sentient beings attaining Perfect Enlightenment" — this is the universal proposition of nature-arising. Not "the Buddha is *able* to liberate sentient beings," but "in the very moment of the Buddha's Enlightenment, all sentient beings are already simultaneously enlightened within the Buddha's body." **Second**, the ground of this simultaneity is "all the same nature, namely, no nature" — it is not that some shared substance binds Buddha and sentient beings together, but that *no self-nature* is the deepest sameness. **Third**, "knowing all dharmas are without nature, he attains omniscience, and great compassion continues unbroken" — no-nature is wisdom; great compassion is its functioning; wisdom and compassion non-dual is the full face of nature-arising.

The same chapter contains the famous *originally-present* declaration. The sixty-fascicle recension (T0278, Fascicle 35) reads: "Marvelous! Marvelous! How is it that **the Tathāgata, possessed of wisdom, is within the body**, and yet is not known, not seen? I shall teach these sentient beings to awaken to the noble path, making them forever depart from the bondage of deluded thinking and inverted defilements, so that they may wholly see the wisdom of the Tathāgata within their own bodies, no different from the Buddha."[^12] The eighty-fascicle version reads: "There is not a single sentient being who does not possess the wisdom of the Tathāgata, but because of deluded thinking, inverted views, and grasping, they do not realize it; if they depart from deluded thinking, omniscience, spontaneous wisdom, and unguided wisdom will at once **become manifest**."[^13]

The verbs that appear here are verbs the Āgamas never used: *become manifest*, *no different from the Buddha*, *if they depart, they will at once attain*. The Āgamic path proceeds through "severing, cutting, extinguishing, attaining" one stage at a time; here in Huayan the verb is *disclose* — it is already there, simply covered; remove the cover, and there it is. This verbal shift is not a choice of translation. The entire base of the soteriology has changed underneath.

### B. Treatise Side: Fazang on "Taking Non-Arising as Arising"

But the "disclosing" of Huayan immediately invites a challenge: "original possession of the Tathāgata's wisdom" sounds like "*tathatā* producing the myriad dharmas," sounds like creationism — is this not the Self-view Buddhism has always most vigorously refused? Fazang addresses this directly in his *Questions and Answers on the Avataṃsaka Sūtra* (T1873, Fascicle 2):

> "Question: What distinguishes nature-arising from dependent co-arising? Answer: Nature-arising refers to what is itself so, not arising from conditions; in nature-arising, the very Dharma-nature **takes non-arising as arising** — and so it is called nature-arising. Dependent co-arising refers to what manifests from conditions and arises through conditions."[^14]

The pivotal phrase: "**takes non-arising as arising**." The "arising" of nature-arising is not the arising of birth-and-cessation (that is dependent co-arising, which has arising, abiding, changing, and perishing), but the manifestation of "so-it-is by its own nature." By defining "arising" from the outset as non-arising, Huayan refuses any generative reading.

In his *Exploring the Mysteries Record* (探玄記, T1733, Fascicle 16), Fazang uses a more intuitive analogy: "Arising is only nature-arising; even the functions of the fruit-state are only the functioning of the true nature. As when gold is made into a ring: the ring is empty, the gold is real; only the gold arises."[^15] When gold is made into a ring, the ring is an apparent form; the gold is the real substance. The "arising" of the ring is not the production of a ring from elsewhere, it is the manifestation of gold in that particular shape. **The form is merely posited, the substance is real; what is called "arising" is only the substance manifesting through the form.**

This answers the question from the previous section. Why can the Āgama's "severing, cutting, attaining" and Huayan's "disclosing, manifesting, originally present" co-obtain in one and the same act of repentance? Because they are not two soteriological paths at all; they are **two descriptive layers of the same instant**. The Āgamas speak from the side of *event*: there is a concealment, lift it. Huayan speaks from the side of *nature*: what appears after it is lifted is not newly made, it was always there. The Āgamic "severing" and the Huayan "disclosing" are not sequential but **simultaneous**: the instant you sever is the instant it discloses.

Fazang's "Gate of Defiled and Pure" adds an important pedagogical limitation: "Although defiled and pure dharmas all rely upon the true, because their directions differ — going against and going along — defilement belongs to ignorance, and purity reverts to nature-arising."[^16] Fazang pedagogically restricts "nature-arising" to the side of the pure: defiled dharmas belong to the dependent co-arising of ignorance, not to nature-arising. Chengguan, in his *Sub-Commentary*, slightly loosens this with the "Gate of the Deluded Concealing the True," which holds that at the very moment the deluded conceals, the true has already nature-arisen.[^17] The two masters do not contradict each other; they look at one reality from different sides. The force of Fazang's restriction is to prevent the reader from abusing nature-arising into "all afflictions are nature-arising, so there is nothing to sever." Huayan has never meant this. Nature-arising is a description of *what appears after* repentance, not an embellishment of *what was being concealed before* it.

## V. The Meeting of Nature-Arising and Emptiness: "All the Same Nature, Namely: No Nature"

We return to the core quotation of §IV.A: "all the same nature, namely, no nature." This is the meeting point of the whole doctrine of nature-arising with the *prajñāpāramitā* of emptiness.

Many readers of Huayan worry: speaking of "the Tathāgata's wisdom originally present," of "the nature-virtues perfect and complete," does Huayan quietly slip back into Self-view? Does it secretly reify some entity called "buddha-nature"? The *Chapter on the Manifestation of the Tathāgata* itself provides the most thorough immunization: **the sameness of nature is the absence of nature**. The sameness of sentient beings and buddhas is not because they share some entity called "buddha-nature," but because both equally lack self-nature. No-nature is the deepest sameness, and the most thorough refusal of substantiation.

This passage must be read alongside S2's Heart Sūtra P04 ("illuminating the emptiness of the five aggregates") and S5's Diamond Sūtra P01 ("the tripartite formula: A → not-A → therefore A"). S2 breaks grasping the aggregates as real. S5 breaks conceptual attachment via the formula *A, non-A, therefore called A*. S8 Huayan, while affirming "all sentient beings possess the Tathāgata's wisdom," immediately self-cancels any substantive reading of that affirmation.

The grammar is consistent across all three: **every affirmation is immediately paired with a cancellation by emptiness**. What is distinctive about Huayan is that this cancellation is bound together with the strongest possible affirmation ("when one attains, all attain"). The more thorough the affirmation, the more thorough the emptiness. This is not a step back and a step forward; it is simultaneous completion.

⚠️ A disciplinary reminder across traditions: "emptiness equals nature-arising" is not a logical identity but a **soteriological identity**. Conceptually you can distinguish emptiness and presence; in realization these are two faces of one and the same present moment. This is why the masters placed nature-arising at the level of Huayan (Perfect Teaching): one must first traverse *prajñāpāramitā* (Emptiness school) — break cleanly — before nature-arising will not be heard as a hidden "True Self." Without the preparatory work of S2 and S5, a direct leap to S8's nature-arising will almost certainly misread.

## VI. Mapping the Śūraṅgama's Three *Tathāgatagarbha*s onto Huayan's Fourfold *Dharmadhātu* (S6 Bridge)

The endpoint of S6 (the thirteen-paper series on the *Śūraṅgama*) is the *tathāgatagarbha*. The fifty skandha-*māras* exhausted, the five delusive thinkings dissolved, what is disclosed is "the wondrous true nature of the original Tathāgata-store." However, the *Śūraṅgama* sub-commentator Tongrun long ago noted a critical incompleteness: "Again, this speaks purely of suchness, showing only the Empty *tathāgatagarbha*; **it has not yet clarified the arising of its function** — this is why it is classified under the true limit."[^18] The Empty *tathāgatagarbha* discloses only the substance of suchness; *how* arising occurs, *why* arising occurs, and *what* relation the arisen dharmas bear to the substance — these the *Śūraṅgama* presents as a skeleton, not as a complete doctrine of nature-arising. What Huayan takes up is precisely this "arising."

In commenting on the fourth fascicle of the *Śūraṅgama*, Tongrun explicitly borrows Huayan's threefold framework to expound the three *tathāgatagarbha*s: "The ancients explicated the three garbhas in two ways: first, the *Yuanjue* commentary took *concealing-covering*, *containing-encompassing*, and *giving-birth* as the three; second, the Huayan commentary used the three greats of *substance*, *attributes*, and *function* in order to explicate the Empty and the other two."[^19] The key equations come from Tongrun himself:

| Śūraṅgama Three Garbhas | Awakening of Faith Two Gates | Huayan *Dharmadhātu*s | Huayan Three Greats |
|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| Empty *tathāgatagarbha* | Gate of Mind-Suchness | *li-dharmadhātu* (principle) | Substance-great |
| Non-Empty *tathāgatagarbha* | Gate of Mind-Birth-Death | *shi-dharmadhātu* (phenomena, from nature arising to form) | Attributes-great |
| Empty-and-Non-Empty | Non-duality of the two gates | Principle-phenomena unobstructed → Phenomena-phenomena unobstructed | Function-great |

**Empty *tathāgatagarbha* = *li-dharmadhātu* (Substance-great).** Tongrun: "Disclosing the Empty *tathāgatagarbha*: this emptiness is not annihilationist emptiness, not the extinction of form, not the emptiness outside of characteristics. In it all dharmas are revealed as unmoving and unbroken… only then does it fully illuminate the Empty *tathāgatagarbha*. And so principle and wisdom are perfectly fused, the object has no partiality — this is only the wondrous, awakened luminosity, perfectly illumining the *li-dharmadhātu*."[^20] The Empty *tathāgatagarbha* is not the emptying of something; it is "form is itself nature, the pure true is emptiness" — precisely the *li-dharmadhātu* rendered in the *Śūraṅgama*'s grammar.

**Non-Empty *tathāgatagarbha* = *shi-dharmadhātu* (Attributes-great: form arising from nature).** Tongrun: "From the true the deluded arises, thereby disclosing the *garbha*-mind's function of following conditions… **the one mind originally contains in principle, and follows conditions in its functioning** — all can be reached through this. Since it is the myriad dharmas, it definitely belongs to the Non-Empty *garbha*."[^21] "The one mind originally contains in principle, and follows conditions in its functioning" — these eight characters are nature-arising expressed in the *Śūraṅgama*'s voice: the nature contains all merits in principle, and manifests these through conditions in its functioning. This *is* Huayan's "form arising from nature." The Non-Empty *tathāgatagarbha* is the *Śūraṅgama*'s version of nature-arising.

**Empty-and-Non-Empty *tathāgatagarbha* = Principle-phenomena unobstructed → Phenomena-phenomena unobstructed (Function-great).** Tongrun: "When emptiness is itself non-empty, nature is wholly form, and nature is unhindered; when non-emptiness is itself empty, form is wholly nature — how then could form be hindered?"[^22] "Nature wholly form, form wholly nature" — this is Huayan's principle-phenomena unobstructed; "pervading all dharmas without falling into any single one" is Huayan's phenomena-phenomena unobstructed. Elsewhere Tongrun directly aligns the *Śūraṅgama*'s perfect penetration with Huayan's bodhisattva stages: "This one practice is the wisdom of principle-phenomena unobstructed… this wisdom of phenomena-phenomena unobstructed is the Gate of Broad-and-Narrow Unobstructed Freedom among the Ten Mysteries."[^23]

Tongrun's most direct statement closes the matter: "**To reach that dependent co-arising is itself nature-arising — all manner of transformations arise from the wondrous, perfect, true mind**."[^24] The *Śūraṅgama*'s great-consciousness transformations are not mere dependent co-arising; in their essence they are nature-arising — born of the wondrous, perfect, true mind. And further: "**Cultivation arises from nature, and cultivation in turn merges back into nature.** Outside the true nature, not a single dharma can be obtained."[^25] *Cultivation arises from nature, and cultivation in turn merges back into nature* — this is the nature-arising closed loop of the *Śūraṅgama*'s soteriology, and it is the doctrinal floor beneath S6's twenty-five perfect penetrations. S6 was reticent about the words "nature-arising" where it could have been explicit; now it can be said plainly. Every one of the *Śūraṅgama*'s twenty-five perfect penetrations is a case of "cultivation arising from nature"; at the utmost of cultivation, each is "cultivation merging back into nature." This closed loop is the *Śūraṅgama* version of Huayan's "the cause embraces the sea of fruit, the fruit penetrates the source of the cause."

The bridge from S6 to S8 is formally established in this section.

## VII. The Gate of Simultaneous Completion of Hidden and Manifest: A Perfect-Teaching Reading of Repentance

### 7.1 The Nature-Arising Reading of Repenting Karmic Obstructions

Samantabhadra's fourth great vow is *Repentance of Karmic Obstructions*: "Again, good son! What is meant by repenting and clearing away karmic obstructions? … I now with pure body-speech-mind, pervading all the infinitesimal lands throughout the *dharmadhātu*, before all buddhas and bodhisattvas, sincerely repent, never again to commit them, always abiding in pure precepts and all merit."[^26]

The ordinary reading of repentance is: there is karmic obstruction, and through repentance it is eliminated. This reading is not wrong, but it is not complete. It remains within the Āgamic *severing* model and has not yet entered the Perfect Teaching.

**The nature-arising reading**: the essence of karmic obstructions is originally empty (this is the side of nature-arising — originally, no karma), yet their phenomenal appearance is vivid and distinct (this is the side of dependent co-arising — karma is vividly before us). In the moment of repentance, "the nature is empty" and "the phenomena are vivid" are simultaneously established — not "first there is karma, then repentance, then elimination," but rather: in the moment karmic obstruction is sincerely beheld, its *hidden* (empty nature) and its *manifest* (phenomenal appearance) arrive together. This corresponds precisely to the **Gate of Simultaneous Completion of Hidden and Manifest** among the Ten Mysteries.

Chengguan's *Sub-Commentary* uses the half-moon simile: "Like the waxing and waning of the moon: when hidden it is not non-existent; when manifest it is not newly produced."[^27] The hidden and manifest of the half-moon obtain simultaneously — the moon's unseen portion is not the absence of moon, and the moon's seen portion is not newly produced moon. Hidden and manifest do not deprive each other; they are two faces of one body. The nature-emptiness (hidden) and phenomenal vividness (manifest) of karmic obstructions have exactly this structure. The one who repents sincerely neither denies the karma (annihilationism) nor is crushed by it (eternalism), but completes the transformation by simultaneously shouldering both:

- **Phenomena**: I did such-and-such; the fruition stands before me vividly. I do not evade it, I take it fully upon myself.
- **Nature-empty**: beneath this deed and its fruit there is no independent, self-existing "karmic entity." It is the manifestation of dependent flow, which is at that very moment empty.
- **Simultaneous completion**: these two are not sequential but simultaneous. The moment of shouldering is the moment of release; the moment of release is the moment of shouldering.

This is why Samantabhadra's vow closes with "never again to commit them, always abiding in pure precepts and all merit." The Perfect-Teaching meaning of repentance is not to erase the past; it is to re-enter, in the instant of nature-arising, the originally pure precepts.

### 7.2 A Perfect-Teaching Re-Reading of the Three Things: Section III's Bodily Experience as the Direct Perception of Hidden-and-Manifest Simultaneity

Section III described three things that happen when one prostrates before the Buddha: the Buddha will hear you, the gods and Dharma-protectors rejoice, the body grows light. Those descriptions were phrased in the language of experience. By the time we reach this section, they can be re-read precisely as the direct perception of the Gate of Simultaneous Completion of Hidden and Manifest.

**The Perfect-Teaching reading of "the Buddha will hear you."** The experience of being heard is not that of some distant Buddha stretching out his ear. It is that **the originally-present structure of "seeing" in the deepest place of your body-mind** — what Huayan calls "the Tathāgata's wisdom is everywhere," "wisdom complete within the body" — re-manifests the moment concealment is opened. The originally-present discloses itself in the act of repentance: this is *manifest*. But you did not newly receive a Buddha — you were always within this structure, merely covered until now: this is *hidden is not non-existent*. Manifest and hidden obtain in the same instant. This is simultaneous completion of hidden and manifest.

**The Perfect-Teaching reading of "the gods and Dharma-protectors rejoice and praise."** This is precisely the experiential face of "all the same nature, namely, no nature" from the *Chapter on the Manifestation of the Tathāgata*. You have reconnected to the field of causes and conditions that was running all along — that field is not something coming in from outside, it is something you have never actually left; your concealment did not seal you outside the field, it caused you to **temporarily mis-recognize your own continuous presence within it**. The moment concealment is opened, the nature-arising structure of "when one attains, all attain" immediately becomes perceptible at the experiential level. The rejoicing of the gods is not a supernatural event; it is you seeing again that you were always in that web — and seeing that the web inclines, in its ground, toward you with benevolence, because its substance is *great compassion continuing unbroken*.

**The Perfect-Teaching reading of "the body grows light."** This is the most direct case of simultaneous completion. In the instant the thing grown in the dark dissolves, it does not "become non-existent" — that would be annihilationism, reading nature-arising as "the karma was never there." It did happen, the phenomenon stands vividly (manifest); but beneath it there was never an independent, self-existing "karmic entity" that could continue pressing on you, and the momentum of its continuing stream has been cut — the weight the body remembered releases (hidden). The Āgama's "waters of Dharma wash the dust" and the *Śūraṅgama*'s "wondrous true nature of the original Tathāgata-store" are, in this instant, the *same thing*. No water comes from outside; no dust is carried away. It is the originally-present purity disclosing itself in the moment concealment is opened.

This is why the Āgamas' *uprooting of grave offense* in Section II and the *Śūraṅgama*'s "cultivation arises from nature, and cultivation in turn merges back into nature" in Section VI are two descriptions of the same action. The Āgamas speak from the side of *uprooting*: tearing the karma from its growing point. Huayan speaks from the side of *merging*: entering the nature one has never left. The momentum is what is uprooted; the originally-present nature is what is merged into — and both occur in the same instant, in the same body, in the same act of voiced disclosure.

### 7.3 Āgama Leakage: Non-Concealment

One more balance must be restored to the Āgama side. The Buddha's root principle for teaching repentance in the Āgamas is: *see the offense as offense, repent according to the Dharma* — the principle of non-concealment. This does not contradict the Perfect-Teaching reading of simultaneous completion; the Āgama's "see the offense as offense" is the honesty of the phenomenal side, and the Perfect Teaching's "nature-empty and phenomena-vivid" is its doctrinal deepening. **Non-concealment is the entry; hidden-and-manifest simultaneity is the completion.**

A practitioner who understands the Perfect Teaching without the Āgamas easily reads "karmic obstructions are empty by nature" as "karmic obstructions do not exist," loses honesty, and concealment returns in a subtler form. Concealment that uses "emptiness" as its alibi is harder to uproot than the original concealment. Conversely, a practitioner who understands the Āgamas without the Perfect Teaching reads repentance as "some real sin is waiting to be washed off," sinks into anxiety, and the more one repents, the heavier it becomes. Holding both at once — phenomenal honesty in seeing the offense as offense, doctrinal depth in seeing hidden-and-manifest simultaneity — is complete repentance in the Perfect-Teaching sense.

## VIII. Middle-Way Corrections

### ❌ Correction 1: Nature-Arising ≠ Creationism

The easiest misreading: "nature-arising" sounds like "*tathatā* producing the myriad dharmas," as if the myriad dharmas were born from *tathatā*, and *tathatā* were some First Cause, some creator of the universe. **This is the most thoroughgoing misreading.** Fazang has rejected it in advance: "taking non-arising as arising — hence called nature-arising." The "arising" of nature-arising is not "produced" but "the way-of-manifestation of what is originally so." Look again at the gold and the ring: gold did not "produce" the ring; the gold simply *is* that way, and the ring is only the gold manifesting through a particular shape. The Middle Way: **nature-arising is not creation; it is another name for "originally thus" (*dharmatā*).**

### ❌ Correction 2: Nature-Arising ≠ Ontological Realism

The second misreading: "*tathatā* originally possesses all merits" sounds as if *tathatā* were a substance possessing merits as attributes hanging on it. **This is also a misreading.** The phrase "all the same nature, namely, no nature" (§V) already immunizes against it. Huayan has never treated *tathatā* as a substance. If a reader extracts from Huayan's "nature-virtues complete" a substantialized *tathatā*, that reader has not yet read the self-cancellation by emptiness present within the *Chapter on the Manifestation* itself. The Middle Way: **nature-arising is a perspective of contemplation, not the name of an ontological item.** When you say "nature-arising," what you are saying is not "there is a thing called *nature* which arises," but rather: "this is the name of the fact that all dharmas are originally so."

### ❌ Correction 3: "The Tathāgata's Wisdom and Virtuous Characteristics" is Not in the Sūtra

This is Pointing at the Moon's small textual contribution to Huayan studies. The most widely circulated version of the famous quotation reads: "Marvelous! Marvelous! All sentient beings possess the Tathāgata's **wisdom and virtuous characteristics** (*zhihui dexiang* 智慧德相), but because of deluded thinking and grasping they cannot realize it." This phrasing circulates so widely in the Chinese-speaking world that it has become the signature quote of Huayan nature-arising. **But the four-character compound "wisdom and virtuous characteristics" does not exist in T0279 (eighty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka*), nor in T0278 (sixty-fascicle), nor in Fazang's *Exploring the Mysteries Record* (T1733), nor in Chengguan's *Avataṃsaka Commentary* (T1735), nor in the *Sub-Commentary* (T1736) — it appears nowhere in these sources.**[^28] The actual words of the sūtras, as quoted in §IV.A, are: T0278, "the Tathāgata, possessed of wisdom, is within the body"; T0279, "there is not a single sentient being who does not possess the wisdom of the Tathāgata."

Where do the two characters *virtuous characteristics* (*dexiang* 德相) come from? RAG traces the nearest source to Chengguan's *Sub-Commentary* citing the withered-flower simile from the *Tathāgatagarbha Sūtra* (T0666): "Within the body of afflictions is a *tathāgatagarbha*, always unstained, **its virtuous characteristics complete**, no different from me."[^29] *Virtuous characteristics complete* is the vocabulary of the *Tathāgatagarbha Sūtra*. At some later point, "the Tathāgata's wisdom" (from the *Chapter on the Manifestation*) and "virtuous characteristics" (from the *Tathāgatagarbha Sūtra*) were welded together into the currently circulating "the Tathāgata's wisdom and virtuous characteristics." This is not a grave error — both sources fall within the Huayan and *tathāgatagarbha* family and their doctrines are compatible — but by the precision of the *sūtras-first, treatises-second* principle, **Pointing at the Moon must return to the original sūtras and not rely on the circulating version**.

### ❌ Correction 4: Repentance ≠ Inner Self-Dialogue

This correction, new to this paper, is the most important one for contemporary readers. Many who read the Āgama program above will assume it can be performed quietly in the mind — seeing the offense in one's mind, confessing it in one's mind, resolving in one's mind not to repeat it. **This path is bound to fail**, for the reasons detailed in §III: the inner monologue is concealment's most comfortable hiding place; the presiding officer and the audience are the same person, and the boundary of concealment is never really opened.

The Middle Way: **repentance must possess a "toward-whom" structure** — the body before the Buddha, the voice leaving the throat, the posture beginning at joined palms. Lacking these three, the three limbs of seeing-disclosing-guarding degrade into three rounds of self-dialogue, and concealment is not only not uprooted, it is reinforced by the delusion "I have already repented." All the scriptural and doctrinal exposition of this paper, if it does not finally come down to a real act of prostration before the Buddha, loses its effect entirely.

## IX. Conclusion: The Perfect-Teaching Re-Positioning of S6's *Tathāgatagarbha*, and Today's Action

The thirteen papers of S6 on the *Śūraṅgama* culminated in the *tathāgatagarbha*, because that is where the *Śūraṅgama* itself ended. The task of S8-P04 is to re-position that endpoint as the *starting point* of the Huayan Perfect Teaching of S8. Tongrun's sub-commentary has already done most of the work for us: Empty *tathāgatagarbha* = *li-dharmadhātu*; Non-Empty *tathāgatagarbha* = *shi-dharmadhātu* (form arising from nature); Empty-and-Non-Empty *tathāgatagarbha* = principle-phenomena unobstructed → phenomena-phenomena unobstructed. The *Śūraṅgama*, speaking from the side of practice, says "phenomena exhausted, nature manifest"; Huayan, speaking from the side of the fruition, says "form arising from nature." In this paper S6 and S8 formally shake hands.

But the more fundamental work of this paper is to bring the entire doctrinal skeleton of "from *tathāgatagarbha* to nature-arising" down to a concrete act you can perform this afternoon: **walk to the Buddha, join your palms, and let the thing you have been carrying for twenty years leave your throat as sound.** All eight sections of scriptural and doctrinal exposition exist only to ensure that when you perform that act, you know why it works, how far it works, and on what grounds it works.

**The Āgamas tell you the method**: seeing the offense, disclosing it, guarding against recurrence — nine characters are a complete operational sequence, capable of uprooting even patricide. **The *Śūraṅgama* tells you the depth**: what is disclosed when the fifty skandha-*māras* are exhausted is nothing other than the *tathāgatagarbha* you always had; the instant repentance lifts concealment is a miniature version of the instant the *skandha-māras* are broken. **Huayan tells you the grounds**: what is disclosed is not something newly made but what is originally thus; "taking non-arising as arising" — you are not constructing a new self, you are recognizing the self that never left. The three scriptures converge perfectly in this single act of repenting karmic obstructions. This is the true weight of Samantabhadra's fourth great vow within the Perfect Teaching.

The next paper, S8-P05, will move from the doctrine of nature-arising into *the completion of Buddhahood at the fullness of faith* — the precise doctrine that at the moment of initial aspiration, Perfect Enlightenment is already attained. Once you have understood through P04 the nature-arising structure of "when one attains, all attain," P05 will follow naturally: the reason the one thought of initial aspiration *is* Perfect Enlightenment lies in the thoroughness of nature-arising doctrine at the seed-site of initial aspiration.

But before you move on to P05, please do not treat this paper as only a paper to be read. **This afternoon, walk to the Buddha.**

S8-P04 ends here.
