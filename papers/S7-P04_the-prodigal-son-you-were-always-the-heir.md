## Abstract

In the *Faith and Understanding* chapter (信解品) of the *Lotus Sūtra*, the four great disciples disclose their awakening through the Parable of the Prodigal Son: a wealthy elder's son leaves home as a child, wanders in poverty for over fifty years as a hired laborer, encounters his father without recognition, is gradually drawn into menial work (twenty years removing excrement), then incrementally entrusted with the family treasury, and finally — at the father's deathbed — publicly declared the heir before the assembly. This paper undertakes four tasks. First, it confirms (via systematic vault search) that the parable has no Āgama seed; it is purely Mahāyāna creation. But its deep motif is not isolated — it forms a structural family with the Jewel-in-the-Garment parable (also from the *Lotus*), the Poor Woman's Treasure and Warrior's Forehead Jewel parables (from the *Mahāparinirvāṇa-sūtra*), and the *Laṅkāvatāra*'s "great jewel wrapped in filthy garments" image. The paper offers the first systematic five-text structural comparison of this "originally-possessed-yet-unknown" (本有不知) family. Second, Vasubandhu's *Lotus Sūtra Treatise* identifies the Prodigal Son as the cure for the second of seven inverted graspings — "the śrāvaka who one-sidedly determines that his vehicle is no different from the Tathāgata's vehicle" — which forms an exact dual to the Burning House's first cure: "treating one as three" versus "treating three as one." Third, where S7-P03 found a rare convergence between Madhyamaka (Jizang) and Yogācāra (Kuiji) on the Burning House, this paper finds a striking divergence between the same three commentators on the Prodigal Son: Zhiyi and Jizang read universally (the prodigal = all śrāvakas), while Kuiji reads restrictively (the prodigal = *only* the indeterminate-lineage śrāvaka; the determinate-lineage one never becomes a Buddha). The paper argues that this P03/P04 reversal is itself a significant doxographical proposition: the Madhyamaka–Yogācāra relation is not constantly aligned nor constantly opposed but issue-specific — converging on epistemological questions (skillful means) and diverging on ontological ones (lineage). Fourth, the paper handles the Lotus–Yogācāra tension through the *Mahāyānasaṃgraha*'s "indeterminate-lineage as bridge" position and Fazang's *Awakening of Faith Commentary* mediation, but refuses cheap resolution. Kuiji's restriction has doctrinal seriousness; the paper presents both sides without pretending the tension has been dissolved.

**Keywords:** Prodigal Son parable; *Faith and Understanding* chapter; originally-possessed-yet-unknown; *tathāgatagarbha*; five-lineage doctrine (*pañca-gotra*); indeterminate lineage; backturned bodhicitta; Vasubandhu's *Lotus Treatise*; Jewel in the Garment; Poor Woman's Treasure; Warrior's Forehead Jewel

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 A Question About Identity

Have you ever had this experience — doing a job, feeling that this is the only job you deserve, and even glancing at any higher position triggers an immediate retreat, with an inner voice saying *no, that is not my place*?

This is not necessarily bad. Knowing one's measure and staying within it is, in worldly terms, often a virtue. But here is the question: how do you know that the measure you assume is your *actual* measure?

The prodigal son in the *Faith and Understanding* chapter has been wandering since childhood, scraping by as a day laborer until past fifty. He arrives, by chance, at his father's city. He enters his father's gate. He sees his father seated on a lion throne, surrounded by brahmins, kṣatriyas, and householders, adorned with pearl necklaces worth millions, his feet on a jeweled stool, a jeweled canopy over his head. The son's first reaction: *this is either a king or someone of a king's rank — this is not where someone like me earns a living*. Then he turns and runs.

The father recognizes him instantly. The father sends servants to bring him back; the son panics. The father later devises a strategy: he sends two shabby, unimposing men to hire the son for a job called "removing excrement" (除糞) — the lowest possible work. The father himself dresses in filthy clothes, picks up a dung-removal tool, and works alongside him, gradually building trust, even calling him "son." But this son, for *twenty years*, "still considered himself a hired laborer of low station" (猶故自謂客作賤人).

Twenty years. A person can spend twenty years living at the very center of his actual inheritance, seeing his father daily, hearing his name daily, being called "son" daily — and his inner self-judgment never moves: *I am only a hired hand*.

This parable strikes a very deep place. The question it asks is not "does the prodigal have money" or "can the prodigal work harder." It is more fundamental: **if your judgment about yourself is itself the wrong judgment, what could you possibly use to judge that this judgment is wrong?**

### 1.2 The Pairing of *Faith and Understanding* and *Parables*

S7-P03 examined the Burning House parable from the *Parables* chapter. There, the narrative protagonist is the father — the father sees the fire, the father devises means, the father calls the children, the father bestows the carriages. The children inside the burning house are "absorbed in their games, unaware, unfrightened" — structurally lacking agency. The father's skillful means (*upāya-kauśalya*) is the entire theme of the Burning House.

The Prodigal Son in the *Faith and Understanding* chapter shifts the angle. The speaker here is not the Buddha and not Ānanda, but the four great disciples — Subhūti, Kātyāyana, Kāśyapa, and Maudgalyāyana. After listening to the Burning House parable, they stand up of their own accord and say: *we now understand. Our situation is like that prodigal son*. Then they tell the parable themselves. This is an extraordinarily rare moment in the *Lotus Sūtra*: **the disciples voluntarily speak the parable, not the Buddha.**

This shift in narrative structure is significant. The Burning House is told by the father about the children (though the canonical setting is the Buddha addressing Śāriputra and others); the Prodigal Son is told by the children about themselves. The first is "the father knows that you do not know"; the second is "the son finally knows that he previously did not know." The first is *upāya* from above; the second is recognition from below. Together, they form the complete loop of skillful means and faith-understanding.

More precisely: the Burning House cures the *power-seeker* (求勢力人) — Vasubandhu's first of seven inverted graspings — one who pursues the contaminated fruits of human and divine pleasure, mistaking a better worldly position for the way out. The Prodigal Son cures the *seeker of śrāvaka liberation* (求聲聞解脫人) — Vasubandhu's second — one who has already entered the path but "one-sidedly determines that his vehicle is no different from the Tathāgata's vehicle," mistaking partial liberation for complete buddhahood. One cures *not knowing there is an exit*; the other cures *believing one has already exited when one is in fact still hired out as a day laborer*. The two inverted graspings face opposite directions: the first mistakes the contaminated for the uncontaminated; the second mistakes the small uncontaminated for the great. The cures are exactly inverse: the Burning House operates by **treating one as three** (in reality only the One Buddha-Vehicle exists, but expediently three carriages are taught), while the Prodigal Son operates by **treating three as one** (the labor of the three vehicles is not discarded, but all of it is gathered into the inheritance of the great vehicle).[^1]

This pairing is not a literary accident. It is the internal architecture of Vasubandhu's seven-parable system in the *Lotus Sūtra Treatise*: seven kinds of inverted graspings paired with seven cures, the first two forming the central dual — to the one who *does not know there is an exit*, the One Vehicle is taught; to the one who *believes himself already home*, his actual sonship is taught.

### 1.3 The Three Tasks of This Paper

From this structure, the research focus on the Prodigal Son lies not in "the father's skillful means" (which belongs to the Burning House) but in "the son's failure of recognition" — why does someone who is in fact the elder's son identify himself, at the very gate of his father's house, as a hired laborer of low station? What is the structure of this failure? How is it reversed? What does the reversal disclose?

The paper takes up three tasks around these questions.

**Task One: Establishing the "Originally-Possessed-Yet-Unknown" Parable Family.** The Prodigal Son has no Āgama seed (§2 will document this with a zero-result systematic vault search). But this absence is not a vacuum. It points to a deeper fact: the parable's roots lie not in the Āgamas but in a cross-textual Mahāyāna motif group — the Jewel in the Garment from the *Lotus*'s own *Five Hundred Disciples Receive Predictions* chapter, the Poor Woman's Treasure and Warrior's Forehead Jewel from the *Mahāparinirvāṇa-sūtra*, and the *Laṅkāvatāra*'s "great jewel wrapped in filthy garments." Together with the Prodigal Son itself, these five texts share an identical deep structure: **what is originally possessed + what is not noticed + skillful means + manifestation**. §3.2 will present the first systematic structural comparison.

**Task Two: The Inverse Structure of P03 and P04 Across the Three Commentators.** S7-P03 found that on the Burning House, Madhyamaka (Jizang) and Yogācāra (Kuiji) converge unusually on "there were no three carriages outside the gate" — a finding the author treated as significant. But on the Prodigal Son, the same three commentators (Zhiyi, Jizang, Kuiji) clearly diverge: Zhiyi and Jizang read universally (the prodigal = all śrāvakas), while Kuiji reads restrictively (the prodigal = the indeterminate-lineage śrāvaka only; the determinate-lineage one never becomes a Buddha). This P03/P04 reversal is not random. §3.3 will argue: **it discloses the issue-sensitivity of the Madhyamaka–Yogācāra relation — convergence on epistemological questions (skillful means) and divergence on ontological ones (lineage)**. This is both a supplement to and a correction of S7-P03's earlier conclusion, and it is a more refined description of the overall Madhyamaka–Yogācāra relation.

**Task Three: A Direct Treatment of the Lotus–Yogācāra Tension, Without Pretending It Is Resolved.** The tension between the *Lotus*'s "gathering three into one" and Yogācāra's "five distinct lineages" is real. The paper takes the *Mahāyānasaṃgraha* position ("for the indeterminate-lineage śrāvakas and other bodhisattvas, [the Buddha] speaks the One Vehicle to draw them into the Mahāyāna") together with Fazang's *Awakening of Faith Commentary* mediation ("under the conventional teaching, five lineages are distinct; under the real teaching, all sentient beings are included") as the mediating framework. But the paper refuses to declare the tension dissolved. Kuiji's stance — "if those bound for quiescence also became buddhas, this would contradict the *Mahāparinirvāṇa-sūtra* and many other passages" — has doctrinal seriousness. §3.4 will present both sides at full strength and let the reader see the actual contour of the tension.

### 1.4 Position in the Series

This paper is the fourth in the S7 Lotus series, following S7-P03 on the Burning House. S7-P03 established the three-layer argument that "skillful means is not deception" (Jizang/Zhiyi/Yogācāra) and offered the first test of the S2-P02 *Heart Sūtra* awakening loop on a *Lotus* parable. The work of the present paper extends P03's gains to the other side — if P03 was *how the father calls the son out of the burning house*, P04 is *how the son finally accepts that he was always the elder's son*. The two papers together form the complete starting point of the *Lotus*'s twin-track of skillful means and faith-understanding; the subsequent parables (Medicinal Herbs, Phantom City, Garment-Jewel, Topknot-Jewel, Physician) will all unfold along this twin track.

## 2. Textual Foundations

### 2.1 Sūtra Sources

#### 2.1.1 The Five-Stage Narrative in the *Faith and Understanding* Chapter

The Prodigal Son parable is initiated by the four great disciples (Subhūti, Kātyāyana, Kāśyapa, Maudgalyāyana) immediately after the Burning House parable. They tell the Buddha that hearing this rare and unprecedented dharma fills them with joy and understanding, and they declare: "Today we are truly *śrāvakas*, causing all to hear the voice of the Buddha-path; today we are truly *arhats*."[^2] This declaration is itself the parable's trigger: they become aware that their previous identification as *śrāvakas* was not the final station, and they cast this awareness into a parable.

The narrative unfolds in five stages.[^3]

**Stage 1 — Departure.** A son leaves home in childhood, wanders for ten, twenty, or fifty years, "growing up, becoming poor, dashing about in the four directions in search of food and clothing." Meanwhile his father settles in another city, becomes vastly wealthy, and — childless — broods on the loss of his son: "If only I could find my son and entrust the wealth to him, I would be at peace." Two parallel narrative lines: the son grows ever more distant and ever poorer; the father grows ever richer and ever more longing. They converge at the father's gate.

**Stage 2 — Approach and First Encounter.** "The destitute son, taking work from place to place, by chance arrived at his father's house." Note the word *by chance* (展轉). The son is not led to his father's door by some guiding force; he simply drifts there in his itinerant labor. To him, this is just one more potential employer. He stands at the gate and sees the elder seated on a lion throne, his feet on a jeweled stool, surrounded by brahmins, kṣatriyas, and householders, adorned with pearl necklaces worth millions. His reaction is immediate fear: "*This must be a king or someone of a king's rank — this is not a place where someone like me can earn his keep. Better to head to the poor quarter where I can work and find food and clothing easily; if I linger here I might be coerced and forced to labor.*" Then he runs.

**This active flight is one of the most critical narrative moments in the entire parable.** It shows that the son's problem is not "he cannot find his father" but "his father is right in front of him and he does not recognize him" — and not even "he fails to recognize and is confused" but "he fails to recognize, fears the recognition, and actively retreats." The son's self-judgment ("I am a hired hand; this is not my place") is strong enough, in the very moment of seeing his father, to drive him to turn and flee.

The father, in that instant, "sees his son and recognizes him, and his heart fills with joy." He immediately sends servants to bring him back. The son, seized, "cries out in terror: I have done nothing wrong, why am I being captured?" — and faints from fear. The father, watching, knows: "*The son's mind is small and weak; my own nobility frightens him*." So the father releases him: "Sprinkle cold water on his face to revive him, and do not speak to him further."

**Stage 3 — Hired Labor and the Removal of Excrement.** "The elder, intending to draw his son in, devised skillful means: he secretly sent two men of haggard appearance and lacking in dignity, instructing them: *Go to him and gently say — there is work here at double the wages. If he agrees, bring him back. If he asks what kind of work, tell him: removing excrement. We two will work alongside you*."

The father's stratagem has three layers. First, **the men sent must be haggard and undignified** — they must be people the son can identify as his own kind, so he will not feel the same distance that triggered his earlier flight. Second, **double wages** — a material attractor above market rate. Third, **the work must be removing excrement** — the lowest-status work imaginable, which the son's self-identification ("I am low-status labor") can accept. "We will work alongside you" — the messengers will be his peers, not supervisors. All three layers are calibrated to keep the gap between *the son's self-judged position* and *the actual position into which he is being drawn* small enough to avoid another panic-flight.

Then the father intervenes personally. "Looking again through a window, he saw his son emaciated and gaunt, smeared with dust and filth. He removed his necklaces, his fine outer garments, and his ornaments, put on coarse and soiled clothing, smeared dust on his body, took up a dung-collecting tool, and **assumed the manner of one who is afraid**." The phrase *assumed the manner of one who is afraid* (狀有所畏) is critical. The father deliberately performs a posture of timidity that matches his son's, to erase any felt power-differential between them.

The father says: "Hey, fellow! Stay and work here, do not go elsewhere, and I will increase your wages. Whatever you need — earthen vessels, rice, flour, salt, vinegar — do not hesitate to ask. There is even an old servant here you can call on. Be at ease in your mind. I am like a father to you; do not worry. I am old and you are young — I have watched you work, and you show none of the deceit, sloth, anger, or complaint of the other laborers. From now on, you are like a son born to me." Then the father formally gives him a name: "*Thus the elder gave him a new name and called him 'son'.*"

But the son's response: "Although the destitute son was glad at this treatment, **he still considered himself a hired laborer of low station** (猶故自謂客作賤人)." Although pleased, his **inner self-judgment did not move**. The two characters *猶故* ("still, as before") carry the entire central tension of the parable. You can raise a person's wages, give him kindness, give him the formal name "son" — and you cannot directly change his fundamental judgment about himself. "On account of this, for twenty years he constantly removed excrement." This state continued for twenty years.

**Stage 4 — Gradual Entrustment.** After twenty years, "the son's heart had gradually opened in mutual trust; he came and went without difficulty — yet he still lived in his original quarters." Note the last clause. The son's inner space had begun to open, but **his physical lodging remained his original simple quarters**. Psychological space had shifted; physical space had not. Acceptance of the father had grown; self-judgment had not.

Then the father falls ill and knows he will not live long. He tells his son: "I now have abundant gold, silver, and jewels; my storehouses overflow. You shall know what is in them, what should be taken and what should be given. This is my mind. Understand my intention. Why? Because now you and I are no different. Apply yourself; let nothing be lost." The father gives the son full management of the estate. The son's response: "Receiving this charge, he managed all the goods, the gold and silver, the jewels and storehouses, **but had no thought of taking even one meal's portion for himself**. And his lodging remained in its old place; **the mind of low station had not yet been cast off**."

Two crucial sentences. *No thought of taking even one meal's portion* — managing the entire estate, he never thought to take even a single meal's worth for himself. *The mind of low station had not yet been cast off* — the *xialie xin* (下劣心), the self-judgment as low and base, was still intact. Material position had completely arrived. Psychological judgment was still in place.

**Stage 5 — The Public Declaration of Inheritance.** "After a little time more, the father saw that his son's mind had gradually broadened, that he had achieved great resolve, and that he himself looked back with shame on his former mind. When death drew near, the father summoned his son, and gathering together his relatives, kings, ministers, kṣatriyas, and householders, all assembled, he then proclaimed before them all: *Gentlemen, take note. This is my son, born of me. He left me in such-and-such a city and wandered in hardship for over fifty years. His original name is such; mine is such. Long ago in my home city, full of grief, I sought him; here, by chance, I have found him. He is truly my son. I am truly his father. **All that I now possess belongs to my son**.*"

The father's actions in this final stage have four components: (1) **Convening witnesses** — relatives, kings, ministers, kṣatriyas, householders, all gathered, so that the declaration takes place under the maximum possible witness. (2) **Formal declaration of paternity** — "this is my son, born of me." (3) **Recounting of history** — the fifty years of separation are made explicit. (4) **Formal transfer of property** — "all that I now possess belongs to my son."

The son's response, this time, is changed: "Hearing his father's words, the son was filled with great joy, gaining what he had never possessed before, and he thought: **I never had any thought of seeking, and now this treasure has come to me of itself**." This sentence — *I never had any thought of seeking, and now this treasure has come to me of itself* — is the core line that all later commentary turns upon. It does not describe "I have at last obtained what I sought." It describes "I never sought, and yet the treasure came on its own." **This is the inverse face of "originally possessed yet unknown": when what is originally there is recognized, the recognizer's posture is not acquisition but acceptance of a fact that was always present.**

#### 2.1.2 The *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka* of Dharmarakṣa for Comparison

Dharmarakṣa's earlier *Zheng-fahua jing* (T0263) preserves the parable in a chapter titled *Faith and Joy* (信樂品) rather than *Faith and Understanding*.[^4] Three differences are worth noting.

**Time scale.** T0263 says "twenty or thirty years"; Kumārajīva's T0262 says "over fifty years." Whether due to a different source text or translator's choice, the doctrinal force is unaffected — both spans suffice to express prolonged wandering.

**No explicit excrement-removal stage.** In T0263, the father directly assigns the son to "tend the horses and carriages and maintain the treasures." The image of filth appears only later in the verse section, where treasures are said to be "buried in dung, unseen and unused." The strong "twenty years removing excrement" image is unique to Kumārajīva. **In Chinese Buddhist history, this single image has become the most concentrated symbol of the gradual *śrāvaka* path.** Zhiyi maps it onto the Thirty-Seven Aids to Awakening; later Chan masters use it as a teaching device. If only Dharmarakṣa's version had circulated, this image would not exist.

**The father's manner.** Dharmarakṣa's father is more openly affectionate: he reveals the son's identity early in a semi-private way, he personally bathes him, he says "I love you as a king loves his crown prince." Kumārajīva's father is restrained and strategic. The two versions can be read as two performances of the same parable — Kumārajīva is the *cool* version, Dharmarakṣa the *warm*. The Chinese tradition selected Kumārajīva, perhaps because the cool version's dramatic tension is greater: the twenty-year silence in which the son "still considered himself a hired laborer of low station" mirrors more accurately the actual inner structure of practitioners.

#### 2.1.3 No Āgama Seed — An Honest Marker

S7-P03 traced the Burning House to the *Saṃyuktāgama*'s *Sermon on Burning* (T0099, fascicle 8) and the conversion of Uruvilvā-Kāśyapa, establishing a clear structural parallel between the early Buddhist "fire" imagery and the *Lotus*'s "the three realms have no peace, like a burning house." The Prodigal Son has no such pedigree.

The RAG phase of this paper executed fourteen-plus keyword searches across all 225 files of the *vault-Āgama* corpus: "father and son separated," "remembering one's son," "father remembering son," "son forgetting father," "destitute son," "poor son," "lost son," "seeking son," "prodigal," "running away," "hired worker," "removing excrement," "menial labor," "recognition / failing to recognize one's father / son not knowing father," "losing oneself / forgetting one's origin." **All searches returned zero or unrelated results.**[^5]

Three near-neighbors exist but none qualify as seeds. (1) The "seven-sons-abandon-father" episode in *Bieyi za-ahan* (T0100, fascicle 13): a brahmin father is driven out by his seven ungrateful sons; the Buddha gives him a verse to recite publicly, and the sons are shamed into reconciliation. **Direction is reversed**: sons abandon father, not father seeks lost son. (2) The childless wealthy elder stories (Bhadrika, Mahānāman) in *Saṃyuktāgama* and *Ekottarāgama*: a rich man dies childless and his estate is forfeit to the king; the Buddha explains his karmic cause (stinginess toward a *pratyekabuddha*). **Structurally inverse**: there is no son at all. (3) The verse simile in *Saṃyuktāgama* fascicle 46 — "as one who travels far returns peacefully after long years" — is a metaphor for merit leading to good rebirth, not a father-son separation narrative.

The defining structural elements of the Prodigal Son — (a) father-son separation, (b) son wandering in poverty while father accumulates wealth, (c) father recognizes son but son does not recognize father, (d) father uses skillful means to gradually lead the son to accept his identity, (e) public revelation of paternity and inheritance at deathbed — **are entirely absent from the Āgama corpus**, not even as fragments or seeds.

This absence is itself a determination. The Prodigal Son is a purely Mahāyāna literary creation, designed to articulate a doctrinal structure not yet fully developed in the Āgamas. But this absence does not leave an empty space — it points to where the parable's actual structural lineage lies: not in the Āgamas, but in a cross-textual Mahāyāna motif group, to which I now turn.

#### 2.1.4 The "Originally-Possessed-Yet-Unknown" Motif Family — The Internal Seed

The deep motif of the Prodigal Son has four structurally identical companions in Mahāyāna literature — one inside the *Lotus* itself (Jewel in the Garment), two in the *Mahāparinirvāṇa-sūtra* (Poor Woman's Treasure and Warrior's Forehead Jewel), and one in the *Laṅkāvatāra* (the *tathāgatagarbha* wrapped in filthy garments).

**Jewel in the Garment** (*Lotus* §8, *Five Hundred Disciples Receive Predictions*).[^6] A man visits a friend's home and falls drunk. The friend, called away on official business, sews a priceless jewel into the inside of his garment and leaves. The man, drunk, knows nothing of it. When he wakes, he travels to another country, struggles for food and clothing, and "is content with a little." Years later, the friend meets him and says: "Why are you struggling so? On such-and-such a day in such-and-such a year, I sewed a priceless jewel into the inside of your garment. It is still there. You did not know. You have been suffering needlessly." Vasubandhu's *Treatise* identifies this parable as the cure for the fifth inverted grasping — "the scattered-mind self-conceit that has no concentration, who possesses past Mahāyāna roots without recognizing them."[^7]

**Poor Woman's Treasure** (*Mahāparinirvāṇa-sūtra*, *Tathāgata Nature* chapter; quoted via Kuiji's *Lotus Compendium*).[^8] "Like a poor woman in whose house there is much pure gold, but neither she nor anyone in her household knows of it. A skillful stranger comes and excavates the gold from within the house. The woman, seeing it, is filled with joy and looks up to him. Good son, the buddha-nature of sentient beings is like this. None can see it, just as the gold-treasured woman did not know. Good son, **I now reveal to all sentient beings their own buddha-nature: the skillful one is the Tathāgata, the poor woman is all immeasurable sentient beings, and the gold treasure is buddha-nature itself.**" The *Mahāparinirvāṇa-sūtra* gives its own gloss in plain text — a rarity in Mahāyāna parables. The Buddha himself names the correspondences and binds "originally possessed yet unknown" directly to the *tathāgatagarbha* claim that all sentient beings possess buddha-nature.

**Warrior's Forehead Jewel** (*Mahāparinirvāṇa-sūtra*, *Tathāgata Nature* chapter).[^9] "There was a strong warrior of the king's house, with a diamond jewel set in his forehead. Wrestling with another warrior, he struck his head and the jewel sank into the flesh; he himself did not know where it was. The wound was treated by a physician, who, knowing the jewel had entered, asked: *Where is your forehead jewel?* The warrior cried out: *Is it gone? Was it an illusion?* The physician comforted him: *In your fight the jewel sank into your flesh; it is now in your skin and shows through. In the heat of combat you did not feel it.* The warrior did not believe him and accused him of deceit. Then the physician held up a mirror, and the jewel appeared clearly in it. Seeing it, the warrior was struck with wonder."

This parable has a critical structural difference from the Prodigal Son: **the mode of revelation is the mirror**. The physician does not directly tell the warrior "the jewel is in your forehead"; he holds up a mirror so the warrior can see it himself. This is the structure of *self-witnessing*. The Prodigal Son is told by the father to the son; the Forehead Jewel is shown by the physician to the warrior so the warrior can see it himself. The first is testimony; the second is self-disclosure. The dual will be developed in §3.2.

**The *Laṅkāvatāra*'s *tathāgatagarbha* wrapped in filthy garments.**[^10] "The *tathāgatagarbha*, intrinsically pure, possessing the thirty-two marks, enters into the bodies of all sentient beings — like a great priceless jewel wrapped in filthy garments. The tathāgata-treasure is constant and unchanging. Yet wrapped by the filthy garments of the aggregates, sense-bases, and sense-elements, defiled by the dust and toil of greed, hatred, delusion, and unreal discriminations — thus do all the buddhas teach." The *Laṅkāvatāra* equates "originally possessed yet unknown" directly with the *tathāgatagarbha*: what is originally possessed is the intrinsically pure *tathāgatagarbha*; what conceals it is the filthy garment of aggregates, sense-bases, sense-elements, and the defilement of the three poisons. The Buddha then immediately distinguishes this from the *ātman* of the heretics — not a substantial *ātman*, but a verbal expedient to release those terrified by no-self. This distinction will be critical for §4.

These four parables together with the Prodigal Son constitute a structurally identical family of five texts. Their shared formula:

> **Originally possessed + not noticed + skillful means + manifestation**

The decisive feature of "manifestation" (*adhigama*) is that **it is not new acquisition**. The prodigal's "I never had any thought of seeking, and now this treasure has come to me of itself"; the drunk man's "I did not know there was a priceless jewel in my garment"; the poor woman's household "knew nothing of the gold beneath their floor"; the warrior's "did not know where the jewel was"; the *Laṅkāvatāra*'s "intrinsically pure yet wrapped in filthy garments" — all say the same thing.

This family is the Prodigal Son's actual seed. It is not in the Āgamas. It lives in the Mahāyāna's own doctrinal architecture — because the Mahāyāna tradition needed a parabolic language for "originally possessed yet unknown," and the same structure was repeated in different texts under different surface forms. The Prodigal Son is the most narratively complete and the most psychologically detailed version of this family.

### 2.2 Commentarial Sources

#### 2.2.1 Vasubandhu's *Lotus Treatise* — The Second Cure and "Treating Three as One"

Vasubandhu's *Saddharmapuṇḍarīkopadeśa* (T1519, trans. Bodhiruci; with parallel T1520 trans. Ratnamati) lays out the seven *Lotus* parables as cures for seven kinds of "self-conceit accompanied by full afflictive nature." The Prodigal Son is the second:[^11]

> The second is *the śrāvaka who one-sidedly determines that his vehicle is no different from the Tathāgata's vehicle* — this is inverted grasping. To cure this, the Prodigal Son parable is taught.

The cure: *treating three as one, leading them into the Mahāyāna* (以三為一,令入大乘).[^12] The precise structure of this cure deserves attention. **"Treating three as one" does not mean discarding the three vehicles** — it does not mean "there are no *śrāvakas*, *pratyekabuddhas*, or *bodhisattvas*." It means gathering the laborious work of all three vehicles (corresponding to the prodigal's twenty years of removing excrement) into the inheritance of the One Vehicle. The labor is not lost; it is recontextualized as belonging to a larger fact: "you were always the heir."

This is the precise dual of the Burning House's *treating one as three* (一作三): the Burning House tells the One Vehicle as three carriages to coax the children out; the Prodigal Son gathers the three vehicles into one inheritance to bring the son home. The first is differentiation for pedagogy; the second is unification for inheritance. The first is the *opening*; the second is the *gathering*. Vasubandhu's own treatment is austere — he gives the formula and moves on, leaving the psychological development to later commentators. But the formula itself is precise enough to anchor everything that follows.

#### 2.2.2 The Three Commentators in Brief

**Zhiyi (*Lotus Wenju*, T1718; *Lotus Xuanyi*, T1716).**[^13] Zhiyi divides the parable into five subdivisions and maps each onto his Five Flavors / Five Periods of teaching: the panic at first contact = Milk (Avataṃsaka); the secret dispatch of the two laborers and twenty years of dung-removal = Cream (Āgamas); growing trust = Curds (Vaipulya); managing the treasury without taking = Butter (Prajñāpāramitā); the public declaration = Ghee (Lotus). The five-flavor mapping is Tiantai's central evidence for its own five-period classification of the entire Buddhist canon. Zhiyi also famously maps the twenty years of removing excrement to the Thirty-Seven Aids to Awakening (the four foundations of mindfulness, four right efforts, and so on, ending in the seven factors of enlightenment). Zhiyi reads the prodigal as **all *śrāvakas***, with no lineage restriction.

**Jizang (*Lotus Yishu*, T1721).**[^14] Jizang divides the parable into ten subdivisions (more granular than Zhiyi's five) and contributes two important points. First, **the Burning House cures the disease of ordinary beings; the Prodigal Son cures the disease of the two vehicles** — a clean partition that aligns precisely with Vasubandhu's seven-cure ordering. Second, his "two teachings, two bodies" analysis: the father's hiring of the son to remove excrement = *concealing the real and raising the expedient* (concealing the true teaching and erecting a provisional one); the father's working alongside the son = *concealing the original and manifesting the trace* (concealing the original body and manifesting an apparitional body). The threefold removal — necklaces (concealing liberation), fine clothing (concealing prajñā), ornaments (concealing dharmakāya) — manifests as the apparitional body wearing the surface marks of affliction. Jizang's insight: **the Prodigal Son's father is not only "concealing the great teaching and revealing the small" (teaching-side) but also "concealing the original body and manifesting the apparitional" (body-side) — together these form the core architecture of Mahāyāna buddhology.** Jizang too reads the prodigal universally, with no lineage restriction.

**Kuiji (*Lotus Xuanzan*, T1723).**[^15] Kuiji's reading stands in starkest contrast. He does not read the prodigal as "all *śrāvakas*" but specifically as **the indeterminate-lineage śrāvaka only**. Following the four-*śrāvaka* taxonomy traditionally attributed to Vasubandhu's *Lotus Treatise*, Kuiji distinguishes (1) the determinate-lineage / quiescence-bound *śrāvaka* (定姓 / 趣寂), who enters final nirvāṇa with body extinguished and consciousness ceased; (2) the conceited *śrāvaka*, who mistakes the fourth dhyāna for arhatship; (3) the back-turned-bodhicitta *śrāvaka* (退菩提心), who originally aroused great mind but later regressed; and (4) the apparitional *śrāvaka*, who is a bodhisattva manifesting in *śrāvaka* form.

Kuiji's key statement: "*The Lotus Treatise says: in this assembly, predictions are given only to two kinds of śrāvakas, namely the back-turned and the apparitional. The quiescence-bound and the conceited do not receive predictions, because their roots are not yet ripe.*"[^16] This is doctrinally hard. In Kuiji's reading, the *Lotus*'s "gathering three into one" does not unconditionally include the entire third — **the determinate-lineage śrāvaka has no Mahāyāna seed and the *Lotus* does not give him a prediction**. His reason: "If those bound for quiescence also became buddhas, this would contradict the *Mahāparinirvāṇa-sūtra* and many other passages."

Thus the prodigal in Kuiji is the indeterminate-lineage *śrāvaka* — one who possesses Mahāyāna seeds but has not yet activated them, who has "regressed into birth-and-death because his mind was not firm and he did not delight in the Buddha," and who at the *Lotus* assembly turns his mind toward the great. Kuiji's Yogācāra technical reading also maps the twenty years to the preparatory and uninterrupted paths, "taking the wages first" to the initial arousal of two-vehicle bodhicitta in study and reflection, and "growing up" to the maturation of the practice-lineage — but these technical mappings are not the focus here.[^17] What matters is the **lineage boundary**: Kuiji draws one; Zhiyi and Jizang do not.

**Summary table:**

| Commentator | School | Prodigal = | Lineage restriction? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Zhiyi (Wenju, Xuanyi) | Tiantai | All *śrāvakas* | No |
| Jizang (Yishu) | Sanlun (Madhyamaka) | All *śrāvakas* | No |
| Kuiji (Xuanzan) | Faxiang (Yogācāra) | **Indeterminate-lineage śrāvakas only** | **Yes** |

This divergence is not philological — it is a difference in fundamental doxographical stance. §3.3 will argue that **this divergence reverses the convergence found in S7-P03 on the Burning House**, and that the reversal itself is a structural fact about the Madhyamaka–Yogācāra relation.

## 3. Core Arguments

### 3.1 The Five-Stage Narrative as an Awakening Loop — Second Test of S2-P02

S7-P03 tested the *Heart Sūtra* awakening loop (illuminate → examine → deepen → cross over) on the Burning House and found structural identity. The Prodigal Son admits the same test:

| *Heart Sūtra* loop (S2-P02) | Prodigal Son | Agent |
|---|---|---|
| Illuminate (seeing the five aggregates as empty) | Father sees and recognizes son | Father |
| Examine (observing grasping) | Father knows the son's mind is small and weak | Father |
| Deepen (entering prajñā) | Father wears filthy clothes and works alongside | Father |
| Cross over (saving from all suffering) | Father convenes assembly and declares paternity | Father |

Note: all four nodes are executed by the father. This is the shared feature of *Lotus* parables in contrast with the Jewel-in-the-Garment parable (where the son tells the story of himself). **The *Heart Sūtra*'s internal awakening loop is "externalized" in the *Lotus* parables** — Avalokiteśvara's inner contemplative loop is recast as the father's outward skillful action. This externalization is a recurring feature; subsequent papers on Medicinal Herbs, Phantom City, and Physician will see it again. The S7 capstone will return to this cross-parable observation as a unified loop-thesis.

### 3.2 The Structural Comparison of the "Originally-Possessed-Yet-Unknown" Family

§2.1.4 has presented the five texts. The structural comparison:

| Parable | Originally possessed | Concealment | Mode of revelation | Revealer | Recognizer's posture |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Prodigal Son | Sonship (inheritance) | Self-judgment "hired hand" | Public assembly declaration | Father | Treasure came of itself |
| Jewel in the Garment | Priceless jewel sewn in | Drunkenness | Friend points to the jewel | Friend | Immediate use |
| Poor Woman's Treasure | Pure gold beneath the floor | None of the household knew | Stranger excavates | Skillful one (= Tathāgata) | Joy and reverence |
| Warrior's Forehead Jewel | Diamond in the forehead | Sunk into the flesh | **Mirror — the warrior sees it himself** | Physician (via mirror) | Wonder |
| *Laṅkāvatāra* *tathāgatagarbha* | Intrinsically pure *garbha* | Filthy garments of aggregates | Buddhas teach it | All buddhas | (not narrated) |

The shared formula: **originally possessed + not noticed + skillful means + manifestation**. "Manifestation" is *not* new acquisition — this is the lifeline of the entire family.

The internal differentiation among the five is itself instructive. The *mode of revelation* column splits four ways:

- **Jewel in the Garment** = individual pointing-out (one-on-one, sudden);
- **Prodigal Son** = institutional confirmation (public, witnessed, formal);
- **Warrior's Forehead Jewel** = self-witnessing (the teacher gives only the mirror; the student sees);
- ***Laṅkāvatāra*** = scriptural exposition (verbal teaching as foundation).

A mature transmission lineage requires all four modes. The Prodigal Son's specific role in the family is **institutional confirmation** — emphasized by the convening of relatives, kings, ministers, kṣatriyas, and householders, the explicit recounting of the fifty-year separation, and the formal proclamation. **This public dimension distinguishes the Prodigal Son from its relatives**: it is not only about an inner turn, but about how that turn becomes publicly established as fact. The *Mahāparinirvāṇa-sūtra*'s gloss on the Poor Woman's Treasure — "the skillful one is the Tathāgata, the poor woman is all sentient beings, the gold treasure is buddha-nature" — links the entire family to the *tathāgatagarbha* claim that all sentient beings possess buddha-nature.[^18]

### 3.3 The P03/P04 Reversal — Issue-Sensitivity of the Madhyamaka–Yogācāra Relation

S7-P03 found that on the Burning House, Madhyamaka (Jizang) and Yogācāra (Kuiji) converge unusually on "there were no three carriages outside the gate." The author treated this as a substantive finding: two schools reaching the same conclusion via different routes — Madhyamaka via "all dharmas are empty, so the expedient need not be substantively real," Yogācāra via "the post-attainment cognition's worldly operation is not a downgraded truth but truth's worldly mode."

In the present paper, the same three commentators on the Prodigal Son visibly diverge. Zhiyi and Jizang are universalist; Kuiji is restrictive. This P03/P04 reversal cannot be explained as random ("Kuiji sometimes agrees with Jizang and sometimes does not"). It has a structural cause:

**The two issues fall on different axes.**

The Burning House's core issue is **upāya** — "Are there really three carriages outside the gate? Is the expedient a deception? Is the post-attainment cognition's operation a downgraded truth?" These are *epistemological* questions, concerning the truth-status of a teaching device. On this axis, Madhyamaka and Yogācāra converge at shallow depth, because both schools agree that "the expedient is not deception" — Madhyamaka because the empty has no need to be substantively real, Yogācāra because the post-attainment cognition just *is* truth's operation in the conventional. The conclusions can align.

The Prodigal Son's core issue is **gotra** — "Can the determinate-lineage *śrāvaka* really become a buddha? Is the difference among the five lineages a fact or an expedient?" These are *ontological* questions, concerning the actual structure of sentient beings. On this axis, Madhyamaka and Yogācāra diverge deeply, because the two schools differ on the status of "the real" itself: Madhyamaka tends to treat lineage difference too as dependently arisen and ultimately empty, so it can incorporate all sentient beings on equal footing. Yogācāra recognizes real differences in the seeds within the storehouse-consciousness, so the fact of five lineages is doctrinally serious and cannot be dissolved at will.

**Hence a refinement of S7-P03's conclusion:** the Madhyamaka–Yogācāra relation is neither constantly aligned nor constantly opposed — it is **issue-sensitive**:

> Convergence on epistemological issues (skillful means, teaching, cognition);
> Divergence on ontological issues (lineage, seeds, the structure of the real).

P03 and P04 are not two independent findings but a paired evidence-set. Any account of the two-school relation that simplifies it to "agreement" or "disagreement" will be falsified by cross-parable systematic study.

### 3.4 The Yogācāra Tension — The *Saṃgraha* Bridge and Its Limits

How are the *Lotus*'s "gathering three into one" and Yogācāra's "five distinct lineages" to be reconciled? Two main routes exist within the Yogācāra tradition.

**Route One: The *Saṃgraha* bridge — using the indeterminate-lineage as the connecting point.** The *Mahāyānasaṃgraha* gives the foundational verse:[^19]

> For the indeterminate-lineage *śrāvakas* and the other bodhisattvas, the One Vehicle is taught to draw them into the Mahāyāna; for the determinate-lineage, the One Vehicle is taught only in the ultimate sense (because dharma, no-self, and liberation are equal) — this is the doctrine of the One Vehicle in the ultimate.

Kuiji presses this position to its sharpest formulation in the *Dasheng Fayuan Yilin Zhang*: "The *Lotus* assembly addresses the indeterminate; the two-vehicle teachings are expedient, the One Vehicle is real... and the *Lotus*'s One Vehicle, taught only in dependence on lineage-possessing beings, is itself expedient [for that audience]; the *Śrīmālā Sūtra*'s One Vehicle, which depends even on those without lineage, is the real."[^20] Kuiji thus *demotes* the *Lotus* from a universalist instrument that gathers all sentient beings into a more restricted vehicle directed at the indeterminate lineage.

**Route Two: Fazang's mediation — the two-tier reading of conventional and real teachings.** Fazang's *Awakening of Faith Commentary* offers the alternative:[^21]

> The audience of this teaching has two modes. First, under the conventional teaching, it concerns the bodhisattva-lineage and the indeterminate, but not the other three (since they have no part) — as the *Yogācārabhūmi* and others say. Second, under the real teaching, all sentient beings are its audience, since none will fail ultimately to attain bodhi.

Fazang reads "five distinct lineages" as a *conventional* judgment, applicable to currently-classifiable capacities. *In the real teaching*, all sentient beings will ultimately attain bodhi. He cites the *Buddha-Nature Treatise*'s claim that "saying there are some without lineage permanently is non-ultimate teaching" and argues that the determinate-lineage *śrāvaka* "must enter the remainderless before he can turn his mind" — i.e., even after entering the remainderless nirvāṇa, he still has the "three remainders" (the abode of ignorance, uncontaminated karma, the mind-made body), and he eventually turns toward the great.[^22]

Together, the two routes provide a workable mediation: the prodigal can be read as **the indeterminate lineage** (the *Saṃgraha* route), or as **the determinate lineage who will eventually turn after entering the remainderless** (Fazang's route). Either way, the *Lotus*'s "gathering three into one" can coexist with Yogācāra's "five distinct lineages."

**But the paper must stop here and offer an honest observation: this mediation does not fully dissolve the tension; it only relocates it.**

The relocation is to the question of *when* the determinate-lineage *śrāvaka* turns. Kuiji's hard position: never. This position is supported directly by the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s *Lineage Chapter* (fascicle 35): "Of the lineage-less *pudgala*, even though one may possess the basis for arousing the mind and undertaking practice, lacking lineage, he is by determination unfit to fulfill supreme perfect awakening."[^23] Fazang's softer position: after entering the remainderless. This position has the *Buddha-Nature Treatise*'s "non-ultimate teaching" determination as support.

**Both positions have scriptural authority, and the underlying scriptural traditions on this question were never fully unified within Mahāyāna itself.** Kuiji's authorities cluster in the early Yogācāra treatises (*Yogācārabhūmi*, *Mahāyāna-sūtrālaṃkāra*); Fazang's cluster in the *tathāgatagarbha* corpus and later treatises (*Buddha-Nature Treatise*, *Ratnagotravibhāga*, *Laṅkāvatāra*). **The two positions represent two fundamentally different Mahāyāna answers to the question of whether the inner structure of sentient beings has real differences** — and to read the *Lotus*'s single phrase "gathering three into one" as directly resolving this question is to read the *Lotus* too lightly.

The paper's stance: **present both, do not pretend to dissolve.** Kuiji's restriction has doctrinal seriousness — it defends a critical principle: that the inner structure of sentient beings cannot be flattened into the rhetoric of equality. The *Saṃgraha*–Fazang mediation has equal seriousness — it defends another principle: that lineage difference cannot be made an unbridgeable ontological gulf that renders the *Lotus*'s One Vehicle a hollow rhetorical gesture. Both principles deserve preservation. Neither should be used to crush the other.

### 3.5 Why the Tension Cannot Be Cheaply Dissolved

A reader may ask: if the *Saṃgraha*–Fazang mediation already provides a workable reconciliation, why does this paper insist that the tension cannot be cheaply dissolved?

The answer is methodological, not doctrinal. **A reader who finishes the *Lotus* and concludes "Kuiji's five lineages have been refuted by the *Lotus*" will miss the entire seriousness of Mahāyāna's wrestling with the *gotra* question.** He will read the *Lotus* as an optimistic slogan rather than as an engagement with a deep doctrinal problem.

The Prodigal Son's own narrative supports caution. Remember the twenty years — "for twenty years he constantly removed excrement... still considering himself a hired laborer of low station." The father knew his son was his son from the very first glance. But the father did not declare it immediately. The father used twenty years to slowly let the son's inner structure shift to the point where it could *receive* the fact. Why? **Because "the son is the son" is a fact that the son himself must accept, and the son's "mind of low station" is real, has weight, and cannot be overwritten by external proclamation.**

The twenty-year wait is the Prodigal Son's own internal handling of the lineage problem. It does not say "you are the heir, so none of this matters." It says "you are the heir, but your mind of low station is real; I must wait for your mind-structure to gradually open before I can declare; and until then, you must work in the form of removal-of-excrement that you can accept." **This is the *Lotus*'s internal respect for the Yogācāra position** — the *Lotus* does not deny the reality of "the mind of low station"; it does not skip the necessity of gradual ripening; it does not use a single phrase "gathering three into one" to overwrite the labor of the *śrāvaka* path.

If this reading holds, then the *Lotus*–Yogācāra tension is not a problem to be *solved* but a fact to be *jointly held*: the *Lotus* gives the ultimate target (the son was always the heir); Yogācāra gives the intermediate structure (the reality of the mind of low station and the necessity of gradual ripening). Together, they constitute the complete meaning of the parable.

## 4. Middle-Way Corrections

❌ **Do not slide "originally possessed buddha-nature" into Christian grace or Hindu *ātman*.** "Always being the heir" is easily assimilated by modern readers to "child of God" (Christianity) or "*ātman* identical to *brahman*" (Vedānta). Both readings are seriously off. Christian grace is actively bestowed by a personal giver in a "fall–redemption" structure; the parable's "originally possessed" has no giver — the inheritance is not what the father gives, it is the relation of paternity itself. Vedānta's *ātman* is a substantial true-self identical with *brahman*; the parable's "originally possessed" does not point to an internal substantial self but to a structural position (inheritance). A position can be recognized or unrecognized, but a position is not a thing. The *Laṅkāvatāra* itself, in the *tathāgatagarbha* passage, immediately distinguishes "not the *ātman* of the heretics" and lists ten-plus expedient synonyms (emptiness, signlessness, wishlessness, suchness, reality-limit, dharmakāya, nirvāṇa, no-arising, original quiescence, intrinsic nirvāṇa) precisely to block substantialist readings.

❌ **Do not read "originally possessed" as substantial existence.** This is the refined version of the previous correction. Even after avoiding the obvious heretical-self trap, a reader may still treat "the originally possessed inheritance" as "an inner thing always existing, waiting to be discovered." The parable does not support this. The prodigal's inheritance is not a *thing* hidden inside him to be unearthed. It is a relational structure that exists from the moment paternity holds, but requires confirmation (by the father convening the assembly) and acceptance (by the son's inner shift) to become *actually* operative inheritance. **"Originally possessed" is** *already there***, not** *substantially there*. The *Awakening of Faith*'s formulation makes this exact: "the *tathāgatagarbha* originally possesses pure qualities exceeding the sands of the Ganges, not different from suchness; defiled qualities exceeding the sands of the Ganges are merely deluded existence with no intrinsic nature, never having corresponded to the *tathāgatagarbha* from beginningless time."[^24] The *garbha*'s "originally possessed" is one with suchness, not a separately erected entity.

❌ **Do not deny Kuiji's doctrinal seriousness in order to defend "*Lotus* universalism."** This is the correction aimed at enthusiastic *Lotus* readers. After finishing the parable, it is easy to conclude "Kuiji is too narrow; the *Lotus* has already gathered all sentient beings into the One Vehicle; the five-lineage doctrine should be abolished." §3.4 and §3.5 have argued at length: this conclusion reads the *Lotus* too lightly. Kuiji's restriction protects an important fact — *the reality of difference in the inner structure of sentient beings*. If "gathering three into one" is read as "all differences are unreal," then the prodigal's "mind of low station," the twenty years of removing excrement, the necessity of gradual ripening — all are vacated. The *Lotus* itself does not vacate them. Kuiji's reading aims to read the *Lotus* *heavier*, not narrower. The genuine Middle Way: the *Lotus* gives the ultimate target, Yogācāra gives the intermediate structure, both held jointly.

❌ **Do not romanticize the twenty years of removing excrement as a "spiritual journey."** Modern spiritual writing likes to read "removing excrement" as "humble and sacred inner work," "the dark night of the soul," "the practice of humility." This reading skips one fact: *for the prodigal, removing excrement was just removing excrement*. It was not romantic and not sacred. It was filthy, exhausting, low-paying (despite the doubled wages) actual labor that lasted twenty years, during which the son's inner state remained "still considering himself a hired laborer of low station" — he did not, in those twenty years, sanctify the work in his own mind. He did what he could do, what he believed himself fit to do. **The parable's force comes precisely from this realism** — it does not let the son have a sudden enlightenment in the middle of the twenty years, does not let removing excrement become a moment of awakening, does not let the father appear midway to disclose the truth. It lets twenty years be twenty years, dung be dung, the mind of low station be the mind of low station. **This realism is the parable's respect for the practitioner's actual situation** — the middle of practice contains a long, unromantic, no-end-in-sight working stretch that cannot be skipped.

## 5. Practice Implications

If the parable is being read by a contemporary practitioner, what is verifiable now?

**First, you do not know whether you are determinate or indeterminate lineage.** Kuiji's classification is an external doctrinal judgment; *who you are* is a question for the practice level, not the doctrinal level. From the standpoint of practice, you must never classify yourself as "determinate-lineage *śrāvaka*, so the *Lotus* does not address me" — that self-classification is a contemporary form of the prodigal turning to flee at his father's gate. At the same time, you must not classify yourself as "I am always the heir, so all this work is unnecessary" — that self-classification is the mind of low station moving in the opposite direction.

**Second, "still considering oneself a hired laborer of low station" is a verifiable inner structure right now.** No need to wait for awakening. Ask yourself: when you are placed in a high position, given an opportunity beyond your usual range, or in the presence of someone whose attainment exceeds yours, is your first reaction *no, that is not my place*? If yes, you have located the contemporary form of the prodigal's mind of low station in yourself. This reaction does not need to be suppressed (suppression becomes another form of arrogance) or affirmed (affirmation misses its hollowness) — it needs to be **seen**. After being seen, it will continue to arise, but the seeing is itself the beginning of change.

**Third, twenty years is an honest time-estimate.** The prodigal's twenty years is not literary exaggeration; it is the realistic order of magnitude for the kind of inner-structural change practice requires. If you expect that finishing a book, hearing a teaching, or completing a retreat will dissolve the mind of low station, you will repeatedly be disappointed. Real change requires sustained, end-not-in-sight, unromantic work. The promise the parable offers is not "rapid awakening" but "the father is waiting" — and *that waiting itself* is a fact the practitioner can rely on.

---

## 6. Conclusion

The core insight of the Prodigal Son can be summarized in one sentence: **you are not acquiring your inheritance; you are recognizing a fact that was always present.**

But this recognition is not easy. It involves twenty years of removing excrement, countless instances of "still considering himself a hired laborer of low station," and the entire long wait before the father convenes the assembly and declares paternity. The *Lotus* does not make this recognition cheap; Yogācāra does not make it impossible. The two together form the parable's complete structure: **the ultimate fact is that you were always the heir (the *Lotus*'s target), but the mind of low station is real and requires gradual transformation (Yogācāra's intermediate structure).**

For daily practice: do not affix lineage labels to yourself (whether "determinate" or "indeterminate"); do not suppress or affirm the mind of low station, only see it; do not expect rapid awakening, accept that twenty years is a reasonable order of magnitude; do not romanticize the removing of excrement, but do not abandon it because it is unromantic. The father is waiting.

**Preview of S7-P05 — Medicinal Herbs Parable.** The Burning House handled skillful means (treating one as three), the Prodigal Son handled faith and understanding (treating three as one). The Medicinal Herbs Parable will handle a different dimension — *the differences within universal moistening*. One cloud rains, and great trees and small grasses each receive what they need: "the moistening pervades all; herbs, trees, and forests each receive their share." The core question of Medicinal Herbs is: **why does the same teaching produce different effects in different capacities? Is the difference a problem of the teaching or a problem of the capacity?** This question will push the lineage tension of §3.4 to a further level and open new space for treatment.
