## Abstract

The ālaya-vijñāna (storehouse consciousness) is the foundational basis of Yogācāra philosophy — the repository of all experiential seeds, the vehicle for karmic continuity, and the ground from which body and world are projected. This paper takes Xuanzang's three verses on the eighth consciousness from the *Verses on the Characteristics of the Eight Consciousnesses* (八識規矩頌) as its structural framework, unfolding the ālaya-vijñāna's complete doctrine with equal weight given to sūtra and treatise sources.

§2 establishes the scriptural foundations in two parts: Part A (Sūtras) opens with the *Ghanavyūha Sūtra* (密嚴經) — the most comprehensive sūtra teaching on the ālaya-vijñāna — followed by the *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra*'s three-name teaching and torrent metaphor, the *Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra*'s storehouse-ocean and tathāgatagarbha, the *Avataṃsaka Sūtra*'s "three realms are mind-only," and the Āgamas' dependent origination and karmic continuity. Part B (Treatises) presents the *Cheng Weishi Lun*'s seven proofs and the *Mahāyānasaṃgraha*'s three aspects with learning-perfumation.

§3 unfolds the core argument in six layers: (1) nature and associated mental factors via the first verse; (2) the three storehouse meanings and ocean-wave metaphor via the second verse; (3) the seed mechanism through the six characteristics and four perfumation conditions; (4) the three great functions — receiving perfumation, holding seeds, and projecting body-and-world; (5) the mechanism of birth and death; (6) the three stages of transformation with learning-perfumation as the active path and the three-school debate as doctrinal depth. §4 offers the "hard drive" as a heuristic cross-disciplinary analogy. §5 provides five middle-way corrections. §6 concludes.

**Keywords:** ālaya-vijñāna, *Ghanavyūha Sūtra*, Verses on Eight Consciousnesses, three storehouse meanings, six seed characteristics, receiving perfumation and holding seeds, body and world, transformation of the basis (āśraya-parāvṛtti), learning-perfumation (śruta-vāsanā), great mirror wisdom (ādarśa-jñāna)

## I. Introduction

### 1.1 Textual Position

S3-P01 unfolded the complete map of the hundred dharmas — the five-category framework and the trisvabhāva methodology. S3-P02 surveyed the eight consciousnesses through the lens of the three transformations (tri-pariṇāma). Beginning with this paper, we enter the first deep coordinate on that map: the ālaya-vijñāna.

Among the eight consciousnesses in the *Śatadharmaprakāśamukha-śāstra* (百法明門論), the ālaya-vijñāna is listed eighth.[^1] But "eighth" does not mean least important — it is the most fundamental (mūlāśraya), the holder of all seeds, the basis from which body and world are projected. Understanding the ālaya-vijñāna is the prerequisite for understanding the entire Yogācāra system.

The three verses on the eighth consciousness in the *Verses on the Characteristics of the Eight Consciousnesses* encompass its complete doctrine:

> Its nature is solely unobscured, with only the five universal factors; / It follows others' karma through realms and grounds; / The Two Vehicles fail to understand, confused by attachment; / Thus the masters of doctrine engage in debate.[^2]

> Vast and fathomless, the three storehouses cannot be exhausted; / Deep as an abyss — seven waves, with sense-objects as wind; / Receiving perfumation, holding seeds, sustaining body and world; / Departing last, arriving first, serving as master.[^3]

> Only before the Immovable Ground is the storehouse name relinquished; / After the vajra path, the vipāka is emptied; / Great Mirror and Immaculate arise simultaneously, / Illuminating the dust-mote lands of the ten directions.[^4]

Three verses, three layers: the first describes its nature — what it is; the second describes its functions — what it does; the third describes its transformation — where it goes. This paper unfolds each layer in sequence.

### 1.2 The Problem

The doctrinal core of the ālaya-vijñāna addresses one of the deepest problems in Buddhist philosophy: **If there is no self, how does karma continue?**

The Buddha taught anātman (no-self), yet also taught that cause and effect are unfailing and that karma continues across lifetimes. Without a permanent "self" bearing the consequences, who receives them? Without a soul traveling from life to life, what is the vehicle of karmic continuity?

The Sectarian Buddhist schools proposed various solutions — the Vātsīputrīya school's "pudgala" (person), the Sautrāntika's "ekarasa-skandha" (single-flavor aggregate), the Mahīśāsaka's "lifetime-spanning aggregate." But these solutions either veered too close to eternalism (a permanent self) or to annihilationism (everything ceases at death).

The Yogācāra answer is the ālaya-vijñāna: a **momentarily arising-and-ceasing torrent of seeds**, impermanent yet uninterrupted, neither annihilated nor eternal. It is not "you," but it continuously sustains the process called "your life."

This paper addresses three core questions. First, how is the ālaya-vijñāna's existence established — why must this consciousness exist? Second, what is its operational mechanism — how do seeds perfume, how are they held, how do they project body and world? Third, how does it transform — what is the path from defilement to purity?

### 1.3 Hundred Dharmas Coverage and Structure

This paper covers six dharmas from the hundred: the ālaya-vijñāna (eighth consciousness) from the mind-dharmas, and its five associated universal mental factors — contact (sparśa), attention (manaskāra), feeling (vedanā), perception (saṃjñā), and volition (cetanā). The ālaya-vijñāna associates only with these five; it does not associate with object-specific, wholesome, or afflictive mental factors.

The paper follows a sūtra-first structure. §2 is divided into two parts: Part A presents the Buddha's own teachings from the *Ghanavyūha*, *Saṃdhinirmocana*, *Laṅkāvatāra*, *Avataṃsaka*, and the Āgamas; Part B presents the systematic treatise expositions in the *Cheng Weishi Lun* and *Mahāyānasaṃgraha*. §3 unfolds the core argument in six layers following the three-verse structure: nature → storehouse meanings → seed mechanism → three functions → birth-and-death mechanism → transformation stages. §4 offers the hard-drive analogy as a heuristic. §5 provides middle-way corrections. §6 concludes.

## II. Scriptural Foundations

### Part A: The Buddha's Teachings

### 2.1 The Ghanavyūha Sūtra — The Buddha Teaches the Ālaya-vijñāna

Among all Mahāyāna sūtras, the *Ghanavyūha Sūtra* (大乘密嚴經) offers the most complete direct teaching on the ālaya-vijñāna. The Buddha, in the Ghanavyūha Pure Land, teaches Vajragarbha Bodhisattva about the nature, functions, and transformation of the ālaya-vijñāna, establishing through canonical authority that the ālaya is not a treatise invention but the Buddha's own teaching.

The sūtra uses the torrent metaphor to describe the ālaya's dynamic operation:

> "The ālaya-vijñāna in the world is likewise: beginningless habitual impressions flow like a torrent, stirred by the winds of sense-objects, generating consciousness-waves without interruption."[^5]

At the same time, the sūtra declares that this consciousness is "inherently luminous in nature" — although perfumed by habitual impressions, its nature remains pure: "The ālaya-vijñāna, though dwelling together with its perfumers, all mental dharmas, and all defiled and pure seeds, is inherently luminous in nature."[^6]

The sūtra uses the maṇi-jewel to illustrate the relationship between seeds and the consciousness: "The storehouse-consciousness holds all seeds, / Wrapped in habitual impressions, / Like a maṇi jewel / That manifests various colors according to conditions. / Though dwelling in the body of sentient beings, / Its essence is pure and undefiled — / It is the definitive nature, / And also great nirvāṇa."[^7] The maṇi jewel displays various colors according to conditions while its substance remains pure. The ālaya holds all seeds and manifests all dharmas according to conditions, yet the consciousness-body itself is "pure and undefiled."

The sūtra's most crucial verse directly establishes the identity of tathāgatagarbha and ālaya-vijñāna:

> "The Buddha teaches that the tathāgatagarbha / Is the ālaya-vijñāna. / Those of inferior wisdom cannot understand / That the garbha is precisely the ālaya. / The tathāgata's pure treasury, / The world's ālaya-vijñāna — / Like gold and a gold ring, / There is ultimately no difference between them."[^8]

The tathāgatagarbha is the pure aspect; the ālaya-vijñāna is the worldly aspect — "like gold and a gold ring, ultimately no different." Gold is the material; the ring is the form. The tathāgatagarbha is the substance; the ālaya-vijñāna is the function. The form can change, but the material does not vanish.

The sūtra further deploys the metaphors of earth and rivers to unfold the ālaya's role as the basis of all consciousnesses: "Just as all things grow from the great earth — all sentient beings and even precious treasures — the storehouse consciousness is likewise the basis of all consciousnesses"; "Just as a hundred rivers flow day and night into the great ocean, the rivers never exhausted, the ocean never discriminating — the storehouse consciousness is likewise fathomlessly deep, and the habitual impressions of all consciousnesses flow into it day and night."[^9] All consciousnesses depend on the ālaya, and all habitual impressions are stored within it — just as a hundred rivers flow ceaselessly into the ocean, which never discriminates.

Regarding how the ālaya sustains the physical body, the sūtra teaches: "The ālaya dwells in the body, holding all seeds, pervading and sustaining lifespan, warmth, and consciousness, like clouds covering the world — its function never ceases, yet sentient beings cannot perceive it."[^10] The phrase "pervading and sustaining lifespan, warmth, and consciousness" is the canonical basis for the verse's "receiving perfumation, holding seeds, sustaining body and world."

Finally, the sūtra closes its teaching with "transformation into perfect awakening": "The storehouse consciousness is likewise — together with discriminations, it increases birth-and-death; through transformation of the basis, it becomes perfect awakening."[^11] In the ordinary state, the ālaya operates together with discriminations, perpetuating saṃsāra; but through practice and transformation, the same consciousness-body becomes perfect awakening.

### 2.2 The Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra — Three Names and the Torrent Metaphor

The *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra*'s chapter on "Mind, Intention, and Consciousness" establishes three names for the eighth consciousness through the Buddha's own words:

> "This consciousness is also called the ādāna consciousness. Why? Because this consciousness follows and holds the body. It is also called the ālaya consciousness. Why? Because this consciousness appropriates, conceals, and shares the body's fortune and misfortune. It is also called mind (citta). Why? Because this consciousness accumulates and nurtures forms, sounds, smells, tastes, and tangibles."[^12]

Three names reveal three facets: ādāna (holding) is the functional dimension, ālaya (concealing/storing) is the structural dimension, and citta (accumulating) is the dynamic dimension.

The same sūtra establishes the ālaya as the foundation upon which the six consciousnesses turn: "Taking the ādāna consciousness as basis and support, the six consciousness-bodies turn."[^13] This is the canonical root of the three-transformation architecture: the eighth consciousness as basis, the six consciousnesses turning upon it.

The sūtra offers two metaphors: the torrent — "Like a great torrential flow, when the condition for one wave arises, only one wave turns" — showing that the consciousness-body flows continuously while consciousness-waves arise according to conditions; and the mirror — "Like a well-polished mirror surface, when the condition for one reflection arises, only one reflection appears; the mirror surface does not transform into the reflection" — showing that the consciousness-body is not altered by what it manifests.[^14]

The Buddha further summarizes in verse: "The ādāna consciousness is extremely deep and subtle; / All seeds are like a torrential stream. / I do not teach this to the foolish and ignorant, / Lest they discriminate and cling to it as a self."[^15] The Buddha himself explains why he withholds this teaching from ordinary beings — they would mistake it for a self. This warning becomes the starting point for this paper's middle-way corrections.

### 2.3 The Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra — Storehouse-Ocean and Tathāgatagarbha

The *Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra* deploys the ocean-wave metaphor to illustrate the dynamic relationship between the ālaya-vijñāna and the seven turning consciousnesses:

> "Just as the mighty ocean's waves / Are stirred up by fierce winds, / Billows churning in the abyss, / Never ceasing for a moment — / The storehouse-consciousness ocean abides constantly, / Stirred by the winds of sense-objects; / The various consciousness-waves / Leap and surge into being."[^16]

The deep ocean is the ālaya-vijñāna — "the storehouse-consciousness ocean abides constantly." Wind represents sense-objects and other conditions. The seven turning consciousnesses are the waves on the surface. Strong wind, large waves; gentle wind, small waves — many conditions produce many consciousnesses, few conditions produce few. But regardless of how high the waves rise, the ocean itself remains.

The same sūtra offers a verse precisely mapping the functional division of the eight consciousnesses: "The storehouse consciousness is called 'mind' (citta); / Deliberation is called 'intention' (manas); / That which cognizes sense-objects / Is called 'consciousness' (vijñāna)."[^17] Storehouse (ālaya) is "mind," deliberation (manas) is "intention," cognition (the first six) is "consciousness." The division of mind, intention, and consciousness is presented with precision in this single verse.

The *Laṅkāvatāra* further discusses the ālaya through the lens of the five dharmas and three self-natures: "If one sees the tathāgatagarbha, the five dharmas, self-natures, and the selflessness of all dharmas, then according to the stages, these are gradually extinguished."[^18] Transformation is not the annihilation of the storehouse consciousness but a gradual turning through the contemplation of the five dharmas and three self-natures. The sūtra further clarifies the relationship between turning and storehouse consciousnesses: "If the turning consciousnesses and the storehouse consciousness were different, the storehouse would not be their cause; if they were not different, when the turning consciousnesses ceased the storehouse should also cease — yet its true characteristic does not cease."[^19] Neither identical nor different — this is the key to avoiding annihilationism.

The sūtra discusses the tathāgatagarbha-ālaya relationship: "The tathāgatagarbha is called the storehouse consciousness... inherently pure, but covered by adventitious defilements, thus appearing impure."[^20] "This tathāgatagarbha-storehouse-consciousness is inherently pure in nature, defiled by adventitious dust."[^21] The tathāgatagarbha in its defiled state is the ālaya-vijñāna — neither identical nor different, like gold within ore that becomes visible after smelting. This provides the theoretical ground for transformation: one does not seek something new but removes the obscurations.

### 2.4 The Avataṃsaka Sūtra — Three Realms Are Mind-Only

The *Avataṃsaka Sūtra*'s chapter on the ten grounds establishes the fundamental "mind-only" position through the Buddha's teaching:

> "All that exists in the three realms is mind alone. The Tathāgata distinguishes and teaches the twelve links of dependent arising, all established in dependence on this one mind."[^22]

Everything in the three realms is "one mind" — this "mind" in Yogācāra is the ālaya-vijñāna. The twelve links of dependent arising are all established in dependence on this single mind. The Juelinpusa verse further unfolds this: "Mind is like a master painter / That paints the entire world; / The five aggregates all arise from it — / There is no dharma it does not create. / As with mind, so with Buddha; / As with Buddha, so with sentient beings. / Mind, Buddha, and sentient beings — / These three are without difference."[^23] Mind can paint the entire world and generate all five aggregates, and "mind, Buddha, and sentient beings — these three are without difference." The ālaya-vijñāna as the fundamental basis of all dharmas finds its broadest canonical foundation in the *Avataṃsaka*.

### 2.5 The Āgama Bridge — Dependent Origination and Karmic Continuity

The core question the ālaya-vijñāna addresses — "no-self yet karmic continuity" — is already explicitly framed in the Āgamas. The *Saṃyukta Āgama*'s Sūtra on Emptiness in the Ultimate Sense directly confronts this problem:

> "When the eye arises, it comes from nowhere; when it ceases, it goes nowhere. Thus the eye arises without substance; having arisen, it utterly ceases. There is karmic retribution but no agent — when this aggregate ceases, a different aggregate continues — except for conventional designation."[^24]

"There is karmic retribution but no agent; when this aggregate ceases, a different aggregate continues" — karmic retribution exists, yet there is no agent; one aggregate ceases, another continues. This is precisely the problem the ālaya-vijñāna resolves: under the premise of "no agent," how does "a different aggregate continuing" work?

The same collection's Kātyāyana Sūtra establishes the middle-way position of dependent origination: "The Tathāgata, departing from both extremes, teaches the middle way: that is, because this exists, that exists; because this arises, that arises — conditioned by ignorance there are formations, and so on through birth, aging, illness, death, grief, lamentation, and the mass of suffering."[^25] The sūtra on dependent arising dharmas further declares: "What are dharmas of dependent arising? That is, because this exists, that exists; conditioned by ignorance there are formations, conditioned by formations there is consciousness... These dharmas are dharma-abiding, dharma-emptiness, dharma-suchness, dharma-necessity — dharma not departing from suchness, dharma not different from suchness — verifiable, real, and non-inverted."[^26] Dependent origination does not change regardless of whether a Buddha appears in the world — this is the eternal truth of "dharma-abiding in the dharma-realm." The ālaya-vijñāna is the Yogācāra school's precise theorization of this principle.

### Part B: The Treatise Masters' Systematization

### 2.6 The Cheng Weishi Lun — Seven Proofs

*Cheng Weishi Lun* (成唯識論), fascicle 3, begins with "sacred teaching and correct reasoning as definitive authority," citing a verse from the *Mahāyānābhidharma Sūtra*:

> "Since beginningless time, the realm (dhātu) / Is the equal support of all dharmas. / Through it there are the destinies of rebirth / And the attainment of nirvāṇa."[^27]

The treatise explains: "realm" (界) means cause, namely the seed-consciousness; "support" (依) means condition, namely the holding-consciousness. This consciousness serves as both cause (directly producing all dharmas) and condition (serving as their basis), enabling both saṃsāric wandering and the attainment of nirvāṇa.

Following the scriptural proofs, fascicle 3 develops seven rational arguments proving the eighth consciousness must exist[^28]:

**1. Seed-holding mind.** The seeds of all defiled and pure dharmas require an uninterrupted consciousness to hold them. The turning consciousnesses (1–7) are interrupted and cannot hold seeds.

**2. Vipāka mind.** The total retributive fruit of wholesome and unwholesome karma must be continuous and uninterrupted, which the turning consciousnesses cannot provide.

**3. Destinies and births.** The entity that constitutes one's actual rebirth-destiny must be real, permanent, pervasive, and unmixed — only the vipāka consciousness and its associates fulfill these four criteria.

**4. Appropriation.** The five sense-faculties and their bases are "appropriated" (maintained as living tissue). Only the vipāka consciousness can pervasively and continuously appropriate the physical body.

**5. Lifespan, warmth, and consciousness.** These three mutually sustain each other. The turning consciousnesses, being intermittent, cannot permanently sustain lifespan and warmth.

**6. Mind at birth and death.** At birth and death, the body-mind is dim and confused; the clear turning consciousnesses do not arise. Yet at these moments one must abide in a scattered mind, not in a mindless absorption — therefore the extremely subtle vipāka consciousness serves as the "birth-and-death mind."[^29]

**7. Consciousness conditions name-and-form.** The sūtra states that consciousness and name-and-form mutually condition each other "like two bundles of reeds." The turning consciousnesses, being intermittent, cannot perpetually serve as the condition for name-and-form.[^30]

The logical structure is unified across all seven proofs: the turning consciousnesses are interrupted, changing, and non-continuous, and therefore cannot perform the functions of seed-holding, retribution, appropriation, or sustaining life. Hence there must be a continuous, extremely subtle eighth consciousness.

### 2.7 The Mahāyānasaṃgraha — Three Aspects and Learning-Perfumation

The *Mahāyānasaṃgraha* establishes the ālaya-vijñāna's complete structure through three aspects[^31]:

The self-characteristic (svalakṣaṇa) — "Depending on the habitual impressions of all impure dharmas, it serves as their generative cause, holding seeds as a receptacle." The causal characteristic (hetulakṣaṇa) — "This all-seed consciousness constantly functions as the cause for generating impure dharmas." The resultant characteristic (phalalakṣaṇa) — "This consciousness arises only through the beginningless habitual impressions of various impure dharmas."

The three aspects form a cycle: the consciousness holds seeds (self-characteristic), seeds generate manifest activity (causal characteristic), and manifest activity perfumes the consciousness again (resultant characteristic). "The ālaya consciousness and defiled dharmas are mutually cause and condition, like wick and flame mutually generating and burning, or like bundles of reeds mutually supporting each other."[^32]

More crucially, the *Mahāyānasaṃgraha* addresses the fundamental question: if the ālaya-vijñāna contains only defiled seeds, where does purity come from? The answer: "Taking as seed the learning-perfumation that flows from the supremely pure dharma-realm, the supramundane mind is born."[^33] Learning-perfumation (śruta-vāsanā) consists of the undefiled seeds formed through hearing, contemplating, and practicing the true Dharma. It "coexists with the retributive consciousness, like milk mixed with water" — sharing space with the ālaya yet differing in nature. "Whether in lower, middle, or upper grades, learning-perfumation should be understood as seeds of the dharma-body" — this is the active path from defilement to purity.[^34]

## III. Core Argument

### 3.1 Layer One: Nature — "Its Nature Is Solely Unobscured, with the Five Universal Factors"

The first verse: "Its nature is solely unobscured, with only the five universal factors; / It follows others' karma through realms and grounds; / The Two Vehicles fail to understand, confused by attachment; / Thus the masters of doctrine engage in debate."

**Solely unobscured.** The ālaya-vijñāna is classified as unobscured and morally neutral (anivṛtāvyākṛta). "Unobscured" means it is not covered by afflictions — unlike manas, which is "obscured and morally neutral." "Morally neutral" means it is neither wholesome nor unwholesome — it does not directly create karma; it serves only as the foundational support for all dharmas. *Cheng Weishi Lun*, fascicle 3: "This consciousness has turned without beginning like a flowing stream; until the transformation of the basis, in all states it is constantly morally neutral."[^35]

Precisely because it is neutral, it can receive perfumation — like a white cloth that can be dyed any color. If the ālaya-vijñāna were itself wholesome or unwholesome, it could not impartially receive seeds of both kinds. The *Ghanavyūha Sūtra*'s declaration that it is "inherently luminous in nature"[^6] accords with this principle — an inherently pure nature that can receive all defiled and pure seeds.

**Five universal factors.** Its associated mental factors are only the five universals — contact, attention, feeling, perception, and volition — and among these, feeling is exclusively equanimity (upekṣā). It does not generate obvious pleasant or unpleasant discrimination; its cognitive activity is extremely subtle, operating only in an automatic seed-holding mode. These five universals constitute the basic operating module of all consciousnesses (to be treated in full in S3-P07); at the ālaya level, they all function at their most attenuated state.

**Following others' karma through realms and grounds.** The ālaya-vijñāna does not itself create karma, yet it is pulled along by the karma created by the seven turning consciousnesses, wandering through the three realms and nine grounds. It is a passive result — the vipāka fruit, drawn forth by prior karma.

**The Two Vehicles fail to understand.** Śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas do not comprehend this consciousness — the phrase "confused by attachment" (因迷執) carries a double meaning: because they are confused they grasp (misdirected by confusion), and because this consciousness is the root-cause of confusion and grasping (the cause of delusion). The Buddha's refusal to teach it to the "foolish and ignorant" underscores that the ālaya is not a self.

### 3.2 Layer Two: Three Storehouse Meanings — "Vast and Fathomless, Three Storehouses Cannot Be Exhausted"

The second verse opens with "vast and fathomless, the three storehouses cannot be exhausted," pointing to the core structural concept:

**Able to store (能藏, dhāraṇa):** The ālaya-vijñāna holds all seeds without loss — it is the warehouse. The *Ghanavyūha Sūtra*'s "holding all seeds"[^10] is precisely this meaning.

**Stored into (所藏, āśraya):** The ālaya-vijñāna is perfumed by the seven turning consciousnesses — it is the object written into. Every activity of the turning consciousnesses leaves seeds in the ālaya, like writing data to a hard drive.

**Grasped as self (執藏, ādāna):** Manas perpetually grasps the ālaya-vijñāna's cognitive aspect as "I" — the ālaya is the object mistaken for self. The *Ghanavyūha Sūtra* describes this relationship: "Manas conditions the storehouse consciousness like a magnet attracting iron... the defiled intention thus grasps the ālaya."[^36]

All three meanings operate simultaneously. *Cheng Weishi Lun* cites the verse: "Dharmas are stored in consciousness, / And consciousness is stored in dharmas; / Each serves as the other's fruit, / And constantly as the other's cause."[^37]

**"Deep as an abyss — seven waves, with sense-objects as wind"** condenses the *Laṅkāvatāra*'s ocean metaphor into verse. The *Commentary on the Verses* explains: "The eighth consciousness in its manifest state is like water; the eight consciousnesses in their seed and manifest modes are like waves; objects and the four conditions are like wind."[^38]

Here a daily-life metaphor illuminates the teaching: **Why are you afraid of snakes?**

Walking along a mountain path, you spot a coiled rope — your heart races, hair stands on end, your body recoils. You haven't "thought" yet — your conscious mind hasn't had time to determine whether it's a rope or a snake — but your body has already responded.

Where does this response come from? From seeds in the ālaya-vijñāna. Perhaps from being startled by a snake in this lifetime, perhaps from impressions stretching back far longer. The seeds wait in the ālaya until a similar situation (wind of external conditions) arises, and they manifest as your body's reaction (consciousness-waves surging up). You think "I" am afraid; in reality, seeds are operating.

### 3.3 Layer Three: Seed Mechanism — Six Characteristics and Four Conditions

Seeds are the fundamental operational unit of the ālaya-vijñāna. *Cheng Weishi Lun*, fascicle 2, defines them: "Within the root consciousness, the differential capacities that directly produce their own results" — seeds are not things but functional differentials within the ālaya.[^39]

Seeds must meet six conditions (the six seed-characteristics)[^40]:

**1. Momentary destruction** (kṣaṇa-nirodha): Seeds perish the instant they arise — "this excludes permanent dharmas, which undergo no change and cannot be said to have generative power."

**2. Co-presence with fruit** (phala-sahabhū): Seeds and their manifest results coexist simultaneously — cause and effect are simultaneous.

**3. Constant accompaniment** (nityānuvartin): Seeds must persist as a continuous flow of the same type — "this excludes the turning consciousnesses, which are interrupted and impermanent."

**4. Determined nature** (niyata): Wholesome causes produce wholesome results, unwholesome causes produce unwholesome results — capacities are functionally determined.

**5. Awaiting conditions** (pratyayāpekṣin): Seeds must await the convergence of conditions before producing results — "this excludes the non-Buddhist claim of a natural cause that produces results spontaneously without conditions."

**6. Producing own fruit** (sva-phala-da): Form-seeds produce form-results, mind-seeds produce mind-results — "each produces its own kind."

The conclusion: "Only the differential capacities within the root consciousness possess all six characteristics and qualify as seeds; nothing else does."[^41]

Seeds operate through the perfumation mechanism. *Cheng Weishi Lun*, fascicle 2, establishes four conditions each for perfumer and perfumed[^42]:

**Four conditions for the perfumed:** persistence (continuous without interruption), moral neutrality (impartial receptivity), receptivity (non-rigid, able to receive impressions), and co-presence with the perfumer (simultaneous, co-located, neither identical nor separate). Conclusion: "Only the vipāka consciousness possesses all four conditions and can be the perfumed."

**Four conditions for the perfumer:** arising and ceasing (not permanent), powerful function (forceful, not the feeble vipāka consciousness), admitting of increase and decrease, and co-presence with the perfumed. Conclusion: "Only the seven turning consciousnesses and their associated mental factors possess these four conditions."

The treatise illustrates their relationship with three similes: sesame perfumed by flowers, wick generating flame and flame charring wick, and two bundles of reeds mutually supporting each other. "When the perfuming consciousnesses arise from seeds, they simultaneously serve as causes, perfuming new seeds in return — the three elements cycle as mutual cause and effect simultaneously."[^43]

### 3.4 Layer Four: Three Great Functions — "Receiving Perfumation, Holding Seeds, Sustaining Body and World"

These seven characters constitute the ālaya-vijñāna's three functional capacities:

**Receiving perfumation.** The ālaya-vijñāna receives the perfumation of the seven turning consciousnesses, causing new seeds to grow. Every thought as you read this paper — understanding, confusion, delight, irritation — is being perfumed into seeds in the ālaya. When you sleep and the conscious mind ceases, the seeds in the ālaya remain — that you remember tomorrow what you read today is proof of the seeds' persistence.

**Holding seeds.** The ālaya-vijñāna holds all seeds of defiled and undefiled dharmas without loss. Seeds reproduce their own type within the consciousness, flowing without interruption since beginningless time.

**Sustaining body and world.** The ālaya-vijñāna projects the physical body through individual-specific seeds and the shared environment through collective seeds. The *Ghanavyūha Sūtra*'s "the storehouse consciousness transforms into all sense-objects, pervading the world"[^44] is the canonical expression of this function.

*Cheng Weishi Lun*, fascicle 2, provides precise analysis[^45]:

Regarding the body — "Through the maturation of individual-specific seeds, the vipāka consciousness projects what appears as the sense-faculties and their bases." Your five sense-faculties are not independently existing "matter" but the projection of individual-specific seeds maturing within your ālaya-vijñāna.

Regarding the shared world — "Through the maturation of collective seeds, the vipāka consciousness projects what appears as form and other aspects of the vessel-world... although each sentient being's projection is distinct, the appearances are similar and the locations identical — like the light of many lamps, each pervading yet appearing as one." Multiple lamps each illuminate independently yet their light merges into a single field — each being projects its own world, yet they appear as one shared environment. The *Ghanavyūha Sūtra*'s statement that "the storehouse consciousness together with all realms accomplishes everything without exception, pervading the inner, outer, consciousness, and world completely"[^46] accords with this principle.

### 3.5 Layer Five: Birth and Death — "Departing Last, Arriving First, Serving as Master"

This describes the ālaya-vijñāna's role in the process of birth and death: at conception, the ālaya-vijñāna arrives first; at death, it departs last.

The *Commentary on the Verses* cites the traditional teaching: "For wholesome karma, cold rises from below; for unwholesome karma, cold descends from above; both reach the heart, where all is simultaneously relinquished."[^47] The six destinies are distinguished by the point of departure: "From the crown — the noble paths; from the eyes — the heavenly realms; from the heart — the human realm; from the belly — the hungry ghost realm; from the knees — the animal realm; from the soles — the hell realm."

This is not mysticism but Yogācāra's precise analysis of the death process. The first six consciousnesses have already become dim and inactive at the moment of death — "at the time of birth and death, body and mind are dim and confused, as in dreamless sleep or deep unconsciousness; the clear turning consciousnesses definitely do not arise."[^48] Only the ālaya-vijñāna, with its extremely subtle cognitive mode, continues to appropriate the body. When it releases a part of the body, that part becomes non-sentient (cold touch arises); when it appropriates new name-and-form, a new life begins. The Āgama's teaching that "when this aggregate ceases, a different aggregate continues"[^24] finds its precise mechanism here.

The word "master" requires careful interpretation. The ālaya-vijñāna is functionally the "master" of the life-process — at the crucial juncture of birth and death, it alone does not cease — but it is not an ontological "ruler." It is itself morally neutral and unobscured, turning according to karma, possessing no autonomous will. This is the precise balance between no-self and karmic continuity.

### 3.6 Layer Six: Three Stages of Transformation — "Only Before the Immovable Ground Is the Storehouse Name Relinquished"

The third verse unfolds the ālaya-vijñāna's path from ordinary being to buddhahood. *Cheng Weishi Lun*, fascicle 3, establishes three names corresponding to three stages[^49]:

**Stage One: Self-love attachment-storage.** From beginningless time until the Immovable Ground (eighth bhūmi), it is called the ālaya-vijñāna. In this stage, manas perpetually grasps this consciousness's cognitive aspect as "I." "Ālaya" means "storehouse," and this name is most closely associated with the "grasped-as-self" meaning — the name representing its "greatest fault," hence relinquished first.

**Stage Two: Wholesome-unwholesome karmic fruition.** From beginningless time until the vajra-samādhi (for bodhisattvas) or entry into nirvāṇa without remainder (for the Two Vehicles), it is called the vipāka consciousness. At this stage manas no longer grasps the storehouse as self (the ālaya name has been shed), but karmic fruition is not yet exhausted.

**Stage Three: Continuous holding.** At the buddha-stage and throughout the future, it is called the immaculate consciousness (amala-vijñāna). This consciousness has been transformed into the supreme undefiled pure body: "The Tathāgata's immaculate consciousness / Is the pure, undefiled realm, / Liberated from all obstructions, / Corresponding to mirror wisdom."[^50] The *Ghanavyūha Sūtra*'s declaration that "the ālaya is precisely the Ghanavyūha — its wondrous body is inherently pure... its substance is truly the perfected nature (pariniṣpanna)"[^51] is the canonical expression of this third stage.

"Only before the Immovable Ground is the storehouse name relinquished" — at the eighth bhūmi, manas's self-view and self-love no longer grasp the ālaya as self. Two interpretations exist: one holds the eighth bhūmi as the point of relinquishment (because innate self-grasping no longer manifests); the other holds the first bhūmi (because discriminative self-grasping has been severed). Dharmapāla's orthodox position takes the eighth bhūmi, reasoning: "Before the seventh ground, innate self-view and self-love still grasp this consciousness as one's inner self — how then could the ālaya name have been relinquished?"[^52]

"After the vajra path, the vipāka is emptied" — when the vajra-like samādhi manifests, the seeds of both obstructions are permanently severed, all defiled seeds and inferior undefiled seeds are shed, and the vipāka name becomes empty.

"Great Mirror and Immaculate arise simultaneously" — the great mirror wisdom (ādarśa-jñāna) and the immaculate consciousness arise at the same moment. The consciousness-body at the buddha-stage has been transformed into the supreme undefiled pure body, "illuminating the dust-mote lands of the ten directions." The great mirror wisdom is the ālaya-vijñāna's ultimate transformation — not the annihilation of the ālaya replaced by a new consciousness, but the same consciousness-body with seeds transformed from defiled to undefiled, function transformed from storing defilements to illuminating the dharma-realm. The *Ghanavyūha Sūtra*'s "increasing birth-and-death; through transformation of the basis, becoming perfect awakening"[^11] is fully consistent with the treatise's concept of "transformation of the basis."

**The active path: learning-perfumation.** Transformation does not begin only at the eighth bhūmi. The *Mahāyānasaṃgraha*'s learning-perfumation is the pure seed that ordinary beings can actively cultivate: hearing the true Dharma, right contemplation, practice — each instance perfumes undefiled seeds into the ālaya. From lower to middle to upper grades, "learning-perfumation should be understood as seeds of the dharma-body."[^34] The retributive consciousness (defiled) gradually decreases while learning-perfumation (undefiled) gradually increases — "the basis thus transforms."

**The three-school debate on innate versus newly-perfumed seeds.** The origin of seeds is Yogācāra's deepest internal controversy. *Cheng Weishi Lun*, fascicle 2, records three positions. The first school (Śīlabhadra and others) holds that all seeds are innately present: "All sentient beings since beginningless time possess various realms, like a cluster of prickly ash fruits — present by nature." Perfumation only enhances innate seeds; it cannot generate new ones. The second school (Nanda and others) holds that seeds are exclusively newly perfumed: "Since seeds are just another name for habitual impressions, they must arise through perfumation, like the fragrance of sesame arising through contact with flowers." Dharmapāla's orthodox synthesis takes the third position: innate and newly arisen combined — "Seeds are of two kinds: first, innate... second, newly arisen."[^53]

Dharmapāla's argument is precise: if seeds were only innate, the turning consciousnesses and the ālaya would not stand in a causal relationship — "how then could the turning consciousnesses and the ālaya have a cause-and-condition relationship?" If seeds were only newly perfumed, undefiled seeds would arise without cause — "undefiled conditioned dharmas, having no cause, should not be able to arise." Therefore both must coexist: innate seeds are present by nature, enhanced through perfumation; newly perfumed seeds are generated through repeated manifest activity — "innate seeds too must be enhanced through perfumation before they can produce results."[^54]

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Dialogue

### 4.1 The Hard Drive Analogy: Heuristic and Boundaries

⚠️ **The following is a heuristic analogy, not a theoretical equivalence.**

If one must employ a modern metaphor for the ālaya-vijñāna, "the hard drive of life" offers a useful entry point:

**Seeds ≈ Data.** Every thought and every action writes data to this hard drive (perfumation creating seeds). Data does not vanish on its own (constant accompaniment), waiting until specific conditions trigger retrieval (awaiting conditions before manifesting).

**Receiving perfumation ≈ Writing.** Every activity of the seven turning consciousnesses is a write operation. Wholesome thoughts write wholesome seeds; unwholesome thoughts write unwholesome seeds — the hard drive itself does not choose (moral neutrality); whatever comes, it writes.

**Holding seeds ≈ Storage.** The hard drive operates continuously; even when you shut down (sleep or coma), data persists. The ālaya-vijñāna, even in mindless absorption when all six turning consciousnesses cease, continues holding seeds without interruption.

**Body and world ≈ Operating system + hardware + display.** The ālaya-vijñāna is not merely a storage device; it is the foundation of the entire "system" — projecting your body (hardware), your living environment (the world on screen), and your entire operating platform (operating system).

**Transformation ≈ Operating system upgrade.** Not replacing the hard drive (soul substitution), but the operating system on the same hard drive upgrading from a bug-ridden old version, through continuous patches and updates (learning-perfumation), to a flawless final version (great mirror wisdom).

### 4.2 The Strict Boundaries of the Analogy

This analogy contains at least four fundamental disanalogies:

First, a hard drive is a physical entity; the ālaya-vijñāna is not. The ālaya-vijñāna is a mind-dharma, occupying no space, having no location or shape. The entire Yogācāra position is: matter is projected by consciousness — the hard drive is inside the ālaya-vijñāna, not the other way around.

Second, a hard drive is inert; the ālaya-vijñāna is dynamic. A hard drive does not write data to itself; the ālaya-vijñāna and the seven turning consciousnesses are "mutually causal," and the ālaya is itself part of the dynamic cycle.

Third, a hard drive has a storage limit; the ālaya-vijñāna does not. "Vast and fathomless, the three storehouses cannot be exhausted" — seeds from beginningless time across countless eons, all held.

Fourth, data on a hard drive is static; seeds undergo momentary arising-and-ceasing. Seeds are not statically stored files but functional differentials arising and ceasing every moment — the first of the six seed-characteristics, "momentary destruction," makes this explicit.

Thus the "hard drive" is only a doorway, helping modern readers build initial understanding. Once one grasps the momentary nature of seeds and the dynamic cyclicality of consciousness, the analogy should be set aside.

## V. Middle-Way Corrections

### 5.1 ❌ Ālaya-vijñāna ≠ Soul

This is the most common and most fundamental misunderstanding. The ālaya-vijñāna is not an eternal, unchanging soul (ātman), for at least three reasons. First, it arises and ceases momentarily — "turning without beginning like a flowing stream," continuously flowing yet never remaining. Second, it is morally neutral and unobscured — it possesses no "self-awareness," does not know it is holding seeds, and never declares "these seeds are mine." Third, the name "ālaya" itself is eventually relinquished — at the eighth bhūmi the storehouse-name is shed; if it were a soul, it could not shed its own designation. The Buddha's warning against teaching it to the "foolish and ignorant," "lest they discriminate and cling to it as a self," itself demonstrates that the ālaya is not a "self."

### 5.2 ❌ Ālaya-vijñāna ≠ Spatial Container

"Storehouse" is a functional description, not a spatial one. The ālaya is not a container "with seeds inside" — seeds are not "inside" the consciousness; seeds are the consciousness's objective aspect. "This and the root consciousness and the produced fruits are neither identical nor different."[^55] Seeds and the consciousness-body are neither one nor separate. Analogy: the "wetting power" of water is not "inside" the water — the wetting power is water itself.

### 5.3 ❌ Ālaya-vijñāna ≠ Purely Private Storehouse

Collective seeds project the shared world — your world is not entirely projected by your ālaya-vijñāna alone. The collective seeds of all sentient beings jointly project the shared environment — "like the light of many lamps, each pervading yet appearing as one." The ālaya is both individual (individual-specific seeds → your body) and collective (collective seeds → shared world). Understanding the ālaya as a purely personal, closed system oversimplifies Yogācāra doctrine.

### 5.4 ❌ Boundaries of the Hard Drive Analogy

The analogy's four fundamental disanalogies were already marked in §4.2. Here the methodological point bears emphasis: the function of cross-disciplinary analogy is "heuristic," not "reductive equivalence." Saying "the ālaya-vijñāna is like a hard drive" does not mean the ālaya "is" an information-storage device. Yogācāra is a complete epistemological system that cannot be reduced to the terminology of modern computer science.

### 5.5 ❌ Transformation ≠ Reformatting

Transformation is not erasing the old and installing something entirely new. *Cheng Weishi Lun* states with absolute clarity: "Not relinquishing the entire body of the eighth consciousness, lest the arhat have no consciousness to hold seeds."[^56] What is relinquished is the name (the ālaya-name), the defiled seeds — not the consciousness-body itself. The consciousness-body from beginning to end is the same — from the ordinary being's ālaya to the buddha's immaculate consciousness, the nature of the consciousness remains unchanged; what changes is the ratio of seeds (defiled → undefiled) and the quality of function (storing defilements → illuminating the dharma-realm). Transformation is a gradual process, not an instantaneous replacement.

## VI. Conclusion

The ālaya-vijñāna is Yogācāra's precise theory answering the fundamental question of "no-self yet karmic continuity." Through the mode of a seed-torrent — momentarily arising-and-ceasing, impermanent yet uninterrupted — it sustains the continuity of life, falling into neither the eternalist extreme (permanent soul) nor the annihilationist extreme (everything ceases at death).

This is not a treatise invention. From the Āgamas' original question — "there is karmic retribution but no agent; when this aggregate ceases, a different aggregate continues" — to the *Ghanavyūha Sūtra*'s direct teaching that "the Buddha teaches the tathāgatagarbha as the ālaya-vijñāna," to the *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra*'s three-name teaching that "the ādāna consciousness is extremely deep and subtle," to the *Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra*'s ocean metaphor that "the storehouse-consciousness ocean abides constantly," to the *Avataṃsaka Sūtra*'s foundational position that "all that exists in the three realms is mind alone" — the Buddha taught this consciousness from multiple angles across multiple sūtras. What the treatise masters did was systematize these sūtra teachings into a precise theory.

The three verses of the *Verses on Eight Consciousnesses* encompass its complete doctrine. The first verse — "Its nature is solely unobscured, with the five universal factors" — describes its nature: morally neutral and unobscured, associating only with the five universals, following others' karma through the three realms. The second verse — "Vast and fathomless, the three storehouses cannot be exhausted" — describes its functions: the three storehouse meanings, seed-perfumation and holding, the projection of body-and-world, and the sustaining of life through death. The third verse — "Only before the Immovable Ground is the storehouse name relinquished" — describes its transformation: from self-love storage to karmic fruition to continuous holding; from ālaya to vipāka to immaculate; and finally "Great Mirror and Immaculate arise simultaneously, illuminating the dust-mote lands of the ten directions."

Yet doctrinal understanding is not meditative realization. Knowing how the ālaya-vijñāna operates and actually achieving transformation of the basis are two different things. The *Mahāyānasaṃgraha*'s learning-perfumation offers a path that ordinary beings can begin right now: every hearing of the true Dharma, every moment of right contemplation, every observation of one's own mind perfumes pure seeds into the ālaya. Seeds are never planted in vain; cause and effect are never wasted.

You have just finished reading this paper. Whether you remember much or little, whether you agree or disagree, these words have already left traces on your "hard drive" — wholesome, neutral, or perhaps just a question mark. Those are seeds. The next time a similar concept arises in your life, they will manifest from their seed-state.

This is the ālaya-vijñāna's most fundamental teaching: **Every thought you plant right now is changing this "hard drive."** The question is not whether you can change it — you are changing it every moment. The question is: are you aware of what you are writing?

[^25]: *Saṃyukta Āgama*, fasc. 10, Sūtra 262 (Kātyāyana): 「如來離於二邊，說於中道，所謂此有故彼有，此生故彼生，謂緣無明有行。」"The Tathāgata, departing from both extremes, teaches the middle way: because this exists, that exists; because this arises, that arises — conditioned by ignorance there are formations." Ibid.

[^26]: *Saṃyukta Āgama*, fasc. 12, Sūtra 296: 「云何為因緣法？謂此有故彼有……此等諸法，法住、法空、法如、法爾，法不離如，法不異如，審諦真實不顛倒。」"What are dharmas of dependent arising? Because this exists, that exists... These dharmas are dharma-abiding, dharma-emptiness, dharma-suchness, dharma-necessity — not departing from suchness, not different from suchness — verifiable, real, and non-inverted." Ibid.

[^28]: *Cheng Weishi Lun*, fasc. 3, seven proofs passage, from 「云何應知此第八識離眼等識有別自體？」"How is it known that this eighth consciousness has a distinct substance apart from the eye-consciousness and others?" through 「故彼識言，顯第八識。」"Therefore the word 'consciousness' [in the sūtra] indicates the eighth consciousness." These are conventionally called the "five great proofs," but fascicle 3 actually contains seven: seed-holding mind, vipāka mind, destinies-and-births, appropriation, lifespan-warmth-consciousness, birth-and-death mind, and consciousness-conditions-name-and-form.

[^29]: *Cheng Weishi Lun*, fasc. 3: 「將死時，由善惡業，下上身分，冷觸漸起……唯異熟心，由先業力，恒遍相續執受身分。捨執受處，冷觸便生。」"At the time of death, due to wholesome or unwholesome karma, cold touch gradually arises from the lower or upper body... only the vipāka consciousness, driven by prior karma, continuously and pervasively appropriates the body. Where it releases its hold, cold touch arises." Ibid.

[^30]: *Cheng Weishi Lun*, fasc. 3: 「識緣名色，名色緣識……故彼識言，顯第八識。」"Consciousness conditions name-and-form, and name-and-form conditions consciousness... therefore the word 'consciousness' indicates the eighth consciousness." Ibid.

[^31]: *Mahāyānasaṃgraha* (Paramārtha trans.), fasc. 1, chapter on characteristics: 「此相略說有三種：一立自相、二立因相、三立果相。」"These characteristics are briefly stated as three: first, the self-characteristic; second, the causal characteristic; third, the resultant characteristic." *Taishō* vol. 31, No. 1593.

[^32]: Ibid.: 「阿黎耶識與雜染法互為因緣，如炷與焰展轉生燒，又如束蘆互相依住。」"The ālaya consciousness and defiled dharmas are mutually cause and condition, like wick and flame mutually generating and burning, or like bundles of reeds mutually supporting each other." This simile also appears in *Cheng Weishi Lun*, fasc. 2.

[^33]: *Mahāyānasaṃgraha* (Paramārtha trans.), fasc. 1: 「最清淨法界所流正聞熏習為種子故，出世心得生。」"Taking as seed the learning-perfumation that flows from the supremely pure dharma-realm, the supramundane mind is born." Ibid.

[^34]: Ibid.: 「是聞熏習若下中上品，應知是法身種子」"Whether in lower, middle, or upper grades, learning-perfumation should be understood as seeds of the dharma-body" and 「此聞熏習非阿黎耶識，屬法身及解脫身攝。」"This learning-perfumation does not belong to the ālaya; it belongs to the dharma-body and liberation-body."

[^35]: *Cheng Weishi Lun*, fasc. 3: 「此識無始恒轉如流，乃至未轉依，於一切位恒無記性。」"This consciousness has turned without beginning like a flowing stream; until the transformation of the basis, in all states it is constantly morally neutral." *Taishō* vol. 31, No. 1585.

[^36]: *Ghanavyūha Sūtra* (Amoghavajra trans.), upper fascicle: 「末那緣藏識，如磁石吸鐵……染意亦如是，執取阿賴耶。」"Manas conditions the storehouse consciousness like a magnet attracting iron... the defiled intention thus grasps the ālaya." *Taishō* vol. 16, No. 682.

[^37]: *Cheng Weishi Lun*, fasc. 2 (Dharmapāla's orthodox position citing verse): 「諸法於識藏，識於法亦爾，更互為果性，亦常為因性。」"Dharmas are stored in consciousness, and consciousness is stored in dharmas; each serves as the other's fruit, and constantly as the other's cause." Original verse from the *Mahāyānābhidharma Sūtra*. *Taishō* vol. 31, No. 1585.

[^38]: *Bajishi Guiju Buzhu*, fasc. lower: 「此言第八現識如水，八識現種如波，境等四緣如風。」"The eighth consciousness in its manifest state is like water; the eight consciousnesses in their seed and manifest modes are like waves; objects and the four conditions are like wind." *Taishō* vol. 45, No. 1865.

[^39]: *Cheng Weishi Lun*, fasc. 2: 「謂本識中親生自果，功能差別。」"Within the root consciousness, the differential capacities that directly produce their own results." *Taishō* vol. 31, No. 1585.

[^40]: *Cheng Weishi Lun*, fasc. 2: 「然種子義，略有六種：一剎那滅、二果俱有、三恒隨轉、四性決定、五待眾緣、六引自果。」"The meaning of 'seed' comprises six kinds: (1) momentary destruction, (2) co-presence with fruit, (3) constant accompaniment, (4) determined nature, (5) awaiting conditions, (6) producing own fruit." Ibid.

[^41]: Ibid.: 「唯本識中功能差別具斯六義，成種非餘。」"Only the differential capacities within the root consciousness possess all six characteristics and qualify as seeds; nothing else does."

[^42]: *Cheng Weishi Lun*, fasc. 2: 「所薰能薰各具四義，令種生長，故名熏習。」"The perfumed and the perfumer each possess four conditions, enabling seeds to grow — this is called perfumation."
