## Abstract

The opening sentence of the Heart Sūtra — "When Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva was practicing the profound prajñāpāramitā, he illuminated the emptiness of the five aggregates and transcended all suffering" — is typically read as narrative preamble, merely establishing who did what and when. This paper argues that the sentence constitutes the sūtra's master program: a self-contained awakening loop comprising four links — Subject (*avalokiteśvara*) → Method (*gambhīrā prajñāpāramitā*) → Insight (*vyavalokayati sma pañca-skandhāḥ*) → Fruit (*sarvaduhkha-atikaraṇa*) — whose endpoint circles back to its origin, embodying the Tathāgata's "neither coming nor going."

Drawing primarily on Kuiji's *Youzhan* (T1710), Woncheuk's *Heart Sūtra Commentary* (T1711), the *Cheng weishi lun* (T1585), and the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* (T1509), the paper demonstrates: (1) "Avalokiteśvara" possesses dual valence — Kuiji explicates it through compassion-wisdom unity and ten sovereign freedoms, Woncheuk through the *pratyavekṣaṇā-jñāna* (wondrous contemplative wisdom) — with both fruition-level and causal-level meanings that are mutually non-contradictory; (2) "deep practice" has two classical dimensions (practice-depth and object-depth per Kuiji) plus an original third dimension concerning transformation at the *saṃskāra* level of the aggregates; (3) "illumination" is *yogic pratyakṣa* (direct perception in meditative equipoise), and "emptiness" specifically means the emptiness of *parikalpita* (the imagined nature), not the emptiness of all phenomena; (4) the four links form a closed loop that mirrors the ultimate meaning of Tathāgata as "one who has neither come from anywhere nor gone anywhere."

**Keywords:** Avalokiteśvara, awakening loop, *pratyavekṣaṇā-jñāna*, deep practice, five-layered contemplation, *āśraya-parāvṛtti*, Kuiji, Woncheuk, *Youzhan* / 觀自在菩薩、覺醒迴路、妙觀察智、行深、五重觀、遣虛存實、窺基、圓測、幽贊

## I. Introduction: The Weight of a Single Sentence

The Heart Sūtra contains 260 characters in Chinese. Its opening sentence —

> When Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva was practicing the profound prajñāpāramitā, he illuminated the emptiness of the five aggregates and transcended all suffering.

In most commentarial traditions, this sentence is treated as the sūtra's *nidāna* (narrative introduction) — who, when, and what. This reading encourages readers to hurry past it toward the seemingly more substantial passages on "form is not different from emptiness" and "no eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, or mind."

This paper proposes a different reading: **the opening sentence is not a preamble but a program.** Its four links — subject, method, insight, fruit — constitute a complete awakening loop. Every subsequent passage in the Heart Sūtra expands, deepens, or verifies a node within this loop.

More importantly, the loop is **closed**: the fruit of transcending suffering is the full disclosure of what was sought at the beginning — the capacity to observe and be free. The endpoint returns to the starting point. This is not circular reasoning but the ultimate structure of the Tathāgata's "neither coming nor going."

## II. Scriptural Basis

### 2.1 Kuiji's Compassion-Wisdom Explication

Kuiji (632–682), in the *Bore boluomiduo xinjing youzhan* (Esoteric Praise of the Heart Sūtra, T1710), explicates "Avalokiteśvara" through two layers of meaning:

> "Observing" (*guān*) means discernment — the wisdom-compassion that watches over and rescues. "Sovereign Freedom" (*zìzài*) means unobstructedness — the wondrous function of delivering beings. ... Furthermore, "observing" means illumination — the wisdom that comprehends emptiness and existence. "Sovereign Freedom" means mastery — the fruition attained. Having practiced the six perfections in former lives and now attaining their fruition, with contemplative wisdom as the foremost, one achieves ten sovereign freedoms.[^1]

The first layer explicates through compassion: "observing" as watching over the suffering of beings, "sovereign freedom" as the unobstructed function of deliverance. The second layer explicates through wisdom: "observing" as the wisdom that illuminates emptiness and existence, "sovereign freedom" as the ten freedoms attained through the perfected six *pāramitā* — sovereignty over lifespan, mind, wealth, karma, birth, aspiration, and so forth.

Fazang's *Brief Commentary on the Heart Sūtra* (T1712) offers a complementary perspective:

> "Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva" is the one who contemplates. With respect to the realm where principle and phenomena are unobstructed, this one contemplates and reaches sovereign freedom — hence the name. Alternatively, this one observes beings' capacities and goes to rescue them, sovereignly unobstructed — hence the name. The former interpretation concerns wisdom; the latter concerns compassion.[^2]

Both patriarchs share a common structure: **Avalokiteśvara is not a single-meaning term but the complete expression of compassion-wisdom unity.**

### 2.2 Woncheuk's Identification with Pratyavekṣaṇā-jñāna

Woncheuk (613–696), in the *Heart Sūtra Commentary* (T1711), explicates "Avalokiteśvara" through the framework of the four wisdoms derived from the transformation of consciousness:

> Third, the mental faculty associated with the *pratyavekṣaṇā-jñāna* (wondrous contemplative wisdom) — which skillfully contemplates the specific and universal characteristics of all dharmas — is named "wondrous contemplative wisdom" from its function. It has ten analogies, as explained extensively in the sūtras. The mental faculty associated with the sixth consciousness is its essential basis.[^3]

> Transforming the eighth consciousness yields the *ādarśa-jñāna* (great mirror wisdom); transforming the seventh consciousness yields the *samatā-jñāna* (equality wisdom); transforming the sixth consciousness yields the *pratyavekṣaṇā-jñāna* (wondrous contemplative wisdom); transforming the five sensory consciousnesses yields the *kṛtyānuṣṭhāna-jñāna* (wisdom of accomplished activity).[^4]

Woncheuk maps the four wisdoms to the four categories of consciousness, explicitly identifying "observing" (*guān*) with *pratyavekṣaṇā-jñāna* — the wisdom that "skillfully contemplates the specific and universal characteristics of all dharmas," a definition drawn from the *Buddhabhūmi-sūtra-śāstra* (T1530).

Kuiji explicates through compassion-wisdom unity; Woncheuk identifies the wisdom-body (*jñāna-svabhāva*). The two systems are complementary: Kuiji describes the "complete portrait" of Avalokiteśvara (compassion and wisdom as one), while Woncheuk identifies its "wisdom-body" (the transformative structure of *pratyavekṣaṇā-jñāna*).

### 2.3 The Sanskrit Name: Avalokiteśvara versus Avalokitasvara

The Sanskrit Avalokiteśvara (*avalokita* + *īśvara*, "sovereign lord of observation") differs from the earlier Avalokitasvara (*avalokita* + *svara*, "one who observes sounds"). The *Yujia lunji* (Record of the Yogācārabhūmi, T1828) states plainly:

> "Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva" — the old translation says "Avalokitasvara" (Observer of the World's Sounds), which is a translator's error. ... Now, according to the Sanskrit, the correct translation is "Avalokiteśvara" (Sovereign Observer).[^5]

The *Heart Sūtra Commentary* (T1713) explains Xuanzang's choice from a pedagogical angle:

> The popular name "Observer of the World's Sounds" is because the function of verbal karma is prominent and response comes easily. The sūtra takes the meaning that is complete, hence "Avalokiteśvara."[^6]

"Avalokitasvara" emphasizes compassionate rescue (observing beings' cries and going to save them); "Avalokiteśvara" emphasizes sovereign freedom within prajñā-wisdom. Xuanzang chose "Avalokiteśvara" for the Heart Sūtra precisely because the sūtra's theme is prajñā, not compassionate rescue — the four characters "the sūtra takes the complete meaning" (*jīng qǔ yì yuán*) illuminate the translator's doctrinal deliberation.

### 2.4 The Two Dimensions of Depth

Kuiji's *Youzhan* explicates "practicing the profound prajñāpāramitā" by distinguishing two dimensions of "depth" (*shēn*):

> "Practicing" means progressive practice — this is the contemplating wisdom. "Profound" means exceedingly deep. Depth has two kinds. First, practice-depth (*xíng shēn*): non-discriminating wisdom internally realizes the twofold emptiness, free from all discriminations, with no characteristics of practice — whether agent or action — as its mode of operation. Hence it is called "deep practice." Therefore the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-sūtra* says: "Not seeing practice, not seeing non-practice — this is called the bodhisattva's deep practice of prajñā." Second, object-depth (*jìng shēn*): the principle of twofold emptiness, free from the characteristics of existence and non-existence, transcending all conceptual elaboration. Non-discriminating wisdom realizes this profound object — hence it is called "deep practice."[^7]

**Practice-depth**: the contemplating wisdom itself is deep — non-discriminating wisdom (*nirvikalpaka-jñāna*) internally realizes person-emptiness (*pudgala-śūnyatā*) and dharma-emptiness (*dharma-śūnyatā*), free from all discriminations, with not even the appearance of "I am practicing" remaining.

**Object-depth**: the contemplated object is deep — the twofold emptiness as *tathatā* (suchness) transcends the characteristics of existence and non-existence, beyond all conceptual elaboration (*prapañca*).

Practice-depth and object-depth, the contemplating and the contemplated, are mutually conditioning — deep wisdom illuminates the deep object, and the deep object discloses deep wisdom.

### 2.5 Prajñā as the Eye of the Six Perfections

The unique position of prajñā among the six *pāramitā* is illustrated in the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* (T1509), fascicle 62, through the analogy of the blind:

> The five *pāramitā* are likewise: separated from *prajñāpāramitā*, they are like the blind without a guide, unable to proceed on the path, unable to attain sarvajña-jñāna (omniscience). ... When the five *pāramitā* are guided by *prajñāpāramitā*, then the five *pāramitā* are said to have eyes.[^8]

The commentary further clarifies:

> Though the five *pāramitā* each have their respective functional capacities, without prajñā to guide them, they cannot even attain the two vehicles, let alone the supreme path! Guided by *prajñāpāramitā*, the five *pāramitā* receive the name *pāramitā* and reach the completion of the Buddha-path.[^9]

Giving, morality, patience, vigor, and meditation each have their functions, but without prajñā to unify them, they remain conditioned (*sāsrava*) merit — unable to reach the further shore. Prajñā is the eye of the five perfections. This is the fundamental reason the sūtra says "practicing the profound *prajñāpāramitā*" rather than "practicing the profound *dāna-pāramitā*."

### 2.6 The Five Stages and the First Arising of Non-Discriminating Wisdom

At which point in the path of practice does the "observation" (*guān*) of Avalokiteśvara first arise as actual function? The *Cheng weishi lun*, fascicle 9, determines this through the five-stage framework of the *Triṃśikā* (Thirty Verses):

> When the bodhisattva, with respect to the apprehended object, through non-discriminating wisdom obtains nothing whatsoever — because one does not grasp the various characteristics of conceptual elaboration — then and only then is it truly called "abiding in the genuine ultimate nature of consciousness-only."[^10]

> When, immediately following the preparatory practices, this wisdom arises and experientially meets suchness, it is called the "penetration stage" (*prativedhāvasthā*); because it first illuminates principle, it is also called "seeing the path" (*darśana-mārga*).[^11]

The twenty-eighth verse of the *Triṃśikā* confirms:

> When, with respect to the apprehended object, wisdom obtains nothing whatsoever — then one abides in consciousness-only, free from the characteristics of the twofold grasping.[^12]

The conclusion is definitive: **non-discriminating wisdom (*nirvikalpaka-jñāna*, root wisdom, true seeing-the-path) first arises in actual function at the penetration stage.** Prior to this — during the accumulation stage (*sambhāra*) and the preparation stage (*prayoga*) — practitioners, while already accumulating merit and contemplating consciousness-only, still harbor "a mind that obtains" and still grasp the "characteristics of consciousness-only nature." Only at the penetration stage, in that decisive moment, does "first illumination of principle" occur.

### 2.7 The Five-Layered Contemplation: Eliminating the False, Preserving the Real

Kuiji's *Youzhan* systematizes Yogācāra contemplative practice into five progressive layers. The first layer, "eliminating the false, preserving the real" (*qiǎn xū cún shí*):

> Examining all that the sacred teachings say about consciousness-only, though immeasurable in variety, there are no more than five layers. First, eliminating the false and preserving the real. Contemplating that the *parikalpita* (imagined nature) arises solely from false construction, utterly devoid of essence or function, it should be correctly eliminated. Contemplating that *paratantra* (dependent nature) and *pariniṣpanna* (perfected nature) have real essence as objects of the two wisdoms, they should be preserved as existent.[^13]

> Eliminating is the emptiness-contemplation, counteracting attachment to existence. Preserving is the existence-contemplation, counteracting attachment to emptiness. Now one contemplates emptiness-and-existence to eliminate existence-and-emptiness; if existence-and-emptiness are absent, then emptiness-and-existence are also absent.[^14]

This passage is the key to understanding "emptiness" in Yogācāra: "empty" specifically means the emptiness of the *parikalpita* (the imagined nature — our superimposed constructions that have no real essence). The *paratantra* (dependent nature — causally arisen phenomena) is not empty; the *pariniṣpanna* (perfected nature — *tathatā*) is not empty. Emptiness-contemplation counteracts attachment to existence; existence-contemplation counteracts attachment to emptiness — both are simultaneously transcended, culminating in the realization of "neither existent nor empty," the inexpressible *dharmatā*.[^15]

### 2.8 The Root of Suffering and the Meaning of Transcendence

Kuiji's *Youzhan* identifies the root of suffering in the preface to fascicle one:

> Because sentient beings, deluded about the true nature of dharmas, generate afflictions and create karma.[^16]

> Now, for sentient beings shrouded by the fetters of habitual tendencies, who respectfully receive heterodox teachings, who slander the Mahāyāna ...[^17]

The commentary on "removing all suffering" in the second fascicle:

> Through faith, study, realization, and exposition, all suffering is removed. Therefore the Great Sūtra says: those who practice the ten dharma-activities with respect to this sūtra cannot be stained by any obstruction, and even if they have committed the most grave evil karma, they can transcend all evil destinies.[^18]

### 2.9 The Difference Between the Buddha and the Two Vehicles in Transcending Suffering

The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*, fascicle 84, distinguishes the Buddha from the *śrāvaka* and *pratyekabuddha* regarding the eradication of defilements:

> The Tathāgata's afflictions and habitual tendencies are completely exhausted. Śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas have exhausted only their afflictions, while residual habitual tendencies remain.[^19]

The text illustrates this with two celebrated examples: the arhat Mṛgāra-vatsā, who after 500 lifetimes as a monkey still "leapt and swung from trees" despite having attained arhatship; and the arhat Pilindavatsa, who after 500 lifetimes as a brahmin still addressed the river goddess as "little servant."

Fascicle 27 concludes with the fire analogy:

> Arhats and pratyekabuddhas, though they have destroyed the three poisons, have not exhausted their residual tendencies — like fragrance remaining in a vessel after the perfume has been removed. ... The Buddha has permanently and completely exhausted the three poisons without remainder — like the fire at the end of an eon that burns Mount Sumeru until the very earth is consumed, with neither smoke nor ash remaining.[^20]

The "all" in "transcending all suffering" points to ultimate purity at the Buddha-level — not the partial cessation where "afflictions are exhausted but habitual tendencies remain," but the complete *āśraya-parāvṛtti* (transformation of the basis) where both afflictions and their residual tendencies are eradicated without remainder.

### 2.10 Avalokiteśvara's Role in the Saṃdhinirmocana-sūtra

Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva does not appear only in the Heart Sūtra. In fascicle 4 of the *Saṃdhinirmocana-sūtra* (T0676), Avalokiteśvara is the primary interlocutor — requesting teachings on the stages (*bhūmi*):

> Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva again addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, what are the characteristics, faults, and virtues of the afflictions that arise in bodhisattvas across the various stages?"[^21]

And questioning the secret intention behind the one-vehicle teaching:

> Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva again addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, when you say 'Whether the śrāvaka vehicle or the Mahāyāna, there is only one vehicle' — what is the hidden intention?"[^22]

And finally requesting the name of the teaching:

> "World-Honored One, in this Saṃdhinirmocana Dharma-gate, what is this teaching called? How should I uphold it?" The Buddha told Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva: "Good son, this is called 'the definitive teaching of the stages and pāramitā.'"[^23]

Avalokiteśvara's role in the *Saṃdhinirmocana* — requesting teachings on the stages, questioning the one-vehicle secret intention, asking for the definitive teaching's name — reveals that this bodhisattva is not merely a symbol of compassionate rescue but a representative of deep doctrinal inquiry. This accords perfectly with the Heart Sūtra's prajñā-centered theme and its placement of "Avalokiteśvara" as the opening word.

## III. Core Arguments

### 3.1 "Avalokiteśvara": The Dual Architecture of Causal and Fruition Levels

Kuiji's compassion-wisdom explication and Woncheuk's identification with *pratyavekṣaṇā-jñāna* both describe the **fruition level**: a bodhisattva above the first *bhūmi* who has already transformed the sixth consciousness into wondrous contemplative wisdom, already severed the two obstructions, and already attained the ten sovereign freedoms.

But if "Avalokiteśvara" carried only fruition-level meaning, the sūtra would be irrelevant to the vast majority of beings — those who have neither seen the path nor transformed consciousness into wisdom. Reading "When Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva was practicing the profound prajñāpāramitā" would be nothing more than spectating a saint's practice log.

This paper proposes that "Avalokiteśvara" simultaneously carries **causal-level meaning**.

The three characters decompose as follows:

- **Observing** (*guān*) — seeing things as they truly are without being overwhelmed by emotional reactivity. The seed (*bīja*) of this capacity — the undefiled seed — is innately present in the *ālayavijñāna*.
- **Sovereign Freedom** (*zìzài*) — freedom from bondage to external conditions and internal entanglements. This is the deepest aspiration of every being caught in suffering.
- **Bodhisattva** (*púsà*) — the subject who has generated *bodhicitta*. One does not become a bodhisattva only after attainment; one becomes a bodhisattva at the moment of aspiration.

*Bodhicitta* typically begins not with religious fervor but with suffering. For most people, the seed of *bodhicitta* sprouts in a moment of being crushed by life — "I cannot go on like this any longer. I must find a way out of this suffering."

That thought is the seed of *bodhicitta*. The person who generates it is, in the causal sense, "Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva."

✅ **Doctrinal assessment:** The two levels are mutually non-contradictory. The fruition level is fully supported by commentarial sources (Kuiji's compassion-wisdom unity, Woncheuk's *pratyavekṣaṇā-jñāna*, the ten sovereign freedoms, Avalokiteśvara's role in the *Saṃdhinirmocana*). The causal level is a pedagogical extension — Yogācāra's own five-stage path (*pañcāvasthā*) presupposes a continuous spectrum from ordinary being to sage. The causal level is where the spectrum begins; the fruition level is where it is fully disclosed.

### 3.2 "Practicing the Profound Prajñāpāramitā": Two Classical Dimensions and the Transformation of Saṃskāra

Kuiji's two dimensions — practice-depth and object-depth — address the contemplating wisdom and the contemplated object, respectively.

**Practice-depth**: non-discriminating wisdom internally realizes the twofold emptiness, free from all discriminations. Even the appearance of "I am practicing" has been transcended. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-sūtra* states: "Not seeing practice, not seeing non-practice — this is the bodhisattva's deep practice of prajñā."

**Object-depth**: the twofold emptiness as suchness transcends the characteristics of existence and non-existence, beyond all conceptual elaboration. The object is not something that can be grasped conceptually — it is *dharmatā* itself, beyond all *prapañca*.

Beyond Kuiji's two dimensions, this paper identifies a third from the perspective of lived practice:

**Transformation-depth: prajñā penetrating from the *saṃjñā* (perception/conception) level to the *saṃskāra* (volitional formation) level.**

Among the five aggregates, *saṃskāra* is the most subtle — it is the directionality of volition, the momentum of habit, the reaction that fires before thought can intervene. Most practitioners' prajñā remains at the *saṃjñā* level: they can explain "form is emptiness" and reflect after the fact that "that was my attachment." But *saṃjñā*-level prajñā requires a prompt to activate — and *saṃskāra* does not wait for prompts.

The ultimate criterion of "deep practice" is not how precise one's *saṃjñā*-level understanding is, but whether *saṃskāra* has been transformed. When prajñā truly penetrates to the *saṃskāra* level, wisdom becomes instinct: one does not need to remind oneself that "form is emptiness" — emptiness is already the default lens through which one perceives.

This is the five-aggregate correlate of "transforming consciousness into wisdom" (*zhuǎn shí chéng zhì*): the sixth consciousness (the primary operational basis of *saṃjñā*) transforms into *pratyavekṣaṇā-jñāna*, but this transformation does not remain at the intellectual level — it must penetrate to *saṃskāra*, saturating the deep structure of volitional tendency with prajñā.

⚠️ **Doctrinal assessment:** Practice-depth and object-depth are explicitly supported by Kuiji's *Youzhan*. Transformation-depth (penetration to the *saṃskāra* level as instinct) is an original insight of this paper — no direct precedent was found in the classical commentaries for interpreting "deep practice" specifically through *saṃskāra* transformation. This insight does not contradict Kuiji's two dimensions but extends them from the angle of lived practice. It should be recognized as an interpretive contribution with pedagogical value rather than a direct scriptural claim.

### 3.3 "Illuminating the Emptiness of the Five Aggregates": Pratyakṣa, Elimination of the False, and the Yogācāra Meaning of Emptiness

"Illumination" (*zhào jiàn*) in Yogācāra belongs to *yogipratyakṣa* (yogic direct perception) — the bodhisattva in deep meditative equipoise directly perceives the true nature of dharmas through non-discriminating wisdom. It is direct insight, not inferential conclusion.[^24]

A crucial doctrinal point must be clarified: **the seed of "illumination" (the potential) is innately present, but its actualization requires cultivation.** Yogācāra holds that the *ālayavijñāna* contains innate undefiled seeds (*anāsrava-bīja*) — the internal basis for Buddhahood. But these seeds require the work of hearing, reflecting, and meditating to actualize — to transform from latent potential into actual wisdom. The mirror's capacity to reflect is innate, but the dust must actually be wiped away. This is "cause innately present, fruit attained through practice" (*yīn běn yǒu, guǒ xiū dé*).

The "emptiness" in "the five aggregates are all empty" has a strict Yogācāra definition. Kuiji's five-layered contemplation, at its first layer of "eliminating the false, preserving the real":

The *parikalpita* (imagined nature) — our superimposed constructions such as "this is my body, my feelings, my consciousness" — arises solely from false construction, utterly devoid of essence or function, and should be correctly eliminated. The *paratantra* (dependent nature — causally arisen phenomena) and *pariniṣpanna* (perfected nature — *tathatā*) have real essence and should be preserved.

Kuiji specifically emphasizes:

> Eliminating is the emptiness-contemplation, counteracting attachment to existence. Preserving is the existence-contemplation, counteracting attachment to emptiness.[^25]

Emptiness-contemplation counteracts the ordinary person's attachment to the five aggregates as real entities. Existence-contemplation counteracts the two-vehicle practitioner's attachment to emptiness as nothingness. Both are simultaneously transcended: "neither existent nor empty," entering the inexpressible *dharmatā*.

✅ **Doctrinal assessment:** Illumination = *yogipratyakṣa* (non-discriminating wisdom's direct perception), not *anumāna* (inference). Emptiness = emptiness of the *parikalpita* (elimination of the false), not the emptiness of all dharmas. This is the critical distinction between Yogācāra and Madhyamaka on "emptiness" — Yogācāra holds "eliminate the false, preserve the real," while Madhyamaka maintains "all dharmas are empty of intrinsic nature." The two systems' understanding of "emptiness" must not be conflated.

### 3.4 "Transcending All Suffering": From Habitual Fetters to Ultimate Purity

Kuiji identifies the root of suffering as "sentient beings deluded about the true nature of dharmas, generating afflictions and creating karma" — beings, failing to see the threefold nature (*trisvabhāva*) of all phenomena, generate afflictions (*kleśa*) and create karma, perpetuating the cycle of suffering.

Yogācāra analyzes the root of suffering into two obstructions: the afflictive obstruction (*kleśāvaraṇa*, arising from self-grasping, obstructing *nirvāṇa*) and the cognitive obstruction (*jñeyāvaraṇa*, arising from dharma-grasping, obstructing *bodhi*). "Illuminating the emptiness of the five aggregates" simultaneously addresses both: seeing that within the aggregates there is no "self" breaks the afflictive obstruction; seeing that the aggregates are projections of consciousness rather than independently real dharma-entities breaks the cognitive obstruction.

The "all" in "transcending all suffering" points to ultimate purity. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* uses the fire analogy: śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas "have destroyed the three poisons but their residual tendencies are not exhausted — like fragrance remaining in a vessel"; the Buddha "has permanently and completely exhausted the three poisons without remainder — like the fire at the end of an eon that burns Mount Sumeru until the very earth is consumed, with neither smoke nor ash."

Residual habitual tendencies (*vāsanā*) — such as the arhat who still leaps through trees after 500 lifetimes as a monkey, or the arhat who still calls the river goddess "little servant" after 500 lifetimes as a brahmin — persist in two-vehicle saints. Only at the Buddha-level are both afflictions and habitual tendencies exhausted without remainder. The Heart Sūtra's "transcending all suffering" as its fourth link points precisely to this ultimate *āśraya-parāvṛtti* — not temporary cessation but fundamental transformation of the basis.

### 3.5 The Awakening Loop: The Endpoint Returns to the Origin

Now the four links assembled:

| Sūtra Text | Structural Role | Significance |
|------------|----------------|--------------|
| Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva | **Subject** (you who long for freedom) | Cause — *bodhicitta* arising from suffering |
| Practicing the profound prajñāpāramitā | **Method** (practice-depth and object-depth) | Process — non-discriminating wisdom realizing twofold emptiness |
| Illuminated the emptiness of the five aggregates | **Insight** (eliminating the false, preserving the real) | Pratyakṣa — prajñā's eye opens |
| Transcended all suffering | **Fruit** (ultimate purity) | *Āśraya-parāvṛtti* — afflictions and habitual tendencies exhausted |

This structure possesses a profound beauty: **the endpoint returns to the starting point.**

What you initially longed for — "Avalokiteśvara," the capacity to observe and be free — is precisely what is disclosed upon completing this loop. It is not a new state but the recognition of what was always already your original face.

The *Vajracchedikā-prajñāpāramitā-sūtra* states:

> The Tathāgata — one who has not come from anywhere and has not gone anywhere — therefore is called Tathāgata.[^26]

The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*, fascicle 72, unfolds this:

> The suchness of the Tathāgata is not separate from the suchness of all dharmas. To correctly contemplate all dharmas is to be called a Buddha. ... The suchness of the Tathāgata is identical to the suchness of all dharmas — therefore it is said that the suchness of form and the rest and the suchness of the Tathāgata are not two, not different. Ordinary beings perceive duality and difference; sages perceive neither duality nor difference.[^27]

The *Cheng weishi lun*, fascicle 10, describes the dharma-body:

> The pure dharma-realm — without arising, without ceasing, its nature unchanging — is therefore called permanent. ... The self-nature body: the true pure dharma-realm of all tathāgatas ... free from characteristics, utterly quiescent, beyond all conceptual elaboration, endowed with boundless true and permanent virtues.[^28]

"Neither coming from anywhere nor going anywhere" — the causal-level "Avalokiteśvara" (longing for the capacity to observe and be free) and the fruition-level "Avalokiteśvara" (*pratyavekṣaṇā-jñāna* and the ten sovereign freedoms) point to the same mind-ground. The difference lies in the amount of dust, not in the mirror itself. Having walked this loop — aspiration, deep practice, illumination, transcendence — one arrives not at a new place but at the recognition of where one has never left.

✅ **Doctrinal assessment:** The closed-loop structure is supported by three layers of scriptural evidence — the *Vajracchedikā*'s "neither coming nor going," the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*'s "the suchness of the Tathāgata is identical to the suchness of all dharmas," and the *Cheng weishi lun*'s dharma-body as "without arising, without ceasing, free from characteristics, utterly quiescent." Cause and fruit as the same ground, neither coming nor going — this is the ultimate meaning of prajñā and the deep structure of the Heart Sūtra's opening sentence as "master program."

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Resonance

### 4.1 Cybernetics: Positive Feedback Loop

The structure of the awakening loop has a structural parallel in cybernetics — the positive feedback loop, where a system's output reinforces its input. The experience of freedom from suffering strengthens the aspiration of "Avalokiteśvara"; deeper aspiration drives deeper practice; deeper practice yields more thorough illumination.

⚠️ Heuristic analogy. A cybernetic feedback loop is causal (the output temporally influences the input); the awakening loop's closure is trans-causal (the endpoint and origin are primordially non-dual). However, the analogy helps modern readers understand why practice can be self-reinforcing and progressively deepening.

### 4.2 Cognitive Psychology: Controlled and Automatic Processing

The third dimension of "deep practice" — prajñā penetrating from conceptual understanding to instinctive response — corresponds in cognitive psychology to the concept of automaticity. Shiffrin & Schneider (1977) distinguish "controlled processing" (requiring attentional resources, slow, consciously monitored) from "automatic processing" (requiring no attention, fast, unconsciously operating).

A beginner's mindful observation is controlled processing: one must deliberately remind oneself to "observe." When practice has penetrated to the *saṃskāra* level, observation becomes automatic processing — prajñā operates as the default mode without requiring prompts.

⚠️ Heuristic analogy. Psychological "automaticity" results from habit formation and can be wholesome or unwholesome; Yogācāra "deep practice" points specifically to the actualization of undefiled seeds, with a determined direction. But the structural shift from "requiring prompts" to "no longer requiring prompts" genuinely helps modern readers understand the substance of *saṃskāra* transformation.

## V. Middle Way Corrections

### ❌ 5.1 Misconception: "Avalokiteśvara" Is Only a Fruition-Level Saint, Irrelevant to Ordinary Beings

If only the commentarial fruition-level interpretation is adopted, the first sentence becomes a spectator's report. But Yogācāra's own five-stage path presupposes a continuous spectrum from ordinary being to sage. The accumulation-stage bodhisattva (*sambhāra-mārga*) is still amassing merit, yet already bears the name "bodhisattva." The causal level of "Avalokiteśvara" is precisely the entry point on this spectrum opened for ordinary beings.

### ❌ 5.2 Misconception: "The Five Aggregates Are All Empty" = Nihilism

"Emptiness" in Yogācāra has a strict definition: "eliminate the false, preserve the real." What is empty is the *parikalpita* (imagined constructions); what is not empty is the *paratantra* (dependent nature) and *pariniṣpanna* (perfected nature). Understanding "the five aggregates are all empty" as "nothing exists" is not only the most common misreading but precisely the "attachment to emptiness" that Kuiji's "preserving the real" counteracts. The annihilationist view (*uccheda-dṛṣṭi*) is more dangerous than the eternalist view because it severs the motivation for practice.

### ❌ 5.3 Misconception: "Transcending All Suffering" = Suffering Disappears

The phenomena of suffering do not disappear. "Transcending" (*dù*) means crossing over, not annihilation. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*'s illustrations of residual habitual tendencies make clear that even arhats, having severed afflictions, still display residual habitual patterns. The difference: those who have not awakened are submerged in suffering (identifying suffering as "my suffering"); those who have awakened cross through suffering without dwelling (seeing suffering as the flow of consciousness-projection without a fixed "self" as its bearer). Ultimate "transcendence of all suffering" — with even habitual tendencies exhausted — is consummated only at the Buddha-level.

### ❌ 5.4 Misconception: The Awakening Loop Is a One-Time Epiphany

"Illuminating the emptiness of the five aggregates" is not a once-and-for-all event. The five stages explicitly indicate that the penetration stage is the moment of non-discriminating wisdom's "first illumination of principle" — but between the penetration stage and the ultimate stage lies the cultivation stage (*bhāvanā-mārga*), potentially spanning three great incalculable eons. Each cycle of "deep practice → illumination" is an incremental process: defiled seeds in the *ālayavijñāna* weaken by one degree, undefiled seeds strengthen by one degree. The loop runs continuously; each cycle goes deeper.

## VI. Conclusion

The opening sentence of the Heart Sūtra is not a preamble but a program. Its four links — Avalokiteśvara (subject), deep practice (method), illumination (insight), transcendence of suffering (fruit) — constitute a closed awakening loop.

The deep significance of this loop lies in four dimensions:

**First, dual architecture.** On the foundation of Kuiji's compassion-wisdom explication and Woncheuk's *pratyavekṣaṇā-jñāna* identification, "Avalokiteśvara" simultaneously carries fruition-level and causal-level meaning. The fruition level is the sage's realized state; the causal level is the entry point for ordinary beings. The two are mutually non-contradictory and together constitute the complete spectrum of practice.

**Second, three dimensions of depth.** Kuiji distinguishes "two kinds of depth" — practice-depth (non-discriminating wisdom internally realizing twofold emptiness) and object-depth (twofold emptiness transcending existence and non-existence). This paper adds a third: transformation-depth — prajñā penetrating from the *saṃjñā* level to the *saṃskāra* level, becoming instinct. All three dimensions together constitute the complete face of "practicing the profound prajñāpāramitā."

**Third, eliminating the false, preserving the real.** "Illuminating the emptiness of the five aggregates" is not nihilism. Kuiji's five-layered contemplation judges emptiness as "eliminating the false, preserving the real" — empty is the *parikalpita*'s false construction; not empty is the *paratantra*'s causal arising and the *pariniṣpanna*'s *tathatā*. Emptiness and existence are both simultaneously transcended, culminating in "neither existent nor empty" — the entry into inexpressible *dharmatā*.

**Fourth, neither coming nor going.** The loop's closure mirrors the ultimate meaning of the Tathāgata. "Neither coming from anywhere nor going anywhere" — the aspiration at the causal level and the sovereign freedom at the fruition level point to the same mind-ground. Having walked the loop, one returns to the starting point — but this time, recognizing the original face that was never departed from.

Every subsequent passage in the Heart Sūtra — the four statements on form and emptiness, the twelve sense-bases, the eighteen elements, the twelve links of dependent origination, the four noble truths, "no wisdom and no attainment" — expands upon different nodes of this master program. Readers who first grasp the four links and the loop structure will read what follows not as scattered fragments but as a unified architecture in which every passage has its precise position and purpose.
