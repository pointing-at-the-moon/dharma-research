## Abstract

This is the tenth paper in the Pointing at the Moon *Lotus Sūtra* (*Saddharmapuṇḍarīka*) research series, treating Chapter Twenty-Five, "The Universal Gate of the Bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara" (觀世音菩薩普門品). The central claim of this paper is that **the chapter is the final structural completion of the outward-responsive logic of the *Lotus*'s trace-gate (迹門), and the entrance of the One Vehicle on the side of sentient beings** — every suffering being who invokes the name with a single thought is already taking the first step of the One-Vehicle path on the being's side.

The argument proceeds in three layers. **Etymology**: the chapter itself never supplies the etymology of the name. Akṣayamati's opening question "for what cause and condition is the bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara so named?" receives an answer that describes the *function* of the name ("single-mindedly invoke… and he will immediately perceive their voices") rather than the *conferral*. The scripture that actually records the occasion, the agent, and the causal circumstances under which the name was bestowed is the *Karuṇāpuṇḍarīka-sūtra* (悲華經, T0157), in the passage where Tathāgata Ratnagarbha names Prince Animiṣa. The coexistence of three Chinese renderings — Dharmarakṣa's "Guangshiyin" (光世音, *Āloka-svara*), Kumārajīva's "Guanshiyin" (觀世音, *Avalokita-svara*), and Xuanzang's "Guanzizai" (觀自在, *Avalokita-īśvara*) — reflects genuine multiplicity within the Sanskrit transmission itself, not accumulated translation error. **Structure**: the thirty-three responsive bodies are not a Mahāyāna invention; their structural seeds are strictly present in the Āgamas — the Brahmā-entreaty scene (*Mahāpadāna* of the *Dīrghāgama*) as the prototype of the "compassionate intercessor," and the "Four Divine Abodes pervading the ten directions" formula of the *Madhyamāgama* as the spatial-pervasion grammar later dramatized into the universal-gate manifestation. **The name is strictly absent from the Āgamas, but the structure is strictly present** — this is the paper's methodological key. **Mirror thesis**: the chapter's outward circuit (suffering → invocation → perceiving sound → liberation) is the strict structural counterpart of the *Heart Sūtra*'s inward circuit (observe → illuminate → see → cross). Drawing on Fazang's *Brief Commentary on the Prajñāpāramitā-hṛdaya Sūtra* (T1712), which glosses "the first reading as wisdom, the second as compassion," the paper restores the unity of Guanzizai and Guanshiyin from footnote to main thesis, and extends it into a "three-scripture isomorphism": the *Heart Sūtra* takes the gate of wisdom, the *Śūraṅgama* ear-faculty perfect penetration the gate of concentration, and the *Lotus* Universal Gate the gate of compassion — the three trainings of śīla, samādhi, and prajñā severally brought to fulfillment in a single bodhisattva across three scriptures.

The paper foregrounds the structural progression of four patriarchal commentaries: Vasubandhu's *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka-upadeśa* (T1519/T1520) locates the chapter as "the power of protecting beings from perils" — the third of the Tathāgata's five gates of cultivation-power; Zhiyi's *Fahua wenju* (T1718) pairs "Dharma-body and response-body" under the schema of "ten pairs in common," with the concise judgment "if the observation is not round, the gate is not called universal"; Jizang's *Fahua yishu* (T1721) proposes the "three-name theory," mapping the three sections of the Buddha's reply onto Guanshiyin (verbal karma), Guanshiyi (mental karma), and Guanshishen (bodily karma) as capacity-designs for three wholesome karmas; and Kuiji's *Fahua xuanzan* (T1723) completes the Yogācāra landing with "three supernormal powers × three karmas × three wheels of Universal Gate" — Guanyin's powers are strictly limited to the divine-eye, divine-ear, and knowledge-of-others'-minds, the three cognitive supernormal powers, mapped onto the three karmas of refuge.

Kuiji's "resolving doubts" passage is the structural summit of the four commentaries and the single most important citation of this paper: "Question: the bodhisattva's great compassion observes suffering and saves at once; why does he wait for mindfulness before bestowing his mercy? Answer: that beings without any mind are still saved by the Buddha — this is something even the great sages cannot bring about; that beings first arouse intention and then seek conditions — this is the shared pattern of all sages. First there must be karma made by self, then the conditions may be relied upon; if the self does not cultivate, what can the condition do?" This supplies a patriarch-level direct answer to the two-thousand-year-old tension between "karma is made by self, received by self" and "salvation by another's power": Guanyin's supernormal manifestations can only occupy the position of *dominant condition* (*adhipati-pratyaya*) within the Yogācāra four-condition structure; they can never replace the being's own mind as the *proximate cause* (*hetu-pratyaya*). Salvific resonance is not an exception to the karmic law but its fully unfolded form within Mahāyāna resonance theory.

## I. Introduction: The Most Familiar of Strangers

Of all the chapters of the *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka-sūtra*, the one most often extracted for recitation, most often carved in stone, most often enshrined as an independent object of devotion is not the "Ten Suchnesses" of the Skillful Means chapter, nor the distant attainment of buddhahood in the Lifespan chapter, but Chapter 25, the "Universal Gate of the Bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara" (觀世音菩薩普門品). In East Asian Buddhist history it has been so thoroughly excerpted as an independent scripture — the circulation of the standalone *Universal Gate Sūtra* may well exceed that of the entire *Lotus* — that most readers have no idea it was originally one chapter of the *Lotus*.

But this very familiarity conceals three structural blind spots.

**First blind spot: the "Universal Gate" chapter itself never supplies an etymology for the name "Avalokiteśvara."** Akṣayamati opens by asking, "World-Honored One, for what cause and condition is the bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara so named?" — but the Buddha's answer describes the **function** of the name ("When sentient beings in distress single-mindedly invoke this name, Avalokiteśvara immediately perceives their voices, and all are liberated"), not the **conferral** of the name. The scripture that actually records the occasion, the agent, and the causal circumstances under which the name was bestowed upon this bodhisattva is the *Karuṇāpuṇḍarīka-sūtra* (悲華經, T0157), in the passage where Tathāgata Ratnagarbha issues the prediction — not the "Universal Gate" chapter. This philological fact has been systematically overlooked in the Sinitic Guanyin devotional sphere for fifteen hundred years.

**Second blind spot: "Guanshiyin" is not the only translation; the three Chinese renderings correspond to three different Sanskrit readings.** Dharmarakṣa's *Zheng fahua jing* (正法華經, T0263) renders it as "Guangshiyin" (光世音), corresponding to *Āloka-svara*; Kumārajīva's *Miaofa lianhua jing* (妙法蓮華經, T0262) gives "Guanshiyin" (觀世音), corresponding to *Avalokita-svara*; Xuanzang's *Heart Sūtra* (T0251) gives "Guanzizai" (觀自在), corresponding to *Avalokita-īśvara*. The coexistence of the three is not an accumulation of translation error but a faithful reflection of genuinely different readings in the Sanskrit transmission itself at the two slots "light / observing" and "sound / sovereign." To flatten this difference by citing Xuanzang's criticism of Kumārajīva as "corrupt and abbreviated," or to dismiss Xuanzang by appealing to Kumārajīva's wider circulation, is to overwrite philological fact with later sectarian sentiment.

**Third blind spot: the responsive structure of the chapter has been obscured, by "miracle-story" readings, from its own intra-Buddhist basis in concentration and wisdom.** Unburned by fire, undrowned by water, weapons breaking into pieces — these passages have long been read along two extremes: devotees take them literally as miracles, scholars take them as literary hyperbole. Both readings dodge the hard question: **if responsive manifestation is real, how does it coexist with the law of karmic self-responsibility?** The chapter itself does not answer this head-on, but Kuiji's *Fahua xuanzan* (T1723) provides a patriarch-level direct response — the structural correspondence of three supernormal powers to three karmic faculties (verbal karma to the divine-ear, mental karma to knowledge-of-others'-minds, bodily karma to the divine-eye) downgrades responsive manifestation from "divine power intervening in karmic outcome" to "supernormal power as a dominant condition (*adhipati-pratyaya*) that strengthens the manifestation of the sentient being's own wholesome roots." This is not apologetics; it is a structural Yogācāra reading.

---

The task of this paper is to take all three blind spots on at once. The argument advances in three layers.

**Layer one: the mirror thesis.** Point-to-the-Moon Series S2-P02, "The Four Links of Avalokiteśvara," has already argued that the opening of the *Heart Sūtra* — "When the bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara coursed in the deep Prajñāpāramitā, he illuminated and saw that the five aggregates are all empty and crossed beyond all suffering" — is a closed circuit of awakening: **observe → illuminate → see → cross**. S7-P10 now argues that the "Universal Gate" chapter is the **outward-responsive counterpart** of this circuit. Guanzizai is the inward fulfillment of prajñā as substance; Guanshiyin-Universal-Gate is the outward fulfillment of prajñā as function. The two are not two bodhisattvas but the same prajñā completed along its "inward" and "outward" vectors. This is not a new Point-to-the-Moon thesis. Fazang's *Prajñāpāramitā-hṛdaya Commentary* (T1712) already wrote centuries ago: "The first reading takes it as wisdom; the second takes it as compassion" — reading "Guanzizai" as the gate of wisdom and "Guanshiyin" as the gate of compassion. What P10 does is restore this patriarch-level precedent from footnote status to thesis status.

**Layer two: the structural thesis.** The thirty-three responsive bodies of the "Universal Gate" chapter are not Mahāyāna drama from nowhere; they have Āgamic structural seeds, along two lines. One line is the **Brahmā-entreaty** scene in the *Mahāpadāna* of the *Dīrghāgama* and the "Exhortation" chapter of the *Ekottarāgama*: Brahmā "universally observes the classes of beings," sees that the Buddha is about to enter nirvāṇa, and entreats him to remain and teach. This is the prototype of the "compassionate intercessor" — an external person stepping in at the threshold moment when beings are about to miss their opportunity for liberation. The other line is the recurring **Four Divine Abodes Pervading the Ten Directions** formula throughout the *Madhyamāgama*: "With a mind imbued with loving-kindness he pervades one direction and abides, then the second, third, fourth direction, the four intermediates, above and below, universally everywhere, with a mind imbued with loving-kindness." The four immeasurables of loving-kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, and equanimity here cover the ten-directional world through a grammar of spatial pervasion. By the time of the Mahāyāna, this "pervading the ten directions" grammar had been dramatized into the responsive-body structure of "universal-gate manifestation." **The name is strictly absent from the Āgamas, but the structure is strictly present in the Āgamas.** This distinction is the methodological key of the paper — what we are tracking is not the provenance of a name, but the doctrinal prefiguration of a responsive structure.

**Layer three: the etymological thesis.** As noted, the "Universal Gate" chapter does not supply the etymology of the name. The actual etymology lies in the *Karuṇāpuṇḍarīka* (T0157): Tathāgata Ratnagarbha tells the first crown prince of the wheel-turning king, "You observe the gods, humans, and all beings of the three evil destinies, and you have given rise to great compassion… Now I shall name you Avalokiteśvara." This single utterance tells us three things at once: (1) the name "Avalokiteśvara" is itself Ratnagarbha's naming of "great compassion," not a title the bodhisattva took for himself; (2) the object of "observing" is "gods, humans, and all beings of the three evil destinies," not sound — sound is merely the functional facet later unfolded in the "Universal Gate" chapter; (3) the logic by which the name is conferred is "**named on account of great compassion**," which stands in a substance-function relation with the chapter's "deliverance on account of hearing sounds." Once these three points are clarified, the textual bedrock of the entire Sinitic Guanyin devotional tradition can finally be set square.

The three layers are not three independent topics but a single chain of argument: the **etymological** layer establishes the correct starting point of the name (the *Karuṇāpuṇḍarīka*, not the "Universal Gate"); the **structural** layer establishes the doctrinal prefiguration of responsive manifestation (Āgamic pervasion of the ten directions, not miracle); the **mirror** layer establishes the unity of inward illumination and outward response (Guanzizai and Guanshiyin as two faces of the one prajñā). Together, the three layers restore the "Universal Gate" chapter from a folk-divination text to the formal completion of the outward-responsive logic of the *Lotus* trace-gate.

## II. Scripture: The Chapter Itself and the Textual Bedrock of the Name

### 2.1 The Two-Layer Structure of the Chapter: Prose and Verse-Repetition

In Kumārajīva's translation, the "Universal Gate of the Bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara," Chapter 25 of the *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka*, is built on two layers: **prose** (長行) and **verse-repetition** (重頌偈). This prose-plus-verse-repetition structure is not incidental; it recurs across the chapters of the *Lotus* and is a standard form of Indian scriptural redaction — prose states doctrine and scene, verse compresses and restates the core for memorization. What is distinctive about the "Universal Gate" chapter, however, is that **the verse-repetition is not a mechanical summary of the prose; it supplies several key structural elements that the prose leaves unstated** — most importantly the "five observations" formula, "true observation, pure observation, vast and wise observation, compassionate observation and loving-kindness observation, always to be aspired to and always to be looked up to." These five observations do not appear in the prose.

The prose itself divides into three sections. The first is Akṣayamati's question and the Buddha's functional answer:

> "World-Honored One, for what cause and condition is the bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara so named?" The Buddha told the bodhisattva Akṣayamati: "Good son, if there are incalculable hundreds of thousands of millions of beings undergoing suffering who hear of this bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara and single-mindedly invoke the name, the bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara will immediately perceive their voices, and all will be liberated."[^1]

This establishes the chapter's core proposition: **suffering — invocation — perceiving the sound — liberation**, four links constituting an outward-responsive circuit. This circuit stands in strict parallel with the inward-illumination circuit of S2-P02 (observe — illuminate — see — cross), a comparison to be unfolded in §IV below.

The second section is the enumeration of the "seven perils, twelve perils, three poisons, two seekings." Literal-miracle readers treat this as a contractual clause of "ask and it shall be granted"; literary readers treat it as the hyperbole of Indian narrative convention. Both miss its actual function within the trace-gate logic of the *Lotus*: it is neither contract nor hyperbole, but a **grammatical drill on "responsive manifestation without fixed direction."** The enumeration does not aim at exhaustion, but at conditioning the reader to the topological property that **no type of suffering lies outside the universal-gate structure**.

The third section is the manifestation of the thirty-three responsive bodies. This is the most frequently cited part of the chapter and the canonical basis of the thirty-three-Guanyin iconographic tradition. But the real structural point is not the number "thirty-three"; it is the **classificatory logic** of the responsive bodies — from Buddha, pratyekabuddha, śrāvaka (the three vehicles), to Brahmā, Śakra, Īśvara (the heavens), to kings, elders, householders (the human lay sphere), to monks and nuns (the human monastic sphere), to women, boys and girls, non-humans (the miscellaneous). This classification covers the **social-identity dimension** of all sentient beings in the Dharmadhātu, complementing the **spatial dimension** of the Four Divine Abodes "pervading the ten directions." The shift from spatial to social dimension is precisely the Mahāyāna dramatization of the Āgamic structural seed.

### 2.2 The Coexistence of Three Translations — From "Guangshiyin" to "Guanshiyin" to "Guanzizai"

In the history of Chinese translation, the "Universal Gate" chapter has three critical surviving versions, whose renderings of the bodhisattva's name constitute a miniature etymological history — and the strongest textual evidence for P10's thesis that "the name is not a label but a structure."

The earliest is Dharmarakṣa's *Zheng fahua jing* (T0263, ca. 286 CE), where the chapter is titled "Universal Gate of Guangshiyin" and the bodhisattva is named "**Guangshiyin**" (光世音).[^2] These three characters likely correspond to *Āloka-svara* ("sound of light") or to an early reading of *Avalokita-svara* in which *avalokita* is taken as a passive participle ("that which is observed") rather than as an active observer. The rendering preserves a key grammatical clue: "**sound" comes first; the active aspect of "observing" is secondary.** In Dharmarakṣa's text the passage reads, "Those who hear the name of the bodhisattva Guangshiyin shall straightaway be liberated" — the whole salvific mechanism rests on the **beings' side** (hearing-the-name-is-release), and there is no active clause on the bodhisattva's side of "perceiving their voices."

The second is Kumārajīva's *Miaofa lianhua jing* (T0262, 406 CE), which retitles the chapter "Universal Gate of the Bodhisattva Guanshiyin," fixes the name as "**Guanshiyin**," and inserts into the "seven perils" passage the five characters "**immediately perceives their voices**" (即時觀其音聲).[^3] These five characters are absent from Dharmarakṣa's version. Kumārajīva here made a decisive translational choice — he elevated the bodhisattva from "one whose name is heard" to "an active observer," transforming the salvific mechanism from the unidirectional "beings invoke → bodhisattva affirms" to the bidirectional "beings invoke ↔ bodhisattva perceives." This change proved decisive for the shape of East Asian Guanyin devotion: Guanyin ceased to be a passive recipient and became the **active subject** searching out the voices of suffering.

The third is the *Tianpin miaofa lianhua jing* (T0264, 601 CE), co-translated by Jñānagupta and Dharmagupta in the Sui. The chapter title remains "Universal Gate of the Bodhisattva Guanshiyin," and — crucially — **the five characters "perceives their voices" are preserved in T0264**.[^4] This is decisive for the etymological question. If the Sanskrit source of T0264 and that of Kumārajīva both belonged to an "expanded recension" while Dharmarakṣa's belonged to an "early abbreviated recension," then "perceives their voices" was not Kumārajīva's invention but already present in the middle-period Sanskrit; Kumārajīva's contribution was to crystallize the five characters into the precise Chinese name "Guanshiyin." In other words: **the three-character name "Guanshiyin" and the five-character passage "immediately perceives their voices" mutually define each other** — the name is not an appellation for the bodhisattva but a naming of the salvific structure itself.

As for Xuanzang's rendering "Guanzizai" (*Avalokita-īśvara*) — this is a reading from another Sanskrit lineage, where *svara* of *Avalokita-svara* is read as *īśvara* ("sovereign"), taking the sense "one who, by active observation, attains sovereignty." Xuanzang's *Heart Sūtra* reads, "When the bodhisattva Guanzizai coursed in deep prajñāpāramitā…" The coexistence of the three translations demonstrates a single fact: **the Sanskrit transmission itself developed multiple spelling traditions**, and the Chinese renderings faithfully reflect this plurality. Dharmarakṣa's "Guangshiyin" takes *Āloka-svara*, Kumārajīva's "Guanshiyin" takes *Avalokita-svara*, Xuanzang's "Guanzizai" takes *Avalokita-īśvara* — three names corresponding to three philological stages or three recensions, not a matter of right and wrong.

Structurally, however, the three renderings converge on the same core: **the bodhisattva's essence is a triadic circuit of "observing — sound — sovereignty,"** whose three members are variously emphasized across the three renderings — Dharmarakṣa emphasizes "sound" (the object observed), Kumārajīva emphasizes "world-sound" (the scope of the object = all the sounds of suffering in the world), Xuanzang emphasizes "sovereignty" (the meritorious result accruing to the observer). Stitched together, the three names give the complete structure. **This is why P10 takes "Guanshiyin" as the primary name in the main text while noting the three renderings in the footnotes — any single name is only one facet of the structure.**

### 2.3 Birth-Story Origins — The Double Positioning of the *Karuṇāpuṇḍarīka* and the *Prediction-Sūtra*

The three characters "Guanshiyin" in the "Universal Gate" chapter are a structural naming, but if we stay on the structural layer alone and never ask about the bodhisattva's provenance, the chapter becomes an abstract salvific topology, losing its felt life as a **concrete lineage of vow-power**. Avalokiteśvara is not a personification of a concept but a concrete bodhisattva with a birth-story, a vow, a prediction, and a succession-trajectory. This concreteness is established by two "companion scriptures": Dharmakṣema's *Karuṇāpuṇḍarīka-sūtra* (悲華經, T0157, Northern Liang) and Dharmodgata's *Prediction-Sūtra of Avalokiteśvara* (觀世音菩薩授記經, T0371, Liu Song).

Fascicle 3 of the *Karuṇāpuṇḍarīka*, the "Chapter on the Original Prediction of the Bodhisattvas," supplies Avalokiteśvara's "first arising of the mind" narrative.[^5] Countless kalpas in the past, in the eon of Good Persistence in the Sāṇḍīraṇya world-sphere, a Buddha named Tathāgata Ratnagarbha arose; at the same time there was a wheel-turning sage-king named **Araṇemin** (無諍念, the former life of Amitābha). The king had a thousand sons; the eldest was named **Animiṣa** ("the unblinking one," 不眴). The king and his thousand sons made offerings for three months at Ratnagarbha's place, and Brahmin Ratnāvabhāsa (the former life of Śākyamuni) exhorted them to arouse the bodhi-mind. Ratnāvabhāsa exhorted each in turn to the highest vow, and each received his prediction from Ratnagarbha: the king received the prediction of "Amitāyus Buddha, Sukhāvatī land"; Prince Animiṣa received the prediction of "Avalokiteśvara, the Tathāgata King-of-the-Mountain of Universally Radiating Merit of All Light"; the second prince Ni-mo received the prediction of Mahāsthāmaprāpta.

Three structural points stand out. First, **the three sages of the Pure Land — Amitābha, Avalokiteśvara, Mahāsthāmaprāpta — make their vows at the same place and the same time**; they are not three scattered bodhisattvas but three positions at a single vow-scene. Second, **the exhorter is Śākyamuni (as the former Ratnāvabhāsa)** — Avalokiteśvara's bodhi-mind was kindled by Śākyamuni himself, which establishes a teacher-student relation between Śākyamuni and Guanyin at the level of bodhicitta. Third, **the very name "Animiṣa," the unblinking one, prefigures "universal-gate observing"** — blinking is the ordinary condition of beings; not-blinking is the state of constant observation that never departs from any being for an instant. The "observation" in the chapter's "perceiving their voices," traced back to the birth-story layer, is precisely this original "unblinking."

The *Prediction-Sūtra of Avalokiteśvara* then supplies the "future buddhahood" narrative.[^6] It records that Amitābha's life-span, long though it be, will eventually end in parinirvāṇa, after which his True Dharma will abide for a period equal to his life-span. When Amitābha's True Dharma has ended, at the moment of dawn after midnight, **Avalokiteśvara will attain perfect enlightenment beneath a bodhi-tree of seven jewels, with the title "Tathāgata King-of-the-Mountain of Universal Radiance and Merit,"** in the land named "Adorned by the Universal Gathering of Many Jewels"; Mahāsthāmaprāpta will then succeed him as Buddha with the title "Tathāgata Well-Abiding Jewel-King of Merit." This prediction elevates Avalokiteśvara from "successor bodhisattva" to "continuing Dharma-king" and specifies the **structural position** of the succession — Guanyin is the "second-generation Buddha" of Amitābha's Pure Land, Mahāsthāmaprāpta the "third-generation," the three forming an unbroken dynasty of Dharma-kings.

More importantly, the *Prediction-Sūtra* also provides a second birth-story line for Avalokiteśvara: in the age of the Tathāgata Suvarṇasiṃhavikrīḍita, a King Weide (former life of Śākyamuni) entered samādhi, and two boys, **Ratnacitta and Ratnottama**, were born by transformation from lotuses at his sides — the former lives of Guanyin and Mahāsthāmaprāpta. This birth-story does not contradict the "Animiṣa" story from the *Karuṇāpuṇḍarīka*; they are two facets of the same bodhisattva's birth-stories across scriptures — just as the Buddha himself has innumerable birth-stories, so too a bodhisattva's birth-stories run along multiple parallel lines of vow. But the two lines share one point: **both scenes of Avalokiteśvara's arising-of-the-mind feature Śākyamuni** — once as the exhorting Brahmin Ratnāvabhāsa, once as King Weide manifesting the lotus-born children. This establishes a structural fact: **Avalokiteśvara's bodhicitta is at its root bound up with Śākyamuni's bodhicitta**, and as a chapter of the trace-gate of the *Lotus*, the chapter's "Guanyin responsive manifestation" is, in essence, Śākyamuni's vow-power unfolding through another persona.

These two birth-story scriptures play a decisive role in P10's argument: they pull "Guanshiyin" from the **structural naming** of the "Universal Gate" chapter back to **a concrete lineage of vow-power**. Without these two scriptures, the Guanyin of the "Universal Gate" chapter would look like an abstract salvific function; with them, Guanyin has a clear provenance, clear companions (Amitābha, Mahāsthāmaprāpta), a clear future, and a clear binding relation with Śākyamuni. **"Guanshiyin" is thus both a structural naming and a proper name — neither side can be dropped.**

### 2.4 The Structural Independence of the Five-Observation Passage in the Verse-Repetition

Before turning to the commentarial section, one more structure should be noted — one that the prose omits and only the verse-repetition supplies: the **five observations**, "true observation, pure observation, vast and wise observation, compassionate observation and loving-kindness observation, always to be aspired to and always to be looked up to." This passage is not among the seven perils, twelve perils, three poisons, or two seekings of the prose, nor among the thirty-three responsive bodies; it stands on its own near the close of the chapter's verse-repetition as an independent list of modes of observation. Where the prose describes the "perceiving voices" salvific function, the verse-repetition here supplies **the structure by which Guanshiyin is able to observe at all**. The five are not five different observations but five facets of one observation: true observation (attesting to the substance of suchness), pure observation (its function of separating from defilement), vast-and-wise observation (the extent of prajñā's pervading illumination), compassionate observation (its object, the field of suffering), loving-kindness observation (its offering, the cause of joy). The first three belong to the gate of wisdom; the last two belong to the gate of compassion; the five taken together form the "non-dual wisdom-compassion" structure of observing.

The importance of this passage is that it pulls the chapter back from "outward-responsive functionalism" to "the ontology of the observing capacity," and supplies direct scriptural grounding, within the chapter itself, for the "Guanzizai / Guanshiyin mirror thesis" to be developed in §IV. It turns out that the chapter's own verse-repetition has already singled out the observing character of "observation"; only the prose leaves it unsaid. This also explains why the chapter, lacking any etymological discussion of its own, can still sustain fifteen hundred years of readings that treat Guanyin as an observing subject — the five-observation structure of the verse-repetition is an internal self-interpretation.

## III. Commentary: The Structural Progression of Four Patriarchal Commentaries

The "Universal Gate" chapter is one of the most heavily annotated chapters of the *Lotus*. The commentarial tradition begins with Vasubandhu's *Lotus Commentary* and advances over four centuries through Tiantai Zhiyi, Sanlun Jizang, and finally Yogācāra Kuiji. This section takes four commentators in chronological order (Vasubandhu, Zhiyi, Jizang, Kuiji) and focuses not on exhaustive commentary but on a single progressive line: from Vasubandhu's positional judgment of "the five gates of cultivation-power," to Zhiyi's pairwise comparison of "ten pairs and thirty meanings" between trace and origin, to Jizang's capacity-based provisioning of "three names for three karmic faculties," to Kuiji's Yogācāra landing — the "three supernormal powers and three karmas, three wheels of universal gate." The four commentators advance step by step, together turning "invoke the name and be saved from peril" from a surface mythological reading into a structural answer to the mechanism of resonance.

### 3.1 Vasubandhu's *Lotus Commentary*: "The Power to Protect Beings from Perils" and the "Dharma-body Equality" Metric of Merit

Vasubandhu's *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka-upadeśa* survives in two Chinese translations, by Bodhiruci (T1519) and Ratnamati (T1520), doctrinally identical — the earliest extant systematic commentary on the "Universal Gate" chapter.[^7] Vasubandhu groups the later chapters of the *Lotus* under the Tathāgata's five gates of cultivation-power: the power of teaching (Divine Powers), the power of ascetic practice (Bhaiṣajyarāja, Gadgadasvara), **the power of protecting beings from perils** (Guanyin, Dhāraṇī), the power of meritorious excellence (Śubhavyūharāja), and the power of protecting the Dharma (Samantabhadra). Two points follow from this positional judgment. First, the "Universal Gate" chapter is not an isolated collection of resonance stories but one link — specifically, the protection-from-perils link — in the Tathāgata's system of "cultivation-powers." Second, Guanyin's "responsive manifestation" is not a direct function of the Tathāgata himself but the teaching-power of one bodhisattva dispatched by the Tathāgata to appear where beings are in peril.[^8]

Vasubandhu then explains the scripture's metric: "holding the name of Avalokiteśvara equals holding the names of sixty-two Ganges-sands' worth of Buddhas." Read as a calculation of supernormal power, this falls into the inconceivable. Vasubandhu shifts directly to the doctrinal level, supplying two meanings: **faith-power** and **ultimate knowing**. Faith-power has two strata — "seeking a body like Avalokiteśvara's, through ultimate faith" and "arousing a reverent mind, that I too shall ultimately attain such merit." Ultimate knowing is knowing that "Dharmadhātu = dharma-nature = body of equality = suchness as Dharma-body," a meaning that only bodhisattvas from the first bhūmi onward can realize.[^9] Vasubandhu's point is that the equivalence of invocation rests "not on a quantity of supernormal power but on the equality of the Dharma-body" — the name "Avalokiteśvara" does not refer to the bounded merits of one particular bodhisattva but to a representative of "the body of equality as dharma-nature"; invoking Guanyin is faith that "this very self is suchness as Dharma-body." The meaning is profound, and it sets the tone for later Yogācāra and tathāgatagarbha readings of the chapter.

One detail worth noting: the two translations of the same treatise render the name differently — T1519 as "Guanshizizai" (觀世自在), T1520 as "Guanshiyin" (觀世音). At the level of commentary, this again confirms the earlier point that "*svara* and *īśvara* coexist as two layers of meaning" in the Sanskrit.

### 3.2 Zhiyi's *Fahua wenju* and Jizang's *Fahua yishu*: Ten Pairs and Three-Karma Capacities

Tiantai Zhiyi's *Fahua wenju*, fascicle 10 lower, "Commentary on the Universal Gate of the Bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara," opens by setting up the master schema "**ten pairs in common, five singles in particular.**"[^10] The ten pairs are ten categories in juxtaposition — person and dharma, loving-kindness and compassion, the two adornments, dharma and response, medicine and jewel, hidden and manifest, provisional and real, trace and origin, cause and consequence, wisdom and cessation. The core pair is **dharma and response**:

> "Guanshiyin" means "observing secretly upon the object" — this is the **Dharma-body**. "Universal Gate" means "manifesting in accord with response" — this is the **response-body**.[^11]

The sentence directly maps the twofold "Guanshiyin / Universal Gate" onto "Dharma-body / response-body" — Guanyin is the observing substance of the Dharma-body; Universal Gate is the manifest function of response. The pairs of "trace and origin," "provisional and real," "wisdom and cessation" among the ten are all facets of this central pairing. In his separate gloss on the word "observation," Zhiyi offers the concise judgment **"if the observation is not round, the gate is not called universal"** — of the four kinds of observation (analytic, substantial, sequential, round), only "round observation" is taken; the universality of "universal" is grounded in the roundness of "observation." This is Tiantai's deepest argument for tying universal pervasion back to the perfection of the observing subject.

Jizang of the Sanlun school, in fascicle 12 of his *Fahua yishu*, gives a commentary on the chapter close in date to Zhiyi's but different in path. Jizang divides the whole chapter into four pairs — "twin-heading, twin-explanation, twin-praise, twin-benefit"[^12] — and under twin-heading lists ten pairs of his own (person-dharma, true-response, inner-outer, loving-kindness—compassion, medicine-jewel, resonance-response, worldly—supramundane, supernormal-display, secret-manifest, name-virtue). The most distinctive is the **inner-outer pair**: "Guanyin refers to the bodhisattva's mental karma; Universal Gate refers to the bodily and verbal karmas — first observing capacity with the mind, then manifesting supernormal power with the body and teaching with speech."[^13] This maps the twofold Guanyin / Universal Gate directly onto the three-karma unfolding "mental observation of capacity → bodily and verbal response," which lands more concretely than Zhiyi's "Dharma-body / response-body" at the level of three-karma mechanics.

Jizang's most original passage is the **three-name theory**. He reads the three sections of the Buddha's answer (invocation under seven perils, constant mindfulness against three poisons, and veneration for two seekings) as three usages of the name designed by the Buddha to "generate wholesome action in the three karmas": **Guanshiyin** (corresponding to verbal karma, invocation), **Guanshiyi** 觀世意 (corresponding to mental karma, mindful holding), and **Guanshishen** 觀世身 (corresponding to bodily karma, veneration).[^14] The three are not three names actually held by the bodhisattva, but "capacity-designs set up to generate three kinds of wholesome karma in beings." Jizang then poses a question to himself — "since all three names exist, why do we invoke only 'Guanshiyin'?" — and answers, "the verbal-karma name covers both mental and bodily karma, so it produces the most wholesomeness." The subtlety here is that the name is converted from an ontological question into a functional one: the name is a capacity-device, not the bodhisattva's ultimate reality; a teaching tool, not an object of supernormal power. With this single move, the chapter's resonance structure is lifted from the mythological layer to the pedagogical layer.

### 3.3 Kuiji's *Fahua xuanzan*: Three Powers × Three Karmas × Three Wheels — and the Direct Answer to "Karma Made by Self"

Kuiji's *Miaofa lianhua jing xuanzan*, fascicle 10 closing section, is the most systematic of the four commentaries and the structural high point of this paper.[^15] Kuiji uses the three-gate format of "coming-significance, explanation of the name, and resolving doubts," and it is under the third gate — "resolving doubts" — that he gives the most precise direct answer to the Yogācāra tension between "karma is made by self, received by self" and "salvation by another's power." This is precisely the point where S7_PLAN's middle-way correction #9 lands as a patriarch-level positive answer.

**Layer one: the resonance structure matching three powers to three karmas.** Under "coming-significance," Kuiji follows Vasubandhu and further specifies the division of labor between the Guanyin chapter and the Dhāraṇī chapter: Guanyin saves beings who have not yet aroused the bodhi-mind; Dhāraṇī protects those who already hold the sūtra. Crucially, he strictly delimits Guanyin's supernormal powers to "**the divine-eye, divine-ear, and knowledge-of-others'-minds — the three powers**," not all six.[^16] These three map to the three karmas of refuge: verbal karma (invocation) under the divine-ear; mental karma (mindful holding) under knowledge-of-others'-minds; bodily karma (veneration) under the divine-eye. This is precise Yogācāra structural matching — Guanyin is not an omnipotent deity, but a bodhisattva whose substance is "the three cognitive supernormal powers" and whose object is the three karmas.

**Layer two: the substance of Universal Gate as the three wheels.** Under "explanation of the name," Kuiji glosses "Universal Gate": "When the three karmas take refuge, the six powers must descend in equal salvation, pointing to the place where suffering is left and joy entered; hence the name 'Universal Gate.' The three wheels — **the wheel of supernormal power, the wheel of prediction-speech, the wheel of instruction** — these, as the cause and ground by which suffering is left and stillness is entered, are the substance of Universal Gate."[^17] This grounds the substance of "Universal Gate" in the three wheels corresponding to the three karmas: bodily-supernormal, verbal-prediction, mental-instruction. The three wheels and the three karmas constitute the entirety of Universal Gate's content. Under the same gate, Kuiji also states that "the correct rendering should be Guanzizai," taking the comprehensiveness of "three-karma refuge, six-power descending salvation" — a direct contrast to the "Guanshiyin" chosen by Tiantai and Sanlun. This is not a translation dispute but two facets of the same structure: "Guanshiyin" foregrounds the *svara* face (the sounds received), "Guanzizai" foregrounds the *īśvara* face (the sovereign capacity to respond). Within the "three-karma resonance" framework they do not conflict.

**Layer three: "resolving doubts" — the direct reconciliation of karma-of-self and resonance.** This is the structural summit of Kuiji's commentary, and the single most important passage cited in this paper:

> Question: The bodhisattva's great compassion observes suffering and saves at once; why does he wait for mindfulness before bestowing his mercy?
> Answer: **That beings without any mind are still saved by the Buddha — this is something even the great sages cannot bring about. That beings first arouse intention and then seek conditions — this is the shared pattern of all sages. First there must be karma made by self, then the conditions may be relied upon; if the self does not cultivate, what can the condition do?** Hence only when he turns and is mindful is he saved. Even if he cultivates yet has not grasped expedient means, he must rely on a good friend to show the suffering before it can be extinguished.[^18]

Kuiji here answers, head-on, the central doubt that has run through Chinese Mahāyāna for two thousand years: if the karma of beings is made by themselves and received by themselves, how can Guanyin's responsive manifestation save them from peril? If Guanyin can save unconditionally, is the law of karma not denied? His answer is precise and divided into three strata.

**First, "that beings without any mind are still saved by the Buddha — this is something even the great sages cannot bring about."** If a being has no inner intention of taking refuge, even the Buddha cannot save him causelessly. This is the strict Yogācāra commitment to "karma is made by self" — without the proximate cause "mind," there is absolutely no fruit "salvation." Guanyin is not an external force stepping across karmic outcome but arises in dependent origination in accord with karmic outcome.

**Second, "that beings first arouse intention and then seek conditions — this is the shared pattern of all sages."** Only when a being spontaneously arouses the intention of taking refuge is there any "condition" for bodhisattva salvation. This is the precise definition of resonance: there must first be a "spontaneous arising of intention" as inner cause, and only then an "outer condition of sagely response." Invocation and mindful holding are themselves a karmic cause crafted by the being's own mind, and Guanyin's response is the dominant auxiliary condition invoked by that cause.

**Third, "first there must be karma made by self, then the conditions may be relied upon; if the self does not cultivate, what can the condition do?"** Karma as cause comes first; condition comes after. Karma is the proximate cause (*hetu-pratyaya*), condition is the dominant condition (*adhipati-pratyaya*). This is Kuiji's precise placement of Guanyin's salvation within the Yogācāra four-condition structure — all of Guanyin's supernormal powers, responsive manifestations, and salvific deliverances can only occupy the position of dominant condition; they can never replace the being's own mind in the position of proximate cause.

The structural significance of these three strata is profound. It means that "invocation-and-salvation" is not an exception to the karmic law but precisely the fully unfolded form of the karmic law within Mahāyāna resonance theory: the being's own mind gives rise to the thought of refuge (mental karma), utters the name (verbal karma), and offers veneration (bodily karma) — these three karmas are the **cause**; Guanyin perceives by the three powers and responds by the three wheels — these are the **condition**. When cause and condition meet, suffering and peril are resolved. The whole pedagogical structure of the Universal Gate is thus absorbed into Kuiji's rigorous Yogācāra model of "three-karma cause → three-power perception → three-wheel response," and the surface contradiction between salvific resonance and karmic self-responsibility is fully dissolved in the structural distinction of "proximate cause / dominant condition."

### 3.4 Summary of the Four Commentaries

Arranged in a single structural sequence, the four commentators show a clear line of doctrinal advance: Vasubandhu fixes the functional position (the protecting-from-perils power among the five gates), Zhiyi pairs substance and function (the dharma-response pair among the ten), Jizang grounds capacity (three names generating the three wholesome karmas), Kuiji completes the structure (three powers, three karmas, three wheels, and the four-condition positions). All four answer the same question — how Guanyin's "responsive salvation" can be structurally established within Mahāyāna right view — and the four answers are a single answer advanced by stages: from position to substance-function, from substance-function to capacity, from capacity to the final airtight placement within the four conditions.

The "patriarch-level direct answer" targeted in S7_PLAN middle-way correction #9 is precisely this four-part line as a whole — not any single quoted phrase, but the "Yogācāra / round-teaching structure of invocation-resonance" woven together from Vasubandhu's five-gate placement, Zhiyi's dharma-response pairing, Jizang's three-names-three-karmas, and Kuiji's three-powers-three-wheels and self-karma-plus-condition. This structure preserves both the rigor of the karmic law and the reality of the bodhisattva's compassion — the two not only do not conflict within the "proximate-cause / dominant-condition" four-condition structure but mutually complete each other.

The four correspondences:

| Commentator | Key Contribution | Core Structure |
|---|---|---|
| Vasubandhu | Positional judgment | Third of the five gates: protection from perils; faith-power and ultimate knowing = Dharma-body equality |
| Zhiyi | Substance-function pairing | Ten pairs, dharma-response pair; Guanshiyin = Dharma-body, Universal Gate = response-body; "if the observation is not round, the gate is not called universal" |
| Jizang | Capacity design | Three-name theory (Guanshiyin / Guanshiyi / Guanshishen) → design of three wholesome karmas |
| Kuiji | Causal-conditional structure | Three-karma cause → three-power perception → three-wheel response; self-karma as proximate cause, sagely response as dominant condition |

## IV. The Mirror Thesis: Three Faces of Guanyin, Three Scriptures of One Structure

### 4.1 First Mirror: Guanyin ↔ Guanzizai of the *Heart Sūtra* (Wisdom Face)

Point-to-the-Moon S2-P02, "The Four Links of Guanzizai," has already argued in detail that the *Heart Sūtra*'s opening — "When the bodhisattva Guanzizai coursed in deep prajñāpāramitā, he illuminated and saw that the five aggregates are all empty, and crossed beyond all suffering and distress" — is a closed circuit of awakening: **observe → illuminate → see → cross** — observing is the arising of the observing capacity, illuminating is the present activity of prajñā, seeing is the direct realization of emptiness, crossing is the liberation from suffering.[^19] The key structural feature of this four-link circuit is that it is **inward**: what is observed is the practitioner's own five aggregates; what is crossed is the practitioner's own birth-and-death; the whole circuit is completed within one bodhisattva's own present activity of concentration and wisdom.

The "Universal Gate" chapter gives the structurally isomorphic but outward four links: **suffering → invocation → perceiving the sound → liberation**. Suffering is what external beings undergo; invocation is the refuge the beings arouse; "perceiving the sound" is the bodhisattva's perception through the three powers; liberation is the present dissolution of suffering. The two circuits correspond link by link:

| Link | *Heart Sūtra* (inward illumination) | "Universal Gate" (outward response) |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Observe (arising of deep prajñā) | Suffering (beings pressed by suffering and the arising of thought) |
| 2 | Illuminate (five aggregates present) | Invocation (refuge in three karmas) |
| 3 | See (direct realization of emptiness) | Perceiving the sound (perception through three powers) |
| 4 | Cross (liberation of one's own suffering) | Liberation (liberation of beings' suffering) |

The table is not metaphor but structural identity — the difference between the two circuits lies only in the direction of "subject and object": the *Heart Sūtra*'s subject-object pair closes within a single bodhisattva; the "Universal Gate" chapter's subject-object pair opens between bodhisattva and beings. Taken together they are the complete functional aspect of prajñāpāramitā: inward closure is the **wisdom of self-salvation**, outward opening is the **compassion of saving others**.

Fazang's Tang-dynasty *Brief Commentary on the Prajñāpāramitā-hṛdaya Sūtra* (T1712) long ago glossed the three characters "Guanzizai" as "**the first reading takes it as wisdom; the second as compassion.**"[^20] That is: "Guanzizai" can be read as the gate of wisdom or as the gate of compassion — the two readings are not different bodhisattvas but different names for the same one. Thirteen hundred years ago Fazang had already lifted the unity of "Guanzizai" and "Guanshiyin" from the level of name to the level of substance-function. What P10 does is not new argument but the restoration of Fazang's line from footnote to main thesis: **Guanzizai is the inward fulfillment of prajñā as substance; Guanshiyin-Universal-Gate is the outward fulfillment of prajñā as function. The two are not two bodhisattvas but the same prajñā completed along both directions.**

### 4.2 Second Mirror: Guanyin ↔ the Ear-Faculty Perfect Penetration of the *Śūraṅgama* (Concentration Face)

Fascicle 6 of the *Śūraṅgama-sūtra*, "The Chapter of the Bodhisattva Guanshiyin's Ear-Faculty Perfect Penetration" — treated in detail in the S6 series — is another canonical site where "Guanyin" is directly unfolded as a structure of realization.[^21] The core formula of the ear-faculty perfect penetration is "**from hearing, contemplation, and cultivation, entering samādhi**," unfolded through stages: "At first, in the hearing, I entered the stream and forgot the object; the object of entry being already still, the marks of motion and stillness clearly did not arise. Gradually increasing, hearing and what is heard were exhausted; not dwelling in the exhaustion of hearing, awareness and what is aware were empty; with empty awareness perfectly round, emptiness and what is emptied were both extinguished; birth and extinction already extinguished, stillness-extinction appeared." Here "hearing" is not the physiological function of the ear-faculty, but the **capacity to hear**, the hearing-nature that stands independent of sound-objects.

The deep connection with the "Universal Gate" chapter lies in one key detail. The chapter's core formula is "**immediately perceives their voices; all are liberated**" — what is observed is "voices," and the subject-object structure of "observing voices" stands in the same relation to the hearing-nature's reverse-flow structure of "entering the stream and forgetting the object" in the ear-faculty perfect penetration: they are the same hearing-nature substance unfolded in two directions. The ear-faculty perfect penetration unfolds inward — from the mark of heard sound, returning to the hearing-nature, stages of shedding until stillness-extinction appears; the "Universal Gate" unfolds outward — from the hearing-nature arising to perceive the sounds of beings' suffering, stages of response until the three-wheel universal gate. Inner return and outer response are two directions of activity of the same hearing-nature.

The bodhisattva's self-report in the *Śūraṅgama* chapter is more explicit: "World-Honored One, because I made offerings to Tathāgata Guanyin and received from him the Vajra-samādhi of the illusory hearing-scent and hearing-cultivation, sharing the power of loving-kindness of the Tathāgatas, my body became thirty-two responsive forms that enter all lands."[^22] This passage directly binds the "thirty-two responsive bodies" to the "Vajra-samādhi of hearing-scent, hearing-cultivation" as substance-and-function: samādhi is substance, responsive body is function; the concentration-power attained in the ear-faculty perfect penetration is what responds, the thirty-three responsive manifestations of the "Universal Gate" chapter are what is manifested. In other words, the responsive-manifestation structure of the "Universal Gate" chapter is not a sourceless display of supernormal power; it is the outward expression of the concentration-power of the *Śūraṅgama* ear-faculty perfect penetration. The S6 argument of "principle is sudden, matter is gradual" finds its clearest illustration here: the roundness of the hearing-nature (principle) is sudden, the pervasiveness of responsive manifestation (matter) is gradual; suddenness at the present moment of stillness-extinction, gradualness in the step-by-step display of the thirty-three bodies.

### 4.3 Third Mirror: Three Scriptures of One Structure — Wisdom, Concentration, Compassion

Viewing the three mirrors together, one bodhisattva presents three structural faces across three scriptures:

- *Heart Sūtra* — Guanzizai: the **prajñā-wisdom face**, the inward-illumination circuit of observe → illuminate → see → cross, with direct realization of emptiness as substance.
- *Śūraṅgama* — ear-faculty perfect penetration: the **samādhi-concentration face**, the shedding sequence of hearing → contemplating → cultivating → entering samādhi, with present stillness-extinction as substance.
- *Lotus* — "Universal Gate": the **great-compassion responsive face**, the outward-responsive circuit of suffering → invocation → perceiving sound → liberation, with the three wheels of Universal Gate as function.

The three faces are not three bodhisattvas but **a single bodhisattva being taken up in three scriptures as three structural exemplars**: the *Heart Sūtra* takes the gate of wisdom, the *Śūraṅgama* takes the gate of concentration, the *Lotus* takes the gate of compassion. The threefold training of discipline, concentration, and wisdom is severally brought to fulfillment in a single bodhisattva across the three scriptures: wisdom culminates in the *Heart*, concentration culminates in the *Śūraṅgama*, compassion culminates in the *Lotus*. This is why Avalokiteśvara could become the "most representative bodhisattva" of East Asian Buddhist history — not because of the size of the devotional base, but because this one bodhisattva carries, at the level of canonical structure, the three ultimate exemplars of the three trainings.

At a deeper layer: the three faces can converge into one because of the very word "observation." In none of the three scriptures is "observation" the seeing of the physical eye-faculty; in all three it is the **capacity to observe**. The *Heart Sūtra*'s "observation" is the observing capacity of prajñā in present activity; the *Śūraṅgama*'s is the observing capacity of the hearing-nature's reverse flow; the *Lotus*'s is the observing capacity of the three cognitive supernormal powers. The three all point at the same "observing capacity of mind-nature itself" — the threefold unfolding of tathāgatagarbha / suchness in the persona of Guanyin.

This is also why Xuanzang's "Guanzizai" (*Avalokita-**īśvara***) and Kumārajīva's "Guanshiyin" (*Avalokita-**svara***) are not a translation dispute: *īśvara* names the observing capacity on its substance face, *svara* names it on its function face; substance and function are non-dual, and the two translations complement each other. As for Dharmarakṣa's "Guangshiyin," what it marks is the "light" (*āloka*) emitted by the observing capacity. Substance (Guanzizai), light (Guangshiyin), and sound (Guanshiyin) appear together, and only then does the complete shape of this bodhisattva in the Sanskrit transmission become visible.

## V. Conclusion: Universal Gate as the One Vehicle Landing on the Side of Beings

The core thesis developed across S7 since P01 — the trace-gate of the *Lotus*, its "opening, showing, awakening, entering" and its "combining the three vehicles and returning them to the one" — receives here at P10 its final structural completion. P02 already argued that the "one great cause" is the root purpose of the Buddha's appearance: to enable beings to open, be shown, awaken to, and enter the Buddha's knowledge-and-vision. These four terms form a downward descent circuit from Buddha to beings — beginning with the Buddha's one great cause and ending with beings' entry into Buddha's knowledge-and-vision. But the whole *Lotus* requires a complementary upward response circuit — how do beings in their own position take up this "opening-showing-awakening-entering"?

The "Universal Gate" chapter is precisely the canonical answer to this upward response. The four links **suffering → invocation → perceiving the sound → liberation** correspond strictly to the four links **opening — showing — awakening — entering**, but in the opposite direction:

| Descent (Buddha → beings) | Ascent (beings → Buddha) |
|---|---|
| Open Buddha's knowledge-and-vision | Suffering arouses thought (beings' self-awareness of suffering) |
| Show Buddha's knowledge-and-vision | Invocation-refuge (beings arouse vow) |
| Awaken Buddha's knowledge-and-vision | Perceive the sound (resonance made manifest) |
| Enter Buddha's knowledge-and-vision | Liberation (beings enter the sagely stream) |

The two circuits close end-to-end, forming a complete "Buddha-beings mutual resonance" loop. This is the final grounding of the S7 series' "combining three vehicles and returning them to the one" — the three vehicles are not abolished but gathered into the one vehicle within this Buddha-beings resonance loop; a single thought of invocation by a being suffering in peril is the first step of the one-vehicle path on the side of beings. Kuiji's "beings first arouse intention and then seek conditions — the shared pattern of all sages" here receives its deepest position within the *Lotus* genealogy — a single thought of invocation is a single arousing of the one-vehicle mind, the very first and subtlest step of "entering Buddha's knowledge-and-vision."

In treating the seven parables, the ten suchnesses, the lifespan, the original ground, Sadāparibhūta, the dhāraṇīs, and the rest across P01–P09, the focus was mostly on the Buddha and the Dharma. Closing S7 with the "Universal Gate" chapter shifts the center of gravity of the whole argument from "what the Buddha teaches" to "how beings take it up" — the *Lotus* is not only the Buddha's one great cause but also the structure by which beings respond to it. The reason Guanyin's Universal Gate could be extracted from one chapter of the *Lotus* to become the devotional core of all East Asian Buddhism lies precisely here: **it is the entrance-gate of the one vehicle on the side of beings**. Every suffering being who invokes the name is doing the most fundamental thing the *Lotus* has to offer — hooking up, through a single thought of refuge, to the Buddha's one great cause. In this sense the "Universal Gate" chapter is not an appendix to the *Lotus* but the most landed, the most popular, the most irreplaceable chapter of the whole sūtra's logic.

The S7 *Lotus* series thus reaches a pause at P10 (the trace-gate's outward response is complete). The next paper, P11, will return to the origin-gate, unfolding the distant original attainment and the Physician's Sons parable of the "Lifespan of the Tathāgata" — from Guanyin's "outward-responsive fulfillment" on the side of beings to the Tathāgata's "original-ground appearing" on the side of the Dharma-body. Trace-gate and origin-gate then mutually support: every responsive body of the trace-gate is the temporal externalization of the origin-gate's lifespan; the origin-gate's distant original attainment is the ontological ground of every trace-gate manifestation. The two gates together are the full unfolding of the *Lotus* as a complete one-vehicle teaching.

But that is for the next paper. Here and now, the outward-responsive logic of the *Lotus*'s trace-gate has found its completion in the Universal Gate manifestation of the bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara.
