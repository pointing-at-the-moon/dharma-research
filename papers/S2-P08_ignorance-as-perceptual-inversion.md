## Abstract

The Heart Sūtra passage "no ignorance, and no ending of ignorance, up to no aging-and-death, and no ending of aging-and-death" negates the twelve links of dependent origination in both directions — origination and cessation — in fourteen characters, constituting prajñā's deepest seeing into the dharma of conditioned arising. This paper argues that the core of avidyā (ignorance) is not "stupidity" or "not knowing" but a perceptual inversion: mistaking what flows for what is fixed, mistaking what consciousness projects for what truly exists, mistaking what is constructed for what has always been. Using sunrise and sunset as analogy: avidyā is mistaking the earth's rotation for the sun moving across the sky. The scriptural foundation draws on the Buddha's teaching of dependent origination in the *Saṃyuktāgama* ("when this exists, that exists; when this arises, that arises"), Nāgārjuna's prajñā contemplation in the *Mahāprajñāpāramitopadeśa* ("the twelve links of dependent origination are empty, like space"), the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra*'s account of how avidyā originates ("the nature of awareness must be luminous; falsely attempting to illuminate awareness itself [creates ignorance]"), and the *Vijñaptimātratāsiddhi*'s Yogācāra definition ("darkness and delusion as its nature; serving as the basis for all defilements"). The core argument unfolds through Kuiji's trisvabhāva analysis in his *Bore boluomiduo xinjing youzhan*: the imagined "agent" and "permanence" of the twelve links are entirely negated, the dependent nature is not a fixed characteristic of arising, and the perfected nature is not the extinction of ignorance. The paper draws particularly on the *Yogācārabhūmi* fascicle 10's precise analysis of the feeling → craving → grasping mechanism, arguing that the intervention point for practice lies not at the root (avidyā is too deep) nor at the terminus (aging-and-death is already formed) but in the infinitesimal gap between craving and grasping — the gap where prajñā can be present. The argument further develops the Abhidharma tradition's three scales of dependent origination observation (one-lifetime, concatenated, and moment-to-moment) to show that the craving–grasping gap is not a single occurrence but opens at every moment for the practitioner whose observational resolution has reached the level of momentary arising and ceasing — the difference in speed of practice is fundamentally a difference in the temporal resolution at which one observes the twelve links. Cross-disciplinary dialogue employs confirmation bias and the Copernican Revolution as heuristic analogies, stated with restraint. Four Middle Way corrections address: avidyā does not equal stupidity, the heliocentric analogy has boundaries, the craving–grasping gap does not equal free will, and confirmation bias does not equal avidyā.

## I. Introduction

### 1.1 Textual Position

S2-P07 deconstructed the eighteen dhātus — what you take to be "seeing the world" is actually living inside a world constructed by consciousness. That paper left a question at its close: the eighteen dhātus deconstructed the structure of cognition, but why does that structure trap you?

The Heart Sūtra's answer is: avidyā — ignorance.

> 無無明，亦無無明盡，乃至無老死，亦無老死盡。
> No ignorance, and no ending of ignorance, up to no aging-and-death, and no ending of aging-and-death.[^1]

These fourteen characters condense the most fundamental causal model in Buddhist teaching — the twelve links of dependent origination (pratītyasamutpāda). The twelve links are the complete map of suffering:

> Avidyā (ignorance) → saṃskāra (formations) → vijñāna (consciousness) → nāmarūpa (name-and-form) → ṣaḍāyatana (six sense-gates) → sparśa (contact) → vedanā (feeling) → tṛṣṇā (craving) → upādāna (grasping) → bhava (becoming) → jāti (birth) → jarāmaraṇa (aging-and-death)

The Heart Sūtra does two things with this map. First, it says "no ignorance… up to no aging-and-death" — in ultimate truth, every link in the chain of origination is without self-nature. Second, it says "and no ending of ignorance… and no ending of aging-and-death" — in ultimate truth, every link in the chain of cessation is equally without self-nature. Origination is empty; cessation is also empty. The chain of suffering is empty; the path to ending suffering is also empty.

The two characters "up to" (乃至, *naiva*) are extraordinarily concise. The twelve links have twelve members, yet the sūtra names only the first and the last — ignorance and aging-and-death — eliding the ten between with "up to." This is not abbreviation but pedagogical design: if the beginning and end are both empty, how could the middle alone be real?

### 1.2 Research Questions

The twelve links have three dimensions in Buddhist doctrine: as the causal chain of suffering in the Āgama system (the gate of origination), as the object of prajñā's seeing of emptiness in the Prajñāpāramitā system (the gate of emptiness), and as the seed-mechanism in the Yogācāra system (the gate of seeds). These are not three different sets of twelve links but the same causal chain observed at three different depths of wisdom.

This paper addresses three core questions.

First, what is avidyā, exactly? "Not knowing" is the common understanding, but it is too crude. A PhD in physics also has avidyā; an illiterate elderly woman may illuminate emptiness. Avidyā is clearly not "lack of knowledge" — so what is it?

Second, what is the doctrinal difference between "no ignorance" (無無明) and "no ending of ignorance" (亦無無明盡)? The former negates ignorance as an entity; the latter negates "the elimination of ignorance" as an entity. Between these two negations lies a precise doctrinal staircase that requires Kuiji's trisvabhāva analysis to articulate.

Third, if the twelve links are empty, does practice still matter? If even "eliminating ignorance" cannot be grasped, what is there to eliminate? This seemingly paradoxical question is, in fact, prajñā's deepest teaching — it is precisely because one does not grasp at "an ignorance to be eliminated" that elimination becomes truly complete.

### 1.3 Paper Structure

The paper unfolds in the sūtra → treatise → commentary layering. §II Scriptural Foundations builds from the Buddha's own words (*Saṃyuktāgama*), through Nāgārjuna's prajñā contemplation (*Mahāprajñāpāramitopadeśa*), the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra*'s account of how avidyā originates, and the *Vijñaptimātratāsiddhi*'s Yogācāra definition, to Kuiji's trisvabhāva analysis and Woncheuk's double-dispelling of origination and cessation. §III Core Argument develops six layers: redefining avidyā, dynamic analysis of the twelve links, the full deployment of Kuiji's trisvabhāva, the craving–grasping gap as the intervention point, three scales of observational resolution, and the loop-back to the master program. §IV Cross-Disciplinary Dialogue. §V Middle Way Corrections. §VI Conclusion.

## II. Scriptural Foundations

### 2.1 The Buddha's First Turning: Establishing the Twelve Links

The teaching of the twelve links finds precise expression in Sūtra 296 of the *Saṃyuktāgama*:

> 云何為因緣法？謂此有故彼有，謂緣無明行，緣行識，乃至如是如是純大苦聚集。
> "What is the dharma of conditions? When this exists, that exists. Conditioned by ignorance, formations arise; conditioned by formations, consciousness arises — up to the entire mass of suffering thus gathered."[^2]

"When this exists, that exists; when this arises, that arises" (此有故彼有，此生故彼生) — these eight characters are the fundamental formula of Buddhist dependent origination. They establish a strict causal structure: not "A causes B" (causal entity theory) but "when A is present, B is correspondingly present" (causal relation theory). The difference: the former presupposes A and B as independent entities; the latter describes only the mutual dependence of conditions.

In the same sūtra, the Buddha further clarifies that this law of causation does not change regardless of whether a Buddha appears in the world:

> 若佛出世，若未出世，此法常住，法住法界。
> "Whether a Buddha appears in the world or does not appear, this dharma abides constantly, established in the dharmadhātu."[^3]

The four characters "established in the dharmadhātu" (法住法界, *dharma-sthitā dharmadhātu*) are critical. The twelve links were not invented by the Buddha; they were illuminated by the Buddha. Dependent origination is the reality of the dharmadhātu — "when this exists, that exists" does not require anyone to announce it; it is simply so. What the Buddha did was only "self-awakened and perfectly enlightened, to teach, reveal, and make manifest for others" — he awakened himself, then told you what he saw.

### 2.2 Nāgārjuna's Prajñā Contemplation: The Twelve Links as Empty as Space

The *Saṃyuktāgama* establishes the fact of the twelve links; Nāgārjuna illuminates their emptiness. *Mahāprajñāpāramitopadeśa* fascicle 80 contains a core passage of prajñā contemplation:

> 說十二因緣空。何以故？若法從因緣和合生，是法無有定性；若法無定性，即是畢竟空寂滅相。離二邊故，假名為中道。是故說：「十二因緣如虛空，無法故不盡。」
> "The twelve links of dependent origination are declared empty. Why? If a dharma arises from the combination of conditions, that dharma has no fixed nature; if it has no fixed nature, it is ultimately empty — the characteristic of quiescent cessation. Because it departs from both extremes, it is provisionally called the Middle Way. Therefore it is said: 'The twelve links are like space — because there is no [fixed] dharma, they are inexhaustible.'"[^4]

The logic is tightly linked: the twelve links arise from conditions, therefore they have no fixed self-nature; having no fixed self-nature, they are ultimately empty; being ultimately empty yet not annihilated ("departing from both extremes"), they are provisionally named the Middle Way. The final phrase "the twelve links are like space" is the conclusion — they are like space itself, not something that can be picked up or put down.

Nāgārjuna extends this contemplation to avidyā itself:

> 癡亦從因緣和合生，故無自相；無自相故畢竟空，如虛空。
> "Delusion (moha) also arises from the combination of conditions, therefore it has no intrinsic characteristic; having no intrinsic characteristic, it is ultimately empty, like space."[^5]

Avidyā too is born of conditions. It is not an entity residing in your mind, not a block of darkness that can be excavated — it too is empty. If avidyā had substantial existence, it could never be removed (substance is by definition permanent and unchanging); precisely because avidyā is empty, awakening is possible.

Fascicle 5 of the same treatise states from the standpoint of practice:

> 癡如虛空不可盡，行如虛空不可盡，乃至眾苦和合集如虛空不可盡。菩薩當作是知！作是知者，為捨癡際，應無所入。作是觀十二因緣起者，則為坐道場，得薩婆若。
> "Delusion is like space — inexhaustible. Formations are like space — inexhaustible. Up to the aggregate mass of suffering is like space — inexhaustible. The bodhisattva should know this! Knowing this, in order to abandon the domain of delusion, one should enter into nothing. One who contemplates dependent origination in this way sits upon the seat of awakening and attains sarvajñā (omniscience)."[^6]

"One who contemplates dependent origination in this way sits upon the seat of awakening and attains sarvajñā" — this is Nāgārjuna's most condensed statement on the relationship between prajñā and the twelve links. Contemplating the emptiness of dependent origination is itself the path to Buddhahood. Not finding another path outside the twelve links, but in the very act of contemplating them, awakening is accomplished.

### 2.3 The Śūraṅgama's Deep Seeing: How Avidyā Originates

If avidyā arises from conditions, one may press further: how did avidyā first occur? The *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* fascicle 2 offers a profoundly deep answer:

> 性覺必明。妄為明覺。覺非所明。因明立所。所既妄立。生汝妄能。無同異中。熾然成異。
> "The nature of awareness is inherently luminous. Falsely attempting to illuminate that awareness [creates ignorance]. Awareness is not an object of illumination. By seeking to illuminate, an 'object' is posited. Once the object is falsely established, a false 'subject' arises in you. Within what is originally neither same nor different, duality blazes forth."[^7]

The doctrinal architecture of this passage: intrinsic nature (性覺, *svabhāva-bodhi*) is originally luminous and does not need to be illuminated — it is itself light. But sentient beings falsely attempt to "illuminate" (to cognize, to objectify) this inherent awareness. The moment the thought "I will recognize my own awareness" arises, awareness flips from subject to object — this is the origination of avidyā.

The four characters "awareness is not an object of illumination" (覺非所明) are the crux: awareness is not something to be observed. When you try to "see your own awareness," you have already pushed it to the other side — it has shifted from the "perceiver" to the "perceived." This subject-object split is the root of avidyā. "By seeking to illuminate, an object is posited" (因明立所): wanting to "illuminate" creates an "illuminated object"; once there is an "object," a false "subject" arises in response. Within what originally admits no differentiation (無同異中), the duality of subject and object "blazes forth" (熾然成異) — and avidyā is born.

This is the deep meaning of "establishing knowing upon knowing is the root of ignorance" (知見立知，即無明本). Knowing is a function of awareness, but erecting a second-order "knowing" upon knowing — knowing that you know — is the point where avidyā originates. In contemporary terms: awareness is not the problem; but when "being aware of being aware" crystallizes into attachment to an awareness-possessing self, avidyā is produced.

### 2.4 Yogācāra Precision: Defining Avidyā and Its Seed Mechanism

The *Vijñaptimātratāsiddhi* fascicle 6 provides a precise Yogācāra definition of avidyā:

> 云何為癡？於諸理、事，迷闇為性；能障無癡，一切雜染所依為業。
> "What is delusion (moha)? Its nature is darkness and confusion regarding principles and phenomena; its function is to obstruct non-delusion (amoha), and to serve as the basis for all defilements."[^8]

Three layers of meaning. First, "darkness and confusion as its nature" (迷闇為性): the essence of avidyā is obscurity, inversion — a fundamental disorientation with respect to truth (principles) and appearances (phenomena). Second, "obstructing non-delusion" (能障無癡): the function of avidyā is to block wisdom. Third, "serving as the basis for all defilements" (一切雜染所依為業): the operational effect of avidyā is to serve as the foundation upon which all afflictions rest.

The third point is especially important. Avidyā is not merely one of the six root afflictions (greed, hatred, delusion, pride, doubt, wrong views) standing alongside the others — it is the ground of the remaining five. The treatise immediately continues:

> 謂由無明，起疑、邪見、貪等煩惱、隨煩惱業，能招後生雜染法故。
> "Due to ignorance, doubt, wrong views, greed, and other afflictions and subsidiary afflictions arise, capable of attracting future defiled dharmas."[^9]

Doubt arises from avidyā, wrong views arise from avidyā, greed and hatred arise from avidyā. Avidyā is the universal root of affliction, not one affliction among many.

Regarding the seed mechanism: avidyā, as a seed of the affliction-obstruction, rests within the ālaya-vijñāna — stored in the eighth consciousness's seed repository, manifesting when conditions ripen, and upon manifesting, perfuming new seeds in return, forming the cycle of habitual tendencies (vāsanā). Yet the seeds of avidyā cannot defile tathatā itself — tathatā is the perfected nature (pariniṣpanna-svabhāva), the "true nature" of the ālaya-vijñāna, unobscured by adventitious defilements. As the guide-commentary puts it: "clouds do not cover space" — clouds may block sunlight, but space does not diminish by one fraction on their account.

### 2.5 Kuiji's Trisvabhāva Analysis: "No Ignorance and No Ending of Ignorance"

In his *Bore boluomiduo xinjing youzhan* fascicle 2, Kuiji develops a systematic trisvabhāva analysis of "no ignorance, and no ending of ignorance":

> 勝空者言，上無遠觀，下無近觀，此無獨覺隣近所觀，故契經言，為求獨覺者說應十二緣起法。又說，無明乃至老死唯有假名，自性空故今說為無。盡者，空也，空亦空故，說無無明盡乃至無老死盡。
> "The Superior Emptiness position states: above, the distant contemplation [of the bodhisattva] is negated; below, the near contemplation [of the śrāvaka] is negated; here, the immediate contemplation of the pratyekabuddha is negated. Thus the sūtra says the twelve links of dependent origination are taught for those seeking pratyekabuddha-awakening. It further states: ignorance through aging-and-death are merely nominal designations; being empty of self-nature, they are now declared 'no.' 'Ending' means emptiness; emptiness too is empty, hence 'no ending of ignorance' through 'no ending of aging-and-death.'"[^10]

This passage first locates the twelve links within the Heart Sūtra's structure — they correspond to the dharma contemplated by the pratyekabuddha. Śrāvakas contemplate the Four Noble Truths (to be treated in S2-P09), pratyekabuddhas contemplate the twelve links, bodhisattvas contemplate the emptiness of all dharmas. Prajñā's seeing transcends all three, so the objects contemplated by all three are "no" within prajñā.

Kuiji then develops the pivotal passage of his trisvabhāva analysis:

> 其所無者，謂無所執作者、常住二種緣生，非無功能緣起滅理，以契經言不壞世諦入於勝義，無造、受者業不亡故。依他既非定緣起相，真理亦非無明等滅，故並無之。
> "What is negated is the imagined 'agent' and 'permanence' — two kinds of [falsely conceived] dependent arising. What is not negated is the functional principle of conditioned arising and cessation, for the sūtras say 'without destroying conventional truth, one enters ultimate truth; there is no creator or receiver, yet karma does not perish.' The dependent nature is not a fixed characteristic of arising; true principle is not the extinction of ignorance and the rest. Therefore both are [declared] 'no.'"[^11]

This passage is the hinge of the entire trisvabhāva analysis and must be parsed sentence by sentence:

**The imagined (parikalpita) layer**: "No imagined 'agent' or 'permanence' in the two kinds of dependent arising" — what is negated are two delusions: first, that there is an "agent" (a creator, a controller) driving the flow of the twelve links; second, that each of the twelve links is "permanent" (possessing an eternal, unchanging entity). These two attachments are entirely negated, without remainder.

**The dependent (paratantra) layer**: "Not negating the functional principle of conditioned arising and cessation" — the function of dependent origination is not negated. The twelve links as a causal principle continue to operate; they are simply "not a fixed characteristic of arising" — not a fixed, deterministic reality.

**The perfected (pariniṣpanna) layer**: "True principle is not the extinction of ignorance and the rest" — even "the extinction of ignorance" cannot be grasped as the content of the perfected nature. Truth is not "what you get after eliminating ignorance" — truth has always been present. The "cessation" of avidyā is merely the dissipation of imagined constructs, not the "production" of the perfected nature.

Kuiji further clarifies the precise operational scope of "no" through his fourfold analysis of defilement and purity:

> 於雜染品唯說無無明乃至無老死，於清淨品唯說無無明盡乃至無老死盡，各但無順而例無逆，舉無初、後例中亦無。
> "For the defiled category, only 'no ignorance up to no aging-and-death' is stated; for the purified category, only 'no ending of ignorance up to no ending of aging-and-death' is stated. Each negates in the forward direction and implies [negation of] the reverse; stating 'no' for the first and last implies 'no' for the middle."[^12]

"No ignorance" negates the defiled category (the gate of origination); "no ending of ignorance" negates the purified category (the gate of cessation). The negation of origination breaks the ordinary person's attachment to suffering; the negation of cessation breaks the practitioner's attachment to "ending suffering." Two layers of negation, each deeper than the last.

### 2.6 Woncheuk: The Double Dispelling of Origination and Cessation

Woncheuk supplements Kuiji's analysis from another angle in his *Bore boluomiduo xinjing zan*:

> 此即第四遣緣生門。然此緣生自有二種，一者流轉，二者還滅。由無明故能起諸行……於空性中無此流轉……無無明盡乃至亦無老死盡。
> "This is the fourth gate of dispelling dependent arising. Dependent arising has two kinds: origination (pravṛtti) and cessation (nivṛtti). Due to ignorance, formations arise… In emptiness there is no such origination… No ending of ignorance, up to no ending of aging-and-death."[^13]

Woncheuk identifies the twelve links passage as the "fourth gate of dispelling dependent arising" — the Heart Sūtra's fourth act of dispelling. He explicitly names the two kinds of dependent arising — origination and cessation — and declares that prajñā dispels both. "In emptiness there is no such origination" — within emptiness, there is no entity called "origination." This complements Kuiji's fourfold defilement-and-purity analysis, converging from different argumentative angles on the same conclusion: prajñā's "no" is the truthful seeing of the twelve links in their totality — including both "suffering" and "the end of suffering."

## III. Core Argument

### 3.1 Redefining Avidyā: Perceptual Inversion

Synthesizing the sūtras and treatises above, a more precise definition of avidyā can be distilled: avidyā is not the absence of knowledge but an inversion of perception.

The *Vijñaptimātratāsiddhi* says "darkness and confusion as its nature" — but this darkness is not "seeing nothing"; it is "seeing everything upside down." The *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* says "falsely attempting to illuminate awareness" — not an absence of awareness but awareness oriented in the wrong direction, flipping the intrinsic nature from subject to object. Nāgārjuna says "delusion too arises from the combination of conditions, therefore it has no intrinsic characteristic" — even this inversion itself has no substantial existence.

Integrating these three layers: avidyā is a structural misplacement of perception — mistaking what flows for what is fixed, mistaking what is dependently arisen for what truly exists, mistaking what is constructed for what has always been so.

An everyday analogy can make this intuitive.

Every day the sun rises in the east and sets in the west. This is what your eyes see, what your body feels (cold in the morning, hot at midday), what thousands of years of human civilization have accepted as "fact." But when wisdom arises, you know: the sun has not moved at all — it is the earth that is rotating.

Your eyes are not broken, your feelings are not wrong, but your interpretation of these perceptions is inverted. "Sunrise" is not the sun rising; it is the ground you stand on turning.

Avidyā is precisely this kind of inversion. In Yogācāra terms, the ālaya-vijñāna is like the unmoving sun — it holds all seeds, all habitual tendencies, but it itself is not in flux. What flows are the waves that seeds produce when conditions ripen — the torrent of vāsanā, the ceaseless change of surface phenomena. The avidyā of sentient beings is not ignorance of the ālaya-vijñāna's existence, but mistaking the waves on the surface of consciousness for external entities — mistaking the earth's rotation for the sun moving across the sky.

The power of this analogy: once you know it is the earth rotating and not the sun moving, your daily experience does not change — the sun still appears to rise from the east. But your understanding has been completely inverted. Awakening does not make you see different things; it gives you a different understanding of what you already see.

### 3.2 Dynamic Analysis of the Twelve Links

Under the definition of "perceptual inversion," the twelve links cease to be an abstract philosophical chain and become a directly observable map of psychological operation.

**Avidyā → saṃskāra**: inverted perception drives action (saṃskāra). Because what flows is mistaken for what is fixed, there is the impulse to grasp; because what is constructed is mistaken for what has always been, there is the refusal to let go. Every contaminated act of body, speech, and mind presupposes this perceptual inversion.

**Saṃskāra → vijñāna → nāmarūpa → ṣaḍāyatana → sparśa → vedanā**: the force of action (karma) shapes the next moment of consciousness (vijñāna); consciousness shapes the combination of mentality and materiality (nāmarūpa); nāmarūpa unfolds into six sense-gates (ṣaḍāyatana); through the gates, contact (sparśa) occurs; contact produces feeling (vedanā). This stretch is the "first half" of the causal chain — from planting causes to experiencing their fruit. The "consciousness" here, in the Yogācāra framework, can be precisely located as the manifestation of seeds within the ālaya-vijñāna — not a "new soul" transmigrating, but karmic seeds deploying their functions under new conditions.

**Vedanā → tṛṣṇā → upādāna → bhava → jāti → jarāmaraṇa**: this is the "second half" — from experiencing fruit to planting new causes. This is the critical battlefield for practice.

### 3.3 "No Ignorance" and "No Ending of Ignorance": The Full Deployment of the Trisvabhāva

Applying Kuiji's trisvabhāva to the twelve links yields a three-layered seeing:

**First layer: no self-nature of the imagined.** "No ignorance" — there is no "agent" within avidyā controlling the cycle, and no "permanent entity" called "avidyā." You cannot treat avidyā as a thing to combat — it is not a block of darkness, not an enemy, not a monster dwelling in your mind. If it had substantial existence, it would be eternal; you could never remove it. Precisely because it has no substantial existence, awakening is possible.

**Second layer: no negation of the dependent arising.** The twelve links as a causal principle genuinely operate. The seeds of avidyā genuinely reside in the ālaya-vijñāna; contact genuinely produces feeling; feeling genuinely produces craving. Prajñā does not deny the existence of this process — it denies the assumption that this process is fixed, deterministic, and unalterable. Kuiji says "not negating the functional principle of conditioned arising and cessation" — the function of dependent origination is not negated; "without destroying conventional truth, one enters ultimate truth."

**Third layer: thereby revealing the tathatā of the perfected.** "No ending of ignorance" — when even the attachment to "I am eliminating ignorance" is released, what you see is tathatā. Tathatā is not "the product of eliminating ignorance" — it has always been present, never obscured by avidyā (clouds do not cover space). So-called "awakening" is not obtaining a new thing but seeing what has always been there. Kuiji says "true principle is not the extinction of ignorance and the rest" — truth is not "the residue after ignorance has been extinguished."

Between these three layers lies a progressive doctrinal logic: the first layer breaks the ordinary person's entity-grasping (believing avidyā is real); the second breaks the practitioner's annihilation-grasping (believing something real has been truly severed); the third breaks the sage's dharma-grasping (believing "enlightenment" is a state that can be possessed). When all three are broken, subject and object both dissolve, and prajñā manifests.

Kuiji's *tri-nihsvabhāva* analysis provides a parallel analytical instrument:

> 此中「空」言，即三無性。謂計所執本體非有，相無自性，所以稱空；諸依他起，色如聚沫，受喻浮泡，想同陽焰，行類芭蕉，識猶幻事，無如所執自然生性，故亦名空；圓成實性因觀所執空無方證，或無如彼所執真性，故真勝義亦名為空。
> "The word 'empty' here means the three non-natures. The imagined has no intrinsic body — its characteristic lacks self-nature, hence it is called empty. The dependent: form is like foam, feeling like a bubble, perception like a mirage, formations like a plantain trunk, consciousness like an illusion — lacking the naturally-arisen nature that is imagined, hence also called empty. The perfected nature is realized only through seeing the imagined as empty, or is without the true nature that was imagined, hence ultimate truth is also called empty."[^14]

Applied to the twelve links: the imagined twelve links "have no intrinsic body" (characteristic non-nature); the dependent twelve links are "like foam and bubbles" (arising non-nature); the perfected "is realized only through seeing the imagined as empty" (ultimate non-nature). Three levels of "emptiness" pointing to three different empty dharmas — not the same emptiness.

### 3.4 The Craving–Grasping Gap: The Intervention Point for Practice

At this stage in the doctrinal analysis, a practical question emerges: since avidyā is the root, should one attack it directly?

In theory, yes. But in actual practice, avidyā is too deep — it operates at the seed level of the ālaya-vijñāna, beyond the reach of the sixth consciousness's discriminations. You cannot "think your way out" of avidyā, because "thinking" itself operates within avidyā's domain.

The Buddha's pedagogical wisdom lies in this: he does not demand that you sever the root directly, but provides an intervention point in the middle of the causal chain.

*Yogācārabhūmi* fascicle 10 offers an extraordinarily precise analysis of the feeling → craving → grasping mechanism:

> 以受勢力故，於相似境，或求和合，或求乖離。由愚癡力，但於諸受起盡等相不如實知，由此不能制御其心。
> "By the force of feeling, in relation to similar objects, one either seeks union or seeks separation. Due to the force of delusion, one does not truly know the characteristics of feelings' arising and cessation, and therefore cannot govern one's mind."[^15]

Feeling (the three feelings — painful, pleasant, and neutral) carries "force" — it drives sentient beings to "seek union" (pursue pleasant feeling) or "seek separation" (flee painful feeling) in relation to similar objects. The key phrase: "due to the force of delusion, one does not truly know the characteristics of feelings' arising and cessation" — because of avidyā, one cannot observe the arising and passing of feelings as they actually are, and so the mind loses control.

How does craving convert to grasping? The same treatise continues:

> 由希望生故，於追求時，能發隨眠，及能引彼隨順法故。
> "Because [craving] arises from hope, during the pursuit, it activates the latent afflictions (anuśaya) and draws forth their concordant dharmas."[^16]

Craving arises from "hope" and, in the process of "pursuit," activates the dormant affliction-seeds (anuśaya) stored in the ālaya-vijñāna, amplifying the force of craving into the powerful act of grasping (upādāna).

The intervention point for practice lies here — after feeling arises, craving (attraction or aversion) follows almost automatically. But between the arising of craving and the solidification of grasping (seizing, clinging, pushing away), there is an infinitesimal gap. *Yogācārabhūmi* fascicle 93 compresses the process into one sentence: "First, feeling born of contact conditioned by ignorance serves as the condition for craving; due to craving, grasping subsequently arises."[^17] From feeling to craving to grasping is a sequence, not an instant. Since it is a sequence, there is an interval; where there is an interval, intervention is possible.

That gap is the place where prajñā can truly be present.

### 3.5 Observational Resolution: From One Lifetime to Each Moment

The craving–grasping gap is not a one-time occurrence — its frequency depends on the resolution at which you observe the twelve links.

The Abhidharma tradition recognizes three modes of observing the twelve links, corresponding to three temporal scales[^18]:

**One-lifetime dependent origination** (segmented birth-and-death). For ordinary persons, the twelve links span a single lifetime — avidyā and saṃskāra are causes planted in past lives, vijñāna through vedanā are fruits borne in this life, tṛṣṇā through bhava are causes planted in this life, and jāti through jarāmaraṇa are fruits in future lives. This is the classical framework of "two rounds of cause-and-effect across three lives" in the *Abhidharmakośa*, and the temporal scale most people envision when they hear "the twelve links." At this scale, the craving–grasping gap may be glimpsed only a few times in a lifetime — or never at all.

**Concatenated dependent origination** (mid-range observation). Practitioners compress the temporal scale of observation: within a single day, a week, or even a single interpersonal interaction, they can see a complete avidyā → jarāmaraṇa cycle. In a particular situation, avidyā fires (mistaking the dependently arisen for the real), and the ensuing saṃskāra → vijñāna → sparśa → vedanā → tṛṣṇā → upādāna forms a new "becoming" (bhava); that becoming arises (jāti) and then dissipates (jarāmaraṇa). Dozens of such cycles may occur in a single day. At this scale, the craving–grasping gap appears densely — every emotional reaction, every interpersonal friction, is a complete twelve-link cycle in operation, each with its own gap available for intervention.

**Momentary dependent origination** (moment-to-moment arising and ceasing). For practitioners who have deepened their observation, every single thought-moment is a complete twelve-link cycle. In the instant a thought arises, all twelve links complete in that single moment — the subtle operation of avidyā drives saṃskāra, which triggers the momentary arising of vijñāna, on through tṛṣṇā, upādāna, bhava arising and ceasing within a single thought. The instant the thought dissolves is "aging-and-death." The next thought arises — a new cycle of twelve links. At this scale, the craving–grasping gap is present at every moment — every instant is a birth-and-death, every instant holds the possibility of illumination.

The difference between the three scales lies not in the twelve links themselves — the causal principle is identical. The difference lies in the observer's resolution. In the same span of time, one person sees only one-lifetime birth-and-death (one cycle across decades), another sees concatenated birth-and-death (dozens of cycles per day), and a third sees momentary birth-and-death (cycles at every thought-moment). The practitioner who sees momentary birth-and-death has the craving–grasping gap opening at every instant, and therefore practices swiftly — not because they are more intelligent, but because their observational resolution is higher.

This is why the speed of practice varies so dramatically. Returning to the heliocentric analogy: an ordinary person realizes once in a lifetime that "the sun wasn't actually moving"; the practitioner at the concatenated scale reconfirms this inversion daily; the practitioner at the momentary scale illuminates it at every thought — no confirmation needed, because illumination has become their baseline state.

### 3.6 Loop-Back to the Master Program: "Going Deep, Illuminating" in the Craving–Grasping Gap

At its deepest level, the method of intervention returns to the Heart Sūtra's opening line.

S2-P02 established the starting point of the awakening loop — Avalokiteśvara: the moment you long for freedom. S2-P03 established the method — "practicing deep prajñāpāramitā": turning inward, going deep into the formations. Now, in the context of the twelve links, this awakening loop has a concrete operational field: the gap between craving and grasping.

In that gap — no analysis is needed, no labeling, no telling yourself "this is feeling, this is craving." Prajñā's seeing is awake in that moment, truthfully seeing the flow of what consciousness projects — to see is already to illuminate. Not grasping, not pushing away, letting it arise as it is, dissolve as it is.

Every instance of "not grasping" is a small awakening. The defiled seeds in the ālaya-vijñāna, at every moment of "not grasping," weaken by one fraction — this is the operation of "transforming consciousness into wisdom" (*vijñāna-parāvṛtti*) described in the *Mahāyānasaṃgrahabhāṣya*, at its most microscopic level. Not a single flash of complete enlightenment, but a moment-by-moment micro-redirection, accumulating into qualitative transformation.

"No ignorance, and no ending of ignorance" — you need not grasp at "I am eliminating ignorance." Every "going deep, illuminating" in the craving–grasping gap is already approaching the awakened awareness that has always been there. You are not "obtaining" enlightenment but "ceasing to obscure" — as clouds scatter, the sky was always clear.

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Dialogue

### 4.1 ⚠️ Heuristic Analogy: Confirmation Bias

The concept of "confirmation bias" in modern cognitive psychology provides a limited but useful analogy. Confirmation bias refers to the tendency to seek, interpret, and remember information that supports one's existing beliefs, while ignoring or discounting contradictory information.

This mechanism shares a structural similarity with avidyā: neither is a matter of "lacking information." A person operating under confirmation bias typically has access to abundant information; a sentient being under the sway of avidyā is not devoid of knowledge. The problem lies in how information is processed being distorted. Confirmation bias is "selective seeing"; avidyā is "inverted seeing."

The heuristic significance: just as research in cognitive psychology demonstrates that confirmation bias cannot be eliminated by "acquiring more information" (more information is simply filtered selectively), avidyā cannot be eliminated by "studying more Dharma" (more knowledge, if filtered through avidyā, may actually reinforce the self-grasping of "I understand now"). Eliminating bias requires observing the operating pattern of bias itself — this is structurally parallel to the methodology of prajñā contemplation.

### 4.2 ⚠️ Heuristic Analogy: The Copernican Revolution

The heliocentric analogy is not merely a rhetorical device; it points to a deeper epistemological structure.

When Copernicus proposed heliocentrism, human perceptual experience did not change at all — the sun still appeared to move. What changed was the interpretive framework for that experience. This parallels the structure of awakening: after awakening, the six sense-bases still contact six sense-objects, the first five consciousnesses still discern, the sixth consciousness still discriminates — but the entire framework has been inverted. You know that the "sun" (external objects) is not moving; what moves is the "earth" (your consciousness).

At a deeper level: the Copernican Revolution encountered enormous resistance not because the evidence was insufficient (the mathematical model was rigorous) but because it dislodged humanity's sense of being at the center of the universe. To acknowledge that the earth is not the center means you are no longer the protagonist.

The "resistance" to prajñā's awakening lies in precisely the same place — to acknowledge that "I" am not the center of cognition (the incessant self-referencing of manas), to acknowledge that the world you experience is not "truly external" (paratantra-svabhāva), means you are no longer the protagonist you believed yourself to be. What S2-P07 described as "what you take to be seeing the world is actually living inside a world constructed by consciousness" — this is the Dharma's Copernican moment.

## V. Middle Way Corrections

### 5.1 ❌ Avidyā Does Not Equal Stupidity

Avidyā is not an intellectual deficiency. A person of high intelligence can fully understand emptiness of dependent arising at the conceptual level, yet when encountering conflicts of interest, emotional setbacks, or existential anxiety, the operating patterns of avidyā will still activate automatically. This is because avidyā does not operate at the conceptual level — it operates at the seed level (ālaya-vijñāna) and within the incessant self-referencing of manas. "Understanding" is the work of the sixth consciousness; the root of avidyā lies deeper. Conversely, an illiterate elderly woman who has cultivated keen awareness in the craving–grasping gap through years of meditation or Buddha-recitation may be closer to awakening than the scholar.

### 5.2 ❌ Boundaries of the Heliocentric Analogy

This paper uses the heliocentric analogy to illustrate the "inversion" structure of avidyā, but the analogy has clear boundaries. First, the ālaya-vijñāna is not the sun — the sun is a physical entity; the ālaya-vijñāna is consciousness. The analogy borrows only the structure (center unmoving, periphery in flux), not the ontology. Second, heliocentrism is a truth of physics, verifiable by anyone through telescopes and mathematics; prajñā contemplation is a matter of cultivation and realization (xiuzheng 修證), not confirmable by external instruments, only by inner awareness. Third, the heliocentric "inversion" is a one-time cognitive leap (once known, it is never forgotten); prajñā's awakening is not a one-time event — before realization stabilizes, the habitual tendencies of avidyā will repeatedly reassert themselves, requiring sustained contemplation.

### 5.3 ❌ The Craving–Grasping Gap Does Not Equal Free Will

This paper proposes that between craving and grasping there exists an interval for intervention, but this does not amount to asserting libertarian free will. The Buddhist view of causation strictly excludes "effects without causes" — your "not grasping" in that gap is itself dependently arisen (requiring the impressions [vāsanā] of hearing prajñā, the accumulation of samādhi power, the provision of merit). The gap is not a "free space" outside of causation but a "node of dependent arising" where awareness can be present when conditions are sufficient. In Yogācāra terms: "not grasping" is also a conditioned dharma (saṃskṛta-dharma); its arising requires the manifestation of antidote-seeds (seeds impressed by hearing the Dharma).

### 5.4 ❌ Confirmation Bias Does Not Equal Avidyā

The confirmation bias analogy in §IV is a structural heuristic, not a theoretical equivalence. The difference between the two is fundamental. Confirmation bias is a cognitive psychology concept dealing with distortions in information processing, belonging to the level of the sixth consciousness's discrimination, and amenable to improvement through methods such as cognitive behavioral therapy. Avidyā is a Buddhist concept pointing to the fundamental inversion of the entire cognitive architecture, involving the seed level of the ālaya-vijñāna and the self-grasping level of manas, not eliminable through the sixth consciousness's "reflection" alone — requiring prajñā's contemplation and the power of samādhi. To equate confirmation bias with avidyā is to misplace a conventional-level psychological mechanism as an ultimate-level ontological problem — which is itself a manifestation of "avidyā."

## VI. Conclusion

### 6.1 Core Insight

This paper has argued that the essence of avidyā is perceptual inversion, not the absence of knowledge. The cyclic flow of the twelve links is not a problem that can be solved by "learning more" but an event requiring a fundamental inversion of one's perceptual mode — like shifting from the geocentric to the heliocentric model.

The Heart Sūtra, with the ten characters "no ignorance, and no ending of ignorance," simultaneously negates the substantiality of both origination (suffering) and cessation (the end of suffering). Kuiji's trisvabhāva analysis clarifies the precise meaning of this negation: the imagined "agent" and "permanence" are thoroughly negated; the dependent function of conditioned arising is preserved but not grasped as a fixed characteristic; the perfected is neither "the product of eliminating ignorance" nor another thing that can be possessed.

Nāgārjuna's conclusion is the most condensed: "delusion is like space — inexhaustible." Avidyā is like space: you cannot "destroy" space, but you need not destroy it, because it never had substantial existence.

### 6.2 The Loop

Loop-back to S2-P02: the awakening loop of Avalokiteśvara — the moment of longing for freedom — finds a concrete operational field in the context of the twelve links: the craving–grasping gap.

Loop-back to S2-P03: "going deep" turns inward into the formations — the formations are precisely the field of avidyā's operation. The "formations" link (saṃskāra) of the twelve links directly corresponds to the "formations" of "going deep into the formations" (saṃskāra).

Loop-back to S2-P06: tathatā is unborn and unceasing — avidyā cannot defile tathatā; "clouds do not cover space." This paper further clarifies: precisely because tathatā is originally pure, awakening is not "acquiring" but "ceasing to obscure."

Loop-back to S2-P07: the eighteen dhātus deconstructed the structure of cognition (how you know); this paper asks why that structure traps you (why you suffer). The answer is avidyā — perceptual inversion causes you to mistake the world constructed by consciousness for a real external.

Loop-back to S1-P07: the discussion of karmic seeds — the operation of avidyā-seeds within the ālaya-vijñāna is the karmic causal chain manifested at its most microscopic level.

Forward to S2-P09: the twelve links are all empty; next, even the Four Noble Truths are empty — "no suffering, arising, cessation, or path." The doctrinal logic of "no ending of ignorance" directly extends into "no suffering, arising, cessation, or path": if even "eliminating ignorance" cannot be grasped, then "suffering, arising, cessation, and path" equally cannot be grasped.

### 6.3 Direction for Practice

The doctrinal analysis of this paper ultimately converges on a single practical direction: cultivating the presence of prajñā's seeing in the gap between craving and grasping.

Not severing — the twelve links are not a chain that can be cut. Illuminating — in the very moment the causal chain operates, truthfully seeing its emptiness. Every "not grasping" in the gap is not suppression (suppression is just another form of grasping), not avoidance (avoidance is just another form of grasping), but truthful seeing — seeing craving arise, seeing grasping begin to form, then without intervention letting it arise as it is and dissolve as it is.

What this requires is not conceptual understanding but the accumulation of practice. Concepts can only tell you where the gap is; practice is what allows you to be truly present within it.

"Practicing deep prajñāpāramitā, illuminating" — the Heart Sūtra's master program can be activated in every turn of the twelve links.
