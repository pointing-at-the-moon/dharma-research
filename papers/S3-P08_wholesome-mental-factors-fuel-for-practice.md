## 摘要

善心所十一法——信、慚、愧、無貪、無瞋、無癡、精進、輕安、不放逸、行捨、不害——不是一張孤立的美德清單，而是一個有機結構。如果遍行心所是引擎（必然點火），別境心所是方向盤（按需啟動），善心所就是讓這輛車朝向解脫前進的燃料。本文以阿含經信根精進慚愧三善根不放逸諸經為阿含根據，以《華嚴經》「信為道元功德母」與精進波羅蜜為大乘拓展，以《解深密經》輕安與奢摩他為禪修判準，以《楞嚴經》行陰區宇為修行驗證，以《大智度論》大悲為不害之般若根據，建立經部基礎。論部以《成唯識論》卷六善心所十一法完整定義為核心。§三核心論證分九層展開。§四跨學科會通以自我效能感、恆毅力、道德情感、自我調節、放鬆反應為啟發性類比。§五中道校正五條。

**關鍵詞：** 善心所、信、三善根、慚愧、精進、輕安、不放逸、行捨、不害、百法明門論

## Abstract

The eleven wholesome mental factors (*kuśala-caitta*) — faith (*śraddhā*), conscience (*hrī*), shame (*apatrāpya*), non-attachment (*alobha*), non-aversion (*adveṣa*), non-delusion (*amoha*), vigor (*vīrya*), pliancy (*praśrabdhi*), non-negligence (*apramāda*), equanimity (*upekṣā*), and non-harming (*ahiṃsā*) — are not an isolated catalogue of virtues but an organic structure. If the universal mental factors are the engine (firing inevitably) and the object-specific factors are the steering wheel (activated on demand), the wholesome factors are the fuel that drives the vehicle toward liberation. This paper establishes the sūtra foundation through the Āgamas (five faculties, four right efforts, conscience and shame, three wholesome roots, non-negligence), the *Avataṃsaka Sūtra* (faith as the origin of the path and vigor pāramitā), the *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra* (pliancy and śamatha), the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* (the domain of the formation-aggregate), and the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* (great compassion and non-harming). The treatise section draws on the *Cheng Weishi Lun* (complete definitions of all eleven), the *Yogācārabhūmi-śāstra* (six bases for wholesome dharmas), the *Bajishi Guiju Buzhu* (verse positioning), and the *Pañcaskandhaka-prakaraṇa* (variant readings). §3 Core Analysis unfolds nine layers: the organic structure, faith's three aspects, the three wholesome roots as core drive, vigor as sustaining force, conscience and shame as moat, pliancy as meditation foundation, the nature-debate on non-negligence and equanimity, non-harming as the caitta basis for compassion, and the distribution of the eleven across consciousnesses. §4 Cross-Disciplinary Dialogue engages self-efficacy, grit, moral emotions, self-regulation, and the relaxation response as heuristic analogies. §5 Middle Way Corrections: five entries.

**Keywords:** wholesome mental factors (kuśala-caitta), faith (śraddhā), three wholesome roots (tri-kuśala-mūla), conscience and shame (hrī-apatrāpya), vigor (vīrya), pliancy (praśrabdhi), non-negligence (apramāda), equanimity (upekṣā), non-harming (ahiṃsā), Śatadharmaprakāśamukha-śāstra

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Scriptural Positioning

S3-P07 unfolded the first two of the six categories of mental factors — the five universals and the five object-specific factors. The universals are the engine that inevitably fires: they pervade all moral natures, all grounds, all times, and all eight consciousnesses. The object-specifics are the steering wheel: activated on demand, each requiring its particular condition. P07's conclusion noted that both engine and steering wheel are morally neutral — universals pervade wholesome, unwholesome, and neutral states alike, and so do the object-specifics. The engine fires and the wheel can turn, but which direction the vehicle moves depends on what fuel drives it.

This paper enters the third of the six categories: the eleven wholesome mental factors.[^1]

The *Śatadharmaprakāśamukha-śāstra* lists them:

> "三善，謂信、慚、愧、無貪、無瞋、無癡、精進、輕安、不放逸、行捨、不害。"
> "The third [category], wholesome, comprises: faith, conscience, shame, non-attachment, non-aversion, non-delusion, vigor, pliancy, non-negligence, equanimity, and non-harming."[^2]

In the *Verses on the Eight Consciousnesses*, the wholesome factors appear at two key positions. The second verse for the first five consciousnesses:

> "遍行別境善十一，中二大八貪瞋癡。"
> "Universals, object-specifics, eleven wholesome, two intermediate, eight major, plus greed, aversion, delusion."[^3]

The second verse for the sixth consciousness:

> "根隨信等總相連，動身發語獨為最。"
> "Fundamental and subsidiary [afflictions], faith and its kin — all are connected; activating body and speech, [the sixth] is supreme."[^4]

"Eleven wholesome" — three characters encompassing eleven dharmas. "Faith and its kin" — faith listed first, the rest following. This ordering is not arbitrary: faith is the gateway to the path, vigor the sustaining force, conscience and shame the defensive moat, the three wholesome roots (non-attachment, non-aversion, non-delusion) the core engine, pliancy the foundation for meditation, non-negligence and equanimity the Middle Way stabilizers, and non-harming the caitta basis for compassion. The eleven form an organic structure, not eleven isolated virtues.

### 1.2 Central Question

Within the fourfold universality framework, the wholesome factors possess only one of the four — universality across grounds (present in all three realms and nine grounds) — while lacking universality across moral natures (exclusively wholesome, not unwholesome or neutral), across times (intermittent, not present in every moment), and across consciousnesses (not associated with the seventh or eighth consciousness).[^5]

The fourfold universality comparison table established in P07 revealed a foundational insight for practice: the universal factors possess all four, meaning the basic operations of consciousness are automatic; afflictions possess none of the four, meaning afflictions are not inevitable. The wholesome factors possess only universality across grounds — what does this mean?

It means wholesome states require effort. They do not fire automatically like the universals — you do not spontaneously generate faith, automatically sustain vigor, or instinctively feel moral conscience. Wholesome states are like fuel: you must actively supply them. Yet neither are they entirely contingent like afflictions — their universality across grounds means that from the desire realm through the form realm to the formless realm, the seeds of wholesome dharmas are always present. The possibility of practice is never foreclosed.

This is the practice-positioning of the wholesome mental factors: not automatic, but possible. Not inevitable, but never absent. The entire effort of cultivation consists in transforming "possible" into "habitual" — from occasional manifestation to continuous manifestation, until after the fundamental transformation (*āśraya-parāvṛtti*) they flow naturally.

### 1.3 Scope and Structure

This paper covers the eleven wholesome mental factors in the Hundred Dharmas framework. Of these, eight are substantially real (possessing independent natures), and three are nominally existent (*prajñapti*): non-negligence, equanimity, and non-harming, which take other mental factors as their substance and lack independent characteristics.[^6]

The paper proceeds through a two-tier sūtra-then-treatise architecture. §2 Scriptural Foundations builds two layers: the sūtra tier draws on the Āgamas, the *Avataṃsaka*, the *Saṃdhinirmocana*, the *Śūraṅgama*, and the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*; the treatise tier draws on the *Cheng Weishi Lun*, the *Yogācārabhūmi*, the *Bajishi Guiju Buzhu*, and the *Pañcaskandhaka*. §3 Core Analysis in nine layers. §4 Cross-Disciplinary Dialogue. §5 Middle Way Corrections. §6 Conclusion.

## 2. Scriptural Foundations

### Part A: Sūtra Tier — The Authority of Buddha's Word

#### 2.1 The Āgamas: Original Teachings on Wholesome Dharmas

The roots of the wholesome mental factors can be traced to the Buddha's direct teachings in the Āgamas. The framework of the five faculties (*pañcendriya*) and five powers (*pañca-bala*) provided the earliest systematic classification for the core members of the wholesome group — faith, vigor, mindfulness, concentration, and wisdom.[^7]

**Faith Faculty: Firmly Rooted, Indestructible**

Sūtra 658 of the *Saṃyuktāgama* defines the faith faculty:

> "何等為信根？謂聖弟子於如來所起信心，根本堅固，諸天、魔、梵、沙門、婆羅門及諸世間法所不能壞，是名信根。"
> "What is the faith faculty? When a noble disciple gives rise to faith in the Tathāgata, firmly rooted at its foundation, which cannot be destroyed by gods, Māra, Brahmā, śramaṇas, brāhmaṇas, or any worldly phenomenon — this is called the faith faculty."[^8]

"Firmly rooted, indestructible" — faith is not a vague affinity or momentary inspiration but a conviction that withstands all external testing. Faith heads the five faculties because without this firm foundation, vigor (four right efforts), mindfulness (four foundations), concentration (four dhyānas), and wisdom (four noble truths) cannot be stably established. Sūtra 656 further identifies the internal cohesion of the five:

> "慧根為其首，以攝持故。譬如堂閣，棟為其首，眾材所依，以攝持故。"
> "The wisdom faculty is their chief, because it unifies and sustains them. Just as the ridgepole crowns a hall, supporting all the beams."[^9]

Faith opens the door; wisdom holds the structure together — faith and wisdom bookend the path. Without faith, one never begins; without wisdom, one cannot arrive.

Sūtra 691 expands the five faculties into seven powers, adding the powers of conscience and shame:

> "何等為慚力？謂恥惡不善法。何等為愧力？於可愧事愧，愧起惡不善法。"
> "What is the power of conscience? Being ashamed of unwholesome dharmas. What is the power of shame? Feeling shame at shameful acts, being ashamed of arising unwholesome dharmas."[^10]

Their independent listing among the seven powers shows that conscience and shame are not merely ordinary wholesome states but constitute "power" — the actual force that halts unwholesome dharmas. These two forces guard wholesome dharmas from within and without.

**Three Wholesome Roots: The Foundation of All Wholesome Dharmas**

Sūtra 344 of the *Saṃyuktāgama* defines the three wholesome roots using the grammar of "knowing as they really are":

> "云何善根如實知？謂三善根——無貪、無恚、無癡——是名三善根。"
> "How does one know the wholesome roots as they really are? The three wholesome roots — non-attachment, non-aversion, non-delusion — these are called the three wholesome roots."[^11]

The correspondence between the three wholesome roots and the three unwholesome roots is not accidental verbal symmetry but the core structure of practice: the antidote for greed is non-attachment, for aversion is non-aversion, for delusion is non-delusion. Sūtra 613 emphasizes their aggregate character:

> "何等為不善聚？謂三不善根，是名正說。所以者何？純不善積聚者，謂三不善根。"
> "What is the unwholesome aggregate? The three unwholesome roots — this is the correct statement. Why? Because they are the pure accumulation of all that is unwholesome."[^12]

The three poisons are not scattered individual problems but the total aggregate of the unwholesome. Correspondingly, the three wholesome roots are the total aggregate of the wholesome. The *Ekottarikāgama* further points to their fruition:

> "若有此三善根者，便有二善處，涅槃為三。"
> "One who possesses these three wholesome roots has two favorable destinies, with nirvāṇa as the third."[^13]

And characterizes them as inexhaustible:

> "有三善根，不可窮盡，漸至涅槃界。"
> "There are three wholesome roots that cannot be exhausted, gradually leading to the realm of nirvāṇa."[^14]

**Vigor: The Four Right Efforts**

The *Ekottarikāgama* directly defines vigor through the four right efforts (*catvāri samyak-pradhānāni*):

> "未生弊惡法，求方便令不生；已生弊惡法，求方便令不生；未生善法，求方便令生；已生善法，求方便令增多。"
> "Unwholesome dharmas not yet arisen — one strives to prevent their arising. Unwholesome dharmas already arisen — one strives to abandon them. Wholesome dharmas not yet arisen — one strives to generate them. Wholesome dharmas already arisen — one strives to increase them."[^15]

These four statements constitute the most concise formula for the entire dynamic of practice — practice always involves one of these four activities.

**Non-Negligence: Root of All Wholesome Dharmas**

Sūtra 882 of the *Saṃyuktāgama* is the most extensive Āgama teaching on non-negligence. The Buddha employs over twenty similes to reinforce a single proposition:

> "譬如百草藥木，皆依於地而得生長；如是種種善法，皆依不放逸為本。"
> "Just as all grasses, herbs, and trees depend on the earth to grow, so do all wholesome dharmas depend on non-negligence as their foundation."[^16]

Earth, sandalwood, elephant tracks, Mount Sumeru, Garuḍa, the Wheel-Turning King, Śakra, Brahmā, the great ocean, moonlight, the Tathāgata — over twenty similes, each returning to the same conclusion: "All wholesome dharmas have non-negligence as their root." The *Ekottarikāgama* states directly in verse:

> "戒為甘露道，放逸為死徑。"
> "Morality is the path of ambrosia; negligence is the road to death."[^17]

The opposite of non-negligence is not relaxation but "the road to death." The Buddha's extreme phrasing marks the irreplaceable position of non-negligence in practice.

#### 2.2 The Avataṃsaka Sūtra: Mahāyāna Expansion of Faith and Vigor

The faith faculty and vigor of the Āgamas unfold in the *Avataṃsaka Sūtra* as core forces of the bodhisattva path.

**Faith as the Origin of the Path, Mother of All Merit**

The Xianshou chapter of the eighty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* contains a celebrated verse elevating faith to the source of all merit:[^18]

> "信為道元功德母，長養一切諸善法，斷除疑網出愛流，開示涅槃無上道。"
> "Faith is the origin of the path, mother of all merit; it nourishes all wholesome dharmas, severs the net of doubt, frees one from the current of craving, and reveals the unsurpassed path of nirvāṇa."[^19]

"Origin of the path" (*dào yuán*), "mother of merit" — the *Avataṃsaka* positions faith through the metaphor of motherhood. "Faith is the first treasure of the Dharma treasury" — among all provisions for practice, faith ranks foremost. "Faith can certainly reach the ground of the Tathāgata" — faith is not merely a starting condition but the driving force that accompanies one all the way to Buddhahood.

Of textual note: the CBETA original reads 道**元** ("origin"), not the commonly cited 道**源** ("source"). *Yuán* emphasizes faith as the primordial starting point of the path.[^20]

**Vigor Pāramitā**

In the Ten Grounds chapter, vigor occupies a special position at the Fourth Ground (Blazing Wisdom):

> "十波羅蜜中，精進偏多。"
> "Among the ten pāramitās, vigor predominates."[^21]

The *Avataṃsaka*'s "Transcending the Worldly" chapter expands bodhisattva vigor into ten forms, with "the vigorous effort to teach and transform all sentient beings" listed first — Mahāyāna vigor is not solitary effort but sustained exertion oriented primarily toward benefiting others.[^22]

#### 2.3 The Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra: Pliancy and Śamatha

Pliancy (*praśrabdhi*), among the wholesome mental factors, receives its precise meditative positioning in the "Discernment of Yoga" chapter of the *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra*:

> "如是正行多安住故，起身輕安及心輕安，是名奢摩他。"
> "Because of abiding extensively in such correct practice, bodily pliancy and mental pliancy arise — this is called śamatha."[^23]

Pliancy is not general comfort but the marker of śamatha's attainment. The same chapter uses negative phrasing to establish pliancy as the threshold for calm abiding:

> "若諸菩薩……乃至未得身心輕安，所有作意，當名何等？……非奢摩他作意，是隨順奢摩他勝解相應作意。"
> "If bodhisattvas... have not yet attained bodily and mental pliancy, what should their mental engagement be called? ...Not śamatha mental engagement, but mental engagement that accords with śamatha and is associated with confident understanding."[^24]

Before pliancy is attained, all efforts at meditation are judged by the Buddha as "not śamatha" but merely "tending toward śamatha." This criterion is strict: pliancy is not a pleasant side-effect of meditation but its minimum threshold. The sūtra further identifies the obstacles to śamatha: attachment to body and possessions, and agitation with remorse.[^25]

#### 2.4 The Śūraṅgama Sūtra: The Domain of the Formation-Aggregate — Verification of Equanimity in Practice

Equanimity (*upekṣā*) — the state in which vigor and the three wholesome roots cause the mind to abide in evenness, uprightness, and effortless stability — finds its most detailed advanced-practice verification in the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra*'s domain of the formation-aggregate (*saṃskāra-skandha*):

> "覺明虛靜猶如晴空，無復麁重前塵影事……猶如野馬熠熠清擾，為浮根塵究竟樞穴；此則名為行陰區宇。"
> "Awareness is bright, empty, and still, like a clear sky, with no more coarseness or residual traces of sense-objects... like shimmering mirages in subtle agitation — this is the foundation of all floating sense-faculties, the ultimate nexus. This is called the domain of the formation-aggregate."[^26]

Yet precisely at this exalted stage, the *Śūraṅgama* lists ten "dhyāna-derangements of the formation-aggregate" — ten kinds of deviant views that arise not from external demons (those belong to the perception-aggregate stage) but from the mind's own aberrant views:[^27]

> "如是十種禪那狂解，皆是行陰用心交互，故現斯悟。"
> "All ten kinds of dhyāna derangement arise from the interactive functioning of the formation-aggregate's mental activity."

Commentaries note: "The ten demons of the formation-aggregate are mental demons, not external ones — unlike the perception-aggregate which draws external demons, the formation-aggregate generates them from one's own aberrant views."[^28] This teaching constitutes the most rigorous Middle Way correction for the equanimity mental factor in actual meditative practice.

#### 2.5 The Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra: Great Compassion and Non-Harming

Non-harming (*ahiṃsā*), whose substance is non-aversion and whose function is compassionate concern, finds its deepest scriptural grounding in the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*.

Fascicle 20 establishes great compassion's position in the entire Buddhist path with a single statement:

> "大悲是一切諸佛菩薩功德之根本，是般若波羅蜜之母，諸佛之祖母。"
> "Great compassion is the root of all merit of all buddhas and bodhisattvas, the mother of prajñā-pāramitā, the grandmother of all buddhas."[^29]

"Mother of prajñā, grandmother of all buddhas" — prajñā is the mother of buddhas (its standard epithet), and great compassion is the mother of prajñā. Compassion's position is more fundamental even than wisdom. Fascicle 27 unfolds three levels of compassion:

> "有三種悲：眾生緣、法緣、無緣。無緣悲從畢竟空生。"
> "There are three kinds of compassion: sentient-being-conditioned, dharma-conditioned, and unconditioned. Unconditioned compassion arises from ultimate emptiness."[^30]

The same fascicle distinguishes great from lesser compassion:

> "小悲，名觀眾生種種身苦心苦，憐愍而已，不能令脫。……大悲，憐愍眾生苦，亦能令脫苦。"
> "Lesser compassion means observing beings' various physical and mental sufferings and merely pitying them, without being able to liberate them... Great compassion means both pitying beings' suffering and being able to free them from it."[^31]

Fascicle 13 further establishes the ethical foundation for non-harming:

> "諸功德中，不殺第一。世間中惜命為第一。"
> "Among all virtues, non-killing is foremost. Among worldly things, cherishing life is foremost."[^32]

### Part B: Treatise Tier — Precise Doctrinal Elaboration

#### 2.6 Cheng Weishi Lun, Fascicle 6: General Framework and Definitions (Part 1)

The *Cheng Weishi Lun* summarizes all eleven wholesome factors in a single verse:[^33]

> "善謂信、慚、愧，無貪等三根，勤、安、不放逸，行捨及不害。"
> "The wholesome comprise: faith, conscience, shame, non-attachment and the other two roots, vigor, pliancy, non-negligence, equanimity, and non-harming."

The commentary states: "唯善心俱，名善心所" — "Only those that accompany wholesome mind are called wholesome mental factors." This is the criterion: these factors manifest only in wholesome states, never in unwholesome or neutral ones.

**Faith** is defined as: "於實德能，深忍樂欲，心淨為性" — "With respect to what is real, what has merit, and what has capacity, [faith involves] deep conviction, delight, and aspiration; mental purity is its nature."[^34] The *Cheng Weishi Lun* illustrates with the "water-clarifying jewel" (*maṇi*): "如水清珠，能清濁水" — "Like a water-clarifying jewel that purifies turbid water."[^35]

**Conscience** (*hrī*): "依自法力，崇重賢善為性" — "Based on the power of self and dharma, its nature is to esteem the virtuous and the good."

**Shame** (*apatrāpya*): "依世間力，輕拒暴惡為性" — "Based on the power of the world, its nature is to spurn the violent and evil."[^36]

The two share "cessation of wrongdoing" as their function but differ in their source of force: conscience relies on "self and dharma" (one's own dignity and respect for the Dharma); shame relies on "worldly power" (social moral censure). The text clarifies: "自法名自，世間名他" — "Self and dharma constitute 'self'; the world constitutes 'other.'"[^37] Conscience is the self-awareness that "given who I am and what I know of the Dharma, how could I do evil?"[^38] Shame is the external restraint of knowing that "the world condemns and abhors such misdeeds."

**Non-attachment** (*alobha*): "於有有具，無著為性" — "Non-clinging toward existence and the provisions of existence."

**Non-aversion** (*adveṣa*): "於苦苦具，無恚為性" — "Non-hostility toward suffering and the causes of suffering."[^39]

These two "pervade all wholesome mind" (*biàn shàn xīn*) — whenever wholesome mind arises, non-attachment and non-aversion are necessarily present.[^40]

#### 2.7 Cheng Weishi Lun, Fascicle 6: Definitions (Part 2)

**Vigor** (*vīrya*): "於善惡品修斷事中，勇悍為性" — "Courage and fortitude in the work of cultivating [the good] and abandoning [the bad]." "Courage" (*yǒng*) marks upward aspiration, distinguishing it from defiled states. "Fortitude" (*hàn*) marks pure intensity, distinguishing it from neutral states. Five subtypes: donning armor, engaging in action, not demeaning oneself, not retreating, and not resting content.[^41][^42]

**Non-delusion** (*amoha*): "於諸理事，明解為性" — "Clear understanding of all principles and affairs."[^43] Two rival interpretations exist: one identifying non-delusion with wisdom (*prajñā*), the other maintaining it as an independent wholesome root directly countering ignorance.[^44]

**Pliancy** (*praśrabdhi*): "遠離麁重，調暢身心，堪任為性" — "Distanced from coarseness, it adjusts and smooths body and mind, with serviceability as its nature."[^45]

**Non-negligence** (*apramāda*): "精進三根，於所斷修，防修為性……非別有體" — "Vigor and the three roots, in the work of abandoning and cultivating, with guarding and cultivating as its nature... It has no separate substance."[^46]

**Equanimity** (*upekṣā*): "精進三根，令心平等、正直、無功用住為性" — "Vigor and the three roots causing the mind to abide in evenness, uprightness, and effortlessness." "由不放逸先除雜染，捨復令心寂靜而住。此無別體" — "Non-negligence first removes defilement; equanimity then causes the mind to dwell in quiescence. It has no separate substance."[^47] The three stages — evenness (initial), uprightness (intermediate), effortless abiding (advanced) — map the progression from novice to adept.[^48]

**Non-harming** (*ahiṃsā*): "於諸有情，不為損惱，無瞋為性；能對治害，悲愍為業" — "Toward all sentient beings, not causing distress, with non-aversion as its nature; counteracting harm, with compassionate concern as its function."[^49] The crucial distinction: "無瞋與樂，不害拔苦" — "Non-aversion bestows happiness; non-harming removes suffering."[^50] This is precisely the definition of loving-kindness and compassion. The reason for establishing non-harming as a separate category: "為顯慈悲二相別故" — "To reveal the distinct characteristics of kindness and compassion."[^51] And three reasons for its establishment: harm occurs frequently, it obstructs the supreme cause of the highest vehicle (great compassion), and its gravity requires specific recognition.[^53]

#### 2.8 Yogācārabhūmi-śāstra: Six Bases for Wholesome Dharmas

The *Yogācārabhūmi*, fascicle 55, systematically assigns each wholesome factor to a specific occasion of arising:[^54]

At the time of determination — faith. At the time of cessation — conscience and shame. At the time of engagement — the three roots plus vigor. At the time of worldly purification — pliancy. At the time of supramundane purification — non-negligence and equanimity. At the time of gathering sentient beings — non-harming, "because it pertains to compassion." This progression from "determination" to "gathering sentient beings" maps the complete bodhisattva path from self-benefit to other-benefit.

The *Yogācārabhūmi* concurs on the nominal status of three factors: "三世俗有。謂不放逸、捨及不害" — "Three are conventionally existent: non-negligence, equanimity, and non-harming."[^55]

#### 2.9 Bajishi Guiju Buzhu: Verse Positioning

The *Bajishi Guiju Buzhu*'s commentary on the second verse of the first five consciousnesses — "遍行別境善十一" — provides annotations on each of the eleven wholesome factors one by one.[^56] It summarizes the real-nominal distinction: "前八法是實，後三是假" — "The first eight are real; the latter three are nominal."[^57]

The commentary on "根隨信等總相連" notes: "等者，等餘遍行別境及不定也" — "'And its kin' extends to the universals, object-specifics, and indeterminates" — meaning the sixth consciousness connects with all categories of mental factors, a mark of its vast functional scope.[^58]

#### 2.10 Pañcaskandhaka-prakaraṇa: Variant Readings

The *Pañcaskandhaka*'s definitions largely agree with the *Cheng Weishi Lun* but offer notable variants: faith is defined as "極正符順" ("perfectly conforming to truth") rather than "deep conviction, delight, and aspiration" — emphasizing faith's correspondence with reality. Non-aversion is directly defined as "以慈為性" ("kindness is its nature") — more concise than "non-hostility." Non-negligence and equanimity are likewise identified as having vigor and the three roots as their substance.[^59][^60]

## 3. Core Analysis

### 3.1 The Organic Structure: Not a List but a System

The ordering of the eleven wholesome factors in the *Cheng Weishi Lun* — faith, conscience, shame, non-attachment, non-aversion, non-delusion, vigor, pliancy, non-negligence, equanimity, non-harming — constitutes an organic structure:

**Faith** is the gateway. Without it, practice never begins — no one strives for what they do not believe in. **Conscience and shame** are the defensive moat: after faith arises, two forces protect wholesome dharmas from regression — internal self-regulation (conscience) and external moral awareness (shame). **The three wholesome roots** are the core engine: the Āgamas call them the "wholesome aggregate," directly countering the three poisons. **Vigor** is sustaining force: the three roots provide direction, vigor provides drive. **Pliancy** is the meditation foundation: the *Saṃdhinirmocana* identifies it as the threshold of śamatha. **Non-negligence and equanimity** are the Middle Way stabilizers: the former removes defilement, the latter establishes quiescence. **Non-harming** is the caitta basis for compassion: representing the ultimate orientation of the wholesome factors — from self-benefit (faith → vigor → pliancy) toward other-benefit (non-harming → great compassion).

Continuing the vehicle metaphor from P07: universals are the engine, object-specifics the steering wheel, wholesome factors the fuel. But not a single fuel — a compound formula: faith is the spark, conscience and shame the leak-proof seal, the three roots the core fuel, vigor the injection pressure, pliancy the combustion efficiency, non-negligence and equanimity the voltage regulators, and non-harming the destination the fuel ultimately drives toward — the shore of benefiting others.

### 3.2 Faith: Gateway to the Path — Analysis of Three Aspects

The *Cheng Weishi Lun* distinguishes three aspects of faith:[^61]

**Faith in what is real** (*xìn shí yǒu*): deep conviction in the actual facts and principles of dharmas — the foundation of faith, for without belief in causality, no motive for practice exists.

**Faith in merit** (*xìn yǒu dé*): deep delight in the genuine, pure merit of the Three Jewels — the direction of faith, trusting that someone has walked this path (Buddha), the path is real (Dharma), and others are walking it (Saṅgha).

**Faith in capacity** (*xìn yǒu néng*): deep hope that all worldly and supramundane wholesome dharmas have the power to be attained and accomplished — the drive of faith, believing one can arrive.

The three are mutually indispensable. Faith in reality without faith in merit is like believing the map is real but not that the destination exists. Faith in merit without faith in capacity is like believing the destination is wonderful but not that one can reach it. Faith in capacity without faith in reality is like believing one can run without knowing where the track is.

### 3.3 The Three Wholesome Roots: Core Drive

The three wholesome roots directly counter the three poisons. Their antidotal function is not "elimination" but replacement with the opposite function: non-attachment is not "removing greed" but "non-clinging in the face of existence"; non-aversion is not "suppressing anger" but "non-hostility in the face of suffering"; non-delusion is not "erasing ignorance" but "clear understanding of principles and affairs."

Each antidote operates on the same object with a transformed attitude: facing "existence," greed clings while non-attachment does not; facing "suffering," aversion resists while non-aversion does not; facing "principles and affairs," delusion misunderstands while non-delusion understands. Practice is not fleeing from objects but transforming one's stance toward them.

The three roots are mutually dependent: non-attachment without non-delusion devolves into "ignorant letting-go"; non-attachment without non-aversion collapses under pressure into aversion; non-aversion without non-delusion becomes "ignorant tolerance." Only simultaneous activation produces the complete structure of wholesome mind.[^62]

### 3.4 Vigor: Sustaining Force

Vigor's five subtypes — donning armor, engaging in action, not demeaning oneself, not retreating, not resting content — map the complete process from aspiration through non-regression.[^63] Its critical feature: it is "exclusively of wholesome nature" — effort directed toward unwholesome goals is not vigor. The direction of vigor is determined by the three wholesome roots.

### 3.5 Conscience and Shame: The Moat

Conscience and shame share "cessation of wrongdoing" as their function but differ in driving force. Conscience (*hrī*) draws on "self and dharma" — the sense that "given who I am and what I know of the Dharma, how could I do evil?"[^64] Shame (*apatrāpya*) draws on "the world" — respect for the moral norms of the broader community. Together they form the complete moral defense: internal self-regulation and external social awareness. The Āgamas list them among the seven powers alongside faith, vigor, mindfulness, concentration, and wisdom, indicating they carry the same rank as meditative attainments.[^65]

### 3.6 Pliancy: The Meditation Foundation

Pliancy is unique among the eleven: it is the only wholesome factor that does not pervade all wholesome mind.[^66] The remaining ten are necessarily present whenever wholesome mind arises, but pliancy requires meditative absorption (*samāhita*) to manifest. Everyday wholesome mind (as in giving or maintaining precepts) can lack pliancy because the body-mind remains in a coarse state. Pliancy's appearance marks the transition from "scattered goodness" to "concentrated goodness."

The *Saṃdhinirmocana* identifies it as the threshold of śamatha — effort before pliancy is attained is only "tending toward" calm abiding. Pliancy is not a bonus of meditation but its criterion, encompassing both bodily pliancy (freedom from rigidity, fatigue, and heaviness) and mental pliancy (freedom from distraction, depression, and agitation).

### 3.7 Non-Negligence and Equanimity: Middle Way Stabilizers — The Nature-Debate

Both are "nominally existent" — lacking independent substance, taking vigor and the three roots as their substance.[^67]

**Why name something without independent substance?** Because functional difference requires labeling. The functional state of vigor and the three roots *guarding against regression and cultivating the good* is called non-negligence. The functional state *causing the mind to abide in evenness and quiescence* is called equanimity — like "water" and "ice," same substance, different state, different name.

**Functional distinction:** Non-negligence "first removes defilement" (protective); equanimity "causes the mind to dwell in quiescence" (stabilizing).

**Non-negligence's special status in the Āgamas** — Sūtra 882's twenty-plus similes declaring it "the root of all wholesome dharmas" — far exceeds the impression that the "nominally existent" designation might give. The Yogācāra analysis of substance and the Āgama analysis of function do not conflict: non-negligence's "lack of independent substance" precisely means it is not an additional thing but the *active state* of vigor and the three roots in operation. It is the marker of wholesome dharmas actually functioning.

### 3.8 Non-Harming: The Caitta Basis for Compassion

Non-harming is the third nominally existent wholesome factor, established as a nominal subset of non-aversion.[^68]

The distinction: non-aversion counters "killing anger"; non-harming counters "harming injury." Non-aversion's function is "bestowing happiness" (loving-kindness); non-harming's function is "removing suffering" (compassion). The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* calls great compassion "the mother of prajñā, the grandmother of all buddhas" — non-harming is this great compassion's caitta-level foundation. The *Cheng Weishi Lun* further cites the treatise's statement that "great compassion pertains to non-aversion and non-delusion" (*大悲無瞋癡攝*)[^52], and identifies three specific reasons for establishing non-harming as a distinct factor:

Non-harming deepens through three levels, corresponding to the three kinds of compassion: sentient-being-conditioned (not causing harm — negative), dharma-conditioned (actively removing suffering — positive), unconditioned (naturally benefiting all beings — effortless).[^69]

### 3.9 Distribution Across Consciousnesses

The eleven do not distribute equally across the eight consciousnesses:[^70]

**Sixth consciousness** — in meditative states, all eleven are present; in non-meditative wholesome states, all except pliancy.

**First five consciousnesses** — ten or eleven, with a doctrinal dispute over whether pliancy manifests in them.[^71]

**Seventh consciousness (manas)** — before transformation, no wholesome factors (the four constant afflictions preclude them); after transformation, wholesome factors flow naturally with the wisdom of equality.

**Eighth consciousness (ālaya-vijñāna)** — no wholesome factors manifest, as it is karmically neutral (*vipāka*); it stores their seeds, which manifest in the active consciousnesses when conditions ripen.

This distribution confirms the fundamental fact of practice: wholesome states must be actively initiated and sustained through the sixth consciousness. They are neither automatic like the universals nor dormant like afflictions — they require conscious effort.[^72]

## 4. Cross-Disciplinary Dialogue

### 4.1 Faith ↔ Self-Efficacy (Bandura) ⚠️

Faith's "belief in capacity" (*xìn yǒu néng*) structurally parallels Bandura's self-efficacy — the strongest predictor of behavioral change. Difference: ⚠️ Bandura's self-efficacy is based on cognitive appraisal of past success; faith's "belief in capacity" includes deep conviction about future possibility even without prior success (as in the initial aspiration). Faith is closer to an "ontological trust" than a "cognitive assessment."

### 4.2 Vigor ↔ Grit (Duckworth) ⚠️

Vigor's "courage and fortitude" parallels Duckworth's grit — passion plus perseverance for long-term goals. Difference: ⚠️ Grit is morally neutral; vigor is exclusively wholesome. One can exhibit high grit with zero vigor (if the long-term goal is unwholesome). Vigor's direction is determined by the three wholesome roots.

### 4.3 Conscience and Shame ↔ Moral Emotions (Lewis, Tangney) ⚠️

The conscience-shame distinction parallels the shame-guilt distinction in moral emotion research. Difference: ⚠️ Western shame research often views shame as harmful (leading to withdrawal); Buddhist conscience is never harmful because its object is *behavior*, not *self*. Buddhism does not recognize a fixed "bad self" to be ashamed of — only unwholesome actions to feel conscience about. This is the direct application of the no-self doctrine to moral emotions.

### 4.4 Non-Negligence ↔ Self-Regulation (Baumeister) ⚠️

Non-negligence parallels Baumeister's self-regulation theory. Difference: ⚠️ Baumeister assumes self-regulation is always a limited resource (ego depletion); Yogācāra assumes non-negligence can transform from effortful to effortless (equanimity's three stages). This difference reflects the contemplative tradition's core conviction: mind can be fundamentally transformed, not permanently constrained by resource limits.

### 4.5 Pliancy ↔ Relaxation Response (Benson) ⚠️

Pliancy's "distanced from coarseness, adjusting body and mind" parallels Benson's relaxation response. Difference: ⚠️ The relaxation response is a physiological description; pliancy is a mental factor — the former describes bodily states, the latter describes mind's functional condition. Pliancy pervades the form and formless realms where no gross body exists, exceeding physiological explanation.

## 5. Middle Way Corrections

### 5.1 ❌ Wholesome Factors = Virtue List

Common misreading: treating the eleven as a static inventory of moral qualities to memorize.

Correction: They are dynamic psychological functions that arise or do not arise in each moment, distribute differently across consciousnesses, and include real-nominal distinctions. "Faith" is not a personality label but a psychological function actually operating in a given moment of wholesome mind. Practice is not "becoming a person of faith" (that is the grasped self-image of *parikalpita*) but "enabling faith to manifest in more moments."

### 5.2 ❌ Wholesome = Nice

Common misreading: equating the Yogācāra category "wholesome" (*kuśala*) with everyday "niceness" or "being a good person."

Correction: The Yogācāra definition is strict — "此世他世俱順益故，名為善" — "Benefiting both this life and future lives constitutes wholesomeness."[^73] A "nice person" acting from mere ignorant tolerance (without non-delusion present) does not meet the Yogācāra standard.

### 5.3 ⚠️ Nominally Existent ≠ Unimportant

Scholarly clarification: the "nominally existent" classification of non-negligence, equanimity, and non-harming may be misread as "unimportant."

Correction: nominal status is a determination of substance, not value. Non-negligence is declared by the Buddha as "the root of all wholesome dharmas"; non-harming is the caitta basis for compassion, which the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* calls "the grandmother of all buddhas." Nominal existence means only that they take other factors as their substance, not that they are dispensable in practice.

### 5.4 ⚠️ Cross-Disciplinary Boundaries

All five cross-disciplinary comparisons are marked ⚠️ heuristic analogy. Psychological concepts operate at the level of observable behavior and physiological markers; wholesome mental factors operate at the level of the functional states of consciousness (*caitta*). The explanatory frameworks, epistemological premises, and goals differ fundamentally.

### 5.5 ❌ Wholesome Factors = Automatically Present After Transformation

Common misreading: since wholesome factors flow naturally after *āśraya-parāvṛtti*, deliberate cultivation before transformation is unnecessary.

Correction: Transformation is not a causeless miracle but the inevitable result of causes and conditions. Every moment of deliberate wholesome cultivation before transformation plants the very seeds that enable natural flow afterward. Expecting the fruit while skipping the cause is precisely the "denial of causality" that the non-delusion mental factor is designed to counter.

---

## 6. Conclusion

The eleven wholesome mental factors, from faith to non-harming, constitute a complete fuel system for practice.

Faith is the spark — the *Avataṃsaka* calls it "the origin of the path, mother of all merit," the Āgamas call it "firmly rooted, indestructible," and the *Cheng Weishi Lun* identifies its essence as "mental purity." Its three aspects (reality, merit, capacity) are mutually indispensable.

Conscience and shame are the moat — the dual defense of "self-and-dharma power" and "worldly power." Their listing among the seven powers shows they are not merely moral emotions but forces of practice.

The three wholesome roots are the core engine — the "wholesome aggregate" of the Āgamas, directly countering the three poisons. They constitute the triple ground-color of wholesome mind: clarity (non-delusion), letting-go (non-attachment), non-hostility (non-aversion).

Vigor is sustaining force — "courage and fortitude," exclusively wholesome, from the four right efforts of self-benefit to the ten vigorous efforts of other-benefit.

Pliancy is the meditation foundation — the only wholesome factor that does not pervade all wholesome mind, requiring meditative absorption. The *Saṃdhinirmocana* identifies it as the threshold of śamatha.

Non-negligence and equanimity are the Middle Way stabilizers — nominal in substance (taking vigor and the three roots as their basis), genuine in function: the former removes defilement, the latter establishes quiescence.

Non-harming is the caitta basis for compassion — "Non-aversion bestows happiness; non-harming removes suffering." The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* calls great compassion "the mother of prajñā, the grandmother of all buddhas."

The vehicle metaphor continues: P07's universals are the engine, the object-specifics the steering wheel. This paper's wholesome factors are the fuel — not one fuel but a precision-engineered compound. Next, we enter the fourth, fifth, and sixth categories of mental factors — the six fundamental afflictions and four indeterminate factors (S3-P09) — exploring the engine of saṃsāra: if wholesome factors are the fuel driving toward liberation, afflictive factors are the contaminants and pollutants. The transition from wholesome to afflictive will reveal why the same vehicle can be driven by two entirely opposite forces.
