## Abstract 摘要

The S8 series will write about the *Avataṃsaka-sūtra*, but the sūtra's own epistemological declaration is that it was not spoken to humans. In the second week after his awakening, the Buddha taught the *Avataṃsaka* from within the ocean-seal samādhi (*sāgara-mudrā-samādhi*). The audience was great bodhisattvas and devas, not human beings. He then "stepped back" to the Deer Park to teach the four noble truths to the five bhikṣus, and this stepping-back is the entry point of S8.

This paper is the methodological declaration for the entire S8 series. It establishes four postures, all of which converge upon a single doctrinal anchor.

The first posture is the *āgama leakage method*. The earlier seven series of the Pointing-at-the-Moon project worked with an "āgama seed → Mahāyāna unfolding" model in which early canonical passages provide the seeds that later Mahāyāna texts dramatize and expand. S8 cannot use this model, because the *Avataṃsaka*'s own narrative reverses the temporal order: it claims to have been spoken first, before the Buddha "stepped back" to teach the āgamas. The leakage method therefore reverses the seed model: passages in the āgamas where the Buddha explicitly refuses to answer metaphysical questions, declares objects unthinkable (*acintya*), describes vast cosmic scales, or converses with non-human audiences are not "small early versions of later doctrines" but *leakage points*—weak signals on the human channel of the same broadcast that the *Avataṃsaka* delivers at full strength on the dharmadhātu channel. The side-effect of this method is the structural dissolution of the latent "the *Avataṃsaka* is not spoken by the Buddha" anxiety, without the project having to enter that historical-critical debate directly.

The second posture is the *ten mystery gates as writing posture*. Each of papers P01 through P10 takes one of Fazang's ten mystery gates as its mode of structurally failing toward the unspeakable object that the *Avataṃsaka* points to. Each gate fails in a different dimension; ten failures together triangulate what cannot be directly written. Form is content—just as Fazang built ten windows for readers to triangulate the dharmadhātu, S8 builds ten papers, each a window, for readers to triangulate the *Avataṃsaka* itself.

The third posture is the *failure point* convention. Every paper must mark explicitly where language stops. This is not rhetorical humility but structural honesty: a three-dimensional pen cannot draw a ten-dimensional object, and the most a writer can do is mark precisely where the pen is laid down so the reader knows that beyond this line they must rely on their own dharma-eye.

The fourth posture is the radicalization of the "scripture-first, treatise-second" principle from citation order to structural order. Whereas S1 through S7 applied this principle only to the order of quotations within a paper's section two, S8 applies it to the ordering of the series itself. Papers P01 through P10 follow the sequence of Samantabhadra's ten great vows—the order spoken by the Buddha himself in the closing fascicle of the forty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* (T0293)—rather than the order of the ten mystery gates, which is the patriarchs' expository order and was repeatedly rearranged by Zhiyan, Fazang, and Chengguan. When a sequence the Buddha himself spoke meets a sequence the patriarchs constructed for teaching purposes, the former takes precedence.

The four postures are not four separate things but four faces of one thing. The single thing they all converge upon is the *simultaneously sufficient correspondence gate* (*tóngshí jùzú xiāngyìng mén*), the first of the ten mystery gates. The pairing of this paper with this gate is not the present author's invention. In *Wǔjiào zhāng* fascicle four, Fazang himself states that the simultaneously sufficient correspondence gate "becomes possible through the power and function of the ocean-seal samādhi of the dharma-nature of true virtue in dependent arising"—and this is the *only* one of the ten gates that Fazang explicitly grounds in the ocean-seal samādhi. The triple chain (homage to all buddhas → simultaneous correspondence → ocean-seal samādhi) was therefore built by Fazang himself; the present author is merely reading it back out.

The doctrinal anchor for the entire series is Fazang's sixteen-character definition of the ocean-seal samādhi from his *Wàngjìn huányuán guān* (T1876): "The ocean-seal is the original awakening of suchness. When delusion ceases and the mind clears, all images appear simultaneously."

S8 系列要寫的是華嚴經，但華嚴經自身的認識論宣言是：這部經不是對人說的。佛陀成道後第二個七日，於海印三昧中宣說華嚴，聽眾是諸大菩薩與天人，不是人類；隨後佛陀「退」到鹿野苑為五比丘講四聖諦，這個「退」字就是 S8 的入口。本文是 S8 的方法論宣言篇，承擔四件事：第一，提出阿含滲漏法（leakage method）——不再使用 S1–S7 的「阿含種子→大乘開展」模型，而是反向論證——阿含中佛陀明說不答的形上問題、明說不可思議的對象、與天人形上聽眾的對話、與梵天勸請的敘事，這些段落是「同一個無形廣播在人類頻道上的滲漏點」，滲漏自動化解「華嚴非佛說」的潛在質疑。第二，宣告十玄門作為 S8 全系列的寫作姿態，每篇論文以一扇玄門承擔「以特定方式失敗以指向不可說對象」的工作，形式即內容。第三，建立止語點（failure point）制度，每篇必標「語言到此為止」的位置——指月在 S8 不是修辭口號，是字面實踐。第四，把「經先論後」原則從引文層推到結構層：S8 全系列 P01–P10 對應普賢十大願王（佛親說次第），而非十玄門（祖師論述次第），這是指月對華嚴學的一個原創方法論貢獻。本文以法藏《修華嚴奧旨妄盡還源觀》「真如本覺也，妄盡心澄，萬象齊現」十六字為核心錨點，論證四條宣言皆收口於「同時具足相應門」——這條配對由法藏《華嚴五教章》卷四「同時具足相應門依海印三昧力用」自我建立，不是指月的硬配。

## 1. Introduction: The Word "Stepped Back"

What did the Buddha do after his awakening?

The āgamas and the *Avataṃsaka-sūtra* give different answers.

The āgama version is familiar to every student of early Buddhism. The Buddha awakened beneath the bodhi tree, spent some weeks walking and contemplating in the surrounding area, then accepted Brahmā's petition and went to the Deer Park at Sarnath to turn the wheel of the dharma for his five former companions. The teaching he gave them was the four noble truths: suffering, its origin, its cessation, and the path to its cessation. This is the standard narrative for most Buddhist traditions, and the foundation of the entire śrāvaka path.

The *Avataṃsaka* version is different. The Buddha awakened beneath the bodhi tree and immediately entered the ocean-seal samādhi. From within that samādhi, he taught a complete round-teaching scripture—the *Avataṃsaka*—to an audience of dharma-body bodhisattvas, great bodhisattvas, and the eightfold assembly of nāgas, devas, and other non-human beings. The audience was not human. The location, while geographically situated at the bodhi tree on Earth, was experientially the lotus-store world ocean (*kusumatala-garbha-vyūha-alaṃkāra-loka-dhātu-samudra*)—the dharmadhātu in its full holographic display. Then—and this is the crucial *then*—the Buddha "stepped back" to the Deer Park, set aside the *Avataṃsaka*, and spoke instead the four noble truths in a register human concept-mind could receive.

This "stepped back" is the entry point of S8.

To step back is not to fail. It is a structural judgment: the audience just addressed cannot receive the teaching just delivered. Not because the audience is insufficiently intelligent, but because there is an order-of-magnitude gap between the bandwidth of human concept-mind and the structure of what the *Avataṃsaka* points to. The Buddha therefore lowered the signal strength, transposed the same reality into a register that conceptual cognition could decode—the four noble truths—and broadcast it again.

What S8 sets out to write is the part that was stepped back from.

This is a structural difficulty. The present paper is academic prose, written by human concept-mind for human concept-mind, and it claims that the object of the *Avataṃsaka* exceeds the capacity of human concept-mind. If this claim is true, then every word I write violates the claim. If this claim is false, then S8 has no reason to exist.

This is the structural *gōng'àn* of S8. The present paper does not attempt to dissolve the *gōng'àn*; it attempts to bear it openly through four methodological declarations. These declarations do not bypass the difficulty—they mark precisely where the difficulty lies, why the writing proceeds as it does, and where the writing must stop.

### Mystery Gate Posture Declaration

The series consists of twelve papers. Of these, P01 through P10 each take one of the ten mystery gates from Fazang's *Wǔjiào zhāng* as a *writing posture*.[^1] Form is content. Just as the *Avataṃsaka* uses ten windows so that readers may triangulate an object that cannot be directly described, S8 will use the same method to write about the *Avataṃsaka* itself. Each paper announces in section one which gate it takes as its posture, and the reader thereby knows which mode of failure that paper will inhabit and which dimension it will use to align with the indescribable object.

This paper takes the *simultaneous mutual correspondence gate* (*tóngshí jùzú xiāngyìng mén*) as its posture.

This pairing is not arbitrary. In the fourth fascicle of the *Huayan yīchéng jiàoyì fēnqí zhāng* (*Treatise on the Distinguishing of the Doctrines of the One Vehicle of Huayan*), Fazang writes:

> Now, on releasing the meaning of the first gate, the simultaneous mutual correspondence gate: ... Why is this so? It is because of the power and function of the ocean-seal samādhi of the dharma-nature of true virtue in dependent arising. It is not something attained through the cultivation of provisional conditions, and that is why it is *simultaneous*.[^2]

Of the ten mystery gates, *only this one* is explicitly grounded by Fazang in the ocean-seal samādhi. When this paper locks together its theme (ocean-seal samādhi), its mystery-gate posture (simultaneous mutual correspondence), and its corresponding Samantabhadra vow (homage to all buddhas), it is not constructing a forced association. Fazang himself built the chain. The present paper is reading it out.

The literal sense of the simultaneous mutual correspondence gate is this: all dharma-gates, all doctrinal meanings, all causes and effects, are present and mutually corresponding in the same instant, without before or after. This is the posture this paper requires, because the four methodological declarations to follow are not a linear progression in which the second depends on the first and the third depends on the second. They are simultaneous, mutually supporting, mutually requiring. By the end of the paper, the reader should feel that they are not four things but four faces of one thing.

## 2. Scriptural and Treatise Sources

Following the S8 methodology, this section presents three layers of sources: Layer A consists of *āgama leakage points*—formless metaphysical residues left in the early canonical scriptures; Layer B presents the two principal *Avataṃsaka* anchors for the ocean-seal samādhi; Layer C presents the core commentarial passages from the Huayan patriarchs.

### A. Four Āgama Leakage Points

#### A.1 The Fourteen Unanswered Questions: "Not Unknowing, but Withheld"

The *Cūḷamāluṅkya-sutta* parallel preserved as the 221st sūtra of the *Madhyamāgama* (T0026, fascicle 60) records the bhikṣu Māluṅkyāputta sitting in meditative seclusion and listing fourteen metaphysical questions: whether the world is eternal, whether the world is not eternal, whether the world is finite, whether the world is infinite, whether the soul is identical with the body, whether the soul is different from the body, whether the *Tathāgata* exists after death, does not exist after death, both exists and does not exist, neither exists nor does not exist. He resolves that if the Buddha will not give him definitive answers to these questions, he will abandon the holy life.

When Māluṅkyāputta brings this resolution to the Buddha, the Buddha rebukes him directly and then delivers what may be the most famous parable in early Buddhism—the *parable of the poisoned arrow*: a man struck by a poisoned arrow refuses to let the surgeon remove it until he knows the name and clan of the archer, the colour of the archer's skin, the type of bow used, the species of feathers fletching the arrow, and so on. He dies before his questions are answered.[^3]

The parable is conventionally read as a *pragmatic prioritization*—metaphysical questions are unimportant; the urgent question is the cessation of suffering. This reading is not wrong, but it is not deep enough. In the passage immediately following the parable, the Buddha makes a more specific declaration:

> "I do not declare in one direction whether the world is eternal. Why do I not declare it in one direction? Because this is not connected with meaning, not connected with the Dharma, not the basis of the holy life; it does not lead to wisdom, does not lead to awakening, does not lead to nirvāṇa. That is why I do not declare it in one direction. ... What do I declare in one direction? I declare in one direction this meaning: suffering, the origin of suffering, the cessation of suffering, the path leading to the cessation of suffering."[^4]

Note the precise phrasing: "*I do not declare in one direction*." Not "I do not know," but "I choose not to declare." The reason is not ignorance but soteriological judgment—answering these questions would not move the listener toward liberation, so the Buddha withholds the answer.

This "knowing-but-withholding" structure is made even more explicit in the 962nd sūtra of the *Saṃyuktāgama* (T0099, fascicle 34). The wandering ascetic Vatsa-gotra asks the Buddha point by point whether he holds any of the fourteen views. The Buddha denies each in turn—"I do not hold such a view, do not make such a statement"—and then explains the defect of the views themselves: they are "inverted views, examined views, vacillating views, contaminated views, fettered views; they are suffering, obstruction, anguish, fever." Then comes the key declaration:

> "What the *Tathāgata* sees is finished. Vatsa, what the *Tathāgata* sees is this: he sees this noble truth of suffering, this noble truth of the origin of suffering, this noble truth of the cessation of suffering, this noble truth of the path leading to the cessation of suffering. Having known and seen thus, with respect to all views, all clingings, all births, all I-and-mine views, all conceits and tendencies to attachment, he has cut off, made still, made cool, made true—such liberation."[^5]

The phrase "*what the Tathāgata sees is finished*" (*rúlái suǒjiàn yǐ bì*) is critical. The Buddha is declaring that his own seeing is complete; it is not the case that there are some things he has not yet seen. He then closes with a fire simile: when a fire goes out, if someone asks "where did the fire go—east? west? south? north?" the question itself is malformed, because the fire did not "go" anywhere; its conditions were exhausted and it ceased. The *Tathāgata* with respect to the five aggregates has likewise exhausted them, "deep, vast, immeasurable, innumerable, perfectly extinguished." If you ask whether the *Tathāgata* exists after death, the very premise of the question—that the five aggregates are still serviceable referents—has collapsed.

The deepest version of this argument appears in the 905th sūtra of the *Saṃyuktāgama* (T0099, fascicle 32), in a dialogue between Mahākāśyapa and Śāriputra. When non-Buddhist ascetics challenge Śāriputra for always answering "this is undeclared" to questions about the *Tathāgata* after death—and mock him for being "like an ignorant child, without inherent wisdom"—Mahākāśyapa explains the true reason:

> "If one says the *Tathāgata* has rebirth after death, this is form. If one says the *Tathāgata* has no rebirth after death, this is form. ... The *Tathāgata*: form is exhausted, the mind is well liberated. To say there is rebirth after death is therefore not so; to say there is no rebirth after death is also not so. ... The *Tathāgata*: form is exhausted, the mind is well liberated, deep, vast, immeasurable, innumerable, perfectly extinguished, nirvāṇa."[^6]

Mahākāśyapa's argument is structural: any answer to the question "does the *Tathāgata* exist after death," whether positive, negative, both, or neither, has already presupposed the five aggregates as functioning referents. But for one whose "form is exhausted, mind well liberated," the five aggregates no longer function as referents. *It is not that the Buddha refuses to answer; it is that the question's premise has collapsed*.

Two parallel passages from the *Saṃyuktāgama* (one with Anuruddha facing wanderers, the other with Ānanda answering Kokanada) confirm the same point with a single phrase. When the wanderers ask whether the Buddha is then "unknowing, unseeing," the disciples respond:

> "The Blessed One is not unknowing, not unseeing."[^7]

Ānanda goes further: "He knows all, he sees all."

Read together, these four passages establish the āgama position on the unanswered questions with full clarity: the Buddha's silence on the fourteen metaphysical questions is *not ignorance, but withholding*; the reason for the withholding is not only soteriological prudence but also a structural recognition that the questions presuppose a frame of reference that has already collapsed in the awakened condition. This is the first leakage point: *the āgamas already concede the existence of a domain that is being deliberately under-broadcast on the human channel*. What the *Avataṃsaka* unfolds at full power is not a Mahāyāna invention; it is a domain the early canon already acknowledged but kept narrow.

#### A.2 The Four Unthinkables: A Domain Marked "Do Not Enter"

The sixth sūtra of the *Suffering and Pleasure Chapter* in the *Ekottarikāgama* (fascicle 21, T0125) records the Buddha addressing the bhikṣus directly:

> "There are four things that absolutely cannot be thought. Which four? Sentient beings cannot be thought; the world cannot be thought; the dragon-realm cannot be thought; the buddha-realm cannot be thought. And why? Because reflection on them does not lead to the cessation that is nirvāṇa."[^8]

The Buddha then expands each. *Sentient beings cannot be thought*—from where do they come, where do they go? *The world cannot be thought*—is it terminating or non-terminating, finite or infinite, who made it? *The dragon-realm cannot be thought*—rain does not come from the dragons' mouths, eyes, ears, or noses; it comes from "the intentions in the dragons' minds." *The buddha-realm cannot be thought*—the *Tathāgata*'s body, lifespan, voice, wisdom, and eloquence all exceed any human measure. The Buddha concludes:

> "Bhikṣus, these four matters cannot be thought; they are not what an ordinary person can think. Yet these four matters have no good root, do not lead to the cultivation of the holy life, do not lead to the place of rest, do not lead to nirvāṇa, and only cause people to become deluded, the mind disordered, doubts and bonds arising. ... Therefore, bhikṣus, you should think upon the four noble truths."[^9]

Note that *acintya* in this āgama context bears a *negative valence*: these things cannot be thought, you should not try to think them, the only effect of trying is mental disorder. Reflect instead on the four noble truths. This is the path.

But notice carefully which four objects the Buddha lists: sentient beings, the world, the dragon-realm, the buddha-realm. What do they have in common? *They all involve a scale-jump from individual to totality*. Sentient beings cannot be thought because the integral picture of the wheel of rebirth across all beings is non-finite. The world cannot be thought because the cosmogonic and boundary questions are non-finite. The dragon-realm cannot be thought because the causal structure between intention and physical event is non-finite. The buddha-realm cannot be thought because the magnitude of buddha-qualities exceeds the concept of magnitude. The āgamas, in other words, *concede the existence of a domain of "totality scale" that the ordinary mind cannot process*. The āgamas' posture toward this domain is "do not go there." But the domain itself is acknowledged.

The *Avataṃsaka* takes the opposite posture toward the very same word. There, *acintya* is no longer a warning but a *positive description*: the dharmadhātu is "unthinkably interfused, infinitely interpenetrated"; the bodhisattva enters "the unthinkable liberation"; the buddha-realm is praised as "unthinkable virtuous adornment." Same Sanskrit word; same domain of objects; opposite functional posture. In the āgamas it is an *epistemological warning*—do not go there. In the *Avataṃsaka* it is an *ontological praise*—reality itself is beyond thought. This semantic transposition is itself a leakage signature: the lexical space was already opened by the early canon; the *Avataṃsaka* simply enters the space the early canon had marked off.

#### A.3 The Three Knowledges: The Buddha's Cosmic Field of Vision

The Buddha's three knowledges (*tevijjā*)—knowledge of past lives (*pubbenivāsānussati-ñāṇa*), the divine eye (*dibba-cakkhu*), and the knowledge of the destruction of the cankers (*āsavakkhaya-ñāṇa*)—encompass a temporal field of vision spanning innumerable kalpas and a spatial field encompassing innumerable world systems in the ten directions.

The *Ambaṭṭha-sutta* parallel in the *Dīrgha-āgama* (T0001, fascicle 13) gives the standard formula for all three knowledges. The first knowledge (knowledge of past lives) allows the practitioner to "remember innumerable kalpas of past lives"—one life, two lives, up to "kalpas of formation, kalpas of dissolution, innumerable kalpas of formation and dissolution." The second knowledge (the divine eye) allows the practitioner to "see all sentient beings dying here, born there ... according to the karma each has done."[^10] The third knowledge is the destruction of the cankers, which actualizes liberation.

The *Ekottarikāgama*, fascicle 23, records the Buddha's first-person account of attaining the three knowledges in the night of his awakening: in the first watch of the night he attained knowledge of past lives, in the middle watch the divine eye, in the last watch the destruction of the cankers.[^11] One night, three knowledges, the entire field of past, present, and future opened simultaneously.

But the most striking passage—the one that comes closest in the early canon to the *Avataṃsaka*'s vision of the lotus-store world ocean—is found in the *Bieyi Saṃyuktāgama* (T0100, fascicle 16):

> "The rain falls densely, like a tightly bound broom; east, west, south, north, and the four intermediate directions, there is no space between. Sentient beings in innumerable worlds in the east are flourishing and at ease; innumerable worlds are completely shattered; innumerable worlds are filled with sentient beings; innumerable worlds are utterly empty, with no sentient beings dwelling within them. The southern, western, northern directions and the four intermediate directions, above and below, are all likewise. Birth and death are without beginning."[^12]

Read this passage carefully. The Buddha is saying: in the east there are innumerable worlds, and these worlds are *simultaneously in different states*—some flourishing with happy beings, some already shattered, some teeming with beings, some completely empty. South, west, north, the four intermediate directions, above, below: all exactly the same.

This is the closest the āgamas come to the *Avataṃsaka*'s "world ocean of innumerable worlds in the ten directions." Innumerable worlds, each in different stages of arising, abiding, decay, and emptiness, all coexisting at once—this is the prototype of the *Avataṃsaka*'s lotus-store world ocean. The difference is only that the āgama uses this image to make a soteriological point ("birth and death are without beginning"—you cannot find a temporal starting point), while the *Avataṃsaka* unfolds the same spatial structure as the panoramic field of vision of buddha-wisdom.

A short verse in the *Mahāpadāna-sutta* parallel from the *Dīrgha-āgama* (T0001, fascicle 1) goes even further, prefiguring the cosmic Buddha of the *Avataṃsaka* in a single line:

> "The bhikṣus are gathered in the dharma hall, expounding the discussion of the noble ones. The *Tathāgata* dwells in the silent chamber; with the divine ear he hears them all. The Buddha-sun shines universally, distinguishing the meanings of the dharmadhātu; he also knows past matters, the parinirvāṇa of the three buddhas."[^13]

"The Buddha-sun shines universally, distinguishing the meanings of the dharmadhātu." The Sanskrit name *Vairocana* literally means "universally shining"—the cosmic Buddha of the *Avataṃsaka* whose body is the lotus-store world ocean and whose light pervades all dharmadhātus. When the *Dīrgha-āgama* describes the Buddha's field of awareness with this single line, it has already given the complete definition of Vairocana. The *Avataṃsaka* simply unfolds these eight characters into an entire scripture.

#### A.4 Brahmā's Petition: The Original Audience of the Deep Dharma Is Not Human

The strongest piece of evidence for the leakage argument is the narrative structure of Brahmā's petition.

The *Petition Chapter* (*Encouraging-the-Request Chapter*) of the *Ekottarikāgama* (fascicle 10, T0125) records the Buddha's interior monologue shortly after his awakening:

> "The deep dharma I have now realized is difficult to fathom, difficult to understand, difficult to know, beyond conception, subtle, and to be known only by the wise. ... Were I to speak this subtle dharma to humans, humans would not believe and would not practice it. The labour would be in vain, and there would be loss. Better that I remain silent—why should I speak?"[^14]

Note the Buddha's precise wording: "*Were I to speak this subtle dharma* to humans, humans would not believe." The hesitation is not a general hesitation; it is specifically a hesitation about speaking *to humans*. The obstruction to the deep dharma is the human listener.

Then comes Brahmā's intervention:

> "At that time, the king of the Brahmā heaven, in his Brahmā heaven, perceiving from afar what the *Tathāgata* was thinking—just as a strong man bends or extends his arm—vanished from the Brahmā heaven and arrived before the World-Honoured One. He bowed his head at the Buddha's feet and stood to one side."[^15]

Brahmā "perceived from afar what the *Tathāgata* was thinking." This is a crucial detail. The non-human being does not need the Buddha to speak; he reads the Buddha's mind directly. The deep dharma, before being heard by any human, has already been received in the heavens.

The *Mahāpadāna-sutta* parallel in the *Dīrgha-āgama* gives an even more complete version of the same scene, applied to the prior buddha Vipaśyin. Vipaśyin says explicitly to Brahmā:

> "The right dharma I have attained is profound, subtle, and difficult; if I were to speak it to them, they would certainly not understand, and would only generate more disturbance. That is why I have remained silent and do not wish to speak the dharma. Across innumerable incalculable kalpas I have practiced unsurpassed conduct without weariness, and only now have I attained this hard-to-attain dharma. If I were to speak it to beings of greed, anger, and delusion, they would certainly not be able to receive and apply it, and the labour would be wasted in vain. *This dharma is subtle, contrary to the world*; sentient beings, dyed by craving, covered by ignorance, are unable to believe and understand."[^16]

"*This dharma is contrary to the world*"—the deep dharma is not merely different from worldly understanding but structurally opposed to it. The Buddha is petitioned three times by Brahmā before finally agreeing, and even then his agreement comes with an explicit condition:

> "Out of compassion for you, I will now open the gate of the ambrosial dharma. This dharma is subtle, hard to understand. *I shall speak it for those who, with faith, delight to listen; I shall not speak it for those who would only generate disturbance.*"[^17]

The Buddha is openly filtering the audience. The deep dharma is not for everyone; it is for those who can receive it.

A still deeper piece of evidence is found in the 1188th sūtra of the *Saṃyuktāgama* (T0099, fascicle 44). Shortly after his awakening, the Buddha sits alone in meditation and considers whether there exists any being—"any deva, māra, brahmā, śramaṇa, brāhmaṇa, divine being, or human"—whom he might revere as superior to himself in virtue, concentration, wisdom, liberation, or the knowledge of liberation. His conclusion is: *no*. There is no such being. He then resolves to revere only the dharma itself. At that moment, the king of the Brahmā heaven of the Sahā world perceives the Buddha's thought and instantly descends to confirm the Buddha's judgment.[^18]

Read together, these passages make Brahmā's petition do three things at once. First, the Buddha's hesitation about speaking to humans is not modesty but a sober assessment of human cognitive capacity. Second, the deep dharma already has non-human "first listeners" before any human ever hears a word. Third, the Buddha's eventual agreement to speak to humans is conditional upon a *down-conversion*—choosing a receivable version for receivable listeners. Together these three points amount to a single declaration: *the original audience of the deep dharma is structurally not human*.

The point is reinforced by an even earlier moment. The *Ekottarikāgama* (fascicle 14) records the Buddha's very first thought after awakening: "To whom should I speak the dharma first?" His first answer was *not* a bhikṣu but the meditation teacher Ālāra Kālāma. Then "a deva in the sky said to the World-Honoured One: 'Ālāra Kālāma died seven days ago.'"[^19] The Buddha's first interlocutors after awakening were *devas*, not humans, and his first plan to teach was nullified by a deva's report.

Putting all four leakage points together—the unanswered questions as the explicit declaration of "knowing-but-withholding," the four unthinkables as the acknowledged domain of non-conceptualizable objects, the three knowledges as the demonstration of the Buddha's cosmic field of vision, and Brahmā's petition as the narrative evidence that the original audience of the deep dharma is non-human—we arrive at a single conclusion: *the āgamas have already conceded a domain that is not being broadcast at full strength on the human channel*. What the *Avataṃsaka* claims as its content is not a Mahāyāna invention but the same domain unfolded on a different channel.

The word "leakage" needs to be understood precisely. It is not a "seed"—a seed implies temporal development, a small early version that grows into a later larger version. Leakage is not that. Leakage is the *bleed-through of the same signal onto a different channel*—the signal was always there, but on this channel it was deliberately turned down so that only weak traces remain. The work of the A-layer is to mark these leakage points precisely, not to demonstrate that "later doctrines have early seeds that can be traced backward."

The methodological transposition has an important side effect: *it dissolves the latent "the* Avataṃsaka *is not buddha-vacana" objection without arguing the point directly*. The traditional version of that objection runs: (1) the *Avataṃsaka*'s vision of the dharmadhātu exceeds what is explicitly taught in the āgamas; (2) anything that exceeds the āgamas can only be a later Mahāyāna expansion; (3) therefore the *Avataṃsaka* is not the historical Buddha's word. The leakage method's response is not to attack the conclusion but to undercut premise (1). If the āgamas already contain four classes of explicit leakage points acknowledging the existence of a non-conceptual totality-scale domain, then the *Avataṃsaka* does not "exceed" the āgamas; it enters the very domain the āgamas had already marked off and left blank. This response does not require resolving any historical-critical question. It works at the level of the texts themselves, showing that the two scriptures point at the same object on different channels with different signal strengths.

### B. The Two Principal *Avataṃsaka* Anchors

#### B.1 The *Xianshou Pin* Verse: The *Locus Classicus*

The single most important *Avataṃsaka* source for the term "ocean-seal samādhi" is a verse in the *Xianshou Pin* (Chapter on Worthy First) of the *Avataṃsaka* (T0279, fascicle 14):

> *Sometimes manifesting the path of śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas,*
> *Sometimes manifesting the universally adorned attainment of buddhahood,*
> *Thus opening up the teachings of the three vehicles,*
> *Universally delivering sentient beings throughout innumerable kalpas.*
> *Sometimes manifesting the form of boys and girls,*
> *Devas, nāgas, and asuras,*
> *And even mahoragas,*
> *All according to what they delight to see.*
> *Sentient beings' shapes are each different,*
> *Their actions and voices likewise innumerable;*
> *All of these can be made to manifest—*
> *Through the awesome power of the ocean-seal samādhi.*[^20]

The decisive line is the closing couplet: "All of these can be made to manifest—through the awesome power of the ocean-seal samādhi." The corresponding passage in the sixty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* (T0278, fascicle 6) reads "all manifestations without exception, through the strength of the ocean-seal samādhi"[^21]—a slight verbal variation on the same point.

Notice the structure of the verse. It first lists the variety of forms in which the Buddha can manifest: śrāvakas, pratyekabuddhas, buddhas, boys and girls, devas, nāgas, asuras, mahoragas. Then it states the condition: "*Sentient beings' shapes are each different, their actions and voices likewise innumerable*"—however many kinds of beings there are, the Buddha manifests as that many. Then the closing summary: "*All of these can be made to manifest—through the awesome power of the ocean-seal samādhi*."

That is to say, the ocean-seal samādhi is not, in the first place, a particular meditative state. It is, in the first place, *a structural capacity for simultaneous manifestation*. The forms, actions, and voices of all beings are simultaneously present in the ocean-seal samādhi and simultaneously responded to. It is not that the Buddha sees being A, then sees being B, then sees being C; A, B, and C are *imprinted in the same instant* of the ocean-seal samādhi and responded to in the same instant. This is the doctrinal foundation of "simultaneous sufficiency."

#### B.2 The *Puxian Xing Pin* Verse: The Metaphorical Anchor for the Ontology

The second principal anchor is in the *Puxian Xing Pin* (Chapter on the Practices of Samantabhadra), T0279, fascicle 52:

> *The fully awakened one knows all dharmas,*
> *Non-dual, free from duality, all equal,*
> *Self-nature pure as space,*
> *Self and non-self not distinguished.*
> *As the ocean-seal manifests the bodies of sentient beings,*
> *And on this account is called the great ocean—*
> *Bodhi universally seals all mental activities,*
> *And on this account is called perfect awakening.*[^22]

This verse provides the metaphorical foundation of the ocean-seal samādhi. *As the ocean reflects the forms of all beings, it is therefore called "ocean"*; *as bodhi universally seals the mental activities of all beings, it is therefore called "perfect awakening."*

The direction of the analogy is crucial. *The ocean is not called "ocean" because it has water; it is called "ocean" because of its capacity to reflect everything*. The essential definition of the ocean is not its physical composition but its *reflective capacity*. Likewise, the essential definition of bodhi is not some inner psychological state but its *capacity to seal all mental activities universally*. Perfect awakening is "perfect awakening" not because the awakened one possesses some special experience, but because bodhi has the structure of *simultaneous imprint of all*.

For S8 as a whole, this verse provides a critically important doctrinal anchor: *the essence of the dharmadhātu is not "some thing" but "the structure of being able to imprint all things at once."* When the later papers in S8 discuss the four dharmadhātus, the ten mystery gates, the six characteristics, and *xìngqǐ* (nature-arising), they will return again and again to this metaphor—the dharmadhātu is not an object, it is a structure.

### C. Patriarchal Commentary

#### C.1 Fazang's *Wàngjìn huányuán guān*: The Core Definition

The Huayan school's most concentrated definition of the ocean-seal samādhi comes from Fazang's *Wàngjìn huányuán guān* (*The Cessation of Delusion and the Return to the Source of the Profound Meaning of the Avataṃsaka*, T1876):

> "First, the ever-abiding function of the ocean-seal as forest of phenomena. To say 'ocean-seal' is to say *the original awakening of suchness*. *When delusion is exhausted and the mind clears, all images appear simultaneously*. It is like the great ocean, which raises waves in response to the wind: when the wind ceases, the ocean water becomes clear, and there is no image it does not manifest. As the *Awakening of Faith* says: 'the immeasurable treasury of merit, the suchness ocean of dharma-nature.' That is why it is called the ocean-seal samādhi."[^23]

Read this passage word by word.

"*The ocean-seal is the original awakening of suchness*"—the ocean-seal is not some advanced meditative state; it is the awakened nature of suchness itself.

"*When delusion is exhausted, the mind clears*"—this is verbal, not static. "Delusion is exhausted" is a process (deluded thoughts are cut off); "the mind clears" is the result (the water of the mind settles into clarity).

"*All images appear simultaneously*"—every phenomenon appears at once. "*Simultaneously*" is the key word. Not in sequence, all at once.

"*Like the great ocean, which raises waves in response to the wind: when the wind ceases, the ocean water becomes clear, and there is no image it does not manifest*"—the analogy. When the great ocean is whipped up by wind, its surface is in motion and cannot reflect external objects. When the wind stops, the water becomes clear, and the images of external objects appear simultaneously on its surface. Deluded thought is the wind. The water of the mind is the ocean. The images of external objects are "all images."

These four lines—"the ocean-seal is the original awakening of suchness; when delusion is exhausted and the mind clears, all images appear simultaneously"—are the doctrinal anchor for the entire S8 series. The four methodological declarations developed in section 3 below all converge upon these four lines. Later papers, particularly P07 (the dedicated paper on the ocean-seal samādhi), will return to this definition repeatedly.

#### C.2 Fazang's *Wǔjiào zhāng*, Fascicle 4: The Internal Textual Evidence

When Fazang opens the ten mystery gates in fascicle 4 of the *Huayan yīchéng jiàoyì fēnqí zhāng* (T1866), he explicitly grounds the first gate, the simultaneously sufficient correspondence gate, in the ocean-seal samādhi:

> "Now, on releasing the meaning of the first gate, the simultaneously sufficient correspondence gate: it makes manifest the ten gates of teaching, doctrine, principle, phenomena, and so on. Simultaneously corresponding, they form a single dependent arising, with no distinction of before, after, beginning, or end. They are sufficient in all things, free in opposing and following, mixed but not confused, forming the boundary of dependent arising. *Why is it possible for them to be like this? It is because of the power and function of the ocean-seal samādhi of the dharma-nature of true virtue in dependent arising. It is not something attained through the cultivation of provisional conditions, and that is why it is simultaneous*."[^24]

Of the ten mystery gates, this is the *only* one Fazang explicitly grounds in the ocean-seal samādhi. The implication for S8-P01 is decisive. When this paper locks together its theme (ocean-seal samādhi), its mystery-gate posture (simultaneously sufficient correspondence), and its corresponding Samantabhadra vow (paying homage to all buddhas), it is not constructing a forced association. Fazang himself has built the chain at one end—the ocean-seal samādhi is the basis of the simultaneously sufficient correspondence gate. The other end—that the simultaneously sufficient correspondence gate corresponds to paying homage to all buddhas—will be argued in section 3.4 below.

#### C.3 Chengguan's *Huayan jing shu*: The Samādhi That Supports the Speaking

In fascicle 1 of his *Huayan jing shu* (T1735), the fourth Huayan patriarch Chengguan explicitly identifies the ocean-seal samādhi as the samādhi from within which the *Avataṃsaka* was spoken:

> "Now, on what samādhi does the speaking of this scripture depend? Upon the ocean-seal samādhi. 'Ocean-seal' is a metaphor; it receives its name from the metaphor. The detailed exposition will be given in the commentary on the *Xianshou Pin*. Here a brief description: the fragrant ocean is calm, settled, deep, and unmoving; the bodily forms of the four continents all bear imprints within it, as a seal imprints an object. Or again: like calm waves stretching ten thousand leagues under a cloudless sky, the constellations and stars and moon manifest themselves all at once, neither coming nor going, neither existing nor non-existing, neither one nor different. In the wisdom-ocean of the *Tathāgata*, the waves of the *vijñāna* do not arise; it is settled, clear, and at the limit of brightness."[^25]

Chengguan's phrase "*the wisdom-ocean of the* Tathāgata*; the waves of the* vijñāna *do not arise*" is his distinctive contribution. He explicitly identifies the "ocean" of "ocean-seal" with the *wisdom-ocean of the* Tathāgata, and the condition for the "imprint" with "the waves of *vijñāna* do not arise." This reading will be unfolded in detail in P07 (the dedicated paper on the ocean-seal samādhi).

This concludes section 2. The relationship among the three layers is: layer A demonstrates that the āgamas have already reserved lexical and ontological space for an ocean-seal-style "simultaneous imprint" capacity; layer B provides two direct definitions of the ocean-seal samādhi from the *Avataṃsaka* itself; layer C provides the core Huayan-school commentary on the ocean-seal samādhi. Section 3 below now enters the central work of this paper—unfolding the four methodological declarations and demonstrating how they converge upon the simultaneously sufficient structure of the ocean-seal samādhi.

## 3. Core Argument: Four Methodological Declarations

The S8 series does not adopt the "three scholarly theses" structure used in S1 through S7, because the core propositions of the *Avataṃsaka* (dharmadhātu dependent arising, one-is-all, the unobstructed interpenetration of phenomena) are, by the *Avataṃsaka*'s own declaration, beyond what conceptual mind can express. Any paper that tried to "prove" these propositions would, on its first page, already be in violation of the *Avataṃsaka*'s own epistemological posture. So S8 does not open with a list of theses but with four methodological declarations—declarations that tell the reader how each paper in the series is to be responsible, in three dimensions, to a ten-dimensional object.

### 3.1 Declaration One: The Āgama Leakage Method (Not the Seed Method)

The methodology of the S7 series is built on a two-layer model of "āgama seeds → Lotus dramatic unfolding." Early āgama passages provide the seeds, and the *Lotus Sūtra* unfolds them dramatically. S7-P02 used this method to argue for the connection between the "one great matter" passage and *Saṃyuktāgama* 299's "the dharma-realm is permanently abiding"; S7-P03 used it to argue for the connection between the *Lotus*'s "burning house" and *Saṃyuktāgama* 197's "everything is on fire." The entire argumentative spine of S7 depends on this two-layer structure.

S8 cannot continue to use this method. The reason is that the *Avataṃsaka*'s own epistemological declaration runs in the opposite direction.

Notice the temporality implicit in the S7 "seed → unfolding" model: early scriptures provide seeds (small, simple, undeveloped); later scriptures provide the unfolding (large, complex, dramatized). This temporality is appropriate in S7—the *Lotus Sūtra* is indeed a later scripture that does indeed dramatically expand earlier doctrines. But the *Avataṃsaka*'s own temporal claim is the reverse: the *Avataṃsaka* claims to have been spoken *first*, after the awakening, before the Buddha "stepped back" to the Deer Park to speak the āgamas. If S8 were to continue using the "āgama seed → *Avataṃsaka* unfolding" two-layer structure, it would be denying the *Avataṃsaka*'s own temporal sequence and its declaration about its audience.

A deeper problem: the seed model implies "later growth," that what is later was not earlier. If the *Avataṃsaka*'s vision of the dharmadhātu were merely a "later growth from āgama seeds," then the *Avataṃsaka* would be only a Mahāyāna expansion, and the latent objection of "the *Avataṃsaka* is not buddha-vacana" would have no structural answer.

S8's section 2 must therefore reverse the two-layer structure into:

> **A1 āgama leakage point → A2 *Avataṃsaka* manifestation**

The passages in the āgamas where the Buddha explicitly refuses to answer (the unanswered questions), explicitly declares things unthinkable (*acintya*), describes vast cosmic scales (the three knowledges, the innumerable worlds in the ten directions), and converses with non-human listeners (Brahmā's petition, the structure of the first audience)—these are *leakage points of the formless broadcast onto the human channel*. Leakage is not a seed (which implies later growth); leakage is the *bleed-through of the same signal onto a different channel*. The signal was always there; on this channel it was deliberately turned down so that only weak traces remain.

The existence of leakage points automatically demonstrates: the dharmadhātu the *Avataṃsaka* describes was never a Mahāyāna fabrication, but a domain that was always present in the Buddha's early teaching and that the Buddha deliberately under-broadcast. When we read the Buddha declaring in the parable of the poisoned arrow "I do not declare this in one direction," that posture is itself leakage—it is telling us "there is something that *could* be said but I am choosing not to say all of it." When we read the *Ekottarikāgama*'s "sentient beings are unthinkable, the world is unthinkable, the dragon-realm is unthinkable, the buddha-realm is unthinkable," the very existence of these four categories is leakage—it is telling us "there are four dimensions of objects that exceed the processing capacity of ordinary mind." When we read the *Bieyi Saṃyuktāgama*'s "in the east, innumerable worlds are flourishing, innumerable worlds are shattered," that ten-direction multi-world image is leakage—it is telling us "the Buddha's field of vision contains a panorama we cannot see." When we read Brahmā petitioning the Buddha to speak the dharma, that narrative structure is itself leakage—it is telling us "the original audience of the deep dharma is not in this room."

Four leakage points are not isolated exceptions; they are four faces of the same structural feature.

This methodological transposition has a technical consequence: the structure of S8's section 2 must differ from S7's. S7's section 2 progresses "A āgama seeds → B Mahāyāna unfolding → C patriarchal commentary," three layers building forward in time. S8's section 2 is "A āgama leakage (subdivided into four points) → B *Avataṃsaka* manifestation → C patriarchal commentary"—but the relationship between A and B is not "earlier small version becoming later large version." It is "two channels broadcasting the same object at different signal strengths." Layer B is not "what the seed grew into"; it is "the same object being broadcast at full power on the dharmadhātu channel." The temporal direction is dissolved; the channel direction is established.

The side effect of declaration one: it automatically dissolves the latent "the *Avataṃsaka* is not buddha-vacana" anxiety. The Pointing-at-the-Moon project does not directly enter that scholarly dispute—but its posture is structurally stable. The traditional argument can be summarized as: (1) the *Avataṃsaka*'s vision of the dharmadhātu exceeds what is explicitly taught in the āgamas; (2) anything that exceeds the āgamas must be later Mahāyāna expansion; (3) therefore the *Avataṃsaka* is not the historical Buddha's word. The leakage method's reply is in premise (1): the *Avataṃsaka*'s vision of the dharmadhātu does *not* "exceed" what is explicitly taught in the āgamas; the āgamas already contain four classes of explicit leakage points acknowledging that this domain exists. The āgama posture toward this domain is "do not go there"; "I do not declare this in one direction"; "this is not what an ordinary person can think." The *Avataṃsaka* simply enters the space the āgamas had already marked off and left blank.

This argument does not require resolving any historical-critical question about whether the *Avataṃsaka* was actually spoken by the historical Buddha at a particular time and place. It works at the level of the texts themselves, showing that the two scriptures point at the same object on different channels with different signal strengths.

### 3.2 Declaration Two: The Ten Mystery Gates as Writing Posture (Not Just Object of Study)

In the seventh century, Fazang already faced precisely the problem we face: how to use a finite human concept-mind to point at an infinitely interpenetrating dharmadhātu. His answer was not to "describe" this object, but to construct *ten windows*, each of which fails in a specific way, allowing the reader to triangulate the indescribable object from the patterns of the failures.

This is the methodological essence of the ten mystery gates.

In fascicle 4 of the *Wǔjiào zhāng*, Fazang lists the ten gates: simultaneously sufficient correspondence, unobstructed wide and narrow freedom, mutual containment of one and many in non-identity, mutual identity and self-mastery of dharmas, simultaneous accomplishment of the hidden and the manifest, free placement of the subtle and refined, the realm of Indra's net, the manifestation of the dharma through entrusting events, the differentiated formation of the ten time periods, the perfect virtue of host and guest in mutual illumination.[^26] Each gate is doing the same thing—pointing at the object of "dependent arising of the dharmadhātu, infinitely interpenetrated"—but pointing at it from a different angle. Simultaneously sufficient correspondence points from the angle of "simultaneity"; unobstructed wide and narrow freedom points from the angle of "the relativity of wide and narrow"; mutual containment of one and many points from the angle of "the relativity of one and many"; the realm of Indra's net points from the angle of "infinite mutual reflection." Each window fails in the same direction—each failure approaches the unspeakable object from a different dimension.

By the time the reader has finished reading the ten gates, the object has not been "described"—it never could be. But the reader can sense, from the trajectory of the ten failures, both the existence of the object and its rough shape. This is the core epistemological function of the ten mystery gates as a tool.

The Pointing-at-the-Moon project's extension on this foundation is: *the ten mystery gates are not only the object of study for S8; they are the writing method of S8*.

Of S8's twelve papers, P01 through P10 each take one mystery gate as a writing posture and announce it explicitly at the start of section 1. The reader thereby knows: this paper will fail in this particular way, will align with the indescribable object in this particular dimension. This paper, P01, takes the simultaneously sufficient correspondence gate as its posture, which is to say: this paper will point at the dharmadhātu through the dimension of "simultaneity," not attempting to do work in the dimensions of "wide and narrow," "one and many," or "infinite mutual reflection." Each paper bears the failure of one dimension; ten papers together constitute a ten-dimensional spectrum of failures, triangulating the unwritable object from ten directions at once.

Form is content. This is itself the bodily realization of "one is all" at the level of writing—the *Avataṃsaka* uses the ten mystery gates to teach how to point at the dharmadhātu, and we use the same method to write about the *Avataṃsaka* itself. The S8 series therefore constitutes a meta-level enactment of the ten mystery gates. Reading S8 is not simply reading papers about the ten gates; it is undergoing the ten-gate methodology firsthand.

P11 and P12 are two meta-level exceptions. P11 is on the ten mystery gates themselves as its subject, and so it is a meta-level review rather than a single-gate paper; P12 is the closing of the entire eight-series project, beyond the S8 system, and likewise does not select a gate.

The side effect of declaration two: reader-expectation management. When the reader sees that P03 takes the gate of mutual identity and self-mastery of dharmas as its posture, the reader knows that the paper will fail in the dimension of "identity"—the paper will not give a complete description of the four dharmadhātus, but will fail repeatedly in the dimension of "how phenomena identify with principle, how principle identifies with phenomena, and how phenomena identify with phenomena," leaving a trajectory of failure behind. The reader will not bring the wrong expectation of "I hope this paper will explain the four dharmadhātus clearly"; they will bring the correct expectation of "this paper will let me see how the four dharmadhātus are unspeakable in the dimension of identity."

### 3.3 Declaration Three: Every Paper Marks a Failure Point

Every paper must contain, in section 3 or section 5, an explicit "language stops here" passage. This is not rhetorical humility; it is structural honesty. The reader needs to see where we lay down the pen, why we lay it down, and what to rely on after crossing that line.

This paper, P01, sets the following passage as the methodological anchor for the entire series; later papers will return to it repeatedly:

> Every paper in this series will stop somewhere. The place where it stops is not an oversight; it is a gesture of reverence. A three-dimensional pen cannot draw a ten-dimensional object. The most one can do is mark precisely the position where the pen is laid down, so that the reader knows: beyond this line, you must rely on your own dharma-eye.

The phrase "pointing at the moon" in S8 is not a brand slogan; it is a literal practice. To point at the moon, then mark explicitly that "my pointing stops here"—the rest of the work is not on the page; it is in the reader's dharma-eye.

Marking the failure point is not an admission of "I cannot continue writing." It is the structural judgment of "by the time the paper has reached this position, words themselves are no longer the appropriate tool, and to continue writing would itself be a violation of the doctrine." A responsible scholar must stop at the failure point, because to continue is to fabricate on paper a false impression that "this object can in fact be said," and this false impression itself misleads the reader.

The relationship between the failure point and the slippage of the unspeakable must be clearly understood. If failure point is misread as "because the object is unspeakable, anything goes," the methodology collapses—it becomes an excuse for abandoning scholarly rigor. If failure point is misread as "because the object is unspeakable, the best thing is to keep silent and write nothing," the methodology also collapses—it becomes an excuse for abandoning writing. Failure point is the middle way: it requires that the paper push the work to its limit within the range where words can function, then mark precisely and stop where words can no longer function. It allows neither the licentiousness of "anything goes" nor the cowardice of "best stay silent."

This declaration distances S8 from all "mystical-style scholarship." Mystical scholarship often uses "ineffability" as a shield to escape the responsibility of scholarly demonstration—phrases like "you have to experience this for yourself" or "words are not enough, please go meditate" effectively outsource the scholarly obligation to the reader's personal experience. S8 does not adopt this posture. S8's job is to push what words *can* say to its absolute limit—to the point where any further word would mislead—and then to stop on that boundary and mark it explicitly. After reading an S8 paper, the reader should feel that "the part where words could work has been done thoroughly," not that "the author was lazy and left the hard part to me."

Failure point is also reverence. In the Huayan tradition, the highest reverence toward an unspeakable object is not the attempt to make it speakable (which is a form of grasping) but the acknowledgement that one's tools end here (which is a form of letting go). The position where the last page of the paper stops is itself an act of reverence.

### 3.4 Declaration Four: The Radicalization of "Scripture First, Treatise Second" at the Structural Level

S1 through S7 applied the "scripture first, treatise second" principle only to the order of citations within section 2. S8 pushes the principle to the structural level: *the ordering of the papers in the series itself follows the sequence the Buddha spoke, not the sequence the patriarchs constructed for exposition*.

#### Why This Is Permissible

The order of the ten mystery gates within Huayan scholarship was never fixed:

First, **Zhiyan**, the second patriarch of the Huayan school, proposed the ten mystery gates in one order.[^27]

Second, **Fazang**, the third patriarch, in his earlier *Wǔjiào zhāng*, proposed the "old ten mystery gates" in a different order, and later replaced two of these gates' contents.[^28]

Third, **Fazang** himself, in his later *Tànxuán jì*, proposed the "new ten mystery gates" in yet a third order—Fazang re-arranged his own earlier arrangement.

Fourth, **Chengguan**, the fourth patriarch, in his *Huayan jing shu chao*, explicitly states: "the first gate (simultaneously sufficient correspondence) is the total; the remaining nine are differentiated"—that is, the ten gates are not a linear sequence at all but a "one total, nine differentiated" structure.[^29]

The orders of three Huayan patriarchs are inconsistent, and Chengguan explicitly says the ten gates are not a linear structure. From within the Huayan school itself, this proves: *the order of the ten mystery gates is a flexible expository order, not a strict ritual order*. The doctrines of the gates themselves are rigorous, but the order in which they are arrayed is pedagogical and rearrangeable.

By contrast, the order of Samantabhadra's ten great vows is:

First, *spoken by the Buddha himself*—in the closing fascicle of the *Puxian xingyuan pin*, Samantabhadra proclaims the ten vows on behalf of the Buddha at the great Huayan assembly and unfolds the content and merit of each vow in turn.

Second, *fixed by scripture*—the order of the vows in T0293's closing fascicle is fixed; later editions cannot rearrange them, and to do so would be to violate the scripture.

Third, *a sequence of cultivation and realization*—the order of the ten vows is itself the sequence of cultivation: vow 1 (homage to all buddhas) establishes the dharma-vessel (the most basic bodily acceptance) → vow 2 (praising the *Tathāgatas*) transforms speech-karma (from silence to praise) → vow 3 (making offerings) extends to action-karma → vow 4 (confessing and repenting karmic obstructions) purifies prior karma → vow 5 (rejoicing in merits) transforms the jealousy of mind-karma → vow 6 (requesting the turning of the dharma-wheel) is outward protection → vow 7 (requesting the Buddha to remain in the world) is the temporal commitment → vow 8 (constantly following the Buddha in study) is the structure of dependence → vow 9 (constantly according with sentient beings) is the return into the realm of beings → vow 10 (universally transferring all merit) is the final transfer. Each vow is the natural extension of the previous one; the sequence has an irreversible practical logic.

When "an expository order rearrangeable by patriarchs" meets "an order of cultivation spoken by the Buddha himself," the latter takes precedence over the former. This is precisely the radicalization of "scripture first, treatise second" at the structural level. S1–S7 applied "scripture first, treatise second" to the order of citations within section 2; S8 applies the same principle to the order of the papers in the series itself.

#### Historical Context

The forty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* (T0293) was translated by Prajñā Tripiṭaka between the twelfth and fourteenth years of the Zhenyuan era of Tang Emperor Dezong (796–798). Fazang passed away in the first year of the Xiantian era (712). *When Fazang established the ten mystery gates, the ten great vows had not yet been translated*.[^30] Fazang could not possibly have known of the existence of the closing fascicle of the *Puxian xingyuan pin*.

If Fazang had seen the ten great vows, he might himself have rearranged the ten mystery gates to correspond with the ten vows. What the present author is now doing may well be precisely what Fazang would have wanted to do but had no opportunity to do—aligning a later-translated scriptural sequence with the patriarchal exposition. *This is precisely what Chinese Buddhism has been doing for over a millennium*: every time a new translation appears, the entire system of exposition is recalibrated. Aligning the ten vows with the ten gates is a continuation of this tradition.

#### The Original Scholarly Thesis

The thesis that "Samantabhadra's ten great vows and the ten mystery gates constitute the cultivation-realization face and the expository face of one and the same round-teaching structure, and may be placed in one-to-one correspondence" has not, so far as the present author is aware, been explicitly proposed by any Huayan patriarch. When Fazang established the ten mystery gates, the ten great vows did not yet exist; Chengguan worked extensively with the *Puxian xingyuan* but did not systematically correspond it with the ten gates; later Huayan masters (Zongmi, Ziyuan, Jingyuan, etc.) likewise do not appear to have made this argument.[^31]

S8 makes this correspondence the *structure of the series itself*, and will formally argue it in the practice-gate correspondence section of P10. This is the Pointing-at-the-Moon project's original scholarly contribution to Huayan studies. This paper only establishes the framework and does not argue the correspondence in detail.

### 3.5 The Meshing of the Four Declarations: Why They All Converge upon the Ocean-Seal Samādhi

The four declarations are not four things; they are four faces of one thing. What gathers them together is the simultaneously sufficient structure of the ocean-seal samādhi.

Return to Fazang's sixteen-character definition from the *Wàngjìn huányuán guān*: "The ocean-seal is the original awakening of suchness; when delusion is exhausted and the mind clears, all images appear simultaneously."

Declaration one (āgama leakage) says: the Buddha's "withholding" in the āgamas and the Buddha's "simultaneously sufficient speaking" in the *Avataṃsaka* are different signal strengths of the same object on two channels. Why is this possible? Because "the original awakening of suchness" is the same original awakening of suchness—pointed at in the āgamas through the mode of the śrāvaka path, and presented in the *Avataṃsaka* through the mode of the round teaching, simultaneously sufficient. "The original awakening of suchness" does not change because the speaker chooses a different audience; what changes is only the breadth of the speaker's exposition. Leakage is possible because both channels point at the same object.

Declaration two (the ten mystery gates as writing posture) says: each paper takes one gate to bear a failure. Why is this possible? Because *all images appear simultaneously*—all the gates are simultaneously established, simultaneously corresponding, simultaneously sufficient in the same instant; therefore taking any one gate as an entrance leads, in the end, to the same object. If the ten gates were a linear sequence (one had to pass the first gate before the second), then it would not be permissible to take a single gate as a posture; it is precisely because the ten gates stand in a "simultaneously sufficient" relationship that any gate is a legitimate entrance.

Declaration three (the failure point) says: the paper must stop at some position and mark the boundary of the unspeakable. Why must it stop? Because *delusion is exhausted, the mind clears*—when the paper enters the position where it would need "simultaneous imprint," the tool of words simply fails. Words are by nature linear, sequential, the "first say A, then say B, then say C" structure; whereas "all images appear simultaneously" is the structure of "A, B, C present in the same instant." Words cannot express simultaneity; they can only express sequentiality. So when the paper has reached the position of "simultaneity," it must stop—to keep using a sequential tool to describe a simultaneous object is essentially to draw a surface with a line; structurally impossible. Failure point is not failure; it is the limit position of using the tool to its end.

Declaration four (the radicalization of scripture first, treatise second at the structural level) says: the ordering of the papers must follow the sequence the Buddha spoke, not the sequence the patriarchs constructed. Why? Because the Buddha's spoken sequence is itself the concrete unfolding of "delusion is exhausted, the mind clears, all images appear simultaneously." Samantabhadra's ten great vows are not ten separate practice items but the bodily realization of one perfect round-teaching structure in ten faces—homage, praise, offering, confession, rejoicing, requesting the turning of the wheel, requesting the Buddha to remain, following the Buddha in study, according with beings, transferring all merit—each vow is a face of the same round-teaching structure, and the ten vows together are the complete display of this structure. When the order of the papers follows this sequence, the papers themselves walk once through the structure of "all images appearing simultaneously." If the order of the papers followed the order of patriarchal exposition, that would be allowing "expository convenience" to override "the round-teaching structure itself"—turning the project into a finger pointing in the wrong direction.

The common root of the four declarations is the simultaneously sufficient correspondence gate—the only gate that Fazang explicitly grounded in the power and function of the ocean-seal samādhi. Simultaneous sufficiency is the doctrinal basis of why leakage is possible (because the object pointed at by the āgamas and the *Avataṃsaka* is the same in "simultaneity"), simultaneous sufficiency is the doctrinal basis of why the mystery gates can be used as posture (because the ten gates stand in a relationship of simultaneous correspondence), simultaneous sufficiency is the doctrinal basis of why the failure point is necessary (because words cannot express simultaneity), simultaneous sufficiency is the doctrinal basis of why the radicalization of scripture first, treatise second at the structural level is justified (because the Buddha's spoken sequence is itself the unfolding of the simultaneously sufficient structure).

The four declarations gather upon the four characters "simultaneously sufficient." "Simultaneously sufficient" gathers upon the ocean-seal samādhi. The ocean-seal samādhi gathers upon the sixteen characters: *the original awakening of suchness; when delusion is exhausted and the mind clears, all images appear simultaneously*.

This is the central closing of section 3. The four declarations are structurally four faces of one thing; the doctrinal basis of this thing is the simultaneously sufficient structure of the ocean-seal samādhi; the doctrinal basis of the ocean-seal samādhi is the original awakening of suchness. Three layers of closing complete.

## 4. Cross-Disciplinary Resonance: The Black Hole Event Horizon as a Physical Metaphor for the Failure Point (Foreshadowing)

⚠️ *This section is a heuristic analogy, not a theoretical equivalence. The full development is reserved for P06 (the Indra's net mystery gate paper).*

### 4.1 Similarities

Physics gives us the structure of the *event horizon* around a black hole. The event horizon is not the physical boundary of the black hole; it is "the last position from which information can escape." Once anything crosses the event horizon, no signal (including light) can return from the interior to the exterior. From an external observer's point of view, the event horizon is "the last layer that can be observed"; the physical events inside it are, in principle, inaccessible to outside knowledge.

Reading this structure together with the failure point of this paper reveals an interesting isomorphism. The failure point of a paper is "the last position where words can function"—once the writing crosses the failure point, no verbal description can point accurately at the object. From the reader's point of view, the failure point is "the last layer that can be read"; the doctrinal object beyond it is, in principle, not communicable through words.

The event horizon is not a failure; it is the precise expression of a physical law—the position determined by the interplay of the speed of light as an upper bound on information transmission and the gravitational gradient. The failure point is also not a failure; it is the precise expression of words as a linear, sequential tool—the position determined by the interplay of language as a linear symbolic system and the "simultaneously sufficient" object structure.

### 4.2 Fundamental Differences

But there are two differences that must be drawn immediately.

The first difference: *the direction is reversed*. The object inside the event horizon (the singularity) is, in physics, *unknown*—theory predicts a singularity in the curvature of spacetime at that position, but all theories collapse there, and physicists themselves cannot say what is there. The object inside the failure point (the dharmadhātu, the ocean-seal samādhi) is, in the Huayan tradition, *already directly realized*. Huayan patriarchs across generations have said clearly that what is there is the realm of the ocean-seal samādhi, the original awakening of suchness, the simultaneous manifestation of all images. What the physical event horizon encloses is "the unknown"; what the failure point encloses is "the known but not expressible in language." These two directions are opposite.

The second difference: *the structure of attestation is different*. In physics, no one claims to have "crossed the event horizon and come back to tell us what is there"—this is physically impossible. In the Huayan tradition, there are people who claim explicitly to have "directly realized the ocean-seal samādhi"—Zhiyan, Fazang, Chengguan, Zongmi, all in different ways, declared their degrees of direct realization of this object. The failure point is not "no one has been there"; it is "those who have been there, when they return, cannot put it into words." This distinction is essential—it makes the ineffability of the *Avataṃsaka* not an epistemological "we do not know" but an expressive "we know but cannot communicate through a linear symbolic system."

### 4.3 Non-Reducibility Statement

Reading the black hole event horizon together with the failure point is a heuristic analogy. The purpose is to give a modern reader an intuitive physical metaphor for "why words must stop at a certain position." But this analogy is not a theoretical equivalence:

- The physical event horizon is a mathematical structure of spacetime geometry, a solution of the equations of general relativity; the failure point is a judgment about the methodology of paper writing, not any mathematical structure.
- The unknowability of the black hole singularity is an unsolved problem in physics and may be resolved in a future theory of quantum gravity. The ineffability of the dharmadhātu is not an epistemological problem; it is a mismatch between the structure of the object (simultaneously sufficient) and the structure of the tool (a linear symbolic system), and this mismatch persists in any symbolic system.
- The physical laws of a black hole do not change because of the observer's state of cultivation; the simultaneous manifestation of all images in the ocean-seal samādhi explicitly depends on the condition "delusion is exhausted, the mind clears"—it is a state-relative description.

Physical models will be updated, revised, and replaced over time—general relativity may be superseded by a future theory of quantum gravity, and the concept of the event horizon may be modified. The failure point, however, does not depend on any specific physical theory. Its validity does not rely on the progress of physics; it depends on the structural fact that "a linear symbolic system cannot express a simultaneously sufficient structure."

The foreshadowing of this section stops here. The full pairing of the black hole and Indra's net is reserved for S8-P06 (the Indra's net mystery gate paper), which will present, under the ⚠️ marker, a complete treatment of "why contemporary physical metaphors—black holes, the holographic universe, quantum entanglement—can help us understand Indra's net but cannot be reduced to Indra's net itself."

## 5. Middle-Way Corrections

### ❌ Correction One: The Latent Anxiety of "The *Avataṃsaka* Is Not Buddha-Vacana" (Pedagogical Hazard #11)

**Misreading:** The *Avataṃsaka*'s vision of the dharmadhātu exceeds what is explicitly taught in the āgamas, so the *Avataṃsaka* must be a later Mahāyāna expansion, not the historical Buddha's word. Is the Pointing-at-the-Moon project now defending an apocryphal scripture in writing S8?

**Correction:** S8 does not directly enter the scholarly dispute about whether the *Avataṃsaka* was spoken by the historical Buddha. The Pointing-at-the-Moon posture is to use the leakage method at the textual level to show that what the *Avataṃsaka* describes as the dharmadhātu and what the Buddha in the āgamas explicitly refuses to answer, explicitly declares unthinkable, explicitly describes as exceeding ordinary thought, are the same object. The difference between the āgamas and the *Avataṃsaka* is not that they point at different objects but that the same object is being broadcast at different signal strengths on different channels. The existence of leakage points automatically demonstrates that the *Avataṃsaka*'s vision of the dharmadhātu is not "in excess of the āgamas' range" but rather "an entry into the range the āgamas had already marked off and left blank." This argument does not require resolving any historical-critical question; it bypasses the premise of the "the *Avataṃsaka* is not buddha-vacana" argument at the textual level.

A deeper point: the legitimacy of Mahāyāna scriptures has never rested on the *external* criterion of "historically, someone spoke these words at a particular time and place" but on the *internal* criterion of "do the doctrines agree with the Buddha's realized wisdom." Whether the *Avataṃsaka*'s doctrines agree with the Buddha's realized wisdom is a question to be answered in the dharma-eye of the directly realized, not in the evidence chain of historical-critical scholarship. The Pointing-at-the-Moon posture is to push the textual work to its limit—marking the leakage points precisely and explaining the *Avataṃsaka*'s pointing relation to the āgamas—and then to stop at the level of internal attestation, leaving that to the reader's own dharma-eye.

### ❌ Correction Two: The Bidirectional Slippage of the Unspeakable (Pedagogical Hazard #12)

**Misreading A:** Because the dharmadhātu is unspeakable, anything one says about it is equally valid—Huayan patriarchs may say one thing, Tibetan traditions may say another, contemporary scholars still another, and since all "point at the same unspeakable object," all are true.

**Misreading B:** Because the dharmadhātu is unspeakable, the best course is to say nothing—any verbal description is wrong, so the most honest posture is not to write papers, not to give lectures, not to transmit the dharma.

**Correction:** Both misreadings are slippages.

To misreading A: "Unspeakable" is not a licence for "anything goes." The same unspeakable object has correct and incorrect modes of pointing at the textual level—correct pointing aligns with the object through structural failure (as with the ten mystery gates); incorrect pointing fabricates the object through structural invention (for example, by anthropomorphizing the dharmadhātu, hypostatizing it, or turning it into a doctrine of creation). Ineffability does not turn incorrect pointings into correct ones. The task of the S8 series is precisely this: to do the pointing correctly within the range where words can function, and to correct the common slippages found in the commentarial tradition one by one.

To misreading B: "Unspeakable" is also not a licence for "stay silent." If Huayan patriarchs had genuinely thought the most honest posture was silence, Zhiyan, Fazang, Chengguan, and Zongmi would not have written hundreds of fascicles of commentary. They wrote because they knew: within the range where words can function, correct pointing helps readers develop a correct attitude toward the object; incorrect pointing leads readers in exactly the wrong direction. Silence would allow readers to remain misled by incorrect pointings, and this would itself be a dereliction of duty.

The failure point is the middle way. It requires the paper to push the work to its limit within the range where words can function, and then to mark precisely and stop where words can no longer function. It allows neither the licence of "anything goes" nor the cowardice of "best stay silent." It is a strict respect for the boundary of the tool—neither speaking recklessly beyond the boundary nor retreating well inside the boundary for the sake of safety.

### ❌ Correction Three: The Semantic Shift of *Acintya* (Pedagogical Hazard #15)

**Misreading:** The āgamas say "the four unthinkables—sentient beings, the world, the dragon-realm, the buddha-realm," explicitly warning bhikṣus not to think about these things. But the *Avataṃsaka* treats "unthinkable" (*acintya*) as an expression of praise—"unthinkable liberation," "unthinkable realms," "unthinkable conduct and vows" everywhere. Is this a contradiction?

**Correction:** Not a contradiction, but a functional transposition of the same Sanskrit word *acintya* between two different epistemological levels.

In the āgama context, *acintya* functions as an *epistemological warning* (do not go there). These objects exceed the processing capacity of ordinary mind; thinking about them leads only to mental disorder, so do not think about them. The audience is śrāvaka-path practitioners whose job is to walk toward liberation through the four noble truths; dispersing their mental power onto metaphysical questions would break the concentration necessary for practice. *Acintya* in the āgamas is a warning to *this* audience.

In the *Avataṃsaka* context, *acintya* functions as an *ontological praise* (reality is beyond thought). The structure of the dharmadhātu itself exceeds linear thought; "unthinkable" is a positive description of that structure. The audience is dharma-body bodhisattvas and great bodhisattvas, whose work is to enter, traverse, and adorn the dharmadhātu; what they need is a positive description of the object, not a warning. *Acintya* in the *Avataṃsaka* is praise addressed to *this* audience.

The same word is used in two different audiences and two different functional positions. This is not a contradiction; it is a precise demonstration of the leakage structure—the same object (the range denoted by *acintya*) is marked in the āgamas as a warning for the human listener, and presented in the *Avataṃsaka* as praise for the bodhisattva listener. Different audiences, different postures; but the same object.

When reading the "unthinkable" in the āgamas, read it with the function of "a priority cue for śrāvaka-path practitioners." When reading the "unthinkable" in the *Avataṃsaka*, read it with the function of "an ontological praise of the structure of the dharmadhātu." The two functions together constitute the complete doctrinal meaning of *acintya*.

## 6. Practice-Gate Correspondence: Paying Homage to All Buddhas · Simultaneously Sufficient Correspondence

> *This section is the first demonstration of a new structural element introduced across the entire S8 series. Its position is between section 5 (middle-way corrections) and section 7 (conclusion). Its task is not to demote the paper into a practice manual, but to point the doctrinal object of the paper toward a bodily entrance—so that the reader, after finishing the paper, can do one concrete thing with their body, realizing in action once what they have read today in doctrine.*

P01 corresponds to the first of Samantabhadra's ten great vows: paying homage to all buddhas.

The closing fascicle of the *Puxian xingyuan pin* unfolds "paying homage to all buddhas" as follows:

> "All the world-honoured buddhas as numerous as particles of dust in all buddha-fields of all dharma-realms and empty space in all the ten directions of the three times—I, by the power of the practices and vows of Samantabhadra, with deep faith and understanding, as if they were face to face before me, with pure body, speech, and mind, constantly pay homage. At the place of each single buddha, I manifest bodies as numerous as the particles of dust of unspeakably unspeakable buddha-fields, and each body pays homage to buddhas as numerous as the particles of dust of unspeakably unspeakable buddha-fields."[^32]

Note the tense and structure of this vow. It does not say "I pay homage to the first buddha, then to the second buddha, then to the third buddha"—that would take innumerable kalpas to complete. It says "with pure body, speech, and mind, constantly pay homage" and "at the place of each single buddha, manifest bodies as numerous as the particles of dust of unspeakably unspeakable buddha-fields." In the very instant of paying homage to one buddha, innumerable bodies are simultaneously manifest paying homage to innumerable buddhas.

This is precisely the bodily realization of "simultaneously sufficient correspondence."

In the moment you bow down, you do not need to first think, "Which buddha am I paying homage to now? Śākyamuni, Amitābha, or Bhaiṣajyaguru? Should I pay homage to the east first, or the south?" You do not need to make this distinction. In the moment of bowing, all the buddhas of the ten directions and three times are already sufficient within this single bow. Your bow is not a homage to a particular buddha; it is a homage to all buddhas. Your body does not need to split itself into innumerable bodies paying homage to innumerable buddhas—the doctrine of "simultaneously sufficient correspondence" is already "one is all." This single bow already is innumerable bows.

Fazang's "when delusion is exhausted and the mind clears, all images appear simultaneously" from the *Wàngjìn huányuán guān* is completely realized in this single action:

"*Delusion is exhausted*"—in the instant of the bow, deluded thoughts momentarily cease. Not because you deliberately stop them, but because when the body enters the posture of reverence, the discriminating mind naturally weakens. Bowing does not require thinking. Bowing does not require choosing. Bowing does not require calculation.

"*The mind clears*"—when deluded thoughts momentarily cease, the water of the mind naturally clears. This clarity is not something cultivated; it is revealed in the moment.

"*All images appear simultaneously*"—in the very moment when the water of the mind clears, all the buddhas of the ten directions and three times are simultaneously imprinted in this bow. You do not need to summon them; they are already here. You do not need to count them; they are all here at once.

This is the bodily realization of paying homage to all buddhas as "simultaneously sufficient."

### A Suggestion for Practice

From this moment—the moment of finishing this paper—the daily practice of bowing can include this contemplation: *before each bow, do not first think "to whom am I paying homage now."* Let the body bear all the homage in the instant of the bow; let the four characters "simultaneously sufficient" be realized in this bow. One bow ends; rise; do the next. Do not count, do not calculate, do not fuss over the number of bows for the day. Each bow is innumerable bows; to fret about the number of bows is already to have returned to the deluded mind of sequentiality.

Do this practice every day for a few months, then re-read section 3 of this paper—declarations one through four. You will see something different then. "Simultaneously sufficient" read at the level of words is one thing; done at the level of the body is another. The former is the finger; the latter is the moon. This paper is the finger; your daily bowing is the moon.

The most important thing to do after finishing this paper is not to memorize the four declarations. The most important thing is, tomorrow morning, the moment you bow down, to take this single bow as innumerable bows.

## 7. Conclusion

### 7.1 Concise Restatement

The S8 series will write about the *Avataṃsaka*, but the *Avataṃsaka* declares that it is not spoken to humans. This paper bears that structural *gōng'àn* through four methodological declarations.

First, *the āgama leakage method*—the passages in the āgamas where the Buddha explicitly refuses to answer metaphysical questions, explicitly declares objects unthinkable, describes the panorama of innumerable worlds in the ten directions, and engages the narrative structure of Brahmā's petition are all leakage points of the same formless broadcast onto the human channel. Leakage is not seed; it is the evidence that the same signal is bleeding through on a different channel at weak strength.

Second, *the ten mystery gates as writing posture*—each paper in the S8 series bears its failure through one mystery gate, aligning with the unwritable object in one dimension. Form is content; the S8 series constitutes a meta-level enactment of the ten mystery gates.

Third, *the failure point convention*—every paper marks explicitly where language stops. Failure is not oversight; it is reverence. "Pointing at the moon" in S8 is not a brand slogan; it is a literal practice.

Fourth, *the radicalization of "scripture first, treatise second" at the structural level*—papers P01 through P10 of S8 correspond to Samantabhadra's ten great vows (the order spoken by the Buddha) rather than to the ten mystery gates (the order constructed by the patriarchs). When the order spoken by the Buddha meets the order constructed by the patriarchs, the former takes precedence. The one-to-one correspondence between the ten vows and the ten gates is the Pointing-at-the-Moon project's original scholarly contribution to Huayan studies and will be formally argued in the practice-gate correspondence section of P10.

The four declarations are not four things but four faces of one thing, whose doctrinal basis is the ocean-seal samādhi defined by Fazang's sixteen characters in the *Wàngjìn huányuán guān*: "the original awakening of suchness; when delusion is exhausted and the mind clears, all images appear simultaneously." The correspondence between this paper and the simultaneously sufficient correspondence gate is established by Fazang himself in *Wǔjiào zhāng* fascicle four—the chain of "ocean-seal samādhi → simultaneously sufficient correspondence gate → paying homage to all buddhas" is not constructed by the present author but read out of Fazang's own text.

### 7.2 Practice Guidance

Two concrete things can be done after finishing this paper.

First, the daily practice of bowing. In the moment of the bow, do not first think "to whom am I paying homage." Let the body bear all the homage; let the four characters "simultaneously sufficient" be realized in this single bow. Each bow is innumerable bows.

Second, when reading subsequent S8 papers, bring the reading posture of "the mystery gate as writing posture." Each S8 paper announces its mystery-gate posture in section 1; the reader should take this announcement as the prediction "this paper will fail in this particular dimension," not as the heading "this paper has this concept as its core." A different reading method produces a different result—the former lets the reader walk a failure trajectory firsthand (this is the Huayan method); the latter only collects a concept (this is not the Huayan method).

### 7.3 Open Question: Handed to P02

The methodological framework established in this paper raises one immediate question. The S8 series writes about the "round teaching" (*yuánjiào*), while the entire Yogācāra toolbox established by the S3 series is the "final teaching" (*zhōngjiào*). Fazang's five-teaching taxonomy in the *Wǔjiào zhāng*—Hīnayāna, beginning teaching, final teaching, sudden teaching, and round teaching—places Yogācāra in the final teaching and the *Avataṃsaka* in the round teaching. Is the relationship between final teaching and round teaching a hierarchical one—final teaching low, round teaching high, *Avataṃsaka* superior to Yogācāra?

If yes, then all the readers of the S3 series would be belittled at the opening of S8—"what you previously studied has now been surpassed." This would be a pedagogical disaster.

If no, then what exactly is the relationship between final teaching and round teaching?

S8-P02 will address this question. A preview of the answer: *Yogācāra is the highest-resolution map of human cognition accessible to humans; the* Avataṃsaka *is the description of the dharmadhātu in which that map is embedded*. They stand in a container relationship, not a hierarchical relationship. The final teaching preserves the upper limit of cognition attainable by humans; the round teaching describes the larger structure in which human cognition is embedded. The final teaching is not replaced by the round teaching; it is *placed* by the round teaching in its proper position.

P02 will take "unobstructed wide and narrow freedom" as its mystery-gate posture, corresponding to Samantabhadra's second vow, "praising the *Tathāgatas*"—praising the *Tathāgatas* at the level of speech-karma is precisely the "unobstructed wide and narrow" that best corresponds to P02's final-teaching / round-teaching relation.

See you in the next paper.

> Every paper in this series will stop somewhere. The place where it stops is not an oversight; it is a gesture of reverence. A three-dimensional pen cannot draw a ten-dimensional object. The most one can do is mark precisely the position where the pen is laid down, so that the reader knows: beyond this line, you must rely on your own dharma-eye.

The pen of this paper stops here.
