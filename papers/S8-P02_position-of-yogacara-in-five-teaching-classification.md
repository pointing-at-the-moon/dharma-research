## Abstract

In Fazang's (法藏, 643–712) *Treatise on the Distinct Doctrinal Categories of the One Vehicle of the Avataṃsaka* (《華嚴一乘教義分齊章》, T1866), all of Buddhist teaching is classified into five tiers: the Lesser Vehicle (小乘教), the Initial Mahāyāna Teaching (大乘始教, hereafter "Initial Teaching"), the Final Teaching (終教), the Sudden Teaching (頓教), and the Round Teaching (圓教). Yogācāra (specifically the Faxiang school of Kuiji and Xuanzang) is classified as Initial Teaching; the *tathāgatagarbha* corpus (the *Awakening of Faith*, the *Laṅkāvatāra*, the *Śrīmālā*, the *Ratnagotravibhāga*, the *Mahāparinirvāṇa-sūtra*) as Final Teaching; the *Avataṃsaka* itself as Round Teaching. For thirteen centuries this five-tier scheme has produced a tenacious misreading: the Round Teaching sits at the top, Yogācāra near the bottom, and *Avataṃsaka* therefore "surpasses" Yogācāra. The present paper argues the contrary: Fazang's classification is not a value hierarchy but a description of how the dharmadhātu broadcasts itself across receivers of different bandwidths. Yogācāra (Initial Teaching) is the highest-resolution map of how mind constructs experience for desire-realm human cognition; *tathāgatagarbha* (Final Teaching) describes how the suchness underlying that construction itself participates in becoming; *Avataṃsaka* (Round Teaching) describes the dharmadhātu in which both descriptions are already embedded. The three layers do not stand in a relation of replacement but in a relation of mutually unobstructed containment — the very structure named by the *Gate of the Unobstructed Mutual Containment of the Broad and the Narrow*. Drawing on Fazang's own framework of the "common one vehicle" (同教一乘) versus the "distinct one vehicle" (別教一乘), this paper argues — using Fazang's own line "*they are the very things being pointed at*" (為彼所目故) — that the three vehicles (including Yogācāra) are not a lower stage to be transcended but the *referents* of the distinct one vehicle's pointing. The paper also corrects a common chronological misplacement: Kuiji (632–682) died before Fazang composed the *Treatise* (c. 685–688), making the supposed "Avataṃsaka victory over Yogācāra" historically impossible — the debate never took place between the two founding patriarchs.

**Keywords:** five-teaching classification, Gate of the Unobstructed Mutual Containment of the Broad and the Narrow, Initial Teaching, Final Teaching, Round Teaching, common one vehicle, distinct one vehicle, praising the Tathāgatas, container model, teaching according to capacity

## I. Problem: What Happens the Moment You Open the *Treatise*

The first time a contemporary reader opens Fazang's *Treatise on the Distinct Doctrinal Categories of the One Vehicle of the Avataṃsaka* (hereafter *Treatise*) and reads the line "the holy teachings, in their ten-thousand differentiations, are essentially of five kinds" (聖教萬差要唯有五), almost everything that follows is overdetermined by a single mental image that forms in that instant: a staircase.

```
                                         ┌─── Round (Avataṃsaka)
                                   ┌─── Sudden
                             ┌─── Final (tathāgatagarbha)
                       ┌─── Initial (Yogācāra, Madhyamaka)
                 └─── Lesser Vehicle
```

Once that staircase is drawn, several conclusions follow almost automatically. The Round Teaching sits above the Sudden, the Sudden above the Final, the Final above the Initial. *Avataṃsaka* surpasses *tathāgatagarbha*; *tathāgatagarbha* surpasses Yogācāra; Yogācāra surpasses the Lesser Vehicle. Within the *Pointing at the Moon* research project, the entire S3 series on the *Treatise on the Hundred Dharmas (Bai-fa Ming-men Lun)* stands at the Initial Teaching tier. On the staircase reading, S3 is precisely the position S8 will eventually leave behind — "the place that *Avataṃsaka* will lead you out of."

If that reading were correct, the present author would have to retract three years of work on S3. The eight-consciousness model, the fifty-one mental factors, the four conditions, the three natures — all of these would be a too-fine-grained, too-analytic, too-"initial" set of distinctions, dissolved at the Round Teaching level into the unimpeded mutual interpenetration of phenomena (事事無礙).

This paper argues, on the contrary, that **the staircase reading was never Fazang's intention to begin with**.

Fazang himself, in the same fascicle of the *Treatise*, classifies Yogācāra unambiguously as Initial Teaching — that part is uncontested. But in another passage of the same fascicle, where he reorganizes the entire scheme using a parallel framework of "common one vehicle" (同教一乘) versus "distinct one vehicle" (別教一乘), Fazang says something that thirteen centuries of readers have systematically passed over:

> "One then comes to know that those very teachings of the three vehicles are not other than the distinct one vehicle. Why is this so? **Because they are the very things being pointed at. There is no other matter at all.**" (即知彼三乘等法。本來不異別教一乘。何以故。為彼所目故。更無異事故。)[^1]

**"Because they are the very things being pointed at."** The three vehicles — including Yogācāra at the Initial tier — are not a lower replacement of the distinct one vehicle (Round Teaching, *Avataṃsaka*); they are the *referents* of the distinct one vehicle. The distinct one vehicle is the "pointer"; the three vehicles are what is "pointed at." Pointer and referent are not in a relation of higher and lower; they are the two ends of a single act of pointing — without a referent there is nothing to point at, without a pointer the referent cannot be indicated. They are mutually constitutive, not hierarchically arranged.

This is the central thesis of the present paper: Fazang's classification is not a value hierarchy but a description of the dharmadhātu's broadcasting structure across different reception bandwidths. When a desire-realm human consciousness receives the dharmadhātu's broadcast through the structure of mind-as-constructor, what is heard is Yogācāra. When a more refined receiver reads that same broadcast through the question of how suchness itself participates in construction, what is heard is *tathāgatagarbha*. When the broadcast is received without any filtering bandwidth at all, what is heard is the *Avataṃsaka*'s description of the dharmadhātu. These are not three different teachings; they are the same teaching as it manifests across three different reception bandwidths.

As my teacher has often put it: "*Different audiences — those in the desire realm, those in the form realm, those in the formless realm — hear and perceive different teachings*." This is not a rhetorical flourish but a literal description of how the *Avataṃsaka-sūtra* itself stages its own preaching. The "seven locations and eight (or nine) assemblies" (七處八會) of the sūtra unfold across the bodhi-seat (on earth), the Hall of Universal Light (on earth), the Trayastriṃśa heaven, the Yāma heaven, the Tuṣita heaven, the Paranirmita-vaśavartin heaven (the topmost desire-realm heaven) — a single teaching given in multiple locations across multiple realms simultaneously, with audiences spanning the desire, form, and formless realms. **Fazang's five-teaching classification is the formal articulation, in classificatory pedagogy, of this multi-bandwidth reception structure already encoded in the sūtra itself.**

### 1.1 Mystery-Gate Posture Statement: The Gate of the Unobstructed Mutual Containment of the Broad and the Narrow

Each paper in the S8 series must explicitly declare which of the ten mystery gates (十玄門) it adopts as its *writing posture* — not merely as an object of study — to avoid the internal contradiction of using a linear argument to talk about a non-linear structure.[^2]

The present paper adopts the **Gate of the Unobstructed Mutual Containment of the Broad and the Narrow** (廣狹自在無礙門), the second of the ten mystery gates in Fazang's later *Exploring the Mysteries of the Avataṃsaka* (《華嚴經探玄記》, T1733). The core definition, given in fascicle one of that text, runs:

> "Insofar as the divided is the undivided and the undivided is the divided, the broad and the narrow are mutually self-determined and unobstructed." (以分即無分。無分即分。廣狹自在無障無礙。)[^3]

The "divided" (*fen* 分) is the narrow — the bounded, the concrete, the local; the "undivided" (*wu-fen* 無分) is the broad — the unbounded, the universal, the total. The core of the gate is not that the broad *contains* the narrow as a larger set contains a smaller subset (which would be ordinary set theory), but that the narrow itself *is* the broad and the broad itself *is* the narrow, with neither requiring expansion or compression to become the other. A single particle of dust does not need to expand in order to be the ten directions; the ten directions do not need to compress in order to be a single particle of dust.

Adopting this gate as a writing posture means: **at no point in this paper can any of the three layers (Initial, Final, Round) be read as a subset of any other layer**. Yogācāra is not a "smaller slice" of *Avataṃsaka*; *Avataṃsaka* is not an "expanded version" of Yogācāra. Their relation is not the relation of larger set to smaller set, but precisely "the divided is the undivided, the undivided is the divided" — when you look at one of the five mental factors of contact and feeling in fine Yogācāra detail, what you are looking at *is* the entire dharmadhātu; when you look at the entire dharmadhātu in *Avataṃsaka* terms, what you are looking at *is* a particular mental factor in fine detail. This is a posture that violates ordinary readerly intuition from the start, but it is internally required by the very claim the paper is making.

If a reader finishes this paper with the sense that "the Round Teaching is bigger than Yogācāra" or "Yogācāra is more precise than the Round Teaching," then the paper has failed. The paper succeeds only if, by the end, the reader has begun to question whether "bigger" and "more precise" are the right comparisons at all.

### 1.2 Failure-Point Disclosure

The argumentative form of this paper is a *linear container analogy*: it talks about three concentric layers, three camera focal lengths, three reception bandwidths. But what the Gate of the Unobstructed Mutual Containment of the Broad and the Narrow names is precisely the *absence* of layered structure. Three concentric circles imply a center and a periphery; three lenses imply a photographer standing outside the scene; three reception bands imply a single total spectrum that can be divided. Each of these analogies translates "broad and narrow" back into "large and small" — and "large and small" is precisely the reading the paper is trying to undo.

When the reader reaches the analogies of §III and §IV, please remember: **every analogy will fail at some point**. The point of failure is not a deficiency but a salutation. The paper will mark these failure points explicitly at the end of §V. Beyond that line, what remains is no longer in the words of this paper but in the reader's own direct sensitivity to the structure being indicated.

## II. Textual Foundations

### 2.A Āgama Leakage: The Original Principle of Teaching According to Capacity

The Five-Teaching classification, considered as a formal systematization of the principle of "teaching according to capacity" (對機說法), has its origin not in patriarchal scholasticism but in the most foundational pedagogical principle of the early Buddhist canon: the Buddha taught different beings different things.

In the *Saṃyukta-āgama*, the *Madhyama-āgama*, and elsewhere, the Buddha is repeatedly shown employing entirely different vocabulary, different similes, different doctrinal sequences depending on the audience. To King Prasenajit he speaks of statecraft and the consequences of action; to the householder Anāthapiṇḍada he speaks of generosity and rebirth in the heavens; to young monks he speaks of impermanence and contemplation of the unattractive (*aśubha-bhāvanā*); to wandering non-Buddhist mendicants he refuses to answer the *avyākṛta* questions (the "fourteen unanswered questions"); to Ānanda he speaks of dependent origination. A single liberation is presented through innumerable doorways, and each doorway is shaped to fit a particular receiver.

In the Abhidharma tradition this principle is formalized as the four *siddhānta* (catvāraḥ siddhāntāḥ): the *worldly siddhānta*, the *individual siddhānta*, the *therapeutic siddhānta*, and the *ultimate siddhānta*.[^4] The first three are all forms of "teaching according to capacity"; only the fourth is "ultimate" (paramārtha-satya). **The Āgama tradition itself already acknowledges that "ultimate" and "according to capacity" lie on different axes**. A teaching's being "non-ultimate" does not entail its being "untrue"; it entails only that the receivers it addresses are not yet prepared to hear the wider version.

**This is precisely the location of the present paper's "Āgama leakage".** The early canon already accepts the legitimacy of multi-track teaching, but it does not systematize this legitimacy into a formal classificatory pedagogy. The leakage that the early canon leaves behind is the question: *if a single liberation has innumerable doorways, what is the relation between those doorways? Is it hierarchical succession, or is it parallel correspondence?* The early canon does not answer the question — its pedagogical focus is realization, not metaclassificatory reflection. The Five-Teaching classification is precisely the later unfolding of this leakage: Fazang inherits the question that the Āgamas left open and offers an answer — these doorways are not in hierarchical succession but in parallel reception bands of a single dharmadhātu broadcast.

A note on what I will call (following the methodological convention established in S3-SUP01) **"same word, different sense"**: the Āgama notion of "teaching according to capacity" is a *pedagogical observation* (different beings need different medicines), while the *Avataṃsaka*'s Five-Teaching classification is an *ontological description* (different receivers natively hear different versions of the same broadcast). The first is "the teacher's choice"; the second is "the structure of the dharmadhātu." The two have different orientations but the same source — that source being the very structure named by the Gate of the Unobstructed Mutual Containment of the Broad and the Narrow. The dharmadhātu's broadcast is "broad" in itself, but any finite receiver can only receive a particular "narrow" band of it. The Buddha, as teacher, knows this structure and therefore teaches according to capacity; Fazang, as classifier, formalizes the same structure into the five teachings.

### 2.B *Avataṃsaka* Anchor: "In Each and Every Particle of Dust"

The present paper corresponds to the second of Samantabhadra's ten great vows, *praising the Tathāgatas* (稱讚如來). The locus classicus is in the fortieth fascicle of the forty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka*:

> "Furthermore, good son, as for praising the Tathāgatas: in each and every particle of dust, throughout all the buddha-fields of the ten directions and the three times — particles as numerous as can be exhausted in the entire dharmadhātu and in space — there are buddhas as numerous as the particles of dust of all worlds; and around each and every one of these buddhas there is an ocean-assembly of bodhisattvas. With profound and surpassing understanding, I directly perceive them. With a wondrous tongue-faculty surpassing that of the goddess of eloquence, from each and every tongue-faculty there issues an ocean of inexhaustible voices, and from each and every voice there issues an ocean of all phrasings, with which I praise the ocean of qualities of all the Tathāgatas, throughout the entire future without interruption, pervading the dharmadhātu without remainder."[^5]

The structure of this passage is the most complete sūtra-level instantiation of the Gate of the Unobstructed Mutual Containment of the Broad and the Narrow. Note the three nested levels of "ocean":

1. From each tongue-faculty issues an *ocean of inexhaustible voices* — one root produces innumerable sounds
2. From each voice issues an *ocean of all phrasings* — one sound contains innumerable words
3. The praise itself is offered to the *ocean of qualities of all Tathāgatas* — innumerable words praise innumerable qualities

The three "oceans" are infinitely nested: each level's "one" expands into the next level's "innumerable," and each level's "innumerable" is the next level's "one." This is neither a linear progression (from few to many) nor a tree structure (from root to leaves) but the structure named by "the divided is the undivided, the undivided is the divided" — every microscopic unit (one root, one sound, one word) is at the same time broad (containing innumerable) and narrow (being just this one).

Even more critically: "**in each and every particle of dust, there are buddhas as numerous as the particles of dust of all worlds**." A single particle of dust contains buddhas as numerous as the particles of all worlds. This is the *Avataṃsaka*'s densest single statement of the unobstructed mutuality of the broad and the narrow. If a reader takes this line as describing physical-spatial compression (an elephant being squeezed into a refrigerator), the reading has translated "broad and narrow" back into "large and small"; if the reader takes it as "this single particle is the dharmadhātu, and the dharmadhātu is this single particle," the reading is the unobstructed mutual containment itself.

The passage also directly stages the multi-receiver broadcast structure: "around each and every one of these buddhas there is an ocean-assembly of bodhisattvas." Each particle's each buddha is surrounded by an ocean-assembly whose members span innumerable dharma-realms and innumerable levels of receivers. A single act of preaching is simultaneously received, on innumerable levels and across innumerable bandwidths. **This is the literal scriptural evidence for the present paper's three-layer container thesis** — except that the *Avataṃsaka*'s version is not three layers but innumerable layers. Fazang's Five-Teaching classification is the convenience of a five-fold gathering of this innumerable multiplicity, designed for human pedagogy.

A second instance of "same word, different sense" should be flagged here: "praising" (*chēng-zàn* 稱讚) appears in the early canon as well — Buddhists praising the Buddha's wisdom, compassion, and liberation — but the early canon's praise is *realization-oriented* (it is the verbal expression of one's own contact with awakening), whereas the *Avataṃsaka*'s praise is *dharmadhātu-oriented* (a single instance of praise pervades the dharmadhātu, and this pervasion is not a subsequent transmission but the very intrinsic structure of the act of praising). The same word, in two different reception bandwidths, manifests as two different acts of practice. This is not a contradiction but the unobstructed mutuality of the broad and the narrow at the level of vocabulary itself: a single word can be broad (*Avataṃsaka*) or narrow (Āgama), and the two are mutually unobstructed.[^6]

### 2.C Patriarchal Discourse: Fazang's *Treatise* and Chengguan's Reinforcement

#### 2.C.1 The Five Teachings as Defined in Fascicle One of the *Treatise*

Fazang formally establishes the five teachings in fascicle one of the *Treatise*, in the section titled "Distinguishing the Teachings and Opening the Schools":

> "The holy teachings, in their ten-thousand differentiations, are essentially of five kinds: first, the Lesser Vehicle Teaching; second, the Initial Mahāyāna Teaching; third, the Final Teaching; fourth, the Sudden Teaching; fifth, the Round Teaching. The first is precisely the dharma-ignoring two-vehicle teaching. The last is precisely the distinct one vehicle."[^7]

The middle three teachings (Initial, Final, Sudden) admit of three distinct treatments in Fazang's text: gathered as one (since all three are received by adherents of the three vehicles), divided into two (gradual versus sudden), or opened into three (Initial, Final, Sudden). The most important distinction for the present paper is the criterion by which Fazang separates the Initial Teaching from the Final Teaching:

> "[According to the *Mahābherī-sūtra*,] taking the way of emptiness as the *Initial* and the way of non-emptiness as the *Final*. ... To explicate this: 'with reference to the principle of emptiness as a partial discourse' — this is named the Initial Teaching; 'with reference to the constantly abiding marvelous scriptures of the *tathāgatagarbha*' — this is named the Final Teaching."[^8]

**The Initial Teaching is "the partial discourse on emptiness"** (Prajñāpāramitā, the *Saṃdhinirmocana*, Yogācāra); **the Final Teaching is "the constantly abiding marvelous scriptures of the *tathāgatagarbha*"** (the *Awakening of Faith*, *Laṅkāvatāra*, *Śrīmālā*, *Ratnagotravibhāga*, *Mahāparinirvāṇa-sūtra*). The boundary is drawn by Fazang himself, citing the *Mahābherī-sūtra*'s assertion that "all sūtras of emptiness are partial discourses; only this sūtra is the unsurpassed discourse."

Fazang then maps the five teachings onto a parallel ten-school taxonomy (十宗), constructing a dual-track classification:

| Teaching | School | Representative |
|---|---|---|
| Lesser Vehicle | Schools 1–6 | Vātsīputrīya, Sarvāstivāda, Mahāsāṃghika, Prajñaptivāda, Lokottaravāda, Ekavyāvahārika |
| Initial Mahāyāna | 7. School That All Dharmas Are Empty | Prajñāpāramitā, Yogācāra (both the Dharmapāla and Bhāviveka wings) |
| Final Teaching | 8. School That True Virtue Is Not Empty | *Awakening of Faith*, *Laṅkāvatāra*, *Śrīmālā*, *Ratnagotravibhāga* |
| Sudden Teaching | 9. School Where Both Mark and Conception Are Cut Off | *Vimalakīrti*'s silent revelation of non-duality |
| Round Teaching | 10. School of Round Brightness and Complete Virtue | *Avataṃsaka* — the distinct one vehicle |

#### 2.C.2 Why Yogācāra Is Classified as Initial, Not Final: Fazang's Five Internal Proofs

This is the most consequential textual point of the paper. If any reader has carried into this discussion the assumption that "Yogācāra equals Final Teaching" (a common error in contemporary Sinophone Buddhism, including in earlier internal drafts of this research project), please consult the following five passages from fascicles two and three of the *Treatise*.

**Proof 1: The doctrine of five-fold gotra and the existence of beings without nature** (Fascicle 2)

> "If according to the three-vehicle teaching, the differentiation of *gotra* is briefly explained in three ways. The first is according to the Initial Teaching, namely, that *gotra* is established within the conditioned and impermanent dharmas, and therefore cannot pervade all sentient beings. Hence among the five *gotras* there is one portion of beings who have no [Buddha-]nature."[^9]

Yogācāra establishes five *gotras* (the *gotra* of the *śrāvaka*, the *pratyekabuddha*, the bodhisattva, the indeterminate, and *no-gotra*) and acknowledges that there exists "one portion of beings without [Buddha-]nature" who will never attain final liberation. From Fazang's perspective, this is precisely the typical mark of the *Initial* Teaching, since the Final Teaching (the *tathāgatagarbha* corpus) maintains that "all sentient beings possess Buddha-nature."

**Proof 2: The *icchantika* without Buddha-nature, classified as definitive** (Fascicle 2)

Yogācāra, following the *Yogācārabhūmi* and the *Xianyang Shengjiao Lun*, holds that the *icchantika* — the being who has cut off all wholesome roots — never attains buddhahood, and that this assertion is definitive (*nītārtha*), not provisional (*neyārtha*). The Final Teaching position is the exact opposite: the assertion that the *icchantika* lacks Buddha-nature is itself a provisional, expedient teaching, while the definitive teaching is "all beings possess Buddha-nature." Fazang explicitly assigns Yogācāra's "definitive" reading of *icchantika*-naturelessness to the Initial Teaching.[^10]

**Proof 3: Definitively determined two-vehicle adherents do not turn back** (Fascicle 3)

> "Those of a definitively determined *gotra* head for the calm [of *nirvāṇa*] and do not turn back; those of an indeterminate *gotra* turn toward the Mahāyāna. As is said in the *Decisive Section on the Śrāvakas* of the *Yogācārabhūmi*. This is presented from the perspective of the Initial Teaching as it draws in the two-vehicle adherents."[^11]

Yogācāra, following the *Śrāvakabhūmi* of the *Yogācārabhūmi*, holds that those of "definitively determined *śrāvaka gotra*" enter *nirvāṇa* without remainder and never reverse course toward bodhisattvahood. This directly conflicts with the Final Teaching position that "all two-vehicle adherents turn back" and that the three vehicles are ultimately gathered into the one. Fazang explicitly assigns this Yogācāra position to "the Initial Teaching as it draws in the two-vehicle adherents."

**Proof 4: Suchness as inert and not producing dharmas** (Fascicle 2)

> "If according to the Initial Teaching, with respect to the *ālayavijñāna*, only the partial sense of arising and ceasing is obtained. Because the way of true principle is not yet thoroughly assimilated, [the Initial Teaching] only says that [suchness] is inert and does not produce dharmas. Hence the *ālayavijñāna* is established within the matter of dependently arising arising-and-ceasing, with its substance arising from the seeds of action and so forth."[^12]

In contrast, on the Final Teaching:

> "If according to the Final Teaching, with respect to this *ālayavijñāna*, the doctrine of the unobstructed mutual interpenetration of principle and phenomena in two aspects is obtained. ... Because [the Final Teaching] permits suchness to be perfumed and to combine [with ignorance] to constitute this fundamental consciousness. This is unlike the previous teaching, where [the *ālayavijñāna*] arises from the seeds of action and so forth."[^13]

Yogācāra's suchness is "inert" (凝然) — unmoving, not participating in dependent origination — and the *ālayavijñāna* arises from karmic seeds. This is a structure in which "principle and phenomena are not interpenetrated." The Final Teaching's suchness "follows conditions" (隨緣) — combining with ignorance to constitute the fundamental consciousness — which is a structure in which "principle and phenomena interpenetrate in two aspects." **Yogācāra's suchness does not participate in *saṃsāra*; the Final Teaching's suchness is the very inner dynamism of *saṃsāra*.** This distinction is the watershed between the Initial and Final Teachings, and Fazang spells it out in the most direct terms.

**Proof 5: Xuanzang's three turnings of the Dharma Wheel do not include the *Avataṃsaka*** (Fascicle 1)

> "According to the great Tang Tripiṭaka master Xuanzang, relying on the *Saṃdhinirmocana-sūtra*, the *Suvarṇaprabhāsa*, and the *Yogācārabhūmi*, [he] established three kinds of teaching, namely, the three turnings of the Dharma Wheel. ... These three turnings of the Dharma Wheel speak only of the Lesser Vehicle and of the Initial and Final teachings within the three vehicles. **They do not include the distinct one vehicle.** Why is this so? Because the *Avataṃsaka-sūtra* was preached at the very beginning, and is not Lesser Vehicle; and that 'sustaining wheel' was preached at the end, and is not the *Avataṃsaka*. Therefore, [Xuanzang's scheme] does not include the dharma-gate of the *Avataṃsaka*."[^14]

Here Fazang names Xuanzang — the founding patriarch of Chinese Yogācāra — explicitly, and observes that Xuanzang's classificatory pedagogy reaches at most "the Initial and Final teachings within the three vehicles" and does not encompass the *Avataṃsaka*'s distinct one vehicle at all. In other words, **from Fazang's perspective, the founding patriarch of Yogācāra had not even completed the Final Teaching's territory in his classificatory pedagogy, let alone the Round Teaching**. This is why Yogācāra, in Fazang's system, sits at the Initial Teaching, not the Final.

The five proofs together yield an unequivocal conclusion: **in Fazang's classificatory pedagogy, Yogācāra is the Initial Teaching**. The Final Teaching is the *tathāgatagarbha* corpus. If anywhere in the *Pointing at the Moon* research project an earlier text loosely associated "Yogācāra" with "Final Teaching," the present paper formally corrects that association.

#### 2.C.3 The Common One Vehicle and the Distinct One Vehicle: The Container Framework in the Patriarch's Own Words

In addition to the five-teaching classification, Fazang establishes a parallel axis in fascicle one of the *Treatise*: the "one vehicle" itself is divided into the "common one vehicle" (同教一乘) and the "distinct one vehicle" (別教一乘). This distinction is the patriarchal locus classicus for the present paper's "container, not hierarchy" thesis.[^15]

> "First, on the establishment of the one vehicle. The doctrinal divisions of this one vehicle are opened into two gates: the first is the distinct teaching, the second is the common teaching."[^16]

The **distinct one vehicle** is the *Avataṃsaka* itself — "with primary and secondary fully complete, gathering virtues without measure," roundly interpenetrating, self-determined, not relying on the three vehicles as expedients.

The **common one vehicle** is the *Lotus Sūtra* — taking the three vehicles as expedients to lead beings into the one vehicle, "the harmonious teaching of three-as-one."

The most critical passage is in the section on "the gate of doctrinal benefit":

> "First, [there is the case where] the great white-ox cart in the open ground has its own doctrine, namely, the doctrine of ten-times-ten inexhaustibility with primary and secondary fully complete, as taught in the *Avataṃsaka*. This corresponds to the **distinct one vehicle**.
>
> Second, [there is the case where] the three carts at the door [of the burning house] have their own doctrine, namely, that what is shown within the [three] realms is the teaching, and getting out is the meaning. This corresponds to the **three-vehicle teaching**, as taught in the other sūtras and in the *Yogācārabhūmi* and so forth.
>
> Third, [there is the case where] the three carts at the door are taken as a provisional teaching that opens [the way], and outside the [three] realms a separate great white-ox cart is bestowed, which then displays the true meaning. This corresponds to the **common one vehicle**, as taught in the *Lotus Sūtra*."[^17]

Fazang distinguishes the structures with great clarity:
- **Distinct one vehicle** = the *Avataṃsaka*'s own "primary and secondary fully complete" doctrine, self-establishing
- **Three-vehicle teaching** = the cross-section of Lesser, Initial (including Yogācāra), Final, and Sudden — using "within the realms" pedagogy
- **Common one vehicle** = the *Lotus Sūtra*'s "gathering the three to return to the one," using the three vehicles as expedients pointed at the one

And in the section "Determining the Meaning," Fazang offers what is, for the present paper, the central container statement:

> "[Of those beings whose] three-vehicle *gotra* is indeterminate in this lifetime [and who are] capable of advancing into the distinct one vehicle: one then comes to know that those very teachings of the three vehicles are not other than the distinct one vehicle. Why is this so? **Because they are the very things being pointed at. There is no other matter at all.** As is said in the *Lotus Sūtra* under the common teaching."[^18]

"**Because they are the very things being pointed at. There is no other matter at all.**" These two clauses are the patriarch's own textual support for the entire argument of the present paper. The three vehicles (which include the Initial Teaching of Yogācāra, the Final Teaching of *tathāgatagarbha*, and the Sudden Teaching of silent revelation) are *not other than* the distinct one vehicle, because they are the distinct one vehicle's *referents* — what the distinct one vehicle is pointing at, the very content of its act of pointing. They are not lower replacements of the distinct one vehicle; they are the distinct one vehicle's way of pointing at itself. Without the referent there is no pointing; without the pointing the referent cannot be indicated.

**This is the patriarch's own articulation of "container, not hierarchy"**: Fazang himself, in a single sentence, locates the three vehicles (including Yogācāra) as the *intrinsic content* of the distinct one vehicle, not as a lower tier to be transcended. The staircase reading is here directly refused by Fazang's own words.

#### 2.C.4 Chengguan's Reinforcement: The Mutual Non-Obstruction of Sequential Distribution and Round Interpenetration

Chengguan (澄觀, 738–839), the fourth patriarch of the Huayan school, supplied a crucial horizontal reinforcement to Fazang's primarily vertical classification. In his *Commentary on the Avataṃsaka-sūtra* he writes:[^19]

> "There are also two [gates here]: first, the gate of *sequential distribution* (行布門), in that it establishes differentiations of position; second, the gate of *round interpenetration* (圓融門), in that one position contains all positions. ... These two are mutually unobstructed: because sequential distribution is the doctrinal arrangement of the teaching, and round interpenetration is the virtuous functioning of the natural principle. ... **Sequential distribution does not obstruct round interpenetration; round interpenetration does not obstruct sequential distribution. Because round interpenetration does not obstruct sequential distribution, the one becomes the innumerable; because sequential distribution does not obstruct round interpenetration, the innumerable becomes the one.**"[^20]

Chengguan's reinforcement is decisive for the present paper. Fazang's five-teaching classification is primarily vertical (Lesser → Initial → Final → Sudden → Round) and is therefore easily misread as a staircase. Chengguan introduces a horizontal axis — sequential distribution versus round interpenetration — that converts the classification from a one-dimensional staircase into a two-dimensional structure. **"Sequential distribution is the doctrinal arrangement of the teaching; round interpenetration is the virtuous functioning of the natural principle"**: sequential distribution is the level of pedagogical scaffolding (how one teaches), round interpenetration is the level of natural-principle virtuous functioning (how the structure of things actually is). The two do not conflict because they belong to different descriptive axes.

Applied to the five teachings: the Initial Teaching's (Yogācāra's) five *gotras*, eight consciousnesses, fifty-one mental factors, seeds and active manifestations — these are sequential distribution; the Final Teaching's (*tathāgatagarbha*'s) suchness following conditions and the unobstructed interpenetration of principle and phenomena — these too are sequential distribution at another level; the Round Teaching's (*Avataṃsaka*'s) phenomenon-to-phenomenon non-obstruction — even this remains, at one level, sequential distribution (the *Avataṃsaka* internally has its own progression of ten faiths, ten dwellings, ten practices, ten dedications, ten grounds). **And round interpenetration is the same single virtuous functioning that runs through every one of these sequential distributions.**

In other words: **the five teachings are not five different things; they are the same virtuous functioning manifesting in five different sequential distributions**. This is, again, the patriarchal endorsement of the "container, not hierarchy" thesis — this time from Chengguan.

## III. The Central Argument: Three Containers and the Unobstructed Mutuality of the Broad and the Narrow

### 3.1 Restating the Thesis: Three Reception Bandwidths of a Single Dharmadhātu Broadcast

The central thesis of the paper can be stated in a single sentence:

> **Fazang's five-teaching classification is not a value hierarchy but a description of the dharmadhātu's broadcasting structure. A single dharma, when received by receivers of different bandwidths, manifests in different resolutions — and these different resolutions exist simultaneously, none replacing any other.**

Concretizing this thesis to the three layers (Initial, Final, Round) directly relevant to the position of Yogācāra:

| Layer | Reception Bandwidth | Corresponding Teaching | Primary Object Described |
|---|---|---|---|
| Initial | Desire-realm human consciousness | Yogācāra | How mind constructs experience (the inner structure of consciousness) |
| Final | More refined receivers | *Tathāgatagarbha* | How the suchness on which mind depends itself participates in construction |
| Round | The dharmadhātu's native bandwidth | *Avataṃsaka* | The dharmadhātu in which all the above is already embedded |

What each layer describes is an *independent existential object*, not a different level of fineness in the description of a single object. The "eight consciousnesses with fifty-one mental factors" of Yogācāra and the "single suchness of the dharmadhātu" of the *Avataṃsaka* are not the detailed-version and the abbreviated-version of one and the same thing. They are two ontologically distinct facts at two different scales.

This is the application of the Gate of the Unobstructed Mutual Containment of the Broad and the Narrow at the level of classificatory pedagogy: Yogācāra's "narrow" (the concrete structure of consciousness) is not an incomplete version of *Avataṃsaka*'s "broad" (the dharmadhātu in its totality); rather, "the divided is the undivided, the undivided is the divided," and the two are mutually unobstructed. A reader who has not studied Yogācāra and reads the *Avataṃsaka* will read it as a poem; a reader who has studied Yogācāra and then reads the *Avataṃsaka* will read it as the dharmadhātu itself — but the *Avataṃsaka* presented in those two readings is not the same *Avataṃsaka*. The latter contains within itself, as its "narrow" face, the entire fine-grained structure of Yogācāra; this is why, for the latter reader, the same *Avataṃsaka* is simultaneously broad (the entire dharmadhātu) and narrow (each of the five mental factors of contact and feeling corresponds to a specific dharmadhātu structure).

### 3.2 Audience Cosmology: The Three Realms as Receivers, and Multi-Track Reception

The decisive doctrinal anchor for this paper, supplied by my teacher in oral conversation, is this: "*Different audiences — those in the desire realm, those in the form realm, those in the formless realm — hear and perceive different teachings*." This single observation transforms the five-teaching classification from a piece of hermeneutic taxonomy into a statement of cosmological fact.

Consider the structure of the *Avataṃsaka-sūtra* itself. The eighty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* unfolds across "seven locations and nine assemblies" (七處九會), with the location of each assembly varying:

| Assembly | Location | Class of Receivers |
|---|---|---|
| First | The bodhi-seat (on earth) | Great bodhisattvas, divine and human assemblies |
| Second | The Hall of Universal Light (on earth) | Same as above |
| Third | The Trayastriṃśa heaven | The devas of the second desire-realm heaven |
| Fourth | The Yāma heaven | The devas of the third desire-realm heaven |
| Fifth | The Tuṣita heaven | The devas of the fourth desire-realm heaven |
| Sixth | The Paranirmita-vaśavartin heaven | The devas of the sixth (topmost) desire-realm heaven |
| Seventh | The Hall of Universal Light (revisited) | Earth-dwelling and divine receivers |
| Eighth | The Jeta Grove | The concrete human protagonists of Sudhana's southward pilgrimage |

The seven locations and eight (or nine) assemblies span both earth and the desire-realm heavens, and these assemblies are not arranged as a linear temporal sequence but as the unobstructed mutuality of the broad and the narrow: "the same teaching, given simultaneously in different places." A single doctrinal content of the *Avataṃsaka* is **simultaneously received in different heavens by receivers at different levels**. This is the literal structure of the sūtra itself, not a later hermeneutic elaboration.

Moreover, the bodhisattva ocean-assemblies described throughout the *Avataṃsaka* include receivers from all three realms: the devas of the desire realm, the devas of the form-realm meditative heavens, and the devas of the four formless attainments. A single act of preaching is heard in one version by desire-realm beings, in another version by form-realm beings, and in a third version by formless-realm beings — and these three versions exist simultaneously, natively, and without mutual obstruction. **This is the concrete cosmological fact of the Gate of the Unobstructed Mutual Containment of the Broad and the Narrow at the level of audience structure.**

Mapping this fact back onto the three layers of teaching:

- **Initial Teaching (Yogācāra)** corresponds to the reception bandwidth of desire-realm human consciousness. Beings in the desire realm are pulled around by gross sense-objects and require a fine-grained description of the structure of consciousness (eight consciousnesses, fifty-one mental factors, seeds and active manifestations, four conditions, three natures) before they can "see" how they are getting stuck. Yogācāra is the highest-resolution map for the desire-realm human bandwidth.

- **Final Teaching (*tathāgatagarbha*)** corresponds to a more refined reception bandwidth. Once a receiver has, through Yogācāra, seen clearly the inner construction of his own consciousness, the next question naturally arises: what is the "suchness" on which this construction depends actually doing? Is suchness inertly unmoving, or does it participate in this construction by following conditions? The Final Teaching corpus (*Awakening of Faith*, *Laṅkāvatāra*, *Śrīmālā*, *Ratnagotravibhāga*, *Mahāparinirvāṇa-sūtra*) is precisely the layer that answers this question, transforming suchness from "static substance" to "dynamism following conditions." This layer corresponds to the reception bandwidth of form-realm meditative receivers, and also to the human practitioner who has completed the basic analysis of consciousness and has begun to ask ontological questions.

- **Round Teaching (*Avataṃsaka*)** corresponds to the dharmadhātu's native broadcast bandwidth. When a receiver is neither stuck in the gross objects of the five desires nor caught up in the conceptual frame "the substance of suchness," what he directly receives is the phenomenon-to-phenomenon non-obstruction structure of the dharmadhātu itself. The Round Teaching describes not "another higher level," but "what the dharmadhātu is saying when no receiver-filter is applied at all." This corresponds to the reception bandwidth of the formless realm and the bodhisattva ocean-assemblies.

**There is no hierarchy between the three layers because there is no hierarchy between the receivers**. A desire-realm human receiver needs Yogācāra's fineness — not because he is "lower," but because his reception bandwidth simply *is* this. Telling a desire-realm human to read the *Avataṃsaka* directly while skipping Yogācāra is like telling a radio that can only receive FM signals to receive a satellite signal — what he hears will be noise.

**This is the fundamental reason the present paper protects the S3 series of *Pointing at the Moon***: S3 (the *Treatise on the Hundred Dharmas* sequence) stands at the position of "the desire-realm human reception bandwidth," giving the highest-resolution map for that bandwidth. Without S3, S8 is only noise to the desire-realm human reader; with S3, S8 becomes a receivable signal. **S3 is not a low-grade version of S8; S3 is the precondition for S8 being receivable at all.**

### 3.3 Why the Three Layers Are Not a Staircase: Three Critical Doctrinal Distinctions

To distinguish the "three-layer container" from the "three-step staircase," three critical doctrinal distinctions must be made explicit.

**Distinction 1: The staircase model implies "upward replacement"; the container model does not.**

The unspoken premise of the staircase model is: once you have climbed to the third step, you no longer need the first step. But the three-layer container is not like this. When you stand within the dharmadhātu of the *Avataṃsaka*, the eight consciousnesses and fifty-one mental factors of Yogācāra have not vanished; they have only been embraced within the wider context of phenomenon-to-phenomenon non-obstruction. A bodhisattva at the *Avataṃsaka* level still has eye-consciousness, ear-consciousness, nose-consciousness, tongue-consciousness, body-consciousness, mind-consciousness, *manas*, and *ālayavijñāna* — only that, in his experience, these eight consciousnesses now operate in the mode of "phenomenon-to-phenomenon non-obstruction" rather than in the Initial Teaching mode of "inert suchness plus arising-and-ceasing *ālayavijñāna*."

Fazang himself, in fascicle two of the *Treatise* on the differentiation of consciousnesses, explicitly says:

> "Because of this, the profound dependent origination of the one mind is endowed with five doctrinal gates. Therefore the holy ones, in accordance with which gate they choose, draw in sentient beings: first, the gate of gathering the meaning according to the name — as taught by the Lesser Vehicle; second, the gate of gathering the principle according to the phenomena — as taught by the Initial Teaching; third, the gate of unobstructed principle and phenomena — as taught by the Final Teaching; fourth, the gate where the phenomenon dissolves and the principle is revealed — as taught by the Sudden Teaching; fifth, the gate where the nature-ocean is endowed with virtues — as taught by the Round Teaching."[^21]

"**The one mind is endowed with five doctrinal gates**" — a single "profoundly dependent-arising one mind" is endowed with five layers of doctrinal gates. The holy one, in accordance with the capacities of beings, selects which gate to use to draw them in. The five gates are not five different minds; they are five aspects of a single mind. **This is Fazang's most direct doctrinal articulation of "container, not hierarchy."**

**Distinction 2: The staircase model has an "absolute summit"; the container model has no absolute summit.**

The staircase model implies that the Round Teaching is the summit, the ultimate truth. But the container model cannot allow this reading: "round" is not "highest," it is "broadest" (containing all the bandwidths of the broadcast). If the "round" of the Round Teaching is read as "the most complete version," it immediately reverts to being the top of a staircase.

Fazang offers a remarkably subtle passage in fascicle one of the *Treatise* on the "five teachings mutually gathered and interpenetrated":

> "These five teachings, mutually gathered and interpenetrated, have five meanings. First, perhaps gathered as one — namely, that root and branch are fused together as a single great skillful means. Second, perhaps opened into two — first, the root teaching (the distinct one vehicle); second, the branch teaching (the Lesser Vehicle and the three vehicles); also called the ultimate and the expedient. Third, perhaps opened into three — namely, the one vehicle, the three vehicles, the Lesser Vehicle. Fourth, perhaps divided into four — namely, the Lesser Vehicle, the gradual, the sudden, the round. Fifth, perhaps scattered into five — as discussed above."[^22]

"**Perhaps gathered as one — namely, that root and branch are fused together as a single great skillful means**." All five teachings, at their highest level of integration, are in fact "one" — a single "great skillful means" (*mahā-upāya*). Note the word *upāya*: "skillful means" is *upāya* (expedient), not ultimate truth. In other words, **even the Round Teaching itself, from the highest perspective, is classified by Fazang as "skillful means," and skillful means, by the common Buddhist understanding, is not the ultimate**. The ultimate is the dharmadhātu itself — and the dharmadhātu itself is not exhaustively described by any teaching, not even the Round Teaching.

This is why the Round Teaching cannot be taken as the absolute summit. If you read the Round Teaching as the top of a staircase, you have shrunk the dharmadhātu to "the Round Teaching's description of the dharmadhātu" — you have read the broad as the large.

**Distinction 3: The staircase model assumes "the broader, the truer"; the container model does not.**

The unspoken rule of the staircase model is: descriptions at broader levels are closer to the truth. But the container model does not assume this. In the container model, **a narrow description is "true" for a narrow receiver and a broad description is "true" for a broad receiver, and there is no hierarchy of truth between them**.

A concrete example: when a desire-realm human practitioner encounters discursive thoughts during meditation, what he needs is not a Final-Teaching-style description like "discursive thoughts are intrinsically empty, fundamentally not a thing" (which, for him, is only knowledge without operational traction). What he needs is "which mental factor is this discursive thought? Is it craving, conceit, or doubt? Whose object of cognition is this mental factor? Is it the sixth consciousness or the seventh? What seed is this consciousness manifesting from?" This is the Yogācāra (Initial Teaching) mode of description, and for this desire-realm receiver, *this* mode is "true" (in the sense of being capable of producing real effects in his experience).

If you classify the Yogācāra description as "non-ultimate" and tell this reader "skip ahead to the *Avataṃsaka*," what he gets is a "broad description" that cannot be translated into practice within his own reception bandwidth — and for him, that broad description is "false" (in the sense of being incapable of producing real effects in his experience).

**"True" and "broad" are two independent axes**. The staircase model collapses them into one (broad = true), and this collapse is the staircase model's fundamental error. The container model preserves the independence of the two axes: in any given reception bandwidth, the description best fitted to that bandwidth is "true," regardless of where it sits in classificatory pedagogy as "broad" or "narrow."

### 3.4 The Common One Vehicle and the Distinct One Vehicle: Unfolding the Patriarch's Container Logic

Returning to the passage from Fazang quoted in §2.C.3:

> "One then comes to know that those very teachings of the three vehicles are not other than the distinct one vehicle. Why is this so? **Because they are the very things being pointed at. There is no other matter at all.**"

The four characters "**為彼所目故**" — "because they are the very things being pointed at" — deserve a section of their own. The key character is *mù* (目), which functions both as a verb (to look at, to point at, to aim at) and as a noun (eye, the object seen, the target aimed at). When Fazang writes "為彼所目故," he is saying two things at once:

1. The three vehicles are what the distinct one vehicle "aims at" — the distinct one vehicle is pointing at them
2. The three vehicles are what the distinct one vehicle "sees" — the distinct one vehicle is looking at them

In other words, **the distinct one vehicle is not a higher level standing above the three vehicles, but a "pointer" standing in the position of pointing at the three vehicles as its "referent."** A pointer without a referent is empty (there is nothing to point at); a referent without a pointer is isolated (it cannot be indicated). Pointer and referent are the two ends of a single act of pointing, mutually constitutive.

Unfolding this logic to the three layers:

- The distinct one vehicle (*Avataṃsaka* / Round Teaching) is the "pointer" — it points at the dharmadhātu itself
- The three vehicles (the Initial Teaching of Yogācāra, the Final Teaching of *tathāgatagarbha*, the Sudden Teaching of silent revelation) are the "pointed at" — they are the dharmadhātu's concrete manifestations in different reception bandwidths
- There is no hierarchy between "pointer" and "referent"; they are the two ends of a single dharmadhātu act of self-pointing

This is why Fazang adds "**and there is no other matter at all**" — there is no "other thing" between pointer and referent; it is all a single matter. There is no "different dharma" between the Round Teaching and the three vehicles; it is a single dharma in two aspects.

Fazang elsewhere employs the concept of "host and companion" (主伴) to further articulate this structure:

> "[The Round Teaching is] the round-bright nature-ocean of the dharmadhātu's dependent origination, where the unobstructed and self-determined 'one is all and all is one' is the round interpenetration of host and companion."[^23]

"**The round interpenetration of host and companion**" is the sister concept of the Gate of the Unobstructed Mutual Containment of the Broad and the Narrow. The core of round interpenetration of host and companion is this: when the dharmadhātu is viewed from a particular angle (a particular dust-particle, a particular consciousness, a particular mental factor), that angle is the "host" and everything else is the "companion"; when you switch to another angle, that new angle becomes the "host" and the original host becomes a "companion." There is no absolute host and no absolute companion — the assignment of host and companion depends entirely on which angle you are viewing from.

Applied to the three layers: when you view from the angle of a desire-realm human, Yogācāra (Initial Teaching) is the host and the Final and Round Teachings are companions; when you view from the angle of a form-realm meditator, *tathāgatagarbha* (Final Teaching) is the host and the Initial and Round Teachings are companions; when you view from the dharmadhātu's native angle, the *Avataṃsaka* (Round Teaching) is the host and the Initial and Final Teachings are companions. All three angle-assignments are "correct," because each is a legitimate vantage cut out of the unobstructed mutual containment of the broad and the narrow.

**This is the fullest articulation of "container, not hierarchy"**: there is no absolute master-slave relation between the three layers; there is only a relative, view-dependent exchange of host and companion.

### 3.5 Doctrinal Convergence: Praising the Tathāgatas as the Reverse Movement of Establishing Faith in Oneself

The four argumentative subsections (3.1 the restated thesis, 3.2 audience cosmology, 3.3 the three doctrinal distinctions, 3.4 the patriarch's container logic) may leave the reader with the impression of "four independent points." This section's function is to gather the four points back to the soteriological axis of the paper — the actual convergence of *praising the Tathāgatas* with the unobstructed mutuality of the broad and the narrow. But the convergence here may surprise the reader, because it is going to *reverse the direction of the act of praising*.

#### The Reader's Unnoticed Reorientation

Set aside, for a moment, the argumentative details of §3.1 through §3.4 and perform a simple introspective experiment. If you are a reader who has followed the *Pointing at the Moon* research project through S1 (Faith), S2 (Understanding — the *Heart Sūtra*), S3 (Practice — the *Hundred Dharmas*), S4 (Inquiry — AI and the Dharma), S5 (Cutting — the *Diamond Sūtra*), S6 (Illumination — the *Śūraṅgama*), S7 (Embracing — the *Lotus Sūtra*), and have now arrived at S8 (Manifestation — the *Avataṃsaka*), please answer this question honestly:

**When you now utter the line "*Namo Original Teacher Śākyamuni Buddha*," is what is actually happening in your mind the same thing that was happening three years ago, when you uttered the same line before encountering this research project?**

For nearly all readers who have walked this far, the honest answer is: **no, it is not the same thing**.

Three years ago, when you uttered this line, what was happening in your mind was the act of "an ordinary being expressing reverence to a distant awakened one." You were standing here, the Buddha was standing far away over there, an immeasurable kalpa of distance separated you, and your praise was a vocal event of looking up from a low place to a high place.

Three years on, when you utter this line, what is happening in your mind has changed. The butterfly effect of the initial aspiration in S1, the form-is-emptiness of S2, the hundred-dharmas-gate of S3, the *tathāgatagarbha* of S6 — this entire sequence of teaching has quietly reorganized your inner reference frame. When you now utter "*Namo Original Teacher Śākyamuni Buddha*," what your mind is actually doing is something else:

> **"If the Buddha's nature and my own nature are the same (the *tathāgatagarbha*; established in S1 and S6), and if the Buddha can attain these self-determined freedoms (*prajñā*, liberation, the unobstructed mutuality of the broad and the narrow; established in S2 and S5), then the possibility of these freedoms is also in me. My present praise is not the gazing-up of one being toward a distant other — it is the confirming of something already inside me, something I had not previously dared to recognize."**

Note this reversal. Your praise has shifted from being **other-directed** (pointing at the Buddha far away) to being **self-confirming** (recognizing the Buddha-nature already in yourself). The six syllables "*Namo Original Teacher Śākyamuni Buddha*" have not changed in sound or form, but their *function* in your inner life has changed: from being "an outward act of veneration" they have become **the reverse movement of establishing faith in oneself**.

#### Why This Reversal Is the Heart of the Unobstructed Mutuality of the Broad and the Narrow

Placed within the doctrinal structure of the unobstructed mutuality of the broad and the narrow, the audience cosmology of §3.2 here clicks into place:

The **narrow** level: you are *this* one person, in *this* present moment, uttering *this* one buddha-name.
The **broad** level: your nature and the Buddha's nature are one and the same; therefore *this* one person's *this* one act of praise is, intrinsically, "the entire *tathāgatagarbha* (which is the entire dharmadhātu) resonating with itself."

**Narrow (this single reader's sincere self-recognition) is broad (the dharmadhātu's response to itself)**. This is not two events in succession (first narrow then broad, or first broad then narrow); it is two intrinsic faces of a single event. At the moment you inwardly recognize "the possibility of these freedoms is also in me," the entire *tathāgatagarbha* structure of the dharmadhātu is activated *in* this recognition — not because you activated it, but because the dharmadhātu is intrinsically already in this recognition.

This is the deepest soteriological reading of the *Avataṃsaka*'s line "**in each and every particle of dust, there are buddhas as numerous as the particles of all worlds**." The "particle of dust" is not "a microscopically small physical unit" but "*this* concrete present moment" — the present this-reader, this-breath, this-buddha-name. The "buddhas as numerous as the particles of all worlds" are not "innumerable buddhas located far away" but "what is being recognized in this present moment as intrinsically pervasive and as having the same nature as you." When this recognition occurs, "narrow" (this single particle) and "broad" (buddhas of all worlds) are intrinsically not divided.

#### The Dharmadhātu's Counter-Praise — The Two-Way Structure of Praising

There is one further structure here that is critical and easily misread: **when the faith you are inwardly establishing is sincere, the dharmadhātu praises you back.**

The phrase "the dharmadhātu praises you back" is most easily misread, at the surface level, as "the Buddha as an external personal deity has noticed your devotion and is giving you a positive evaluation." This reading is wrong — it puts the Buddha back into the position of "external other," which directly contradicts the inward reversal just described.

The correct reading is this: when the faith you are inwardly establishing is sincere (not performed, not coerced, not anxiously seeking confirmation), the very sincerity of that faith generates a *resonance* within the unobstructed mutuality of the broad and the narrow. This resonance is not a Buddha "out there" awarding you points; it is the dharmadhātu itself, in this single "narrow" act of self-recognition, simultaneously displaying its "broad" face — and you will, at certain moments (not necessarily at the moment of praising itself; possibly days, months, or years later), feel that "something has responded to me." This feeling is not a hallucination, nor is it psychological self-suggestion; it is the concrete manifestation, in your reception bandwidth, of the intrinsic structure of the unobstructed mutuality of the broad and the narrow.

In other words: **the truthful content of "the Buddha praises you" is that, when your inward faith is real, the structure of the unobstructed mutuality of the broad and the narrow allows you to experience the "broad"'s response to the "narrow."** The form of the response varies from person to person — some experience a clear insight in meditation, some encounter a key person or book in daily life, some feel an inexpressible something quiet their inner restlessness in a single moment, some discover an unaccountable steadiness in the face of an impossible decision. All of these are the dharmadhātu's resonance to your own inner self-recognition; they are the "broad"'s call-and-response to the "narrow."

But this resonance has one strict condition: **it occurs only when the self-recognition is sincere**. If your praise still has the surface structure of "I am an ordinary being, the Buddha is a sage, I am asking the Buddha for a favor," then no resonance occurs — because the structure of the unobstructed mutuality of the broad and the narrow requires the "narrow" face actually to acknowledge that it is itself the "broad," before the resonance has a "matching reception bandwidth" on which to land. A false praise is not unanswered because the dharmadhātu is stingy; a false praise is unanswered because, structurally, no reception bandwidth has been opened to receive a response. **The dharmadhātu plays no favorites — but the dharmadhātu also does not transmit signals into a closed receiver.**

This is also why so many practitioners have recited buddha-names for an entire lifetime and yet feel they have "no resonance." It is not that the Buddha is stingy; it is that the inner structure of the praising has never reoriented. Every recitation has remained "other-directed"; none has become "self-confirming." Every recitation has pushed the Buddha further away; none has let the Buddha be recognized within. Under this other-directed structure, of course "no response" comes back — because the structure has been placing the dharmadhātu outside as an object from the very beginning, and the dharmadhātu has never been an object.

#### Why the S1–S7 Series Must Be Read Before S8

**This is the soteriological justification for "reading S1–S7 before S8."** S1 through S7 are not merely "preparing you to understand the doctrinal content of the *Avataṃsaka*"; they are, more fundamentally, **slowly reorganizing your inner reference frame, so that your praising shifts from outward-directed to self-confirming, and the reception bandwidth opens.**

To be specific:
- **S1 (Faith)** establishes the seed-doctrine of the initial aspiration: your initial aspiration already contains all the conditions for buddhahood. This is the first action of placing "the possibility of buddhahood" inside yourself.
- **S2 (Understanding — the *Heart Sūtra*)** establishes the structure of emptiness: the rigid boundary between "self" and "Buddha" begins to dissolve.
- **S3 (Practice — the *Hundred Dharmas*)** establishes a fine-grained map of consciousness: this map itself is training in "looking inward."
- **S4 (Inquiry — AI)** establishes that AI cannot replace cultivation: cultivation must be your own inner work; it cannot be outsourced.
- **S5 (Cutting — the *Diamond Sūtra*)** establishes the *je-fei* structure: every external form, including the very concept "Buddha," is swept clean.
- **S6 (Illumination — the *Śūraṅgama*)** establishes the *tathāgatagarbha*: it explicitly states that the *tathāgatagarbha* is *in your body*. This is the most direct doctrinal foundation for the inward reversal.
- **S7 (Embracing — the *Lotus Sūtra*)** establishes the one-buddha-vehicle: Buddha-nature shifts from being the privilege of a few to being a fact intrinsic to all sentient beings.

Readers who have completed these seven series have had their inner reference frame thoroughly reorganized. When such a reader enters S8 and reads the second of Samantabhadra's vows — *praising the Tathāgatas* — he can no longer use the outward-directed structure of three years ago to praise; his praise has automatically become self-confirming, even if he himself has not yet noticed the shift. **One of the functions of S8-P02 is to render this background shift thematically explicit, so that the reader becomes aware of it.**

Without this reorganization, the *Avataṃsaka* is, for you, only a beautiful poem; with this reorganization, the unobstructed mutuality of the broad and the narrow of the *Avataṃsaka* directly *is* the present-moment actuality of every buddha-name in your daily practice — there is nothing further to "cultivate and attain," because it is already operative in your simplest act of praising.

#### The Final Form of the Doctrinal Convergence

Putting the three-layer container (§3.1–3.4) together with this two-way praise structure:

- The **Initial Teaching (Yogācāra)** view of praise: praise is a vocal act of the sixth consciousness, initiated by mental cognition and instantiated by body-consciousness, with seeds being perfumed and manifesting in continuous succession.
- The **Final Teaching (*tathāgatagarbha*)** view of praise: praise is the manifestation, following conditions, of the original awareness of suchness in the moment of "false thoughts exhausted, mind clear" — it is suchness's remembering of itself.
- The **Round Teaching (*Avataṃsaka*)** view of praise: praise is the dharmadhātu in its totality, in this single "narrow" present moment, intrinsically resounding to its own "broad" face — narrow is broad, broad is narrow, no inside or outside, no praiser or praised.

**These three views are not three different acts of praising; they are three intrinsic layers of a single act of praising.** When you (a reader who has completed S1–S7) sincerely utter "*Namo Original Teacher Śākyamuni Buddha*," all three layers are operating intrinsically and simultaneously — the Initial Teaching's vocal act lands at the bodily level, the Final Teaching's suchness responds at the mind-ground level, the Round Teaching's unobstructed mutuality of the broad and the narrow resounds at the dharmadhātu level. The three layers do not need you to "activate" any one of them, nor do you need to "upgrade" between the three layers — you only need to sincerely acknowledge "this self-determined freedom is intrinsically already in me," and the three layers will operate simultaneously of their own accord, and the dharmadhātu's resonance will arise of its own accord.

This is the soteriological closing-up of the entire argument of the present paper: **the unobstructed mutuality of the broad and the narrow is not a distant attainment but the intrinsic structure that is already operative in the moment of your sincere acknowledgment of your own nature**. All the doctrinal discussions of the container thesis — the five teachings, the ten schools, the common and distinct one vehicles, the one mind endowed with five doctrinal gates — point to a single extremely simple inner movement: **shifting praise from outward-directed to self-confirming**. Once this turn occurs, the three-layer container is no longer a concept in a paper; it is the present-moment reality of every buddha-name in your daily practice; and the dharmadhātu's counter-praise is no longer a miraculous account in a sūtra; it is the intrinsic resonance you will naturally come to experience when your inward sincerity is real.

What the practitioner needs to do is not "upgrade praising from the Initial Teaching to the Round Teaching" (this very phrasing translates the broad and the narrow back into the large and the small); it is "stop blocking the intrinsic simultaneous operation of the three layers in the present-moment act of praise." There is only one way to block this: by putting the Buddha back outside and leaving yourself inside — this inside-versus-outside structure is the most direct opposite of the unobstructed mutuality of the broad and the narrow. There is also only one way to open it: **by acknowledging that what is outside is what is inside**.

## IV. Interdisciplinary Heuristic: The Camera Lens Analogy (⚠️)

This section uses one simple analogy — the focal lengths of camera lenses — to capture the *non-staircase* character of the three-layer container. The fuller interdisciplinary unfolding (black holes, the information paradox, AdS/CFT duality) is reserved for S8-P06 (the *Gate of Indra's Net*), where the doctrinal soil is ready to receive it. The present section is strictly under 600 words and offers only an analogical hint, with no claims of theoretical equivalence.

**Wide-angle lens, normal lens, macro lens.** A camera can mount three lenses of three different focal lengths to photograph the same scene.

- The **macro lens** corresponds to the Initial Teaching (Yogācāra): photographing a single insect on a single leaf, you can see the bristles on the insect's legs, the cells of its compound eye, the structure of its mouthparts. This focal length captures "the finest structure" but cannot capture "which tree the leaf belongs to, which forest the tree belongs to."
- The **normal lens** corresponds to the Final Teaching (*tathāgatagarbha*): photographing the tree on which the leaf grows, you see the whole posture of the tree, the relation of root and crown, the texture of the bark. This focal length captures "the individual and its environment," but cannot capture "the inner detail of the individual" and cannot capture "the layout of the entire forest."
- The **wide-angle lens** corresponds to the Round Teaching (*Avataṃsaka*): photographing the entire forest in a single panoramic frame, you see the relations among trees, between forest and valley, the structure of the entire ecology. This focal length captures "the overall layout" but cannot capture "any single insect on any single leaf."

**The point of the analogy is that the three lenses are not in a hierarchy.** The macro lens is not "an inadequate wide-angle lens"; the wide-angle lens is not "a macro lens that fails to capture detail." They are three independent tools, corresponding to three independent photographic targets. A photographer who carries only a wide-angle cannot photograph the bristles on the insect's legs; one who carries only a macro cannot photograph the panoramic layout of the forest. **A complete photographer needs all three lenses on hand at all times.**

Mapping the analogy back to the classification: a complete practitioner needs the Initial Teaching (to see the inner structure of his own consciousness clearly), the Final Teaching (to see how the suchness on which his consciousness rests follows conditions), and the Round Teaching (to see the dharmadhātu layout in which all of this is embedded). The three layers cannot replace one another.

**⚠️ Disclaimer of irreducibility:** The camera lens is only a heuristic; it is not a reduction of the classificatory pedagogy. The lens analogy has three critical points of failure:

1. **The lens analogy implies a "photographer standing outside" who switches lenses** — but the core of the unobstructed mutuality of the broad and the narrow is that there is no "outside"; the dharmadhātu has no external observer
2. **The lens analogy implies a "sequential switching" between three focal lengths** — but the sūtra emphasizes that the three layers of reception are *simultaneous*, not sequential
3. **The lens analogy implies "the same scene photographed in three ways"** — but the heart of the container thesis is that the three layers describe *different existential objects*, not merely "three resolutions of the same object"

When the reader reaches this section, please remember: the lens is only a temporary ladder propped up at the entry to the container thesis; once one has stepped inside, the ladder should be removed. If the reader, having finished this paper, is still using the image of "a photographer choosing lenses" to think about the five-teaching classification, this section will have done the reader harm.

## V. Doctrinal Self-Correction: Three ❌ and the Failure-Point Markers

### ❌ Correction 1: Refusing the Hierarchical Misreading "*Avataṃsaka* Surpasses Yogācāra" (Pedagogical Hazards #1, #11)

This is the principal corrective concern of the present paper, and the entire argument of §I through §III has been in service of it. To state it explicitly:

**The mistaken reading:** The five-teaching classification is a value hierarchy, with the Round Teaching (*Avataṃsaka*) at the summit, the Final Teaching next, the Initial Teaching (Yogācāra) lower, and the Lesser Vehicle at the bottom. To study Yogācāra is to remain passively, immaturely, at a lower level; to study the *Avataṃsaka* is the true ultimate gate. If you are still studying Yogācāra, this means you are not yet ready to receive the vast dharma-gate of the Round Teaching.

**Why it is wrong:** This reading collapses "broad" into "true" (broad = true, narrow = not true). Fazang himself, in the passage on "the one mind endowed with five doctrinal gates" in fascicle two of the *Treatise* (cited in §3.3), explicitly states that the five teachings are five doctrinal gates of "the same mind," not five hierarchically ordered tiers of one and the same gate. Fazang himself, in the passage on "because they are the very things being pointed at, there is no other matter at all" in fascicle one (cited in §3.4), explicitly locates the three vehicles (including Yogācāra) and the distinct one vehicle as the two ends of a single act of pointing, with no hierarchy. Fazang himself, in the passage "perhaps gathered as one — namely, that root and branch are fused together as a single great skillful means" in fascicle one (cited in §3.3), even classifies the entire five teachings (including the Round Teaching) as "skillful means," and skillful means in the common Buddhist understanding is not ultimate.

**The correction:** The five-teaching classification is the simultaneous manifestation of the dharmadhātu's broadcast across different reception bandwidths; the relation among the three layers is the unobstructed mutual containment of the broad and the narrow, not a hierarchical succession. A desire-realm human practitioner needs Yogācāra as the highest-resolution map of his own reception bandwidth; he does not need to, and should not, "upgrade upward" to the *Avataṃsaka* and abandon Yogācāra. The opposite is in fact true: **without Yogācāra as a base, the *Avataṃsaka* is, in the desire-realm human reception bandwidth, only a beautiful but unactualizable poem**. The S3 series of *Pointing at the Moon* is not a low-grade version of S8; **S3 is the necessary precondition for S8 to be receivable in the desire-realm human reception bandwidth**.

### ❌ Correction 2: Chronological Misplacement — The "*Avataṃsaka* Victory over Yogācāra" Never Took Place (Pedagogical Hazard #11, Reinforcement)

In contemporary Buddhist discourse there is a frequent unconscious historical imagination: that the Huayan school (Fazang) "defeated" the Yogācāra school (Kuiji) in classificatory pedagogy, that this was a real polemical contest, and that the position of the Round Teaching was established in the course of this contest.

**This historical imagination is not supported by the historical record.** The present section uses the chronological evidence assembled in Q09 of the supporting RAG search as a hard argument.

**The historical record:**
- **Kuiji** (632–682): died in the first year of the Yongchun era of Tang Gaozong
- **Fazang** (643–712): established Huayan classificatory pedagogy under Empress Wu Zetian
- **The *Treatise on the Distinct Doctrinal Categories of the One Vehicle of the Avataṃsaka***: composed approximately during the Chuigong years (685–688), that is, three to six years after Kuiji's death

**The conclusion:** Fazang's five-teaching classification did not exist during Kuiji's lifetime. **Kuiji could not, physically, respond to Fazang's classification.** The historical narrative "Huayan defeated Yogācāra in classificatory pedagogy" is, from the start, not history — this "debate" never took place between the two founding patriarchs, because one of them had already died before the other had proposed the position.

A more concrete piece of evidence: when Fazang in fascicle one of the *Treatise* reviews the "ten schools of classification, ancient and contemporary," he lists the ten schools known to him at the time (Bodhiruci's one-sound teaching, Dharmapāla's gradual-and-sudden two teachings, Guangtong's three teachings ... and the tenth, Xuanzang's three turnings of the Dharma Wheel).[^24] **Among these ten, Fazang names Xuanzang but does not name Kuiji** — because at the time Fazang was writing the *Treatise*, Kuiji's system had not yet stood out as an independent "school" (Kuiji's representative works, the *Cheng Weishi Lun Shuji* and the *Dasheng Fayuan Yilin Zhang*, had not yet circulated widely, and the name "Cien school" only crystallized after his death). The classification Fazang criticizes is the one *established by Xuanzang*, and Xuanzang's classification reaches at most the "three turnings of the Dharma Wheel" (turning, illuminating, sustaining), which does not yet have the developed precision of Kuiji's later system.

**The true historical picture:** Xuanzang (602–664) → Kuiji (632–682) → Fazang (643–712). The three masters are *successive* in time, not contemporaries in polemic. Kuiji inherited Xuanzang's Yogācāra tradition and significantly refined it (the *Cheng Weishi Lun Shuji* is Kuiji's most complete commentary on Yogācāra); Fazang, after Kuiji, established the Huayan school separately and created the five-teaching classification. **There was no real polemical encounter between the three masters on classificatory pedagogy** — they were founding patriarchs of separate systems built in temporal succession, not co-debating opponents on the same stage.

In Tang times, the Yogācāra school (the Cien school) and the Huayan school were two flourishing parallel traditions, each commanding deep respect from the other. Kuiji himself held the *Avataṃsaka* in profound regard, and Fazang gave clear acknowledgment to Xuanzang's translation enterprise.[^25] The "Huayan defeated Yogācāra" narrative is a retrospective imagination of later periods (especially after the Song), not a Tang-dynasty historical fact.

**The significance of the correction:** If even between the two founding patriarchs there was no real polemical encounter, then on what grounds should contemporary readers continue to read "Huayan vs. Yogācāra" as a victory contest? The container thesis is not a rhetorical reconciliation but a *historical recovery* — the five-teaching classification was, from the start, not a value hierarchy but a metaclassificatory ordering of teachings; Yogācāra and the *Avataṃsaka* were, from the start, not opponents but two professional positions within a single tradition.

### ❌ Correction 3: The Unobstructed Mutuality of the Broad and the Narrow Is Not Physical Scale-Freedom (Extension of Pedagogical Hazard #10)

**The mistaken reading:** The unobstructed mutuality of the broad and the narrow means that size can be freely toggled, in a manner analogous to the "shrinking ray" or "enlarging ray" of science fiction, or to "fractal self-similarity" in mathematics.

**Why it is wrong:** The unobstructed mutuality of the broad and the narrow is a statement about a doctrinal structure, not about a physical event. "A particle of dust contains the ten directions" is not the assertion that the particle's physical volume has increased to that of the ten directions, nor that the volume of the ten directions has been compressed to that of the particle — both of these readings translate "broad and narrow" back into "large and small" (a quantitative concept), whereas the "broad and narrow" of the unobstructed mutuality is a qualitative concept, unrelated to physical magnitude.

More precisely: in the language of the *Avataṃsaka*, "a single particle" and "the ten directions" are not two values along a single dimension; they are two *different descriptive axes* altogether. "A single particle" is the endpoint of the *concreteness* axis (extreme concreteness); "the ten directions" is the endpoint of the *totality* axis (extreme totality). Concreteness and totality are not two mutually exclusive values; they are two independent descriptive dimensions — an event can be simultaneously extremely concrete (this one, just this) and extremely total (containing all, pervading the dharmadhātu), and the two are mutually unobstructed.

**The correction:** When the reader encounters any of the analogies in this paper — the three layers, the camera focal lengths, the reception bandwidths — please remember that all of these are temporary translations of "broad and narrow" into "large and small." The genuine unobstructed mutuality of the broad and the narrow is "the simultaneous operation of concreteness and totality," not "a free toggle between large scale and small scale."

Any discourse that equates the unobstructed mutuality of the broad and the narrow with quantum entanglement, the holographic principle, fractal geometry, or AdS/CFT duality is reducing a doctrinal structure to a physical event, and this is ❌. Physical concepts can serve as heuristics for entry into the doctrinal structure of the unobstructed mutuality of the broad and the narrow (this is what S8-P06 will do), but they cannot be equated with it. Physical concepts will become outdated; the unobstructed mutuality of the broad and the narrow will not.

### Failure-Point Markers

The argumentative form of this paper has three explicit points of failure:

1. **The very phrase "three layers" is a failure point.** The reception bandwidths of the dharmadhātu's broadcast are not three layers but innumerable layers; the present paper uses "three layers" to align with the range of the five-teaching classification relevant to Yogācāra (Initial, Final, Round), and this is a pedagogical slicing for convenience, not the intrinsic structure of the dharmadhātu.

2. **The very metaphor "container" is a failure point.** "Container" implies an inside-versus-outside relation between "container" and "contained," but the heart of the unobstructed mutuality of the broad and the narrow is the absence of inside and outside; the present paper uses "container, not hierarchy" to correct the staircase reading, but the container metaphor itself, once it has done its corrective work, should also be set down.

3. **The very word "simultaneous" is a failure point.** The paper repeatedly says that the three layers are "simultaneously" present, but "simultaneous" is a temporal concept, while the unobstructed mutuality of the broad and the narrow is not on the temporal axis at all — it is neither simultaneous nor sequential; it is a mode of being "not structured by time."

When the reader reaches these three failure points, please understand: **these three failure points are not places where the present paper is poorly written; they are places where the unobstructed mutuality of the broad and the narrow, as the object of discourse, intrinsically resists every discursive form**. Any paper that uses linear language to talk about the unobstructed mutuality of the broad and the narrow will fail at these three places, no matter how carefully its author works. The present paper's choice is to mark these three places of failure explicitly, so that the reader knows where the brush is set down, and what to rely on beyond that line.

> *A three-dimensional brush cannot write a ten-dimensional object. All it can do is to mark the place where the brush is set down — so that the reader knows that, beyond this line, what remains is the reader's own dharma-eye.*[^26]

## VI. The Practice Correlation: Praising the Tathāgatas · The Gate of the Unobstructed Mutual Containment of the Broad and the Narrow

### 6.1 Samantabhadra's Vow

> "Furthermore, good son, as for praising the Tathāgatas: in each and every particle of dust, throughout all the buddha-fields of the ten directions and the three times — particles as numerous as can be exhausted in the entire dharmadhātu and in space — there are buddhas as numerous as the particles of dust of all worlds; and around each and every one of these buddhas there is an ocean-assembly of bodhisattvas. ... With a wondrous tongue-faculty surpassing that of the goddess of eloquence, from each and every tongue-faculty there issues an ocean of inexhaustible voices, and from each and every voice there issues an ocean of all phrasings, with which I praise the ocean of qualities of all the Tathāgatas, throughout the entire future without interruption, pervading the dharmadhātu without remainder." (*Avataṃsaka-sūtra*, fascicle 40, *Practices and Vows of Samantabhadra*)

### 6.2 The Mystery Gate: Definition

**The Gate of the Unobstructed Mutual Containment of the Broad and the Narrow** (廣狹自在無礙門) is the second of Fazang's ten mystery gates. As defined in fascicle one of the *Exploring the Mysteries of the Avataṃsaka*: "Insofar as the divided is the undivided and the undivided is the divided, the broad and the narrow are mutually self-determined and unobstructed." The "divided" (narrow) and "undivided" (broad) are not in the relation of larger set to smaller set; they are the two intrinsic faces of a single event. A particle of dust does not need to expand in order to be the ten directions; the ten directions do not need to compress in order to be a single particle of dust.

### 6.3 Practice Correlation Table

| Vow Element | Mystery-Gate Correlate | Doctrinal Convergence | Practice Pointer |
|---|---|---|---|
| "In each and every particle of dust" | Narrow (extreme concreteness) | The entry to practice must be *this* concrete present moment | Do not wait until you are "ready" to praise |
| "There are buddhas as numerous as the particles of all worlds" | Broad (extreme totality) | The entry's present moment intrinsically contains the totality | A single buddha-name pervades the dharmadhātu in the moment of utterance |
| "From each and every tongue-faculty there issues an ocean of inexhaustible voices" | Broad within the narrow | A single bodily act contains innumerable utterances | Sincerity is the only condition |
| "Throughout the entire future without interruption" | The unobstructed mutuality of broad and narrow on the temporal axis | A single moment of praise pervades all three times | Continuity is not "straining not to stop"; it is "not blocking the intrinsic non-stopping" |

### 6.4 Practice Pointers

For desire-realm human readers, the practice of *praising the Tathāgatas* is most easily misunderstood as "I must say many words of praise, I must be enthusiastic, I must persist, I must not stop." This reading translates the "broad" of the unobstructed mutuality of the broad and the narrow into "quantitatively large" — say more, say longer, say more frequently. But the structure of the sūtra is not this. "From each and every tongue-faculty there issues an ocean of inexhaustible voices" does not mean that you must strenuously cause your tongue-faculty to produce inexhaustible voices; it means that when you sincerely utter a single word of praise, the intrinsic structure of *that single word* is already an ocean of inexhaustible voices — you do not need to perform addition.

To make it concrete in daily practice: in the morning, upon waking, place your palms together and utter a single line: "*Namo Original Teacher Śākyamuni Buddha*." **In the moment of this single line, the unobstructed mutuality of the broad and the narrow is already complete** — you do not need to make up the next ninety-nine, you do not need to extend it for an hour, you do not need to mobilize all the bodhisattvas to praise alongside you. One line, sincere, present-and-complete. If you then wish to utter another, that is a separate present-and-complete event in its own right, not an extension of the first.

"Throughout the entire future without interruption" likewise does not mean "you must strain to keep praising without stopping"; it means "the moment of sincere praise intrinsically pervades the three times" — and this pervading is not done *by you*; it is the intrinsic structure of the unobstructed mutuality of the broad and the narrow in operation. What the practitioner is to do is not to strain to pervade the three times, but not to block the intrinsic pervasion.

**The most common inner obstacle**: the inner obstacle most often encountered in the practice of *praising the Tathāgatas* is "Is my praising any use? Is the praising of an ordinary being like me worthy of the ocean of qualities of the Tathāgatas?" The root of this obstacle is the translation of broad-and-narrow into large-and-small — "How could a being as small as I am be worthy of qualities as great as those?" The reply of the unobstructed mutuality of the broad and the narrow is: you are not "worthy of"; your praise is *itself* part of the ocean of qualities. There is no "worthiness" question between your praise and you, between the ocean of qualities and the Tathāgatas — because the question of worthiness presupposes that the two are separate things, whereas the structure of the sūtra is that the two are intrinsically not separate.

**The deepest pointer**: the direction of the praising decides everything. The detailed doctrinal handling has been given in §3.5; here only the simplest formula: **when you praise, look inward, do not look outward**. When you utter "*Namo Original Teacher Śākyamuni Buddha*," do not picture the Buddha far away in some pure land — let this buddha-name be recognized in your own chest, let the word "Buddha" point at *the something already in you* (not the small self, but the intrinsic praising-thing already there behind the small self). When this self-confirming praise sincerely takes place, **the dharmadhātu praises you back** — not as a reward, but as the concrete resonance of the intrinsic structure of the unobstructed mutuality of the broad and the narrow within your reception bandwidth. The form of the resonance varies from person to person, but the resonance will surely come, provided that the direction of the praising is genuinely inward.

If you have not yet experienced this resonance, do not be anxious — this does not mean that you have done something wrong. It only means that the direction of your praising is still in the slow process of turning. Continue to praise, one line, sincere, present-and-complete. The turning will happen of itself.

如法讚嘆，如是無礙。

> *Praising in accordance with the dharma, thus is unobstructedness.*

## VII. Conclusion

### 7.1 Concise Restatement

Fazang's five-teaching classification is not a value hierarchy but the dharmadhātu's broadcasting structure. A single dharma, when received by the reception bandwidth of desire-realm human consciousness, manifests as Yogācāra (Initial Teaching); when received by a more refined bandwidth, manifests as *tathāgatagarbha* (Final Teaching); when received in the dharmadhātu's native bandwidth, manifests as the *Avataṃsaka* (Round Teaching). The relation among the three layers is the unobstructed mutual containment of the broad and the narrow, not a hierarchical succession. Fazang himself, with the single line "because they are the very things being pointed at, there is no other matter at all," locates the three vehicles (including Yogācāra) as the *intrinsic content* of the distinct one vehicle (the *Avataṃsaka*), not as a lower tier to be transcended.

### 7.2 Practice Guidance

For readers of the S3 (*Hundred Dharmas*) series of *Pointing at the Moon*: please continue to study Yogācāra. Yogācāra is the highest-resolution map for the desire-realm human reception bandwidth; without it, the *Avataṃsaka* is, in your bandwidth, only a poem. **S3 is not a low-grade version of S8; S3 is the precondition for S8 to be receivable for you.**

For readers who want to skip directly into S8: please first check whether you have, through S2 (the *Heart Sūtra*) and S3 (the *Hundred Dharmas*), established a basic reception bandwidth. If you have not, S8 will be, for you, a highly beautiful but ungroundable broad description, and after reading you will retain nothing that can operate in your daily experience. If you have already established a basic reception bandwidth, S8 will, for you, be the dharmadhātu's intrinsic unfolding — you do not need to "discard" your earlier study; what you need is to allow your earlier study and S8's broad description to operate simultaneously.

For the daily practice of Samantabhadra's second vow, *praising the Tathāgatas*: please remember — *one line, sincere, present-and-complete*. Do not translate the "broad" of the unobstructed mutuality into "quantitatively large." You do not need to perform addition — when you sincerely praise a single line, the unobstructed mutuality of the broad and the narrow is already complete, and what remains is not in your hands.

Or *tathāgatagarbha*'s "suchness following conditions" intuition (Final Teaching)? Or the *Avataṃsaka*'s "phenomenon-to-phenomenon non-obstruction" panorama (Round Teaching)? What does "simultaneous operation of the three layers" actually mean?

S8-P03 (*Broadly Cultivating Offerings · The Gate of the Mutually Self-Determined Inclusion of All Dharmas*) will address this question: how, at the level of the body, the three layers of description are simultaneously operated. Offerings as the most concrete bodily action (a single stick of incense, a single cup of water, a single bouquet of flowers) — how are these simultaneously offerings at the level of the dharma-realm of phenomena (事法界), at the level of the dharma-realm of principle (理法界), and at the level of the unobstructed dharma-realm of phenomena and phenomena (事事無礙法界)? How does the "simultaneous" operation of these three levels unfold at the bodily level? This is the open question of P03.
