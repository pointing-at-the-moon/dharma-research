## Abstract

This paper continues from S6-P08 (*The Fifty Skandha-Māras: General Framework and the Ten Realms of the Rūpa-Skandha*) into the second mirror of the *Śūraṅgama*'s diagnostic apparatus: the ten demons (魔) of the *vedanā*-skandha (受陰). Its central thesis is that **the ten *vedanā*-demons are not a new category invented by the *Śūraṅgama*; they are the early Buddhist "second arrow" of the *Sallatha Sutta* (*Saṃyukta-āgama* 470) returning in refined form at the depths of *samādhi*.** Where the rūpa-skandha's danger lies in misidentifying an *object* of awareness (a vision of buddhas, an experience of psychic vision, an apparent attainment of empty space), the vedanā-skandha's danger lies in misidentifying an *act of judgment about a state* — the practitioner does not mistake what is seen, but what he himself is feeling. The former still admits of an external referent that can be refused; the latter has no referential content to refuse, only the texture of the mind itself, and that texture is precisely what the meditator has been pursuing.

The paper proceeds along four axes. First, it takes the *Sallatha Sutta*'s parable of the two arrows — the unawakened person is wounded by both bodily feeling and the mental reaction to that feeling, while the noble disciple is struck by only the first — as the controlling image, and reframes the ten *vedanā*-demons as the *concealed* return of the second arrow at the depths of meditative absorption. Second, it grounds this reframing in four Āgama loci on *vedanā*: the *Madhyama-āgama*'s *Satipaṭṭhāna-sutta* (no. 98) on *vedanānupassanā* ("contemplation of feelings as feelings"), and the triad of *Saṃyukta-āgama* 466 (feelings arise dependent on contact), 467 (the three feelings as suffering, sword-thrust, and impermanence), and 468 (the three feelings as the basis of the three latent tendencies — *rāgānusaya*, *paṭighānusaya*, *avijjānusaya*). Third, it offers an original structural reading of the *Śūraṅgama*'s ten demons as **five paired reverse-overextensions** (compassion ↔ frenzy, recollection ↔ contentment, sorrow ↔ delight, conceit ↔ self-satisfaction, emptiness ↔ desire) — a structure that emerges from the text itself once one stops reading the ten as a linear progression. Fourth, it draws on Zǐxuán's *Yìshū zhùjīng* (T1799) on the structural placement of *vedanā* as "the head of the four mind-skandhas" (四心初首), and on Jiāoguāng Zhēnjiàn's *Zhèngmài shū* (X0275) on the phenomenology of "external demons that have been summoned but have not yet manifested in person," to explain why the demonic activity at this stage is "self-overturning" (自顛, Jiāoguāng's term) rather than "possession of another."

Zǐxuán supplies the line that anchors the entire paper. In his commentary on the first of the ten demons he writes: **"This [state] is not sagely realization; the moment one grasps it, demons are drawn in. The other [nine] all follow this pattern."** Twelve characters that contain the whole of S6-P09: the demonic event at this stage is not the arising of these states, but the act of taking them as evidence of attainment.

## I. Introduction

### 1.1 The Two Arrows

In the fifth century BCE the Buddha is recorded as offering, to a gathering of monks, a parable that has come down to us in the *Saṃyukta-āgama* as Sūtra 470 and in the Pāli canon as the *Sallatha Sutta* (SN 36.6).[^1]

> 「愚癡無聞凡夫生苦樂受、不苦不樂受，多聞聖弟子亦生苦樂受、不苦不樂受。諸比丘！凡夫、聖人有何差別？……譬如士夫身被雙毒箭，極生苦痛，愚癡無聞凡夫亦復如是。增長二受，身受、心受，極生苦痛。……多聞聖弟子身觸生苦受，大苦逼迫，乃至奪命，不起憂悲稱怨、啼哭號呼、心亂發狂，當於爾時，唯生一受，所謂身受，不生心受。譬如士夫被一毒箭，不被第二毒箭。」
>
> "The ignorant unlearned worldling experiences pleasant feeling, painful feeling, and neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling; the well-instructed noble disciple also experiences these three feelings. What, monks, is the difference? … It is as if a person were struck by two poisoned arrows: he gives rise to two feelings — bodily feeling and mental feeling — and is wracked with pain. … When a well-instructed noble disciple is touched by painful bodily feeling, even unto the loss of life, he does not give rise to grief, lament, weeping, or derangement. At that time he experiences one feeling only: bodily feeling, not mental feeling. It is as if he were struck by one arrow but not by a second."[^2]

The strength of the parable lies in its dissection of "suffering" into two distinct events. The first arrow is the bodily feeling itself — the physical impact of the wound, which any sentient being will receive. The second arrow is the mental response to the first: the interpretation, the resistance, the longing, the story. The noble disciple is not free of the first arrow; he is free of the second. Suffering, on this analysis, lies not in feeling itself but in the *judgment about* feeling. The body is wounded, but it is the second arrow — not the first — that drives the worldling into "grief, lament, weeping, derangement."

A simple question follows. When a meditator has crossed the threshold of the rūpa-skandha and entered the territory described in the *Śūraṅgama*'s ninth fascicle as "the minds of all the buddhas appearing as images in a clear mirror" — that is, when he has entered a state of absorption in which ordinary bodily feeling no longer disturbs him — where has the second arrow gone?

The answer this paper proposes: **the second arrow has not vanished. It has become invisible.**

### 1.2 Central Thesis: The Refined Return of the Second Arrow

In the worldling's condition the two arrows are easy to tell apart. When you are insulted, the first arrow is the sound; the second is the interpretation "he is humiliating me." When you receive bad news, the first arrow is the auditory sense-datum; the second is the verdict "I am ruined." The two arrows in worldly experience stand in vivid contrast: one is sensory fact, the other is mental story. The traditional teaching of *vedanānupassanā* in the *Satipaṭṭhāna-sutta* trains the meditator to distinguish them at this coarse level — "when feeling a pleasant feeling, he knows: 'I am feeling a pleasant feeling.'"

In the depths of *samādhi*, the situation changes. When the rūpa-skandha is exhausted and the *vedanā*-skandha's "region of confinement" (受陰區宇) appears, the meditator has already passed beyond the coarse five hindrances (*kāma-cchanda*, *vyāpāda*, *thīna-middha*, *uddhacca-kukkucca*, *vicikicchā*) and can perceive interior visions in deep concentration. At this stage "bodily feeling" is no longer the rough sensation of the physical body — it has become "the savor of meditative absorption," "subtle joy," "boundless lightness," "the vast openness of luminous emptiness." And "mental feeling"? Mental feeling has become **the verdict passed upon these subtle feelings** — "I have attained sagely realization," "I am already Vairocana," "I have obtained unhindered liberation."

Both arrows are now refined. They no longer present themselves with the worldling's clarity of contrast. The first arrow (the subtle bodily savor of absorption) and the second arrow (the verdict that this savor is a mark of sainthood) arise in the depths of meditation almost simultaneously, almost identically in appearance, almost indistinguishable to the meditator himself. This is the foundational difficulty of the ten *vedanā*-demons: **how do you refuse "deep peaceful joy"? How do you identify, as a demonic event, a verdict that looks so much like sagely realization?**

The thesis of this paper is therefore: **the ten *vedanā*-demons are not a new category invented by the *Śūraṅgama* but the refined return, at the depths of *samādhi*, of the *Sallatha Sutta*'s second arrow.** Each of the ten — pity, frenzy, brooding recollection, premature satisfaction, ceaseless sorrow, uncontrolled delight, towering conceit, satisfied lightness, nihilistic emptiness, and refined desire — is a transformation of the second arrow. The shape has changed, the object has changed, the texture has been refined past recognition; but the structural function is unchanged: **a wrong verdict passed upon some meditative feeling, mistaking what is not sagely realization for sagely realization.**

The Song-dynasty commentator Zǐxuán places, in his note on the first of the ten demons, a single line that can serve as the spine of the entire paper: **"This is not sagely realization; the moment one grasps it, demons are drawn in. The other [nine] all follow this pattern."** (非為聖證，取即引魔。他皆倣此。)[^3] Translated: these states themselves are not sagely realization; the moment you grasp them as such, the demonic event has begun. The other nine cases follow the same logic. Twelve characters; nothing more is needed.

### 1.3 Why the Vedanā-Skandha Is Harder Than the Rūpa-Skandha

At the close of S6-P08 I left an open question: why is the *vedanā*-skandha harder than the rūpa-skandha? The answer can now be stated directly.

Each of the ten realms of the rūpa-skandha has a clear *object*: the body that passes through walls (no. 1), the inner body from which one can pluck out worms (no. 2), the spirit that separates from the body (no. 3), the visions of buddhas in the surrounding space (no. 4), the radiance that pervades empty space (no. 5), the ability to see in darkness (no. 6), the body that becomes like grass and wood (no. 7), the vision that perceives the ten directions at once (no. 8), the perception of distant sights and sounds (no. 9), the bodily transformation (no. 10). Each of these has an external, identifiable, refusable object. When the practitioner refrains from "sagely conceit," he can say: "this body that passes through walls is not my sagely realization; this image of the buddha is not my sagely realization; this vision of the ten directions is not my sagely realization." The thing being refused is plainly delineated.

The ten realms of the *vedanā*-skandha are wholly different. The first is "boundless pity," the second "boundless courage," the third "fixed brooding without dispersal," the fourth "settling down with little gained," the fifth "ceaseless sorrow," the sixth "uncontrolled welling joy," the seventh "the towering of great conceit," the eighth "boundless pacification," the ninth "the verdict of nihilistic emptiness," the tenth "boundless arising love." None of these is an *object*; each is a *state*. They are not what the practitioner sees but what the practitioner is feeling. **They do not stand opposite the awareness; they are the very coloration of the awareness itself.**

A structural double-bind follows: how does one refuse a *state*? One can refuse an object — "the buddha-image I am seeing is not my sagely realization" — but one cannot refuse a state by the same gesture. One cannot say "this boundless lightness I am feeling is not my sagely realization," because the subject of the sentence "I am feeling boundless lightness" is itself an "I" presently being defined by that lightness. The state and the verdict arise simultaneously; there is no temporal interval into which the practitioner can insert the act of "not grasping."

A deeper layer follows: **these feelings are precisely what the meditator has been pursuing all along.** Who undertakes meditative training without aiming at "boundless welling joy," "boundless lightness," "the heart at ease in pure stillness," "the round comprehension of the subtle principle in luminous clarity"? If none of these is sagely realization, what then is the point of the practice? If "boundless lightness" is to be refused, what remains of meditation as such?

The answer is concealed in the most ancient stratum of the *vedanānupassanā* teaching: **the goal of meditation is not to possess any feeling, but to know any feeling as it is.** "When feeling a pleasant feeling, he knows: 'I am feeling a pleasant feeling'" is not enjoyment of pleasant feeling; it is awareness that pleasant feeling is occurring. The former is endorsement; the latter is observation. The whole of the ten *vedanā*-demons is the slippage from "observing pleasant feeling" to "endorsing pleasant feeling": the practitioner is no longer watching the feeling, he is owning it. Once he owns it, the verdict "I have attained" follows of itself, and the second arrow is loosed.

Zǐxuán makes an additional structural observation that bears directly here. To the question "if exhausting the rūpa-skandha is itself a meditative attainment, why does the sūtra not say that one who exhausts the rūpa-skandha 'ascends through the sixty sagely stations,' but only says this of one who exhausts the *vedanā*-skandha?" he answers: **"The rūpa-skandha, though broken, has not yet broken the mind, so it does not constitute the ascent through the sagely stations — it is the most coarse. The *vedanā*-skandha is the head of the four mind-[skandhas], so when one breaks the *vedanā*-skandha, one is said to ascend through the sixty sagely stations."**[^4] The decisive phrase is **"the head of the four mind-[skandhas]"** (四心初首). The *vedanā*-skandha is the first of the four mind-skandhas (vedanā, saṃjñā, saṃskāra, vijñāna); break the head, and the other three follow. The rūpa-skandha is matter (form); the four that follow are mind. The transition from rūpa to *vedanā* is the transition from "the practice of the body" to "the practice of the mind." The rūpa-skandha is only the threshold; the *vedanā*-skandha is the doorway.

It is precisely because the *vedanā*-skandha is "the head of the four mind-skandhas" that its diagnostic apparatus carries deeper structural weight than the rūpa-skandha's. The worst outcome of misidentifying the tenth realm of the rūpa-skandha is "this is called a deviant mind, harboring the māras" (此名邪心，含受魑魅);[^5] the misidentification of any of the ten *vedanā*-realms produces the verdict, repeated ten times, "**he loses correct samādhi and falls into sinking destruction**" (失於正受，當從淪墜). To "lose *samādhi*" is gravely worse than "to harbor the māras" — to lose *samādhi* is to lose the meditative ground itself, which is to say, to nullify everything that has been built before.

This is why the *Śūraṅgama*'s tone in the *vedanā* section abruptly tightens. The tenfold repetition of "he loses correct samādhi and falls into sinking destruction" is not textual redundancy; it is structural alarm.

## II. Scriptural Foundations

This section follows the project's "sūtra first, śāstra second" ordering. Part A presents two clusters of canonical texts: the early Āgama materials on *vedanā* and the *Śūraṅgama*'s own exposition of the ten demons. Part B presents the two commentarial supports: Zǐxuán's *Yìshū zhùjīng* (T1799) and Jiāoguāng Zhēnjiàn's *Zhèngmài shū* (X0275).

### A. Sūtra: Āgama and Śūraṅgama

#### 2.1 The Āgama Foundations of *Vedanā*-Practice

**2.1.1 *Vedanānupassanā* in the *Satipaṭṭhāna-sutta***

The *Madhyama-āgama*'s *Satipaṭṭhāna-sutta* (no. 98) preserves the most complete early teaching on the four foundations of mindfulness. Its second foundation — "contemplation of *vedanā* as *vedanā*" (觀覺如覺念處) — corresponds to *vedanānupassanā*. The text unfolds the practice as the three feelings cross-cut by six dimensions:[^6]

> 「云何觀覺如覺念處？比丘者，覺樂覺時，便知覺樂覺。覺苦覺時，便知覺苦覺。覺不苦不樂覺時，便知覺不苦不樂覺。覺樂身、苦身、不苦不樂身；樂心、苦心、不苦不樂心；樂食、苦食、不苦不樂食；樂無食、苦無食、不苦不樂無食；樂欲、苦欲、不苦不樂欲；樂無欲、苦無欲覺、不苦不樂無欲覺時，便知覺不苦不樂無欲覺。如是比丘觀內覺如覺，觀外覺如覺，立念在覺，有知有見，有明有達，是謂比丘觀覺如覺。」
>
> "How does a monk contemplate feelings as feelings? When feeling a pleasant feeling, he knows: 'I am feeling a pleasant feeling.' When feeling a painful feeling, he knows: 'I am feeling a painful feeling.' When feeling a neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling, he knows: 'I am feeling a neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling.' [And likewise for] pleasant-of-the-body, painful-of-the-body, neutral-of-the-body; pleasant-of-the-mind, painful-of-the-mind, neutral-of-the-mind; pleasant-with-attachment, painful-with-attachment, neutral-with-attachment; pleasant-without-attachment, painful-without-attachment, neutral-without-attachment; pleasant-with-desire, painful-with-desire, neutral-with-desire; pleasant-without-desire, painful-without-desire, neutral-without-desire — when feeling each, he knows it. Thus does a monk contemplate feelings as feelings within and without, with mindfulness established in feelings, with knowing, with vision, with clarity, with penetration."[^7]

The decisive grammatical structure is "便知覺 X 覺" — "feeling X, he knows feeling X." When feeling X arises, he knows, in the very moment of arising, that X is the feeling that is occurring. This is the bare structure of *vedanānupassanā*: **no resistance, no pursuit, no misidentification — only knowing-as-it-is.** Note that this "knowing-as-it-is" is pure observation, not appraisal. The training is not to evaluate pleasant, painful, and neutral feelings as "good" or "bad," but to recognize, in the moment of their arising, that what is occurring is pleasant or painful or neutral — and nothing more.

This basic training is, at the worldly level, a threshold exercise; at the depths of *samādhi*, it becomes the central craft that must be sustained even after the threshold is crossed. The ten *vedanā*-demons are precisely what occurs when *vedanānupassanā* is forgotten.

**2.1.2 Feelings Arise Dependent on Contact (*Saṃyukta-āgama* 466)**

> 「有三受——苦受、樂受、不苦不樂受。此三受何因、何集、何生、何轉？謂此三受，觸因、觸集、觸生、觸轉；彼彼觸因、彼彼受生，若彼彼觸滅，彼彼受亦滅、止、清涼、沒。」
>
> "There are three feelings: painful, pleasant, and neither-painful-nor-pleasant. What is the cause of these three feelings, their origin, their genesis, their unfolding? These three feelings have contact as cause, contact as origin, contact as genesis, contact as unfolding; from this or that contact this or that feeling arises, and when this or that contact ceases, the corresponding feeling also ceases, comes to rest, becomes cool, vanishes."[^8]

The decisive term is *sparśa* (觸, contact). The fundamental mechanism of the three feelings is "contact gives rise to feeling": the meeting of sense-faculty, sense-object, and consciousness yields contact, and contact yields feeling. This mechanism is the most basic link in the chain of dependent origination as it appears in both Āgama and Yogācāra. It tells us: **feeling is always dependently arisen; there is no independently subsisting feeling.** Without contact, there is no feeling.

For the ten *vedanā*-demons this is essential. Each of the ten demons is a "subtle feeling" arising in the wake of some "meditative state" (a kind of contact). If the practitioner can know-as-it-is at the level of contact, without passing on a verdict at the level of feeling, the door to the ten demons is shut. Conversely, the moment the practitioner forgets that "feeling depends on contact" and treats feeling as an independent, possessable, verifiable thing, the ten demons have an entry point.

**2.1.3 The Three Feelings as Suffering, Sword-Thrust, and Impermanence (*Saṃyukta-āgama* 467)**

> 「觀於樂受而作苦想，觀於苦受作劍刺想，觀不苦不樂受作無常想。若彼比丘觀於樂受而作苦想，觀於苦受作劍刺想，觀不苦不樂受作無常、滅想者，是名正見。」
>
> "Contemplate pleasant feeling as suffering; contemplate painful feeling as a sword-thrust; contemplate neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling as impermanent. If a monk contemplates pleasant feeling as suffering, painful feeling as a sword-thrust, and neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling as impermanent and subject to cessation, this is called right view."[^9]

The counter-intuitive force of this passage is essential. The Buddha does not teach that pleasant feeling is good, painful feeling bad, and neutral feeling best. He teaches the meditator to *contemplate pleasant feeling as suffering* (because it must inevitably decay), *painful feeling as a sword-thrust* (because it is direct and immediate oppression), and *neutral feeling as impermanent* (because it is the latent ground for further attachment). This is **counter-pursuit** in its strictest form: the most welcome feeling is to be regarded with the greatest vigilance.

Translated directly into the Śūraṅgama's framework: **the "boundless welling joy," "boundless lightness," and "boundless arising love" of the meditative depths are the most dangerous states, not the best ones.** The sixth, eighth, and tenth demons of the *vedanā*-skandha — uncontrolled delight, satisfied lightness, and refined desire — all belong to the pleasant-feeling system. Each is the specific failure to apply *Saṃyukta-āgama* 467's "contemplate pleasant feeling as suffering" at the depth where it is most needed.

**2.1.4 The Three Feelings and the Three Latent Tendencies (*Saṃyukta-āgama* 468)**

> 「有三受——苦受、樂受、不苦不樂受。觀於樂受，為斷樂受貪使故，於我所修梵行；斷苦受瞋恚使故，於我所修梵行；斷不苦不樂受癡使故，於我所修梵行。」
>
> "There are three feelings: painful, pleasant, and neither-painful-nor-pleasant. Contemplate pleasant feeling: in order to abandon the latent tendency to greed [*rāgānusaya*] that arises with pleasant feeling, train in the holy life under me. To abandon the latent tendency to aversion [*paṭighānusaya*] that arises with painful feeling, train in the holy life under me. To abandon the latent tendency to ignorance [*avijjānusaya*] that arises with neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling, train in the holy life under me."[^10]

The mapping is: pleasant feeling → latent greed, painful feeling → latent aversion, neutral feeling → latent ignorance. Each of the three feelings has its own corresponding mechanism of defilement. Pleasant feeling tends to provoke greed (the wish to keep it); painful feeling tends to provoke aversion (the wish to push it away); neutral feeling tends to provoke ignorance (overlooking its dependently arisen nature). This will function in §3.6 as a clinical differential-diagnostic instrument for the ten *vedanā*-demons, splitting them into three groups according to which latent tendency dominates.

**2.1.5 The Two Arrows (*Saṃyukta-āgama* 470)**

The fifth Āgama text is the *Sallatha Sutta* itself, already quoted in §1.1. For completeness it is registered here as the structural pivot of the entire paper. **The final point of *vedanānupassanā* is not the elimination of the first arrow (which is impossible) but the severing of the second** — the verdict the mind passes upon the body's feeling. The noble disciple is not without pain; he is in pain without derangement, in pain without the second arrow's added story.

These five passages — the *vedanānupassanā* of the *Satipaṭṭhāna-sutta*, contact-as-cause-of-feeling (SĀ 466), the three feelings as suffering/sword/impermanence (SĀ 467), the three feelings and the three latent tendencies (SĀ 468), and the two arrows (SĀ 470) — together constitute the early canonical foundation of this paper's argument. Taken together they tell us one thing: **a thousand years before the *Śūraṅgama* appeared, the handling of *vedanā* was already the central craft of Buddhist practice. The *Śūraṅgama*'s ten *vedanā*-demons are not a new invention — they are the refined version of *vedanānupassanā* at the depths of meditation.**

#### 2.2 The *Śūraṅgama*'s Ten Vedanā-Demons

The ninth fascicle of the *Śūraṅgama* presents, after the rūpa-skandha section, the second major treatment of demonic territory. The sūtra first defines "the region of confinement of the *vedanā*-skandha" (受陰區宇) and "the exhaustion of the *vedanā*-skandha" (受陰盡), then unfolds the ten demonic realms in sequence. Each is structured according to a near-formula: **occasion of arising → excessive reaction → "this is called X" → "if one is awake to it, no harm; if one takes it as sagely realization, the X-demon enters his heart-organ" → "he loses correct *samādhi* and falls into sinking destruction."** The repetition of this formula is itself didactic — it inscribes the diagnostic procedure into the reader's memory.

**2.2.1 The Definition of the Vedanā-Region**[^11]

> 「阿難！彼善男子修三摩提，奢摩他中色陰盡者，見諸佛心，如明鏡中顯現其像。若有所得而未能用，猶如魘人，手足宛然，見聞不惑，心觸客邪而不能動；此則名為受陰區宇。若魘咎歇，其心離身，返觀其面，去住自由，無復留礙，名受陰盡，是人則能超越見濁。觀其所由，虛明妄想以為其本。」
>
> "Ānanda, when this good man cultivating *samādhi* has, in *śamatha*, exhausted the rūpa-skandha, he sees the minds of all the buddhas as images appearing in a clear mirror. If he attains something but cannot yet make use of it, he is like a person under the spell of a nightmare: his hands and feet are intact, his seeing and hearing are unconfused, but though his mind is touched by intruding deviance, he cannot move. This is called the region of confinement of the *vedanā*-skandha. When the nightmare lifts and his mind departs from his body, he is able to look back upon his own face and to come and go freely without further obstruction. This is called the exhaustion of the *vedanā*-skandha; this person is then able to transcend the turbidity of views. Examining its source, *empty-luminous false thinking* is its root."

Several points are decisive. First, the marker of the rūpa-skandha's exhaustion is "seeing the minds of all the buddhas as images in a clear mirror" — this is an extraordinarily lofty attainment that the unawakened can scarcely imagine. Second, despite this vision, "what is attained cannot yet be used." Third, the simile of the dream-paralysis: the practitioner sees but cannot move. This is the essential phenomenology of the *vedanā*-region — **already seeing but not yet usable; already opened but not yet free of the body.** Fourth, the marker of the *vedanā*-skandha's exhaustion is "the mind departs from the body and looks back upon its own face," able to come and go without obstruction. Fifth, the root of the *vedanā*-skandha is "empty-luminous false thinking" — Zǐxuán glosses this as the false-thinking structure of "empty-penetrating reception" (虛通領納). Where the rūpa-skandha's root is "solid false thinking" (the fixedness of the sense of materiality), the *vedanā*-skandha's root is "empty-luminous false thinking" (the empty-penetrating receptivity of feeling). The shift from the solid to the empty-luminous is a transition from the formed to the formless. And this is precisely what makes the *vedanā*-skandha harder to cure: the solid can be broken, but the empty-luminous cannot — it can only be seen through.

**2.2.2 The Ten Demons in Sequence**

For brevity I summarize each demon by occasion, content, verdict, and consequence, preserving the key technical phrases. All passages are from the ninth fascicle of the *Śūraṅgama*; further footnotes are not given for each demon individually.[^12]

**1. The Demon of Pity** ("inward suppression in excess"). Occasion: seeing great radiance; the heart awakens; inward suppression becomes excessive. Content: boundless pity arises, viewing even mosquitoes and gnats as one's own infants; tears flow involuntarily. Verdict: this is "the function of self-reproach gone to excess" (功用抑摧過越). The original intention was to spur and reproach oneself in order to break the *vedanā*-skandha; but suppression in excess produces a pathological pity. Recognized, it dissipates of itself; taken as sagely realization, the pity-demon enters the heart-organ.

**2. The Demon of Frenzy** ("rashness in excess"). Occasion: seeing the rūpa-skandha dissolve and the *vedanā*-skandha clarify; "victorious signs" appear; gratitude in excess. Content: boundless courage, the will to equal all buddhas, the conviction that "the three incalculable kalpas can be transcended in a single thought." Verdict: "the function of rashness gone to excess" (功用凌率過越) — what was meant to spur progress collapses into the delusion that the three kalpas need not be traversed.

**3. The Demon of Recollection** ("training without wisdom, losing oneself"). Occasion: nothing new has been realized, the old ground has been left behind, intellectual force weakens, falling into the middle ground with nothing to see. Content: a great parched longing arises; brooding recollection without dispersal; this is mistaken for "the appearance of diligent effort." Verdict: "training the mind without wisdom, losing oneself" (修心無慧自失) — having no new attainment ahead and no familiar ground behind, the practitioner is suspended between the two pillars and clings to brooding as if it were progress.

**4. The Demon of Premature Contentment** ("losing the constant deliberation, sinking into knowledge"). Occasion: wisdom-power exceeds concentration-power; the keen edge is lost. Content: holding "victorious natures" within the heart, the practitioner privately suspects himself to be Vairocana and settles down with what little he has gained. Verdict: "the use of mind that has lost the constant deliberation and sunk into knowledge" — concentration and wisdom are imbalanced, and wisdom in excess of concentration produces this pathological satisfaction.

**5. The Demon of Ceaseless Sorrow** ("losing skillful means in cultivation"). Occasion: nothing new has been realized; the old mind is gone; surveying both edges, peril is born of itself. Content: boundless sorrow; like sitting on a bed of iron, like drinking poison; the mind no longer wishes to live; the practitioner constantly seeks others to take his life so as to obtain liberation early. Verdict: "cultivation that has lost skillful means" (修行失於方便) — without skillful means to bear with the mind in absorption, anxiety becomes unbearable. Note that the fifth demon has progressed to the literal level of self-mutilation and the wish for death — this is not metaphor; this is the *Śūraṅgama*'s explicit warning of a meditative pathology.

**6. The Demon of Uncontrolled Delight** ("pacification without the wisdom to restrain"). Occasion: the mind dwells in clear stillness, becomes settled and at ease. Content: boundless welling joy, irrepressible laughter, singing and dancing in the public street; the practitioner declares himself to have attained unhindered liberation. Verdict: "pacification without the wisdom to restrain" (輕安無慧自禁). Pacification (*praśrabdhi*) is one of the seven factors of awakening — itself a legitimate branch of the path — but "without the wisdom to restrain" it becomes a seed of agitation.

**7. The Demon of Towering Conceit** ("seeing the victorious without the wisdom to save oneself"). Occasion: the practitioner privately declares himself to be sufficient. Content: the seven kinds of conceit erupt at once; "the heart becomes contemptuous even of the tathāgatas of the ten directions, let alone the lower vehicles of *śrāvakas* and *pratyekabuddhas*." The practitioner refuses to bow at *stūpas* or shrines and "destroys *stūpas* and shrines, smashes scriptures and images," declaring metal and clay images to be only metal and clay, scriptures only leaves and bark, while "this body of flesh is truly eternal." Verdict: "seeing the victorious without the wisdom to save oneself" (見勝無慧自救). The consequence is the literal destruction of scriptures and images — this directly echoes the consequence S6-P07 traced for the fourth pure injunction's prohibition against falsely claiming sagely attainment.

**8. The Demon of Satisfied Lightness** ("wisdom acquiring lightness"). Occasion: in subtle clarity the principle is roundly understood; great accord is attained. Content: boundless lightness arises; the practitioner declares himself to have become a sage, attained great mastery, satisfied himself, and seeks no further advancement. Verdict: "from wisdom acquiring various kinds of lightness" (因慧獲諸輕清). This is, in Zǐxuán's gloss, the case in which "wisdom arises within concentration and accords momentarily with principle"; the practitioner, finding mind-and-body harmonious, declares himself enlightened. The terror of the eighth demon lies in its lack of obvious pathology (unlike the fifth's despair or the seventh's destruction): it is a *satisfied stagnation*. The "ignorant *bhikṣus*" (無聞比丘) of the canonical accounts are precisely this type.

**9. The Demon of Nihilistic Emptiness** ("turning toward eternal extinction"). Occasion: in luminous understanding the practitioner attains the empty-luminous nature; suddenly he turns toward eternal extinction. Content: he denies cause and effect; he plunges entirely into emptiness; an annihilationist verdict hardens. He slanders the keeping of precepts as a "lesser vehicle" — "for the bodhisattva who has realized emptiness, what observance and what violation can there be?" — and begins, before donors of faith, to drink wine, eat meat, and engage broadly in defiled conduct. Verdict: this is the case in which "from the absorption of perfect *samādhi* a wisdom of emptiness arises; understanding the principle of intrinsic emptiness, [the practitioner] develops on this basis a view, becoming the ill-grasped emptiness, and so denies cause and effect and produces the verdict of annihilation" (Zǐxuán). The ninth demon is the most theoretical of the ten *vedanā*-demons: it misreads "emptiness" as "annihilation" and applies that misreading to conduct, producing the literal thesis "there is no need to keep precepts."

**10. The Demon of Refined Desire** ("savoring the empty-luminous, penetrating to the bone"). Occasion: the practitioner savors the empty-luminous and lets it penetrate to the bone. Content: boundless love arises; love at its peak becomes derangement, then becomes lust. The practitioner teaches that "desire is the path of awakening," gathers white-robed lay followers and instructs them to engage in "equal-handed sexual practice," names the fornicators "true sons of the Dharma," and so on, until the demonic power departs and worldly punishment overtakes him. Verdict: "the meditative state has settled smoothly into the heart; without the wisdom to hold, [the practitioner] has wrongly entered the realm of desires" (定境安順入心，無慧自持，誤入諸欲). The decisive word is **"savor" (味)**. To savor is to taste, to dwell on, to feel finely. When the practitioner begins to *savor* the meditative state of empty-luminosity itself, love arises; when love reaches its peak, it transmutes into lust. Note the order of the sequence: *first* the savoring of the meditative state, *then* lust; first the aesthetic absorption, *then* the eruption of desire. The tenth demon is the terminus of the *vedanā* section and at the same time the bridge to the *saṃjñā* section, where P10 will pick up the thread.

**2.2.3 The Vedanā Conclusion: "Manifestations Arising in *dhyāna*" and "Mutual Working of Mind"**

> 「阿難！如是十種禪那現境，皆是受陰用心交互，故現斯事。眾生頑迷，不自忖量，逢此因緣，迷不自識，謂言登聖；大妄語成，墮無間獄。」
>
> "Ānanda, these ten kinds of manifestations arising in *dhyāna* are all the mutual working of mind in the *vedanā*-skandha, and that is why they appear. Sentient beings, dull and confused, do not measure themselves; encountering these conditions, they fail to recognize them and declare that they have ascended to sainthood. Great false speech is formed, and they fall into the Avīci hell."[^13]

Two phrases in this conclusion deserve attention. First, **"manifestations arising in *dhyāna*"** (禪那現境). The sūtra does not call the ten "demons" or "obstacles" or "errors" — it calls them *manifestations arising in dhyāna*. This wording is decisive: **it tells us that these states are themselves natural to the depths of meditation; they are not the failure of practice. The failure lies in misjudgment, not in arising.** Zǐxuán's gloss is the most precise: "they are not sagely realization; the moment one grasps them, demons are drawn in. The other [nine] all follow this pattern."

Second, **"the mutual working of mind"** (用心交互). Zǐxuán glosses this as "concentration and wisdom out of balance; skillful means and patient endurance lost; from the loss of method, errant thoughts arise; and so there are these ten kinds of states — all the mutual working within the heart, drawing in the demons from without."[^14] The mutual working of mind is the root mechanism of the ten *vedanā*-demons: an imbalance of concentration and wisdom — concentration over wisdom, or wisdom over concentration — produces a fluctuating mutual working in which errant thoughts arise. Note Zǐxuán's wording: **"the mutual working within the heart, drawing in the demons from without."** The root of the demonic event is within; its manifestation may be described as "drawn in from without"; but the root is always within.

### B. Śāstra: Zǐxuán and Jiāoguāng

#### 2.3 Zǐxuán's *Yìshū zhùjīng* (T1799)

The Northern Song commentator Chángshuǐ Zǐxuán (965–1038) produced what is arguably the most rigorous commentary on the *Śūraṅgama*. His handling of the *vedanā* section yields three structurally significant points.

**2.3.1 *Vedanā* Is "the Head of the Four Mind-Skandhas"**

Zǐxuán's explanation of why the exhaustion of the *vedanā*-skandha (and not of the rūpa-skandha) qualifies the practitioner to "ascend through the sixty sagely stations" rests on a structural observation of the first importance. He sets the question himself:

> 「問：破色陰時已是成就觀行，何故不說上歷聖位耶？答：色陰雖破，未能破心，故未成就上歷聖位，以最麁故。受陰既是四心初首，故破受陰，得說上歷六十聖位。問：若言破色是麁故不說者，今破受陰，望於想行亦即是麁，何故得說上歷聖位耶？答：雖望餘陰亦即是麁，然受等四陰同是心法，故利根人若破受陰，三蘊隨破，不同破色鈍根之者未能破想，觀力弱故，故在區宇。」
>
> "Question: when the rūpa-skandha is broken, this is already an accomplishment of contemplative practice; why does the [sūtra] not say that one then ascends through the sagely stations? Answer: the rūpa-skandha, though broken, has not yet broken the mind, so it does not constitute the ascent through the sagely stations — it is the most coarse. The *vedanā*-skandha is the head of the four mind-skandhas, so when one breaks the *vedanā*-skandha, one is said to ascend through the sixty sagely stations. Question: if the breaking of the rūpa-skandha is omitted because it is coarse, then the breaking of the *vedanā*-skandha, viewed in relation to *saṃjñā* and *saṃskāra*, is also coarse — why is the [practitioner] said to ascend through the sagely stations? Answer: though [the *vedanā*-skandha] is also coarse in relation to the remaining skandhas, the four [mind-]skandhas — *vedanā* and the others — are all mind-dharmas; for the keen-faculty practitioner, when the *vedanā*-skandha is broken, the [following] three skandhas are broken with it, unlike the dull-faculty practitioner who, after breaking the rūpa-skandha, has not yet broken the *saṃjñā*-skandha, his contemplative power being weak, and therefore remains in the region of confinement."[^15]

The decisive phrase is "the *vedanā*-skandha is the head of the four mind-skandhas" — the *vedanā*-skandha is the first of the four mind-skandhas, that is, the *head*. The rūpa-skandha is rūpa-dharma, of the body; the transition from rūpa to *vedanā* is the transition from the practice of the body to the practice of the mind. For the keen-faculty practitioner, breaking the *vedanā*-skandha entails the breaking of *saṃjñā*, *saṃskāra*, and *vijñāna* with it, since they are all mind-dharmas. The dull-faculty practitioner alone needs to break each skandha in turn. **The rūpa-skandha is only the threshold; the *vedanā*-skandha is the doorway. Once the *vedanā*-skandha has been misjudged as sagely realization, the further three skandhas have nothing to stand on.**

**2.3.2 "This Is Not Sagely Realization; the Moment One Grasps It, Demons Are Drawn In"**

In his gloss on the first demon (the demon of pity), Zǐxuán sets down a verdict that he then explicitly extends to the other nine:[^16]

> 「此結判也。心光忽現，內抑太過，憂悲種發，非為聖證，取即引魔。他皆倣此。」
>
> "This is the verdict. The light of the heart suddenly appears; inward suppression becomes excessive; the seeds of sorrow and pity sprout. This is not sagely realization; the moment one grasps it, demons are drawn in. The other [nine] all follow this pattern."

These twelve characters — **"this is not sagely realization; the moment one grasps it, demons are drawn in. The other all follow this pattern"** — are the spine of the entire chapter. They tell us three things. First, these states themselves are not sagely realization. Second, the moment one grasps any of them as sagely realization, the demonic event has begun. Third, the other nine cases are governed by the identical logic; Zǐxuán explicitly forbids the rereading of the same logic in the remaining nine commentaries.

The phrase "the moment one grasps it" (取即) deserves close reading. *Upādāna* (取) is a technical term in the Āgamas, denoting the act of seizing, appropriating, treating something as one's own, treating it as real and possessable. The ten *vedanā*-states are themselves merely "manifestations arising in *dhyāna*"; the moment the practitioner generates *upādāna* toward them — the moment he overlays the verdict "this is my sagely realization" on the manifestation — the demonic event arises. Note the precise structural point: **"upādāna" is not the manifestation but the verdict passed upon it; the ten demons are not the failure of meditation but the failure of judgment.**

**2.3.3 "Drawing in the Demons from Without" and "Each According to Its Kind"**

Zǐxuán's gloss on the *vedanā*-conclusion:[^17] "the mutual working of the *vedanā*-skandha is the failure to balance concentration and wisdom and to establish skillful endurance; from the loss of method, errant thoughts arise; and so there are these ten kinds of states — all the mutual working within the heart, drawing in the demons from without. If one can recognize them, one will not fall into deviant views."

Note the structure of "the mutual working within the heart, drawing in the demons from without": the root of the demon is within the heart (mutual working within), the manifestation is described as "drawn in by kind" (各以類至). Zǐxuán refuses to read the demonic event of the *vedanā*-skandha as literal possession by external spirits; he reads it instead as a structure of "calling by kind" — the practitioner generates a certain category of errant thought within, and this draws into resonance an external demonic force of the matching category, the two echoing each other. Even so, the initiative remains within: "if one can recognize it, one will not fall into deviant views."

#### 2.4 Jiāoguāng Zhēnjiàn's *Zhèngmài shū* (X0275)

The late-Ming commentator Jiāoguāng Zhēnjiàn (d. 1606) produced what is arguably the most architecturally rigorous of the *Śūraṅgama*'s commentaries. He proposed a famous threefold division of the fifty skandha-māras that S6-P08 has already deployed for the rūpa-skandha and that S6-P09 must extend to the *vedanā*-skandha.

**2.4.1 The Threefold Division: rūpa as "Self-Manifesting," vedanā as "Concealed Penetration," saṃjñā as "Open Manifestation"**

Jiāoguāng's gloss on the line "or your skandha-demons, or the heavenly demons; or attached to spirits, or beset by māras":[^18]

> 「或汝陰魔者，色陰中十種，方是初心自現，尚無外魔，故言汝陰魔也。受中十種，已召外魔，然且潛入身中，而魔未現身。想中十種，方有天魔、鬼神、魑魅，若不聞知，則認賊為子，在所不免。或自任為聖，或認魔為聖，皆名認賊為子。」
>
> "'Your skandha-demons' refers to the ten realms of the rūpa-skandha, which are the initial manifestations arising from one's own mind, with no external demon involved — hence 'your skandha-demons.' The ten of the *vedanā*-skandha have already summoned external demons, but these have only crept into the body and have not yet manifested in person. The ten of the *saṃjñā*-skandha at last bring forth heavenly demons, spirits, and *māras*. If one does not know them by hearing, one will inevitably 'recognize the thief as one's son.' To take oneself for a sage, or to take the demon for a sage — both are 'recognizing the thief as one's son.'"

This division parses the first thirty skandha-māras into three layers:

- **Rūpa-skandha (skandha-demons proper):** "initial manifestations arising from one's own mind, with no external demon" — purely the practitioner's own mental display, no external demonic agency.
- **Vedanā-skandha (external demons concealed but unmanifest):** "they have already summoned external demons, but these have only crept into the body and have not yet manifested in person" — the external demons have been called but only enter the body in concealment, without taking visible form.
- **Saṃjñā-skandha (heavenly demons and spirits manifesting openly):** "at last there are heavenly demons, spirits, and *māras*" — only at this stage do genuine external demons take visible form.

S6-P09 must look closely at the middle layer. What does "external demons concealed but unmanifest" mean? Jiāoguāng makes it clearer in his gloss on the *vedanā*-conclusion:[^19]

> 「全同色陰結意。於此十種魔事已成，非但如前方為引發之端，然亦但言魔以類至，而不歷言天魔飛精。又魔即暗入本行人心，令其不覺自顛，亦不同於後十，待至後文自見。」
>
> "[The conclusion] is in full agreement with the rūpa-skandha conclusion. In these ten realms the demonic event is now complete; it is no longer merely an occasion for arising, as in [the rūpa-skandha], but is described only as 'demons drawn in by kind,' with no specific reference to heavenly demons sending forth their spirit-emanations. The demon, that is, **darkly enters the heart of the practitioner himself and causes him to overturn himself unaware** [魔即暗入本行人心，令其不覺自顛]. This is unlike the [demonic event of] the latter ten, which we shall see in due course."

The decisive sentence is "**the demon darkly enters the heart of the practitioner himself and causes him to overturn himself unaware**." Here the practitioner "overturns himself unaware" (不覺自顛) — he has, without noticing, become his own undoing. This is the central dynamic of the *vedanā*-skandha's demonic event: **the demon does not need to manifest from outside, because the practitioner himself is already the demon's mouthpiece.** The *vedanā*-skandha has no "possession of another" — that belongs to the *saṃjñā*-skandha. The *vedanā*-skandha has only "self-overturning" — the practitioner overturns himself.

**2.4.2 The Gradient "from the Subtle to the Manifest"**

In his note on the tenth realm of the rūpa-skandha, Jiāoguāng gives an articulation of the rūpa→*vedanā*→*saṃjñā* gradient:[^20]

> 「當知行人用功自淺而深，魔魅肆擾由微而著，故此節與下受陰十境皆且暗中入心令自發亂，直至想陰中方以飛附旁人顯然誑惑，豈有發端之初即遣實人來惑亂哉。」
>
> "One should know that the practitioner's effort proceeds from the shallow to the deep, and that demonic disturbance proceeds from the subtle to the manifest. Therefore in this section [the rūpa-skandha] and in the following ten realms of the *vedanā*-skandha, [the demons] only enter the mind in the dark and cause the practitioner to be disordered of himself, until in the *saṃjñā*-skandha alone they 'fly and possess another' and openly deceive. How could the initiating phase have already sent real persons to come and disturb?"

"From the subtle to the manifest" is the key. The demonic event of the rūpa-skandha is at its most subtle (purely the mind's own manifestation), the *vedanā*-skandha's at the middle (covertly entering the mind to cause self-disorder), the *saṃjñā*-skandha's at its most manifest (flying spirit-possession of others, open deceit). This is not arbitrary order; it is the reflection of meditative depth: **the deeper the practice, the subtler the form of the demon; the subtler the demon, the harder it is to recognize.** The middle position of the *vedanā*-skandha — between the wholly internal (rūpa) and the wholly external (*saṃjñā*) — is precisely what makes it hardest to treat. It cannot be assigned to "one's own mind" (as the rūpa-skandha can), nor to "external demons" (as the *saṃjñā*-skandha can); it is "external demons darkly entering the practitioner's own heart" — internal and external mixed, subject and object hard to tell apart.

Zǐxuán and Jiāoguāng share a decisive point: **both refuse to read the demonic event of the *vedanā*-skandha as literal spirit-possession.** Zǐxuán speaks of "calling by kind" and "mutual working within"; Jiāoguāng speaks of "self-overturning" and "darkly entering the heart." Both locate the root of the demonic event in the practitioner's inner structure. This is essential for what follows: it allows the language of phenomenology and clinical description to operate in this terrain without sliding into a literal demonology.

## III. Core Argument

This section unfolds in seven layers. §3.1 answers the open question left by S6-P08 (why is the *vedanā*-skandha harder than the rūpa-skandha?). §3.2 reframes the ten demons as the pathological inverse of *vedanānupassanā*. §3.3 presents the paper's principal original contribution: the "five paired reverse-overextensions" structure. §3.4 deepens the phenomenology of "external demons concealed but unmanifest." §3.5 retraces the two-arrows parable in its refined form at the depths of meditation. §3.6 deploys the three latent tendencies as a clinical differential diagnosis. §3.7 returns to Zǐxuán's "head of the four mind-skandhas" to explain the structural weight of the *vedanā* section.

### 3.1 Object versus Act of Judgment: Why the Vedanā-Skandha Is Harder

The *vedanā*-skandha is harder than the rūpa-skandha for one reason that can be stated in a sentence: **the rūpa-skandha's danger is the misidentification of an *object*; the *vedanā*-skandha's danger is the misidentification of an *act of judgment about a state*.** These are two wholly different kinds of danger.

Each of the rūpa-skandha's ten realms has a clear *object* — the body that passes through walls, the inner body from which one can pluck out worms, visions of buddhas, radiance pervading space, and so on. Each of these objects is "external" — it stands opposite the awareness; it is the thing being observed. When the practitioner refrains from "sagely conceit," he can say: "this body that passes through walls is not my sagely realization; this image of the buddha is not my sagely realization; this vision is not my sagely realization." The thing to be refused is plain, identifiable, refusable.

The ten realms of the *vedanā*-skandha are wholly different. The first's "boundless pity," the second's "boundless courage," the third's "fixed brooding," the fourth's "settling for little," the fifth's "ceaseless sorrow," the sixth's "boundless welling joy," the seventh's "the towering of great conceit," the eighth's "boundless lightness," the ninth's "the verdict of nihilistic emptiness," the tenth's "boundless arising love" — none of these is an *object*; each is a *state*. They are not what the practitioner sees but what the practitioner feels. **They do not stand opposite the awareness; they are the very coloration of the awareness.**

A structural double-bind follows. How does one refuse a *state*? One can refuse an object — "this image I see is not sagely realization" — but one cannot refuse a state by the same gesture. The sentence "this boundless lightness I am feeling is not my sagely realization" has, as its grammatical subject, the very "I" that is presently being defined by lightness. State and verdict arise simultaneously; there is no temporal interval into which the practitioner can insert the act of "not grasping."

Deeper still: **these feelings are precisely what the meditator has been pursuing all along.** Who undertakes meditative training without aiming at "boundless welling joy" and "boundless lightness"? Who sits through long retreats without longing to "dwell in the heart at ease in pure stillness"? If none of these is sagely realization, what then is meditation for?

The answer is hidden in the most ancient stratum of the *vedanānupassanā* teaching: **the goal of meditation is not to possess any feeling, but to know any feeling as it is.** "When feeling a pleasant feeling, he knows" is not the enjoyment of pleasant feeling; it is the awareness that pleasant feeling is occurring. The first is endorsement; the second is observation. The whole of the ten *vedanā*-demons is the slippage from observation to endorsement: the practitioner is no longer watching the feeling, he is owning it. Once he owns it, the verdict "I have attained" follows of itself, and the second arrow has flown.

### 3.2 The Ten Demons as the Pathological Inverse of *Vedanānupassanā*

The ten *vedanā*-demons are not a new category; they are the pathological inverse of *vedanānupassanā*. The positive practice of *vedanānupassanā* is "feeling X, he knows feeling X" — when feeling X arises, he knows-as-it-is, without resistance, pursuit, or misidentification. The inverse is "feeling X, he grasps feeling X" — when feeling X arises, the practitioner takes it as his sagely realization, makes it his possession, makes it his identity.

The contrast is exact:

- **Vedanānupassanā (positive):** feeling X arises → knowing-as-it-is → feeling X passes → quiescence.
- **Ten vedanā-demons (inverse):** feeling X arises → taken as sagely realization → "I have attained!" → loss of *samādhi*.

The training of *vedanānupassanā* inserts "knowing-as-it-is" between "feeling arises" and "verdict arises," intercepting the second arrow. The failure of the ten *vedanā*-demons is the replacement of "knowing-as-it-is" with "grasping-as-such" — the practitioner is no longer observing the feeling; he is endorsing it.

A finer question follows: **why does *vedanānupassanā*, which is foundational at the worldly level, fail at the depths of meditation?**

At the worldly level the five hindrances have not yet been suppressed, so the objects of "knowing-as-it-is" are coarse — "right now greed is arising," "right now aversion is arising" — coarse defilements that can be identified by ordinary attention. In the depths of *samādhi*, the five hindrances have been suppressed, and subtle meditative joy has displaced coarse sensual joy, and subtle dharmic joy has displaced coarse worldly joy. The objects facing "knowing-as-it-is" have been refined: it must now recognize, in "boundless lightness," that "this is only lightness, not sagely realization"; in "the round comprehension of the subtle principle," that "this is only a momentary accord between intellect and principle, not sagely realization." **The objects of "knowing-as-it-is" have changed from coarse defilements to subtle meditative states, but the instrument of knowing remains the same.** The instrument has not been refined; the objects have. The instrument is therefore overstretched.

This is why the *Śūraṅgama* in the *vedanā* section repeatedly stresses "balance of concentration and wisdom," "skillful endurance," "without the wisdom to restrain," "without the wisdom to save oneself," "without the wisdom to hold," "without wisdom, losing oneself." **The character "wisdom" appears five times in the verdicts of the ten *vedanā*-demons.** What is wisdom here? Wisdom is the precise extension of "knowing-as-it-is" at the subtle level. The loss of wisdom is not loss of mindfulness; the loss of wisdom is loss of *discernment* — the loss of the capacity to render precise diagnostic judgments at the level of subtle feeling.

**3.2.1** It should be noted in passing that the ten *vedanā*-demons are not the five hindrances. The standard exposition of the five hindrances in *Saṃyukta-āgama* 715 — sensual desire, aversion, sloth-and-torpor, restlessness-and-worry, doubt — names the coarse defilements of the worldly stage. The ten *vedanā*-demons arise only at the depths of meditation, after the five hindrances have already been suppressed. To mistake an everyday sadness for "the demon of pity," or an everyday excitement for "the demon of frenzy," is a confusion of levels. Everyday emotions belong to the level of the five hindrances; the fifty skandha-māras belong to the level of *dhyāna*. The former are met by foundational *vedanānupassanā* and mindfulness training; the latter, by the precise discernment of "not generating sagely conceit."

### 3.3 The Five Paired Reverse-Overextensions (Original Contribution)

The ten realms of the rūpa-skandha unfold as a five-layer progression from near to far: body (1–2), hearing (3), feeling (4–7), emptiness (8–9), other (10) — as detailed in S6-P08. The ten *vedanā*-demons present a wholly different internal structure: **five pairs of reverse-overextensions.**

| Pair | Demons | Reverse-Overextension | Common Occasion |
|------|--------|----------------------|----------------|
| I | Pity (1) ↔ Frenzy (2) | Inward suppression vs. outward rashness | Reaction to "seeing the minds of all the buddhas" |
| II | Recollection (3) ↔ Premature Contentment (4) | Backward longing vs. early stopping | Reaction to "no new attainment" |
| III | Ceaseless Sorrow (5) ↔ Uncontrolled Delight (6) | Despair vs. ecstasy | Reaction to "the perils of the two edges" |
| IV | Towering Conceit (7) ↔ Satisfied Lightness (8) | Aggressive arrogance vs. stagnant self-satisfaction | Reaction to "self-attainment" |
| V | Nihilistic Emptiness (9) ↔ Refined Desire (10) | Annihilation vs. savoring | Reaction to "the empty-luminous nature" |

The decisive observation is that **the two demons of each pair are not parallel but opposed** — each pair is two reverse-overextensions of the same underlying meditative occasion.

**Pair I (pity ↔ frenzy)** both arise on the occasion of "the rūpa-skandha dissolving, seeing the minds of the buddhas, attaining great radiance." The pity-demon's response is "inward suppression in excess" (excessive self-reproach); the frenzy-demon's is "rashness in excess" (excessive self-elevation). One collapses inward into self-reproach; the other inflates outward into bravado. One says "sentient beings are too miserable, and I have realized too late"; the other says "the three incalculable kalpas can be transcended in a single thought." These are two reverse-overextensions of one and the same occasion (the sudden vision of inner attainment).

**Pair II (recollection ↔ premature contentment)** both arise on the occasion of "the rūpa-skandha exhausted, but no new ground attained." The recollection-demon's response is "longing for the lost old place" (looking back); the contentment-demon's response is "I am Vairocana — what little I have is enough" (stopping ahead). One says "I have lost what I had"; the other says "I am already home." Two reverse-overextensions of the same threshold (no progress yet) — one tries to retreat, one tries to halt.

**Pair III (sorrow ↔ delight)** both arise on the occasion of "surveying both edges with attainment yet to be reached." The sorrow-demon's response is "boundless sorrow, like sitting on a bed of iron, seeking others to take one's life"; the delight-demon's, "boundless welling joy, singing and dancing in the public street." One wants death; the other goes mad with revelry. Note that this pair reaches the most extreme limit: the sorrow-demon to literal suicide, the delight-demon to literal derangement. They are two reverse-extremes of the same occasion — the perils of the two edges.

**Pair IV (towering conceit ↔ satisfied lightness)** both arise on the occasion of "declaring oneself sufficient and self-realized." The conceit-demon's response is "the seven kinds of conceit erupting at once; destroying *stūpas* and shrines, smashing scriptures and images" (outward aggression); the lightness-demon's, "satisfied with what one has, seeking no further advancement" (inward stagnation). One is aggressive arrogance; the other is stagnant complacency. Two reverse-overextensions of "self-attainment" — one expresses "I am higher than all" through attack; the other expresses "I have enough" through stasis.

**Pair V (emptiness ↔ desire)** both arise on the occasion of "attaining the empty-luminous nature; savoring the empty-luminous." The emptiness-demon's response is "turning toward eternal extinction; denying cause and effect; engaging broadly in defiled conduct, all things equally empty" (annihilation as proof); the desire-demon's, "boundless love arising; love at its peak becoming derangement and lust; teaching desire as the path of awakening" (savoring as proof). On the surface the two endings look opposed — one rejects everything, one embraces everything — but the *behavior* is strangely the same. The emptiness-demon "engages broadly in defiled conduct"; the desire-demon "instructs lay followers in equal-handed sexual practice." One uses "emptiness" to legitimize lust; the other legitimizes lust directly. The terminus is identical; the path of legitimation is reversed.

#### 3.3.1 Why "Opposition" Rather Than "Progression"?

The rūpa-skandha is a problem of *the layers of the object*: body, hearing, feeling, emptiness, other are different domains of the awareness, ranging from the near to the far. The *vedanā*-skandha is a problem of *the polarities of judgment*: each pair's two demons are not different "objects" but two reverse "verdicts" facing the same occasion.

This difference reflects the ontological difference between rūpa and *vedanā*. The rūpa-skandha is the layer of matter and sense, so its internal structure is material (near to far, inner to outer); the *vedanā*-skandha is the layer of the mind's judgment about its own states, so its internal structure is reverse (suppression/rashness, brooding/contentment, despair/delight, conceit/satisfaction, annihilation/savoring). **The structure of objects is layered; the structure of judgments is paired.**

#### 3.3.2 Where Does the Middle Way Lie?

The five pairs of reverse-overextensions imply that **the Middle Way lies on neither side, but between the two**. To pity is not to swing to frenzy; to frenzy is not to swing back to pity. To recollect is not to switch to contentment; to be content is not to revert to recollection. The Middle Way is the unbiased position between each pair's two extremes — the position of "not generating sagely conceit." Zǐxuán's "balance of concentration and wisdom, skillful endurance" names this unbiased position.

Each pair's two demons are two failure-modes of the same imbalance. Pair I fails through "concentration in excess of wisdom" (no wisdom to restrain in suppression, no wisdom to save in rashness); Pair II through "wisdom in excess of concentration" (intellectual force weakening into brooding, wisdom in excess of concentration into satisfaction); Pair III through "loss of skillful means" (no skillful endurance generating sorrow, pacification without restraint generating delight); Pair IV through "loss of self-examination" (seeing the victorious without self-saving generating conceit, acquiring lightness from wisdom generating contentment); Pair V through "loss of subtle discernment" (attaining the empty-luminous in clear understanding generating emptiness, savoring the empty-luminous to the bone generating desire).

Five pairs of opposition × five modes of imbalance = the internal map of the ten *vedanā*-demons. This map is not invented; it is in the text. The *Śūraṅgama* simply does not organize its presentation as "opposition"; it organizes it as a temporal sequence of practice. The structural reading proposed here makes explicit what the text already contains.

### 3.4 The Phenomenology of "External Demons Concealed but Unmanifest"

In Jiāoguāng's threefold division, the description of the *vedanā*-skandha is the most subtle: "the external demons have already been summoned but have only crept into the body and have not yet manifested in person." How is this to be read? If the demon has already been summoned, why has it not yet manifested? If the demon has not yet manifested, where is it?

The reading proposed here is that **"external demons concealed but unmanifest" is a phenomenological description, not an ontological assertion**. It describes the *mode of appearance* of the *vedanā*-skandha's demonic event, not the *physical location* of an independent entity.

Phenomenologically, "external demons concealed but unmanifest" means three things.

First, **the demonic event at this stage requires no external other as carrier**. The demonic event of the *saṃjñā*-skandha requires "flying spirits and possessing another" (Jiāoguāng) — the heavenly demon dispatches a spirit to possess another person (typically a "good friend" near the practitioner), and through that person seeks to deceive the practitioner. The *vedanā*-skandha needs no such mediation. The demonic event of the *vedanā*-skandha occurs directly within the practitioner himself, without intermediary. There is no "possession of another"; only "darkly entering the practitioner's own heart."

Second, **the demonic event at this stage is the practitioner's own act of judgment in deformation**. Jiāoguāng's phrase "the demon darkly enters the practitioner's heart and causes him to overturn himself unaware" deserves attention to the phrase "overturn himself unaware" (不覺自顛). "Self-overturning" means: the practitioner himself has overturned himself. "Unaware" means: this overturning is invisible to the practitioner — he does not know that he has been overturned. Why invisible? Because the content of the overturning is "self-endorsement" — "I have attained sagely realization." When you are in the act of endorsing the "I," how can you simultaneously see the "I" overturning? The act of endorsing the I is itself the overturning, and there is no second I that can stand apart from this act and see it.

Third, **"external demon" here is a dynamic description of "calling by kind," not a positional description of a physical entity**. Zǐxuán glosses the dynamic with "called by kind" and "the mutual working within, drawing in the demons from without" — the practitioner generates a certain category of errant thought, and this draws into resonance an external demonic force of the same nature, as a magnet draws iron filings. The "calling" is not spatial (a demon flying in from elsewhere) but qualitative (like calling like). The "external" of "external demon" is not "outside the body"; it is "outside right mindfulness" — any psychological dynamic outside right mindfulness can be called "external."

Putting these three layers together, one can see why the *vedanā*-skandha is "external demons concealed but unmanifest": **the external demon has no need to manifest, because the practitioner himself is already speaking on behalf of the demon. The verdict "I have attained sagely realization" is the entire content of the demon.** There is no second voice, no second body, no second face; only the practitioner himself, in the depths of meditation, gently saying to himself "I have arrived" — and that is enough.

This phenomenological analysis also explains why the *vedanā*-skandha is "harder to detect" than the *saṃjñā*-skandha (even though the *saṃjñā*-skandha's demonic event is more openly external). In the *saṃjñā*-skandha you can still be on guard against "this 'good friend' who has come to me may not be reliable"; in the *vedanā*-skandha you cannot even find the object of vigilance — the object of your vigilance is your own act of judgment, and judgment and vigilance occur in the same heart.

### 3.5 The Refined Form of the Two Arrows

Returning to the controlling parable: the two arrows. The first arrow is bodily feeling; the second is the mental verdict the mind passes on bodily feeling. At the worldly level the two are easy to separate — the body's pain is the first arrow; "I am ruined" is the second. In the depths of the *vedanā*-skandha, the situation has changed.

**The refinement of the first arrow:** bodily feeling is no longer the rough sensation of the physical body — it has become "the savor of meditative absorption," "subtle joy," "boundless lightness," "the vast openness of the empty-luminous," "the cool of stillness." Are these still bodily feelings? In the broader sense of "what can be received" (*vedanā*'s root meaning of *vid-* — to know, to receive), they are still feelings — they are the mind's reception of some inner attainment. They are not objects of rūpa, but they are the essential nature of the *vedanā*-skandha — "empty-penetrating reception."

**The refinement of the second arrow:** mental feeling is no longer the rough story but the bare verdict "I have attained sagely realization." This verdict requires no words, no narrative, no time — it can be completed in a single moment. "I privately suspect myself to be Vairocana" (fourth demon), "I declare myself a sage with great mastery" (eighth), "I declare myself to have attained unhindered liberation" (sixth) — these are all verdicts, but each is silent, instantaneous, self-endorsing.

The two arrows in the *vedanā*-skandha take the following form:

| Worldly | Vedanā Depth |
|---------|--------------|
| First arrow: poison-arrow strikes the body | First arrow: subtle feeling arising in *samādhi* (boundless lightness, boundless joy, savoring the empty-luminous…) |
| Second arrow: "I am ruined" | Second arrow: the verdict "I have attained sagely realization" |
| Two arrows distinct | Two arrows nearly simultaneous |
| Easily told apart | Almost indistinguishable |

Note the last row. In the worldly state the two arrows are distinct, and the practitioner has time to insert "knowing-as-it-is" between bodily feeling and mental feeling. In the depths of the *vedanā*-skandha the two arrows arise almost together, and the practitioner's "knowing-as-it-is" must be completed in a single instant. This is why the *vedanā*-skandha demands the balance of concentration and wisdom far more than the worldly stage does — wisdom's response time must keep pace with the rate at which feeling arises.

The text of the tenth demon (the demon of refined desire) most clearly depicts this instantaneous transition. "Savoring the empty-luminous, penetrating to the bone; suddenly boundless love arises; love at its peak becomes derangement, then lust." This is a three-step instantaneous sequence:

1. **Savoring the empty-luminous** (refined first arrow: aesthetic dwelling in the meditative state)
2. **Boundless love arising** (germinal second arrow: the verdict of love)
3. **Love at its peak becoming derangement, becoming lust** (eruption of the second arrow: from the savoring of aesthetic absorption directly into the behavior of lust)

The whole sequence has no temporal interval. The single character "suddenly" (忽) accurately conveys its instantaneity. By the time the practitioner notices that he is "in lust," it is over — he has already lost *samādhi*.

This is why the tenth demon stands at the end of the *vedanā* section: it is the apotheosis of "savoring," and the terminus of the *vedanā*-skandha. **"Savoring" is the essence of *vedanā* — *vedanā* = reception = tasting.** When the practitioner begins to "savor" his own meditative state, he has already taken the *vedanā*-skandha as a possession; when he begins to savor it "to the bone," the empty-luminous has become the bone of his attachment; when "love at its peak becomes derangement," lust has erupted. The tenth demon is the implosion of the *vedanā*-skandha.

### 3.6 The Three Latent Tendencies as Differential Diagnosis

*Saṃyukta-āgama* 468's mapping of the three feelings to the three latent tendencies — pleasant feeling → latent greed, painful feeling → latent aversion, neutral feeling → latent ignorance — can be applied to the ten *vedanā*-demons as a differential-diagnostic instrument. A methodological caveat is required: **the mapping is *tendential*, not *exclusive*.** Each demon contains traces of all three feelings; the table marks only the dominant component, as a starting point for diagnosis, not as a classificatory dogma.

| Feeling-System | Latent Tendency | Vedanā-Demons | Structural Feature |
|---------------|-----------------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Pleasant** | Greed | 6 Delight, 7 Conceit, 8 Lightness, 10 Desire | Retention and expansion of "self-attainment" |
| **Painful** | Aversion | 1 Pity, 3 Recollection, 5 Sorrow | Reaction to "loss" |
| **Neutral** | Ignorance | 2 Frenzy, 4 Contentment, 9 Emptiness | Misjudgment of "the present condition" |

**Pleasant-feeling demons (delight, conceit, lightness, desire):** the common structure is "grasping at some positive feeling" — delight grasps at "joy," conceit grasps at "self-victory," lightness grasps at "tranquility," and the desire-demon grasps at "the savor of the empty-luminous." All are pleasant feelings misidentified as sagely realization, all corresponding to the latent tendency to greed. Note that the demon of conceit may seem unexpected here — "conceit" does not at first sound like a pleasant feeling. But if one traces conceit to its root, its core is "I declare myself sufficient" and "holding victorious natures within the heart" — a self-satisfied sense of pleasure that merely happens to express itself outwardly as aggression. The substance of conceit is the distortion of pleasant feeling.

**Painful-feeling demons (pity, recollection, sorrow):** the common structure is "reaction to some loss or lack" — the pity-demon reacts to "sentient beings are too miserable and I have realized too late," the recollection-demon reacts to "no new attainment ahead, the old ground gone behind," and the sorrow-demon reacts to "the new attainment ungained, the old mind departed." All are painful feelings misidentified as sagely realization (mistaking such pain for "the appearance of effort"), all corresponding to the latent tendency to aversion. Note that the object of aversion need not be another person; it can also be oneself. The pity-demon's aversion is directed at "myself for having realized late"; the recollection-demon's, at "myself for having lost what I had"; the sorrow-demon's, at "myself for being alive." The latent aversion in the *vedanā*-skandha is, in its refined form, *self*-aversion rather than aversion toward others.

**Neutral-feeling demons (frenzy, contentment, emptiness):** the common structure is "misjudgment of a neutral state" — the frenzy-demon misjudges "the appearance of victorious signs" as "transcending the three kalpas in a single thought," the contentment-demon misjudges "victorious natures held in the heart" as "I am Vairocana," the emptiness-demon misjudges "attaining the empty-luminous nature" as "turning toward eternal extinction." None is pleasant or painful; all are neutral, seemingly tranquil, seemingly transcendent states that have been misjudged as ultimate. All correspond to the latent tendency to ignorance — and the essence of ignorance is precisely "not knowing things as they are," taking what is not ultimate to be ultimate.

The clinical use of this differential diagnosis is to provide a structured triage: when the practitioner suspects himself of having grasped at some meditative state, he can ask — "is this the grasping of pleasant feeling (greed)? the grasping of painful feeling (aversion)? or the misjudgment of a neutral state (ignorance)?" The three directions have three different antidotes: for the pleasant-feeling system, "contemplate pleasant feeling as suffering" (SĀ 467); for the painful-feeling system, "contemplate painful feeling as a sword-thrust"; for the neutral-feeling system, "contemplate neutral feeling as impermanent." **The three contemplations of the three feelings are the antidote to the ten *vedanā*-demons** — only this antidote, foundational at the worldly level, must be applied at the depths of the *vedanā*-skandha with extraordinary precision.

### 3.7 "The Head of the Four Mind-Skandhas": Why Breaking *Vedanā* Is the Doorway

The final layer of argument returns to Zǐxuán's structural insight: "*vedanā* is the head of the four mind-skandhas." Why is this so important?

The rūpa-skandha is rūpa-dharma, of the body; the four — *vedanā*, *saṃjñā*, *saṃskāra*, *vijñāna* — are mind-dharmas, of the mind. The transition from rūpa to *vedanā* is the transition from "the practice of the body" to "the practice of the mind." The rūpa-skandha is only the threshold; the *vedanā*-skandha is the doorway — once one passes through it, one has entered the genuine territory of psychological practice.

"The head of the four mind-skandhas" means three things.

First, **the *vedanā*-skandha is the first of the mind-skandhas**. Among the four, *vedanā* comes first: the meeting of sense-faculty, sense-object, and consciousness yields contact, contact yields *vedanā*, *vedanā* yields *saṃjñā*, *saṃjñā* yields *saṃskāra*, *saṃskāra* turns into *vijñāna*. *Vedanā* is the source link of the chain of psychological dependent origination. If *vedanā* is misjudged, the *saṃjñā*, *saṃskāra*, and *vijñāna* skandhas that follow will be misjudged with it. A practitioner who is convinced of "I have attained sagely realization" will, on the basis of that conviction, distort his *saṃjñā* (his constructions of what sagely realization contains), his *saṃskāra* (his actions based on that conviction), and his *vijñāna* (his self-identification as a sage).

Second, **breaking the *vedanā*-skandha entails the breaking of the *saṃjñā*, *saṃskāra*, and *vijñāna* skandhas** (for the keen-faculty practitioner). Zǐxuán's "for the keen-faculty practitioner, when the *vedanā*-skandha is broken, the three skandhas are broken with it" does not mean that *saṃjñā*, *saṃskāra*, and *vijñāna* vanish of themselves; it means that, having lost the *vedanā* foundation, they have nothing to stand on. *Saṃjñā* is the construction that depends on *vedanā* — without *vedanā*, there is nothing to construct upon. *Saṃskāra* is the action that depends on *saṃjñā* — without *saṃjñā*, there is no action. *Vijñāna* is the identification that depends on *saṃskāra* — without *saṃskāra*, there is no identification. *Vedanā* is the first link of the chain; break the first link, and the whole chain is broken.

Third, **breaking the *vedanā*-skandha allows one to "ascend through the sixty sagely stations"**. The text's explicit "is now able to ascend, from this ordinary body, through the sixty sagely stations of the bodhisattva," in Zǐxuán's reading, is structurally significant: it shows that the *vedanā*-skandha occupies a position among the five skandhas wholly its own. It is not "one of the five skandhas" but "the doorway into the sagely stations." The exhaustion of the rūpa-skandha is "entering the gate"; the exhaustion of the *vedanā*-skandha is "setting out on the path."

Restored to the argument of S6-P09, this structural placement explains why the diagnostic apparatus of the ten *vedanā*-demons carries deeper responsibility than that of the rūpa-skandha: **each of the ten realms of the *vedanā*-skandha carries the possibility of ending the entire path to the sagely stations.** The worst outcome of the rūpa-skandha is "harboring the māras"; the worst outcome of the *vedanā*-skandha is the tenfold repetition of "loses correct *samādhi* and falls into sinking destruction" — and that "correct *samādhi*" is the meditative ground itself; to lose it is to lose meditation as such.

This is the structural reason for the abrupt tightening of the *Śūraṅgama*'s tone in the *vedanā* section. The text is not frightening anyone; it is warning that, from this point forward, any small misjudgment can end the whole road. **The six characters "this is not sagely realization; the moment one grasps it, demons are drawn in" carry far more weight in the *vedanā* section than in the rūpa section.**

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Dialogue

### 4.1 ⚠️ A Minimal Contemporary Analogy: Nociception and Pain Perception

Contemporary pain science distinguishes two layers: *nociception* (the sensory transmission of tissue-damage signals by sensory neurons) and *pain perception* (the brain's interpretive and emotional response to those signals). Research on chronic pain has found that the two are not always synchronized: some patients have intense nociception but low pain perception (the soldier whose adrenaline masks the pain of his wound), while others have low nociception but high pain perception (fibromyalgia patients with extreme pain in the absence of detectable tissue damage).[^21]

This distinction provides a **heuristic contemporary analogy** (not an ontological equivalence) for the two arrows:

- *Nociception* loosely corresponds to the first arrow (bodily feeling, sensory signal)
- *Pain perception* loosely corresponds to the second arrow (mental feeling, interpretive response)

A treatment strategy in chronic pain known as "pain reframing" trains patients to reinterpret pain signals from "threat" to "signal," thereby reducing the intensity of pain perception without first having to eliminate nociception. The structural shape of this strategy is close to the *vedanānupassanā* practice of "feeling painful feeling, he knows" — both preserve the first arrow while severing the second.

⚠️ But the analogy is structural-heuristic only. There are three points of non-equivalence to mark plainly:

1. Contemporary pain science treats the coarse pain of the worldly stage, not the subtle feelings of meditative depth.
2. The nociception/perception distinction is *neurological*, while the two-arrows distinction is *psychological-ethical*. The first describes a mechanism; the second describes a path.
3. The goal of pain reframing is to reduce subjective suffering; the goal of "not generating sagely conceit" is non-attachment to *any* feeling, including pleasant feeling. The two move in different directions.

S6-P08 has already established the methodology of clinical differential diagnosis. This section merely extends that methodology to a finer contemporary analogy and does not develop further. The core treatment of the ten *vedanā*-demons remains the *vedanānupassanā* of the Āgamas and the "not generating sagely conceit" of the *Śūraṅgama*, not contemporary pain science.

## V. Middle Way Corrections

This section offers seven Middle Way corrections to prevent the handling of the ten *vedanā*-demons from being pushed into literal, extreme, or misapplied directions.

### 5.1 ❌ The Ten Vedanā-Demons Are Not Everyday Emotions

A common misreading: identifying everyday sadness, excitement, complacency, despair, or lust as "the *vedanā*-demons." This is a serious confusion of levels. The ten *vedanā*-demons arise only in the depths of meditation after "the rūpa-skandha is exhausted, the *vedanā*-skandha is clarified" — their precondition is that the practitioner has crossed the rūpa-skandha, suppressed the five hindrances, and entered deep *samādhi*. Everyday emotions belong to the level of the five hindrances, the coarse defilements; their treatment is foundational *vedanānupassanā* and mindfulness training, not the framework of the fifty skandha-māras.

Calling everyday sadness "the demon of pity" is like calling the common cold "lung cancer" — wrong classification leads to wrong treatment.

### 5.2 ❌ "External Demons Concealed" Is Not a Literal Spirit Possession

Jiāoguāng's "external demons have already been summoned but have only crept into the body" is often read literally as "an external spirit-entity has entered the practitioner's body." This reading turns the entire *vedanā* section into mysticism and forfeits its function as an instrument of clinical psychological diagnosis.

The correct reading is given in §3.4: "external demons concealed" is a phenomenological description, not an ontological assertion. It describes the mode of appearance of the *vedanā*-skandha's demonic event (the deformation of the practitioner's own act of judgment), not the physical location of an independent spirit-entity. Zǐxuán's "called by kind" and "the mutual working within, drawing in the demons from without," together with Jiāoguāng's own "the demon darkly enters the practitioner's heart and causes him to overturn himself unaware," explicitly support this reading.

### 5.3 ❌ "*Vedanā* Is Harder Than Rūpa" Does Not Mean "Rūpa Is Easy"

The paper has repeatedly emphasized that the *vedanā*-skandha is harder than the rūpa-skandha, but this does not mean the rūpa-skandha is easy. The ten realms of the rūpa-skandha are themselves an extraordinarily advanced meditative discrimination — anyone who can enter them is already an advanced practitioner. "Harder" is a relative refinement, not a difficulty ordering. To misread "the *vedanā*-skandha is harder" as "the rūpa-skandha is easy, skip directly" is wholly mistaken. Without the firm ground of the rūpa-skandha there is no doorway into the *vedanā*-skandha at all — every passage in the ten *vedanā*-realms takes "the rūpa-skandha exhausted, the *vedanā*-skandha clarified" as its precondition.

### 5.4 ❌ The Treatment Is Not the Suppression of Feeling, but Not Generating Sagely Conceit

Another common misreading: taking "the treatment of the *vedanā*-demons" to mean "suppressing or eliminating these feelings." This is wrong. The core training of *vedanānupassanā* is "feeling X, he knows feeling X" — knowing-as-it-is, not elimination. Likewise, "not generating sagely conceit" treats the verdict, not the phenomenon.

The treatment of the first demon ("boundless pity") is not the suppression of compassion but not taking that compassion to be sagely realization. The treatment of the sixth ("boundless welling joy") is not the suppression of joy but not taking that joy to be unhindered liberation. The treatment of the tenth ("savoring the empty-luminous") is not the rejection of the meditative state itself but not generating "savoring" (the possessive tasting) toward that state. The object of treatment is always the verdict, never the phenomenon.

To misread treatment as suppression is to lead the practitioner into the new attachment "I must suppress my joy," and that very attachment is itself another form of "losing correct *samādhi*."

### 5.5 ❌ The Tenth Demon Is Not a Literal Anti-Sexual Doctrine

The sūtra's description of the desire-demon — "instructing white-robed lay followers in equal-handed sexual practice; calling fornicators true sons of the Dharma" — is so vivid that it is sometimes read as the *Śūraṅgama*'s "anti-sexual creed." This is a surface misreading.

The core of the tenth demon is not "sexual conduct" but the subtle inner transition by which "the meditative savor becomes the bone of attachment." The order of the text is clear: first "savoring the empty-luminous, penetrating to the bone" (the aesthetic tasting of the meditative state), then "boundless love arises" (the budding of love), then "love at its peak becomes derangement and lust" (the eruption of lust). Lust is the eruption of the inner transition, but it is not the starting point. The starting point is *savoring*.

The object of treatment is *savoring*, not *lust*. To read the tenth demon as "do not have sex" is to mistake the result for the cause, the ending for the starting point. The actual treatment is to see through the savoring of the empty-luminous in the moment of its arising — to refuse to take the meditative state as something that can be tasted. Once tasting begins, love is born; once love is born, lust is on the way.

### 5.6 ❌ The Fifty Skandha-Māras Are Not a Discouragement from Meditation

The most important correction. The fifty skandha-māras as a whole — including S6-P08's ten rūpa-realms, this paper's ten *vedanā*-realms, S6-P10's ten *saṃjñā*-realms, and the ten *saṃskāra* and *vijñāna*-realms of S6-P11/12 — are not a warning *not* to meditate.

On the contrary, the entire structure of the fifty skandha-māras presupposes an exceptionally accomplished meditator: anyone who can enter these territories is already an advanced practitioner. The *Śūraṅgama*'s elaborate exposition is meant to **protect** these advanced practitioners in the final stages from "losing correct *samādhi*," not to **discourage** anyone from entering meditation.

To take the fifty skandha-māras as the argument "meditation is dangerous, so do not meditate" is like taking a medical textbook's "drug side effects" as the argument "do not take any medication" — a misuse of logic. The fifty skandha-māras are a manual of advanced clinical diagnosis prepared for those already on the path, not a letter of dissuasion for those who have not yet set out.

### 5.7 ❌ "Losing Correct *Samādhi*" Is Not the Literal "Loss of Concentration"

The verdict "loses correct *samādhi*" is repeated ten times in the *vedanā* section and deserves precise reading. "Correct *samādhi*" (正受) carries two layers of meaning. The surface meaning is "correct reception" (the correct reception of the meditative state). The deeper meaning is "*samādhi*" itself — right concentration. "Losing correct *samādhi*" is not the practitioner suddenly falling out of *samādhi* overnight; it is the *correctness* of his *samādhi* being undermined by misjudgment — the practitioner is still "in concentration," but his concentration is no longer "correct concentration," because he has now taken what is not sagely realization to be sagely realization.

This distinction matters. "Losing correct *samādhi*" is not exit from *samādhi* but the transformation of *samādhi*'s nature from "correct" to "deviant" — the practitioner remains in deep concentration, but that concentration has now been contaminated by the verdict "I have attained sagely realization." The moment correct *samādhi* becomes deviant *samādhi* is invisible from the outside, but the inner structure has changed completely.

## VI. Conclusion

### 6.1 P09 in Summary: The Refined Return of the Second Arrow

The central thesis of this paper is that **the ten *vedanā*-demons are not a new category invented by the *Śūraṅgama* but the refined return, at the depths of *samādhi*, of the *Sallatha Sutta*'s second arrow.** The *Saṃyukta-āgama* 470 parable tells us that the worldling's suffering comes from two arrows — bodily feeling is the first; mental feeling is the second. The noble disciple is not free of the first arrow but is able to sever the second. *Vedanānupassanā* (the *Madhyama-āgama*'s "feeling X, he knows feeling X") is the training in this severance.

When the practitioner enters the depths of *samādhi*, exhausts the rūpa-skandha, and sees the minds of all the buddhas as images in a clear mirror, the second arrow has not vanished — it has become invisible. The first arrow (bodily feeling) is refined into "the savor of meditative absorption," "subtle joy," "boundless lightness"; the second arrow (mental feeling) is refined into the instantaneous, silent, self-endorsing verdict "I have attained sagely realization." The two arrows arise almost simultaneously and become almost indistinguishable. The ten *vedanā*-demons — pity, frenzy, brooding recollection, premature contentment, ceaseless sorrow, uncontrolled delight, towering conceit, satisfied lightness, nihilistic emptiness, refined desire — are each a transformation of the second arrow at the depths of meditation.

Zǐxuán's twelve characters are decisive: **"This is not sagely realization; the moment one grasps it, demons are drawn in. The other [nine] all follow this pattern."**

The paper has unfolded seven layers of argument to substantiate this thesis: (1) why the *vedanā*-skandha is harder than the rūpa-skandha — the rūpa-skandha's danger is the misidentification of an object, the *vedanā*-skandha's the misidentification of an act of judgment about a state; (2) the ten *vedanā*-demons as the pathological inverse of *vedanānupassanā* — "knowing X" becomes "grasping X"; (3) the original "five paired reverse-overextensions" structure — pity↔frenzy, recollection↔contentment, sorrow↔delight, conceit↔lightness, emptiness↔desire, five paired oppositions rather than five layered progressions; (4) the phenomenology of "external demons concealed but unmanifest" — the demon does not need to manifest because the practitioner himself is already its mouthpiece; (5) the refinement of the two arrows — first and second arrow arising almost together, almost indistinguishable; (6) the three latent tendencies as differential diagnosis — four pleasant-feeling demons, three painful, three neutral; (7) Zǐxuán's "head of the four mind-skandhas" — breaking the *vedanā*-skandha is the doorway into the sagely stations, and the tenfold repetition of "loses correct *samādhi*" is structural alarm.

The "five paired reverse-overextensions" is the paper's principal original contribution. It reveals that the inner architecture of the ten *vedanā*-demons is not an arbitrary list of ten items but five paired oppositions — each pair's two demons two reverse-overextensions of one and the same occasion. The structure of the rūpa-skandha is **layered** (body→hearing→feeling→emptiness→other); the structure of the *vedanā*-skandha is **paired** (suppression↔rashness, brooding↔contentment…). The structure of objects is layered; the structure of judgments is paired. This difference reflects the ontological difference between rūpa and *vedanā* — the former is the object, the latter the act of receiving.

### 6.2 Toward P10: From Inner Implosion to Outer Pursuit

The *vedanā* section ends with the demon of refined desire (the implosion of "savoring the empty-luminous"); the *saṃjñā* section opens with "craving for skill in expedient teaching" (an outward pursuit of an external capacity). **The same craving, but the form has shifted from "inward implosion in the savor of meditative state" to "outward pursuit of capacities to be performed."** This shift from inward to outward forms the opening of S6-P10.

Note where this shift sits in Jiāoguāng's threefold division. The *vedanā*-skandha is "external demons concealed but unmanifest" (mixed inner and outer); the *saṃjñā*-skandha is "heavenly demons and spirits openly manifest" (wholly outer). From *vedanā* to *saṃjñā*, the principal arena of the demonic event moves from the practitioner's inner heart to his outer world — concretely, to "another person possessed by flying spirits," typically a "good friend" of the practitioner. The *vedanā*-skandha's demon is within the heart; the *saṃjñā*-skandha's demon is in the surrounding scene.

But there is an internal reason for this outward movement: **only when the *vedanā*-skandha has been exhausted (when the practitioner has severed his attachment to the savor of his own meditative states) is the demon forced to enter from outside.** While the *vedanā*-skandha remains unexhausted, the demon can darkly enter the practitioner's heart and cause him to overturn himself — there is space within the heart that the demon can enter. Once the *vedanā*-skandha is exhausted, the inner space is closed, and the demon can only manifest from outside, through the mouth, the form, and the "good friend"-status of another person. This is why the ten *saṃjñā*-demons require "the heavenly demon, finding its opportunity, sends forth a spirit and possesses another, who then speaks the dharma" (*Śūraṅgama* IX) — not because the heavenly demon has grown stronger, but because the practitioner's inner space has shrunk, and the demon must change its theater.

### 6.3 The Open Question for P10: Why Does "Pursuit" Require the Cooperation of an External Demon?

The core question left for P10: **Why must the demonic event of the *saṃjñā*-skandha involve "the open manifestation of an external demon," whereas the demonic event of the *vedanā*-skandha can be "self-overturning"? Why does "pursuit" require the cooperation of an external demon possessing another person, while "misjudgment of feeling" can be completed alone?**

A preliminary direction (P10 will argue this directly): the *vedanā*-skandha is "the misidentification of one's own state" (inward); the *saṃjñā*-skandha is "the pursuit of an external capacity" (outward). The former requires only the practitioner himself; the latter requires an external object. When the practitioner "pursues skill" (in expedient teaching), he needs "someone capable of speaking the dharma skillfully" as object; when he "pursues divine power," he needs "someone able to manifest divine powers" as object. Each of the ten *saṃjñā*-pursuits — skill, peripatetic experience, melding, analytical discernment, mysterious resonance, deep stillness, knowledge of past lives, divine power, profound emptiness, eternal life — requires an external object as the concretization of its grasping. This is why the heavenly demon must "send forth a spirit and possess another" — because the *saṃjñā*-skandha's pursuits require a possessed person to play the role of the object pursued.

The contrast can also be put in Āgama terms: the *vedanā*-skandha is "the grasping of the meditative branches themselves after the five hindrances have been suppressed"; the *saṃjñā*-skandha is "the grasping of divine powers and capacities after the meditative branches themselves have been suppressed." The former is inward (toward one's own meditative state); the latter is outward (toward external capacities). The treatment of the *vedanā*-skandha is the refined version of *vedanānupassanā* (contemplation of feelings as feelings at the subtle level); the treatment of the *saṃjñā*-skandha is the seeing-through of "pursuit" itself (contemplation of craving as craving — which will be the central topic of S6-P10).

### 6.4 A Final Word to the Reader

If you have read this far, you may worry: I cannot perform such subtle discrimination — how do I know whether or not I have fallen into the ten *vedanā*-demons?

The answer has two parts.

The first is reassurance: **if you have not entered the meditative depths in which the rūpa-skandha is exhausted, you cannot fall into the ten *vedanā*-demons.** The precondition of the ten *vedanā*-demons is an exceptionally advanced meditative foundation — "seeing the minds of all the buddhas as images in a clear mirror" is the entrance ticket; without that ticket, you cannot even reach the stage on which the *vedanā*-demons appear. Everyday sadness, excitement, lust, and despair belong to the level of the five hindrances — coarse defilements treated by foundational *vedanānupassanā* and mindfulness training, not by the framework of the fifty skandha-māras. To mistake an everyday emotion for a *vedanā*-demon is to mistake a cold for lung cancer.

The second is preparation: **if you are practicing seriously and are committed to going deep, then to inscribe the inner architecture of the ten *vedanā*-demons in memory is a preventive instrument of discrimination.** When one day you enter that level — you may not, but if you do — you need to recognize it in the moment. Zǐxuán's twelve characters, "this is not sagely realization; the moment one grasps it, demons are drawn in. The other all follow this pattern," are worth committing to memory. They are not asking you to fear meditation; they are asking you to maintain, in the depths of meditation, an extraordinarily precise vigilance: **any feeling, however subtle, however peaceful, however close to the appearance of sagely realization, is not to be grasped as sagely realization. Recognized, it dissipates of itself; taken as sagely realization, the host of deviances enters in.**

"Not generating sagely conceit, this is called a good state" — the verdict of the rūpa-skandha.
"This is not sagely realization; the moment one grasps it, demons are drawn in" — the verdict of the *vedanā*-skandha.

The structure is the same; the depth is different. For the rūpa-skandha "not generating" suffices; for the *vedanā*-skandha one must "not grasp" — not only does one not generate the conscious conceit of sagely realization, one does not even *grasp* the meditative state at all. *Upādāna* is more refined than the conscious act, because the conscious act is voluntary, while grasping can be unconscious. The *vedanā*-skandha's demand for "not grasping" is the refined version of the rūpa-skandha's demand for "not generating."

From "not generating" to "not grasping" is the whole of S6-P09.
