**A Doctrinal Study of the Six Root Kleśas and Four Indeterminate Mental Factors in the Śatadharmaprakāśamukha-śāstra**

## Abstract

This paper examines the six root afflictions (kleśas) — craving (lobha), aversion (dveṣa), ignorance (moha), pride (māna), doubt (vicikitsā), and wrong views (dṛṣṭi) — constituting the fourth category of the Śatadharmaprakāśamukha-śāstra's mental factors, together with the four indeterminate factors (aniyata) — regret (kaukṛtya), sleep (middha), examination (vitarka), and investigation (vicāra) — constituting the sixth category. This study argues that the root afflictions form a structured system of defilement rather than a disconnected list: ignorance serves as the foundation, craving and aversion as twin wings, pride and doubt as subtle obstacles, and the five views as intellectual-level contamination — together constituting the engine of saṃsāra. The four indeterminates are neutral instruments whose soteriological valence depends on the practitioner's direction of awareness. Drawing on Āgama teachings on the three poisons and the graduated path of the four fruits, the Śūraṅgama-sūtra's fifty skandha-demons, the Saṃdhinirmocana-sūtra's two obstructions and graduated affliction-remediation, and the Avataṃsaka-sūtra's five-layered affliction pedagogy, and grounded in the Cheng Weishi Lun's definitions, the innate/discriminative distinction, consciousness-correlation analysis, and two-obstruction framework, this paper demonstrates that the "four non-universalities" of affliction — not universal in moral quality, realm, time, or consciousness-correlation — constitute the doctrinal basis for the possibility of practice.

## I. The Problem

The previous paper (S3-P08) analyzed the eleven wholesome mental factors — the positive fuel for practice. If wholesome factors are the fuel propelling the vehicle toward liberation, the root afflictions are the contaminants and impurities driving it toward saṃsāra. This shift from wholesome to afflictive reveals why the same vehicle can be driven by two diametrically opposed forces.

The Śatadharmaprakāśamukha-śāstra divides afflictions into two tiers among the fifty-one mental factors: the fourth category of six root afflictions (craving, aversion, ignorance, pride, doubt, wrong views), and the fifth category of twenty subsidiary afflictions (ten minor, two intermediate, eight major). The root afflictions are the engine core of cyclic existence; the subsidiary afflictions are the exhaust and derivative problems generated by that engine — the latter will be treated in the next paper (S3-P10). The sixth category of four indeterminate factors (regret, sleep, examination, investigation) are neutral tools whose direction depends entirely on the practitioner's state of awareness — like a gearbox that can accelerate forward or reverse into suffering.

The central thesis of this paper is that the six root afflictions are not six isolated negative qualities but a structured system of defilement with clear internal hierarchy:

**First layer: ignorance (moha) as the root.** The Cheng Weishi Lun explicitly states: "From ignorance arise doubt, wrong views, craving and other afflictions, subsidiary afflictions and karma, which bring about future contaminated dharmas."[^1] Ignorance is the soil of all afflictions; without it, craving, aversion, pride, doubt, and views have no ground from which to arise.

**Second layer: craving and aversion as twin wings.** Craving is attachment to "existence" (bhava); aversion is resistance to "suffering" (duḥkha) — one pulls, one pushes, driving sentient beings ceaselessly through the six destinies. Together with ignorance, they form the "three unwholesome roots" (akuśala-mūla), the oldest affliction classification in Buddhism.[^2]

**Third layer: pride and doubt as subtle obstacles.** Pride is not as dramatic as craving or aversion, yet it is the most tenacious reef on the path — the Elder Khemaka's testimony that he had realized non-self yet not eliminated self-conceit is compelling evidence.[^3] Doubt prevents wholesome qualities from arising and constitutes the first critical barrier to entering the path.

**Fourth layer: the five views as intellectual-level contamination.** The view of a self-entity (satkāya-dṛṣṭi), extreme views (antagrāha-dṛṣṭi), wrong views (mithyā-dṛṣṭi), attachment to views (dṛṣṭi-parāmarśa), and attachment to precepts and rituals (śīla-vrata-parāmarśa) — these five operate through "defiled wisdom" (kliṣṭa-prajñā), not emotional impulse but systematic cognitive error.

In traditional pedagogy, these six are classified as the "five dull afflictions" (pañca-manda-anuśaya: craving, aversion, ignorance, pride, doubt) and the "five sharp afflictions" (pañca-tīkṣṇa-anuśaya: the five views). "Dull" refers to afflictions of the affective-volitional layer — deeply rooted, slow in manifestation but enduring in force; "sharp" refers to afflictions of the intellectual layer — acute in manifestation but relatively amenable to correction through right view. This classification maps directly to two stages of practice: the path of seeing (darśana-mārga) eliminates discriminatively-arisen afflictions, and the path of cultivation (bhāvanā-mārga) eliminates innately-arisen afflictions.

The argument will reveal a key soteriological principle: afflictions possess "four non-universalities" — they are not universal in moral quality (purely defiled, never wholesome), not universal across realms (more prevalent in lower realms; aversion exists only in the desire realm), not universal in time (intermittent, not permanent), and not universal in consciousness-correlation (the eighth consciousness has no afflictions at all). These four non-universalities constitute the doctrinal basis for the possibility of practice — if afflictions were necessary, omnipresent, unbroken, and total, practice would have no meaning. Precisely because afflictions are conditioned, bounded, intermittent, and partial, there exists space for awareness, treatment, and transformation.

## II. Scriptural Foundations

### A. Sūtra Sources

#### 2.1 The Āgamas: The Three Poisons and Affliction Taxonomy

The Āgamas provide the foundational Buddha-word on afflictions. The teaching of the "three unwholesome roots" establishes the entire affliction theory in its most concise form.

The Saṃyuktāgama uses the metaphor of "aggregation" to declare the three unwholesome roots as the sum of all unwholesomeness:

> 「有不善聚、善聚。何等為不善聚？謂三不善根，是名正說。所以者何？純不善積聚者，謂三不善根。云何為三？謂貪不善根、恚不善根、癡不善根。」[^4]

"What is the unwholesome aggregate? The three unwholesome roots — this is the correct teaching. Why? Because the purely unwholesome accumulation consists of the three unwholesome roots: the unwholesome root of craving, the unwholesome root of aversion, the unwholesome root of delusion."

The same sūtra further unfolds the three roots into a systematic structure spanning evil conduct, thought, intention, and realm:

> 「如三不善根，如是三惡行——身惡行、口惡行、意惡行，三想——欲想、恚想、害想，三覺——欲覺、恚覺、害覺，三界——欲界、恚界、害界。」[^5]

This passage reveals that the three unwholesome roots are not merely three emotions but a systematic contamination pervading four dimensions — from "root" to "thought" to "intention" to "conduct" to "realm." This is the Āgama prototype of the Yogācāra mechanism later described as "seed → manifestation → perfumation."

The Ekottarāgama connects the three unwholesome roots with the three evil destinies:

> 「有此三不善根。……若比丘有此三不善根者，墮三惡趣。……是故，諸比丘！當離三不善根，修三善根。」[^6]

"If a bhikṣu possesses these three unwholesome roots, they fall into the three evil destinies. … Therefore, bhikṣus, abandon the three unwholesome roots and cultivate the three wholesome roots."

The Āgamas simultaneously establish the treatment structure: the three unwholesome roots are directly treated by the three wholesome roots (non-craving, non-aversion, non-delusion) — forming a precise complementary pair with the wholesome mental factors analyzed in the previous paper (S3-P08).

#### 2.2 The Āgamas: The Five Lower Fetters and the Graduated Path

If the three unwholesome roots represent the "quality" of affliction, the five lower fetters (pañcāvarabhāgīya-saṃyojana) represent its "measure" — marking the bonds that must be broken in sequence along the path. The Saṃyuktāgama frames this through the four fruits of the śramaṇa:

> 「何等為須陀洹果？謂三結斷。何等為斯陀含果？謂三結斷，貪、恚、癡薄。何等為阿那含果？謂五下分結盡。何等為阿羅漢果？謂貪、恚、癡永盡，一切煩惱永盡。」[^7]

"What is the fruit of stream-entry? The cutting of three fetters. What is the fruit of once-returning? The cutting of three fetters, with craving, aversion, and delusion thinned. What is the fruit of non-returning? The exhaustion of the five lower fetters. What is the fruit of arhatship? The permanent exhaustion of craving, aversion, and delusion — the permanent exhaustion of all afflictions."

The three fetters (self-view, attachment to precepts and rituals, doubt) are all at the level of "view" — cognitive errors. The stream-enterer's breaking of the three fetters is the prototype of "seeing the path." The five lower fetters add sensual desire and aversion — moving from the intellectual to the affective level. Arhatship requires the "permanent exhaustion" of the three unwholesome roots.

The Saṃyuktāgama further links the five lower fetters to knowing the four noble truths as they truly are:

> 「苦聖諦如實知……作如是知、如是見已，斷五下分結，謂身見、戒取、疑、貪欲、瞋恚。」[^8]

"Having known as it truly is, having seen as it truly is — the five lower fetters are cut." The key term is "as it truly is" (yathābhūta) — the antidote to affliction is not suppression but accurate knowing. This spirit is entirely consonant with the Yogācāra principle of "transforming consciousness into wisdom."

#### 2.3 The Āgamas: Self-View — The Root of Twenty Identity-Views

Among the six root afflictions, satkāya-dṛṣṭi holds a special position. The Saṃyuktāgama's analysis of twenty identity-views reveals the precise architecture of self-grasping:

> 「若諸沙門、婆羅門見有我者，一切皆於此五受陰見我。諸沙門、婆羅門見色是我，色異我，我在色，色在我；見受、想、行、識是我，識異我，我在識，識在我。」[^9]

"All who see a self see it within the five appropriated aggregates." Each of the five aggregates admits four modes of self-grasping (the aggregate is the self, the self is other than the aggregate, the self is within the aggregate, the aggregate is within the self), yielding twenty identity-views. This is not an ancient exercise in nomenclature — it systematically exhausts every location where "self" can hide. Yogācāra develops this into the complete theory of "pervasive discrimination" (parikalpita), while the Āgamas already provide the original analytical framework.

#### 2.4 The Āgamas: Pride — The Elder Khemaka's Residual Trace of Realization

Pride is the subtlest of the six root afflictions, and Khemaka's testimony is one of the most poignant records of practice in the entire Āgama corpus:

> 「我不言色是我，我異色；受、想、行、識是我，我異識。然於五受陰我慢、我欲、我使，未斷、未知、未離、未吐。」[^10]

"I do not say form is my self, nor that my self is other than form. … Yet regarding the five appropriated aggregates, self-conceit, self-desire, and self-tendency remain uncut, unknown, unabandoned, unvomited."

Khemaka had correctly seen that the five aggregates are not self — no error of view. But "self-conceit, self-desire, self-tendency" persisted. He likened it to the fragrance of flowers remaining on a washed cloth. Finally, he described the remedy:

> 「然後於五受陰增進思惟，觀察生滅……我慢、我欲、我使，一切悉除，是名真實正觀。」[^11]

"Then, through progressive contemplation of the five aggregates, observing arising and passing away … self-conceit, self-desire, and self-tendency are all eliminated — this is called true right observation."

This passage is crucial to this paper: it proves that "seeing" (correct understanding at the intellectual level) does not equal "severing" (complete clearing at the dispositional level). Discriminatively-arisen self-view can be eliminated at the path of seeing, but innately-arisen self-conceit requires repeated observation of arising and passing at the path of cultivation. This is the Āgama root of the Yogācāra distinction between discriminatively-arisen and innately-arisen afflictions.

The Saṃyuktāgama further links the perception of impermanence directly to the elimination of self-conceit:

> 「無常想者，能建立無我想，聖弟子住無我想，心離我慢，順得涅槃。」[^12]

"The perception of impermanence establishes the perception of non-self; the noble disciple dwelling in the perception of non-self, the mind departs from self-conceit and accords with nirvāṇa."

#### 2.5 The Āgamas: Doubt and the Five Hindrances

Doubt functions both as one of the "three fetters" (the barrier at the path of seeing) and as one of the "five hindrances" (the barrier in meditation). The Saṃyuktāgama analyzes the doubt-hindrance through the framework of its "nutriment" and "non-nutriment":

> 「何等為疑蓋食？……於過去世猶豫、未來世猶豫、現在世猶豫，於彼起不正思惟，未起疑蓋令起，已起疑蓋能令增廣，是名疑蓋食。」[^13]

"What nourishes the doubt-hindrance? Vacillation regarding the past, future, and present, with unwholesome attention — this causes the unarisen doubt-hindrance to arise and the arisen doubt-hindrance to increase."

> 「何等為疑蓋不食？彼緣起法思惟……是名疑蓋不食。」[^14]

"What starves the doubt-hindrance? Contemplation of dependent origination." The antidote to doubt is not faith (an affective response) but contemplation of dependent origination (rational cognition).

The five hindrances as a complete system — sensual desire, aversion, sloth-and-torpor, restlessness-and-regret, doubt — bear direct relevance to the indeterminate mental factors: the sloth-and-torpor hindrance involves "sleep" (middha), and the restlessness-and-regret hindrance involves "regret" (kaukṛtya), demonstrating the dual character of the four indeterminates in practice.[^16][^17]

#### 2.6 The Śūraṅgama-sūtra: Afflictions Manifest as the Fifty Skandha-Demons

If the Āgamas provide the foundational taxonomy of affliction, the Śūraṅgama-sūtra's fifty skandha-demons constitute a clinical record of affliction manifesting during meditation. The sūtra first establishes its guiding principle:

> 「心中不明，認賊為子。」[^18]

"The mind not clearly seeing, one takes the thief for one's own child." These four characters are the core of the entire fifty skandha-demon teaching — the greatest danger of affliction lies not in its power but in the practitioner's mistaking affliction for realization. This is perfectly consonant with the Yogācāra definition of ignorance as "darkness regarding principles and things."

Among the ten demons of the sensation-aggregate, all six root afflictions manifest. The mad-demon of pride is especially vivid:

> 「感激過分……謂三僧祇一念能越。……若作聖解，則有狂魔入其心腑，見人則誇，我慢無比。」[^19]

"Excessive exaltation … claiming to surpass three incalculable eons in a single thought. … If one takes this as saintly realization, the mad demon enters one's heart — boasting to everyone, pride beyond compare."

The great-pride demon even more dramatically:

> 「慢與過慢，及慢過慢，或增上慢，或卑劣慢，一時俱發。心中尚輕十方如來。」[^20]

"Conceit, over-conceit, conceit-exceeding-conceit, superiority conceit, inferiority conceit — all erupt simultaneously. One's mind even looks down upon the Tathāgatas of the ten directions."

The emptiness-demon corresponds to the extreme form of ignorance:

> 「撥無因果，一向入空。……若作聖解，則有空魔入其心腑，乃謗持戒名為小乘。」[^21]

"Denying causality, plunging entirely into emptiness. … If one takes this as saintly realization, the emptiness-demon enters one's heart — slandering those who uphold precepts as 'Hīnayāna.'"

The desire-demon reveals craving's transformation during practice:

> 「味其虛明深入心骨，其心忽有無限愛生，愛極發狂，便為貪欲。」[^22]

"Savoring that luminous emptiness deep into the marrow, the mind suddenly gives rise to limitless love; love pushed to its extreme becomes craving."

The common pattern across the thought-aggregate's ten demons is even more striking — each arises from "craving" (craving for skill, craving for experience, craving for accord, craving for analysis …), and each attracts demons that "covertly practice sensual desire." This demonstrates that craving at advanced stages of practice does not disappear but disguises itself as diligence, meditative joy, or spiritual attainment.

The sūtra provides the master antidote:

> 「主人若迷，客得其便。當處禪那覺悟無惑，則彼魔事無奈汝何。」[^23]

"If the host is confused, the guest seizes the advantage. Remain in meditative awareness without delusion, and those demonic affairs can do nothing to you." Afflictions are "guests"; awareness is the "host."

#### 2.7 The Saṃdhinirmocana-sūtra: Two Obstructions

As the canonical authority for S3, the Saṃdhinirmocana-sūtra's core contribution to affliction theory is the two-obstruction system and graduated treatment.

The sūtra assigns the five hindrances to śamatha and vipaśyanā:

> 「掉舉、惡作，是奢摩他障；惛沈、睡眠、疑，是毘鉢舍那障；貪欲、瞋恚，當知俱障。」[^24]

"Restlessness and regret obstruct śamatha; torpor, sleep, and doubt obstruct vipaśyanā; sensual desire and aversion obstruct both."

The sūtra's verse summarizes the bondage of affliction and its remedy:

> 「眾生為相縛，及彼麁重縛；要勤修止觀，爾乃得解脫。」[^25]

"Sentient beings are bound by characteristics and by heaviness; only through diligent cultivation of śamatha and vipaśyanā can they attain liberation."

The three types of latent affliction describe progressively deeper layers:

> 「略有三種：一者、害伴隨眠……二者、羸劣隨眠……三者、微細隨眠，謂於第八地已上，從此已去，一切煩惱不復現行，唯有所知障為依止故。」[^26]

"In brief, three types: (1) affliction whose companions have been destroyed — the first five bhūmis; (2) weakened affliction — the sixth and seventh bhūmis, subdued by cultivation; (3) subtle affliction — from the eighth bhūmi onward, where all afflictions cease to manifest, with only the cognitive obstruction as basis."

Three stages of pāramitā mastery mark the shift in affliction-power relations:

> 「而諸煩惱猶故現行，未能制伏然為彼伏……是名波羅蜜多。復於無量時……然能制伏非彼所伏……是名近波羅蜜多。復於無量時……一切煩惱皆不現行……是名大波羅蜜多。」[^27]

From "subdued by afflictions" to "subduing afflictions" to "afflictions ceasing to manifest" — this is not annihilation but a progressive reversal of the power relationship.

The sūtra also specifies the three pāramitās that treat afflictions:

> 「由精進故，雖未永伏一切煩惱……而能勇猛修諸善品，彼諸煩惱不能傾動善品加行；由靜慮故，永伏煩惱；由般若故，永害隨眠。」[^28]

Exertion prevents afflictions from toppling practice; dhyāna permanently suppresses afflictive manifestation; prajñā permanently destroys latent seeds — three functions, each indispensable.

#### 2.8 The Avataṃsaka-sūtra: Five Layers of Affliction Pedagogy

The Avataṃsaka-sūtra provides the most multidimensional affliction teaching, articulated across five layers.

**Layer one: dependent origination analysis** (sixth bhūmi). The three links of ignorance, craving, and grasping constitute the "affliction path" within the twelve-link chain:

> 「此中無明、愛、取不斷是煩惱道，行、有不斷是業道，餘分不斷是苦道……如是三道離我、我所，但有生滅，猶如束蘆。」[^29]

"The three paths — affliction, karma, suffering — devoid of self and self-possession, consisting only of arising and ceasing, like bound reeds leaning on each other."

**Layer two: the critical transition from the seventh to the eighth bhūmi:**

> 「不名有煩惱者，不名無煩惱者。何以故？一切煩惱不現行故，不名有者；求如來智心未滿故，不名無者。」[^30]

"Neither called 'one with afflictions' nor 'one without afflictions.' Why? Because all afflictions have ceased to manifest, one is not called 'with afflictions'; because the aspiration for Tathāgata-wisdom is not yet fulfilled, one is not called 'without afflictions.'" This is the Middle Way applied precisely to affliction theory.

**Layer three:** the ninth-bhūmi bodhisattva observes the "dense forest of afflictions," knowing their aspects: "long-accompanying nature, boundless triggering nature, innate inseparability, the oneness of dormancy and arising, aspects of mental association and non-association."[^31]

**Layer four: transformative utilization** in the Gaṇḍavyūha chapter:

> 「略說貪、瞋、癡等一切煩惱是菩薩器仗，以煩惱門度眾生故。」[^32]

"In brief, all afflictions including craving, aversion, and delusion are the bodhisattva's instruments — because through the gate of afflictions, sentient beings are liberated."

> 「住於涅槃而示現生死，知無眾生而勤行教化，究竟寂滅而現起煩惱。」[^33]

"Abiding in nirvāṇa yet manifesting birth-and-death; knowing there are no sentient beings yet diligently teaching; ultimately quiescent yet manifesting afflictions."

Simultaneously, the sūtra warns against the opposite extreme:

> 「樂處寂滅，斷除煩惱，魔所攝持。」[^34]

"Delighting in quiescence, eliminating afflictions — seized by Māra." Eagerness to annihilate affliction itself becomes a form of clinging.

**Layer five:** the Entering the Dharma-Realm chapter uses bodhicitta as the universal antidote:

> 「菩提心者，猶如淨水，能洗一切煩惱垢故。菩提心者，猶如良藥，能治一切煩惱病故。菩提心者，猶如利刀，能斬一切煩惱首故。」[^35]

From analysis to treatment to transcendence to utilization to the return to bodhicitta — this is the most complete pedagogical arc in Mahāyāna affliction theory.

### B. Treatise Sources

#### 2.9 Cheng Weishi Lun, Fascicle 6: Definitions of the Six Root Afflictions

The Cheng Weishi Lun defines each affliction through the standard framework of "nature–function":

**Craving (lobha):** "Attachment to existence and its conditions; its nature is defiled clinging. Its function is to obstruct non-craving and produce suffering."[^36]

**Aversion (dveṣa):** "Resentful hostility toward suffering and its sources; its nature is hostility. Its function is to obstruct non-aversion, generate agitation of body and mind, and serve as the basis for evil conduct."[^37]

**Ignorance (moha):** "Darkness regarding all principles and things; its nature is obscuration. Its function is to serve as the basis of all contamination."[^38]

**Pride (māna):** "Exalting oneself over others; its nature is haughtiness. Its function is to obstruct humility and produce suffering."[^39]

**Doubt (vicikitsā):** "Vacillation regarding the truths; its nature is indecision. Its function is to obstruct wholesome qualities."[^40]

**Wrong views (dṛṣṭi):** "Inverted assessment of the truths; its nature is defiled wisdom. Its function is to obstruct right view and bring about suffering."[^41]

All six share a common structure: "Regarding X, Y is its nature; it obstructs Z and produces W." Each affliction has its corresponding wholesome factor as antidote (craving obstructs non-craving, aversion obstructs non-aversion, ignorance obstructs non-delusion), forming the precise complementary relationship with S3-P08.

#### 2.10 Cheng Weishi Lun, Fascicle 6: The Five Views Expanded

The five views constitute the complete map of intellectual-level affliction:

**Satkāya-dṛṣṭi** — "Grasping at self and what belongs to self within the five appropriated aggregates; its function is to serve as the basis of all views."[^42]

**Antagrāha-dṛṣṭi** — "Grasping at eternalism or annihilationism; it obstructs the middle path and liberation."[^43]

**Mithyā-dṛṣṭi** — "Denying causality, function, and real entities."[^44]

**Dṛṣṭi-parāmarśa** — "Grasping views and their supporting aggregates as supreme; it serves as the basis of all disputes."[^45]

**Śīla-vrata-parāmarśa** — "Grasping precepts, rituals, and their supporting aggregates as supreme; it serves as the basis of fruitless asceticism."[^46]

The five views have a strict hierarchical relationship: satkāya-dṛṣṭi is "the basis of all views" — first comes self-grasping, then extreme views, wrong views, view-attachment, and precept-attachment follow. Simultaneously, "the five views are mutually exclusive in arising, for there cannot be multiple wisdoms in a single mind-moment."[^47]

#### 2.11 Cheng Weishi Lun, Fascicle 6: Discriminative vs. Innate Arising

This is one of the paper's core analyses:

> 「六通俱生及分別起，任運、思察，俱得生故。疑後三見，唯分別起，要由惡友或邪教力，自審思察，方得生故。」[^48]

"Six [of the ten] are both innate and discriminative in arising — craving, aversion, ignorance, pride, satkāya-dṛṣṭi, and antagrāha-dṛṣṭi — because they can arise both spontaneously and through deliberation. Doubt and the latter three views are exclusively discriminative, because they arise only through the influence of unwholesome companions or false teachings, through deliberate investigation."

The soteriological significance:

> 「分別起者，唯見所斷，麁易斷故；若俱生者，唯修所斷，細難斷故。」[^49]

"Discriminatively-arisen afflictions are eliminated only by the path of seeing — being coarse, they are easily severed. Innately-arisen afflictions are eliminated only by the path of cultivation — being subtle, they are difficult to sever."

Discriminatively-arisen afflictions are like malware installed on a phone — identifiable, uninstallable, permanently removable. Innately-arisen afflictions are like deep operating-system defaults — you know they're there, but they're embedded too deeply for a single override. You must manually adjust again and again, overriding the defaults until new settings are truly written into the base layer. The path of seeing uninstalls the malware; the path of cultivation rewrites the operating system.

The debate on whether antagrāha-dṛṣṭi has an innate form is instructive: one position holds that only annihilationism is innate (animals fear death, implying "I will be destroyed"); another holds that eternalism is also innate (animals hoard food, implying "I will continue to exist").[^50] The second example vividly illustrates the "spontaneous" character of innate afflictions — no teaching required, no reasoning necessary; they simply operate.

The Cheng Weishi Lun further determines that discriminatively-arisen afflictions do not arise in the pure suffering destinies (the three evil paths):

> 「分別慢等，純苦趣無，彼無邪師、邪教等故。然彼不造引惡趣業，要分別起能發彼故。」[^51]

Beings in the three evil paths have no unwholesome teachers or false doctrines, so discriminatively-arisen afflictions cannot arise. Yet the karma that propels beings into evil destinies is precisely what discriminatively-arisen afflictions produce — an exquisite causal loop.

#### 2.12 Cheng Weishi Lun: Affliction-Consciousness Correlations

> 「此十煩惱，何識相應？藏識全無；末那有四；意識具十；五識唯三：謂貪、瞋、癡。無分別故。」[^52]

"Which consciousnesses do these ten afflictions accompany? The store-consciousness — none at all. Manas — four. The mental consciousness — all ten. The five sense-consciousnesses — only three: craving, aversion, and ignorance — because they lack discrimination."

The distribution is decisive:
- **Eighth consciousness (ālaya-vijñāna):** entirely free of affliction — it is "unobscured and indeterminate" (anivṛta-avyākṛta), holding seeds but never manifesting afflictions.[^53]
- **Seventh consciousness (manas):** perpetually accompanied by four afflictions — self-delusion, self-view, self-conceit, self-love — unbroken, innate, obscured-indeterminate.[^54]
- **Sixth consciousness (mano-vijñāna):** all ten can accompany it — this is the primary battlefield of affliction.
- **First five consciousnesses:** only craving, aversion, and ignorance in their innate forms — "because they lack discrimination," they cannot give rise to pride (which requires comparison) or views (which require assessment).

The Yogācārabhūmi corroborates: "Of the ten afflictions, seven belong exclusively to the mental consciousness; craving, aversion, and ignorance also pervade the five sense-consciousnesses."[^55]

#### 2.13 Cheng Weishi Lun: Three Moral Natures and Realm-Binding

> 「瞋唯不善，損自、他故，餘九通二。」[^56]

"Aversion is exclusively unwholesome, because it necessarily harms both self and other. The remaining nine admit both unwholesome and indeterminate natures."

> 「瞋唯在欲，餘通三界。」[^57]

"Aversion exists only in the desire realm; the rest pervade all three realms."

Aversion is the only affliction judged as "exclusively unwholesome" and "desire-realm only." Attaining dhyāna does not eliminate afflictions — it merely suppresses them. Upon leaving meditation and encountering conditions, aversion manifests as before. "Suppressed by concentration" is not "severed by the path."

#### 2.14 Cheng Weishi Lun, Fascicle 9: The Two Obstructions

The two-obstruction system represents the highest-level architecture of Yogācāra affliction theory:

> 「煩惱障者，謂執遍計所執實我……能障涅槃，名煩惱障。」[^58]

> 「所知障者，謂執遍計所執實法……能障菩提，名所知障。」[^59]

"The afflictive obstruction takes the grasping at a real self as its chief; it obstructs nirvāṇa. The cognitive obstruction takes the grasping at real dharmas as its chief; it obstructs bodhi."

Both share satkāya-dṛṣṭi as their "chief," but differ in object: the afflictive obstruction grasps at a "real self" (pudgala-ātman), the cognitive obstruction grasps at "real dharmas" (dharma-ātman). The former obstructs liberation from saṃsāra; the latter obstructs omniscient awakening.

The path-differentiation is explicit: "The two vehicles can only sever the afflictive obstruction; bodhisattvas sever both. Permanent severance of both requires the noble path; suppression of both manifestations can be achieved through mundane paths as well."[^60]

The bodhisattva path's distinctive strategy:

> 「唯修永滅所知障道，留煩惱障助願受生。」[^61]

"Only cultivating the path that permanently eliminates the cognitive obstruction, retaining the afflictive obstruction to assist vow-powered rebirth." The bodhisattva deliberately does not exhaust the afflictive obstruction — if it were fully eliminated, they would enter nirvāṇa and could no longer take birth among sentient beings. This is the Yogācāra precision-equivalent of the Avataṃsaka's "ultimate quiescence yet manifesting afflictions."

The final simultaneous severance occurs in the vajra-like samādhi:

> 「金剛喻定現在前時，彼皆頓斷，入如來地。」[^62]

#### 2.15 Cheng Weishi Lun, Fascicle 7: The Four Indeterminate Mental Factors

The core characteristic of the four indeterminates is their "triple indeterminacy":

> 「悔、眠、尋、伺於善、染等皆不定故，非如觸等定遍心故，非如欲等定遍地故，立不定名。」[^63]

Indeterminate in moral quality (unlike the universals, which pervade all three natures; unlike the wholesome factors, which are exclusively wholesome; unlike afflictions, which are exclusively defiled), indeterminate in mental pervasion, and indeterminate in realm-pervasion.

**Regret (kaukṛtya):** "Remorse over what was done; its nature is repentance. Its function is to obstruct śamatha."[^64] Regret pulls the mind back to the past, preventing present-moment stability.

**Sleep (middha):** "Making the body uncontrollable, with obscuration and contraction as its nature. Its function is to obstruct vipaśyanā."[^65] Sleep obscures the mind's clarity, blocking insight.

**Examination (vitarka) and investigation (vicāra):** "Examination causes the mind to move hastily in the domain of internal speech, with coarse movement as its nature. Investigation … with fine movement as its nature. Both use a portion of volition (cetanā) and wisdom (prajñā) as their substance."[^66]

Examination and investigation are not independent entities but the coarse and fine operations of volition and wisdom in the dimension of "internal speech" (mano-jalpa) — the linguistically mediated inner thinking that constitutes the basis of human conceptual reasoning.

Realm-binding: "Regret and sleep exist only in the desire realm. Examination and investigation exist in the desire realm and the first dhyāna."[^67] From the second dhyāna onward, examination and investigation cease — hence the first dhyāna is called "possessing examination and investigation."

The affliction-accompaniment of the four indeterminates: "Regret can only accompany ignorance, because its mode of operation is coarse while craving and other afflictions are subtle. Sleep, examination, and investigation can accompany all ten afflictions, because they are mutually non-contradictory."[^68]

#### 2.16 Yogācārabhūmi: The Affliction Section

The Yogācārabhūmi provides the systematic taxonomy and six-cause analysis.

The general definition of affliction:

> 「煩惱自性者，謂若法生時，其相自然不寂靜起；由彼起故，不寂靜行相續而轉。」[^69]

"The intrinsic nature of affliction: when it arises, its characteristic is naturally non-tranquil; due to its arising, a non-tranquil mode continues." "Non-tranquility" — this is the most essential feature of affliction. It is not a "bad thing" but a state of "non-tranquility." This definition forms a precise contrast with the wholesome factor of "ease" (praśrabdhi, tranquil well-being).

The six causes of affliction:

> 「煩惱因者，謂六種因。一由所依故，二由所緣故，三由親近故，四由邪教故，五由數習故，六由作意故。」[^70]

"Basis" (latent seeds) and "habituation" (past perfumation) are internal causes; "objects" (favorable conditions), "companionship" (unwholesome associates), and "false teachings" are external conditions; "attention" (ayoniśo-manaskāra) is the trigger. This six-cause framework traces directly to the Āgama teaching that unwholesome attention (ayoniśo-manaskāra) gives rise to affliction.

The seven positions of affliction describe the complete spectrum from latency to manifestation:

> 「煩惱位者，略有七種。一隨眠位，二纏位，三分別起位，四俱生位，五耎位，六中位，七上位。」[^71]

From latency (dormant seeds) to entanglement (binding the mind) to discriminative/innate arising (mode of manifestation) to soft/medium/intense (three grades of strength) — a three-dimensional analysis from "seed" to "manifestation" to "intensity."

#### 2.17 Bāshí Guījǔ Bǔzhù: Affliction-Related Verse Lines

The Eight Consciousnesses Verse Annotations maps each consciousness to its affliction profile:

First five consciousnesses: "Universals, object-specific, eleven wholesome, two intermediate, eight major, craving, aversion, ignorance."[^72]

Sixth consciousness: "Root, subsidiary, faith, et cetera — all connected" — all fifty-one mental factors accompany it, including all six root afflictions and twenty subsidiary afflictions.[^73]

Seventh consciousness: "Four afflictions and eight major always arise together" — "Sentient beings day and night remain in perpetual confusion."[^74]

Eighth consciousness: "Its nature is solely unobscured [and indeterminate], with only the five universals" — no affliction accompanies it at all.[^75]

#### 2.18 Pañcaskandhaka-prakaraṇa: Concise Definitions

Vasubandhu's Pañcaskandhaka-prakaraṇa provides more concise definitions than the Cheng Weishi Lun: craving is "defiled attachment and clinging to the five appropriated aggregates"; aversion is "delighting in causing harm to sentient beings"; ignorance is "lack of understanding regarding karmic results and the truths and jewels."[^76]

The treatise confirms the discriminative-innate classification: "Among the afflictions, the latter three views and doubt are exclusively discriminatively-arisen; the rest are both innate and discriminatively-arisen."[^77]

## III. Core Argument

### 3.1 The Defilement Structure: A System, Not a List

Traditional pedagogy often presents the six root afflictions as a flat list of negative qualities. But synthesizing the scriptural and treatise evidence reveals clear structural hierarchy among the six.

**Layer one: ignorance as the total root.** The Cheng Weishi Lun declares ignorance "the basis of all contamination."[^78] If the affliction system is a tree, ignorance is its roots. While the roots remain, pruning branches achieves nothing lasting.

**Layer two: craving and aversion as twin wings.** On the foundation of ignorance, the mind generates attachment to "existence" (craving) and hostility toward "suffering" (aversion) — one pulling, one pushing, driving sentient beings through the six destinies. Together with ignorance, they constitute the "three unwholesome roots."

**Layer three: pride and doubt as subtle obstacles.** Pride permeates all self-comparison without the drama of craving or aversion. Khemaka's experience proves that even after right view is established, self-conceit persists.[^88] Doubt prevents wholesome qualities from arising — the first critical barrier to practice.

**Layer four: the five views as systematic cognitive error.** Satkāya-dṛṣṭi is "the basis of all views"[^79] — operating through defiled wisdom, the five views are not impulses but reasoning gone wrong — one can be "rationally thinking" while deeply mired in wrong views.

Extending this series' vehicle metaphor: if universals are the engine, object-specific factors the steering wheel, and wholesome factors the fuel for liberation, then root afflictions are the contaminants mixed into the fuel. Ignorance is the sediment at the bottom of the tank — every fuel load is polluted by it. Craving and aversion are two types of corrosive agent — craving adheres, aversion burns. Pride and doubt are microscopic particles clogging the fuel line — tiny yet lethal. The five views are counterfeit fuel labels — making you believe you've loaded the right fuel when the direction is entirely wrong.

### 3.2 Ignorance: The Root of Saṃsāra

Among the six root afflictions, ignorance holds a unique position. It is not one affliction among peers but the foundational condition for all afflictions.

The Cheng Weishi Lun definition — "darkness regarding all principles and things" — reveals ignorance's nature: not "not knowing some fact" but a fundamental misdirection of cognition regarding all principles and things. This is not an information deficit but a foundational cognitive deviation.

The Yogācārabhūmi defines the affliction-nature as "non-tranquility"[^80] — and ignorance is the source of all non-tranquility. The mind should cognize principles and things as they truly are, but ignorance causes "darkness," such that every cognitive act deviates from reality.

From the perspective of the twelve links: "ignorance conditions formations, formations condition consciousness …" — ignorance is the first link. The sixth-bhūmi bodhisattva observes that "ignorance, craving, and grasping constitute the affliction path"[^81] — ignorance is the initiator.

But a key Middle Way determination applies: the universality of ignorance does not entail its necessity. Ignorance is intermittent, has antidotes (non-delusion, prajñā), and has boundaries (it does not enter the eighth consciousness). "Basis of all contamination" does not mean "permanently ineliminable" — precisely because ignorance is a conditioned phenomenon of the dependent-nature (paratantra), it admits treatment.

### 3.3 Craving and Aversion: The Twin Wings of Ignorance

If ignorance is the root, craving and aversion are the two most robust branches growing from it.

**Craving** — "attachment to existence and its conditions." "Existence" means the three realms of existence; "its conditions" means the causes producing them. The Pañcaskandhaka-prakaraṇa defines it as "defiled attachment and clinging to the five appropriated aggregates,"[^82] directly pointing to craving's essential bond with psychophysical existence. Craving's scope covers everything from desire for food to clinging to meditative bliss.

**Aversion** — "resentful hostility toward suffering and its sources." The Cheng Weishi Lun specially emphasizes: "Aversion necessarily causes agitation of body and mind, giving rise to evil karma."[^83] Aversion is the only affliction judged as "exclusively unwholesome" — no exceptions.

A notable asymmetry: craving pervades all three realms; aversion exists only in the desire realm.[^84] In the form and formless realms, concentration power suppresses aversion. This means that attaining dhyāna can temporarily suppress anger, but it cannot truly sever the seeds of aversion. Upon returning from concentration and encountering conditions, aversion manifests as before.

### 3.4 The Views: Intellectual-Level Contamination

The five views are special in that they take "defiled wisdom" (kliṣṭa-prajñā) as their substance — not affective impulse but cognitive systematic error. One can be "rationally thinking" while deeply entrenched in wrong views.

Satkāya-dṛṣṭi is the root of all views. The Āgama analysis of twenty identity-views exhaustively maps every location where "self" can hide. Yogācāra develops this into the theory of "pervasive discrimination": upon dependent-nature dharmas, one discriminates a real self and real dharmas.

The five views have a strict genetic sequence: self-view → extreme views → wrong views → attachment to views / attachment to precepts and rituals. Once self-grasping exists, the questions "Am I eternal or will I be destroyed?" necessarily follow. The debate on whether antagrāha-dṛṣṭi has an innate form is itself instructive — animals hoarding food implies an innate eternalist assumption.[^85] Wrong views encompass "denying causality, function, and real entities"[^86] — the broadest scope among the five. Attachment to views — "grasping one's views as supreme, the basis of all disputes"[^87] — is the psychological root of sectarian conflict.

### 3.5 Pride and Doubt: The Subtle Obstacles

**Pride's** seven varieties (conceit, over-conceit, conceit-exceeding-conceit, self-conceit, superiority conceit, inferiority conceit, wrong conceit) display the many faces of self-exaltation. "Inferiority conceit" deserves special note — "deeming oneself only slightly inferior to the greatly superior" — seemingly humble, yet still comparing, still conceit.

Khemaka's experience is pride's finest clinical case.[^88] The Śūraṅgama-sūtra's sensation-aggregate demons dramatize it further — "seven types of conceit erupting simultaneously, one's mind even looking down upon the Tathāgatas of the ten directions."[^89] From the Yogācāra perspective, Khemaka had severed discriminatively-arisen self-view (path of seeing) but not yet cleared innately-arisen self-conceit (the target of the path of cultivation).

**Doubt** appears mild but is lethal. "Vacillation as its nature; its function is to obstruct wholesome qualities"[^90] — doubt's harm is not in doing wrong but in doing nothing. Wholesome qualities simply fail to arise.

The Āgamas determine that doubt's antidote is "contemplation of dependent origination"[^91] — not faith, but rational observation. The Ekottarāgama specifies doubt's content: "Is there a self? Is there no self? Is there birth? Is there no birth? … Are there those who attain realization? Are there none?"[^92] — these are not casual hesitations but fundamental wavering on the most basic questions of practice. Doubt appears last among both the five hindrances and the three fetters — it is the final threshold that must be crossed for irreversible entry onto the path.

### 3.6 Discriminative vs. Innate Arising: Path of Seeing and Path of Cultivation

This is the most precise analysis in Yogācāra affliction theory and the distinction with the greatest impact on practical guidance.

**Discriminatively-arisen afflictions** require three conditions: unwholesome companions, false teachings, and deliberate investigation through unwholesome attention.[^93] All three are acquired — no one is born with wrong views; they must be taught, learned, and reasoned into existence.

**Innately-arisen afflictions** are characterized by "spontaneity" (anābhoga) — from beginningless time, without external triggers, they simply operate. An infant does not need to be taught to crave its mother's milk; an animal does not need instruction to fear death. The soteriological implication: "Innately-arisen afflictions are eliminated only by the path of cultivation — being subtle, they are difficult to sever."[^94]

The Saṃdhinirmocana-sūtra's three types of latent affliction map perfectly: destroyed-companion latency (first five bhūmis — discriminatively-arisen eliminated), weakened latency (sixth–seventh bhūmis — innate subtle manifestations subdued), subtle latency (eighth bhūmi onward — afflictions cease to manifest entirely).[^95]

The Yogācārabhūmi's six causes also map: "basis" (latent seeds) and "habituation" correspond to innate internal causes; "companionship" and "false teachings" correspond to discriminative external conditions; "attention" is the shared trigger.[^96]

### 3.7 The Two Obstructions

The two-obstruction system is the most distinctively bodhisattva-path feature of Yogācāra affliction theory.

The afflictive obstruction grasps at a "real self" (person-self); the cognitive obstruction grasps at "real dharmas" (dharma-self).[^97] The former obstructs nirvāṇa; the latter obstructs bodhi. The śrāvaka and pratyekabuddha paths focus solely on eliminating the afflictive obstruction to attain nirvāṇa. The bodhisattva path must eliminate both to attain complete awakening.

The bodhisattva's distinctive strategy — "retaining the afflictive obstruction to assist vow-powered rebirth"[^98] — means the bodhisattva deliberately preserves the afflictive obstruction so as to continue taking birth among sentient beings. The precondition is that the bodhisattva can already "subdue" it (not driven by afflictions to create karma) but chooses not to "sever" it (preserving the conditions for rebirth).

Only at the final vajra-like samādhi are both obstructions simultaneously severed in a single moment.[^99]

### 3.8 The Four Indeterminates: Neutral Instruments

The key characteristic of the four indeterminate factors is directional neutrality — neither inherently wholesome nor afflictive; direction depends entirely on usage.

**Regret** has dual potential. Wholesome regret — regretting evil done — is the psychological basis of repentance, the starting point for moral transformation. Unwholesome regret — regretting good done — erodes the power of merit. But regardless of type, regret "obstructs śamatha" — it pulls the mind into the past, preventing present-moment stability. Hence the meditative tradition emphasizes "letting go" — not never repenting, but releasing regret once repentance is complete.

**Sleep's** duality is thoroughly everyday. Wholesome sleep restores energy as a necessary condition for vigorous practice. Unwholesome sleep is excessive torpor obstructing practice. The Āgama records five "nutriments" of the sloth-and-torpor hindrance: weakness, displeasure, yawning, overeating, and lassitude[^100] — all conditions that can be noticed and adjusted.

**Examination and investigation** are the coarse and fine thinking functions of the mind. Wholesome: careful contemplation of dharma, investigation of meaning, observation of body and mind. Unwholesome: proliferating fantasy, unwholesome deliberation, obsessive rumination on afflictions.

Examination and investigation use "a portion of volition and wisdom as their substance"[^101] — they are not independent entities but the operations of volition and wisdom in the dimension of "internal speech." This means examination and investigation are the mental-factor basis of human linguistic thought — the reason we can engage in conceptual reasoning and verbal expression.

Extending the vehicle metaphor: the four indeterminate factors are the gearbox. Regret is reverse gear — one can back up to correct errors (wholesome regret) or reverse into confusion (unwholesome regret). Sleep is neutral gear — the engine can rest and recover (wholesome sleep) or the vehicle stalls entirely (unwholesome sleep). Examination and investigation are manual shifting — coarse and fine adjustments of the mind's operational direction; skillfully used, they enable precise control; poorly used, they produce the anxiety of constant gear-shifting.

### 3.9 The Four Non-Universalities: The Doctrinal Basis for the Possibility of Practice

S3-P07 analyzed the "four universalities" of the universal mental factors. S3-P08 showed that wholesome factors possess only one (all realms) and lack the other three. The present paper's affliction factors lack all four:

**Not universal in moral quality:** afflictions are exclusively defiled (aversion is exclusively unwholesome; the remaining nine admit both unwholesome and indeterminate, but never wholesome).[^102]

**Not universal across realms:** aversion exists only in the desire realm; the rest pervade all three realms but are weakened by concentration in higher realms. Regret and sleep exist only in the desire realm; examination and investigation exist only in the desire realm and first dhyāna.[^103]

**Not universal in time:** afflictions are intermittent — one is not craving every moment, not angry every moment. (Manas's four afflictions are the exception — they are permanent, but that is the seventh consciousness's special condition, not the norm for the six operative consciousnesses.)

**Not universal in consciousness-correlation:** the eighth consciousness has no afflictions at all; the seventh has only four; the first five have only three; only the sixth admits all ten.[^104]

The doctrinal significance is immense: **if afflictions were necessary, eternal, omnipresent, and total, practice would have no space.** Precisely because afflictions are conditioned (requiring six causes to arise), bounded (not pervading all realms), intermittent (not permanent), and partial (not occupying the entire consciousness-system), there exists space for awareness, treatment, and transformation.

### 3.10 The Affliction–Wholesome Treatment Structure: The P08 ↔ P09 Complementary Loop

S3-P08's eleven wholesome factors and S3-P09's six root afflictions (plus four indeterminates) form a precise complementary pair:

Non-craving treats craving; non-aversion treats aversion; non-delusion treats ignorance — the three wholesome roots directly treat the three unwholesome roots.[^105] Faith treats doubt — faith's three aspects (trusting in reality, trusting in virtue, trusting in capacity) precisely answer doubt's three layers (Is causality real? Do the Three Jewels have virtue? Am I capable?). Conscientiousness and shame treat attachment to views and precept-attachment. Non-harming treats the extreme form of aversion.

But treatment is not annihilation. The Cheng Weishi Lun's language is "obstructs non-craving" and "obstructs non-aversion" — affliction "obstructs" wholesome qualities; wholesome qualities also "obstruct" affliction. The two are alternative possible directions for the same mental-factor position: facing the same object, one can generate craving (defiled attachment) or non-craving (dispassion or equanimity). This is not destroying one and establishing another but a directional shift in each moment of choice.

Returning to the vehicle metaphor for a complete summation: the engine (five universals) starts in every moment; the steering wheel (five object-specific factors) determines the focus of attention; the fuel tank simultaneously contains clean fuel (eleven wholesome factors) and contaminants (six affliction factors); the gearbox (four indeterminate factors) can accelerate forward or reverse — and the vehicle's direction of travel depends on which force dominates in each moment. Practice is not replacing the vehicle but progressively purifying the fuel, correcting the direction, and training the controls until the vehicle naturally drives toward liberation.

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Dialogue

⚠️ **All analogies below are heuristic comparisons, not theoretical equivalences. The precise doctrinal meaning of Buddhist concepts is determined by the canonical sources.**

### 4.1 Craving ↔ Addiction Neurocircuitry ⚠️

Modern neuroscience research on addiction reveals the mechanism of the dopamine reward circuit: the brain releases dopamine in anticipation of reward, generating a "wanting" impulse that is neurally distinct from "liking." The addict's suffering is that they intensely "want" something, but the "liking" upon obtaining it has dramatically diminished.[^106]

Craving's definition — "defiled attachment to existence and its conditions" — shares a structural similarity with the reward circuit's "wanting": both are compulsive approach-drives, both disconnected from actual satisfaction. Yet Yogācāra analysis goes deeper: craving is not merely a neural impulse but a dispositional tendency at the seed level — even if the neural circuit is adjusted, the seed remains in the eighth consciousness. ⚠️

### 4.2 Aversion ↔ Amygdala Hijack ⚠️

LeDoux's research describes the "amygdala hijack" — a threat stimulus bypasses the prefrontal cortex's rational assessment to directly trigger the fight-or-flight response.[^107]

Aversion's operation in the first five consciousnesses — contact-triggered anger — shares a speed characteristic with amygdala hijack: no reasoning required, no discrimination involved, direct response. The first five consciousnesses possess only innate craving, aversion, and ignorance, "because they lack discrimination" — this has a suggestive parallel with the "low road" rapid-response pathway described in neuroscience.

But Yogācāra does not reduce aversion to amygdala activity. Aversion in the first five consciousnesses is a function of "consciousness" (vijñāna), not of matter — consciousness depends on its physical basis (indriya) but is not equivalent to it. ⚠️

### 4.3 Ignorance ↔ Cognitive Bias ⚠️

Kahneman's System 1/System 2 theory and cognitive bias research reveal the "boundedness" of human rationality — humans are not rational decision-makers but cognitively biased subjects. Confirmation bias, availability heuristic, anchoring effect, framing effect — all are instances of cognition systematically deviating from "as it truly is."[^108]

Ignorance's "darkness regarding all principles and things" has a structural parallel with cognitive bias. But Yogācāra's "ignorance" is deeper: cognitive biases can be improved through education and training, but innate ignorance requires the cultivation of prajñāpāramitā for complete eradication. Cognitive bias is a problem at the level of thinking; ignorance is a problem at the level of existence. ⚠️

### 4.4 Discriminative and Innate Arising ↔ Explicit and Implicit Bias ⚠️

Greenwald et al.'s Implicit Association Test (IAT) research reveals a dissociation between explicit attitudes and implicit attitudes: a person can be entirely opposed to racial discrimination at the conscious level while still exhibiting racial bias at the unconscious level.[^109]

Discriminatively-arisen afflictions — triggered by false teachings and unwholesome companions, eliminable through correct education — have a parallel with explicit bias. Innately-arisen afflictions — spontaneously operating from beginningless time, not automatically eliminated by changes in intellectual understanding — have a suggestive similarity to implicit bias.

Khemaka's experience is the best illustration: he had fully understood at the explicit level that the five aggregates are not self (like someone who has completed anti-bias training), yet at the implicit level, self-conceit persisted (like unconscious bias still appearing in IAT results). The long-term cultivation of the bhāvanā-mārga is, in some respects, analogous to the long-term "debiasing" training in psychology — though Yogācāra points to a level deeper than bias. ⚠️

### 4.5 Examination and Investigation ↔ Inner Speech ⚠️

In Baddeley's working memory model, the "phonological loop" maintains and operates linguistically mediated internal representations — people "talk to themselves" when thinking.[^110]

The definition of examination and investigation — "causing the mind to move hastily in the domain of internal speech (mano-jalpa), with coarse/fine movement as its nature" — directly addresses linguistically mediated inner thinking. Examination's coarse movement and investigation's fine movement correspond to the spectrum from rough thinking to careful deliberation in inner speech.

Vygotsky's theory of "inner speech" further holds that inner speech is the internalization of external social speech — children first converse with others, then internalize dialogue as self-dialogue.[^111] This has a suggestive parallel with the Yogācāra determination that examination and investigation "use a portion of volition and wisdom as their substance" — they are not independent entities but the operations of volition (motivational-intentional) and wisdom (discriminative-judicative) in the linguistic dimension. ⚠️

## V. Middle Way Corrections

### ❌ 5.1 Afflictions = Enemies to Be Annihilated

This is the most common misreading of affliction. Treating afflictions as enemies turns practice into warfare — which is itself aversion. The Avataṃsaka-sūtra explicitly warns: "Delighting in quiescence, eliminating afflictions — seized by Māra."[^112]

The correct stance is "seeing" rather than "annihilating." The Śūraṅgama-sūtra teaches: "If the host is confused, the guest seizes the advantage." Afflictions are guests; as long as the host is not confused, the guest has no leverage. The bodhisattva path goes further — "through the gate of afflictions, sentient beings are liberated": understanding the structure of affliction is what makes transformation and skillful utilization possible.

### ❌ 5.2 Knowing Equals Severing

Khemaka's experience is the most powerful counterexample. He had perfectly correct understanding of non-self — no intellectual error. Yet innate self-conceit remained. "Knowing" operates at the level of discrimination; "severing" operates at the level of seeds. Academic papers operate in the domain of "knowing"; "realization" requires actual practice. Reading this paper does not eliminate afflictions — but accurately understanding the structure of affliction is a necessary precondition for treating it.

### ❌ 5.3 Innate Afflictions Are "Born Bad" and Therefore Unchangeable

The "spontaneous" character of innate afflictions is easily misread as fatalism — "they've existed since beginningless time, so they can't be changed." But "beginningless" only means their origin is untraceable, not that their end is unreachable. Innate afflictions are conditioned phenomena of the dependent-nature — they manifest when conditions are present and change when conditions change. The path of cultivation is precisely the process of altering conditions — not overnight elimination, but genuine gradual attenuation.

### ❌ 5.4 The Four Indeterminate Factors Are Unimportant

Regret, sleep, examination, and investigation may seem trivial, yet they are critical variables in practice quality. The two great obstacles in meditation — restlessness-and-regret obstructing śamatha, and torpor-and-sleep obstructing vipaśyanā — are precisely the unwholesome manifestations of regret and sleep. The difference between skillful and unskillful examination-investigation (wise contemplation versus mental proliferation) directly determines the quality of study, reflection, and meditation. Ignoring the indeterminates means ignoring the foundational tools of practice.

### ⚠️ 5.5 The Boundaries of Cross-Disciplinary Analogies

The dopamine circuit is not craving; amygdala hijack is not aversion; cognitive bias is not ignorance; implicit bias is not innate affliction. Neuroscience describes mechanisms at the brain level; Yogācāra analyzes functions at the consciousness level — the former is "physical basis" (indriya), the latter is "consciousness" (vijñāna). Basis does not equal consciousness, just as computer hardware does not equal software. Heuristic analogies can help contemporary readers understand how afflictions operate, but brain activity cannot "prove" or "disprove" Yogācāra determinations. Reason with logic, not with physical reductionism.

## VI. Conclusion

The six root afflictions are not six isolated negative qualities but a structured system of defilement. Ignorance as the root — "the basis of all contamination"; craving and aversion as twin wings — one pulling, one pushing through saṃsāra; pride and doubt as subtle obstacles — Khemaka's residual self-conceit and the Āgama doubt-hindrance teaching reveal their tenacity; the five views as systematic intellectual error — operating through defiled wisdom, the more one thinks, the further one strays.

The discriminative-versus-innate analysis is the most soteriologically significant core of this paper: discriminatively-arisen afflictions are triggered by external conditions, treated at the path of seeing through right view — here the importance of hearing the dharma is established. Innately-arisen afflictions operate spontaneously from beginningless time, gradually cleared at the path of cultivation through meditation — here the indispensability of actual practice is established. Intellectual transformation is the first step, but only the first step.

The two-obstruction system reveals the fundamental difference between the bodhisattva and śrāvaka paths: the śrāvaka severs the afflictive obstruction to enter nirvāṇa; the bodhisattva severs both to attain complete awakening — and even "retains the afflictive obstruction to assist vow-powered rebirth" during the ten bhūmis, demonstrating the Mahāyāna attitude of transcendence toward affliction.

The four indeterminate mental factors (regret, sleep, examination, investigation) are neutral instruments for practice quality: skillfully used, they assist the path; unskillfully used, they obstruct it. Regret obstructs śamatha, sleep obstructs vipaśyanā, examination and investigation admit both wholesome and unwholesome directions — the daily calibration of meditation occurs precisely in the fine-tuning of these four factors.

The four non-universalities of affliction — not universal in moral quality, realm, time, or consciousness-correlation — constitute the doctrinal basis for the possibility of practice. If afflictions were necessary, eternal, omnipresent, and total, practice would have no meaning. Precisely because afflictions are conditioned, bounded, intermittent, and partial, awareness has space, treatment has traction, and transformation has direction.

Next, we turn to the fifth category of mental factors — the twenty subsidiary afflictions (S3-P10) — to explore the branches of affliction: if root afflictions are the core contaminants in the engine, subsidiary afflictions are the exhaust and derivative problems generated during the engine's operation. The unfolding from root to branch will reveal the complete ecology of the affliction system.
