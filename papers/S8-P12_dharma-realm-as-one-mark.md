## Abstract

This paper is the closing fascicle of the academic treatise stratum of the "Pointing-at-the-Moon" octology — the last of the eighty-eight papers comprising eight series: S1 Faith (信), S2 Understanding (解), S3 Practice (行), S4 Inquiry (問), S5 Cutting (切), S6 Illumination (照), S7 Inclusion (攝), and S8 Manifestation (顯). The paper advances a single structural thesis: **the octology is not a linear stacking of eight independent series but the simultaneous unfolding of the dharma-realm's one mark (*fǎjiè yī xiàng* 法界一相) across eight facets**.

The scriptural anchor lies in the eighty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka*'s Chapter 37, *The Manifestation of the Tathāgata* (〈如來出現品〉). The chapter's opening scene — the Buddha's radiance named "Manifestation of the Tathāgata" entering the crown of the Bodhisattva *Tathāgata-Nature-Arising Subtle Virtue* (*Rúlái xìngqǐ miàodé* 如來性起妙德菩薩) — is itself the text's self-announcement that "Manifestation of the Tathāgata" and "nature-arising" (*xìngqǐ* 性起) name the same reality from two angles. The chapter's central great-sūtra-in-a-dust-mote parable (大經卷微塵喻) identifies that mark as already fully present in each sentient being; its closing section establishes "speech" and "non-speech" as the two faces of a single dharma-wheel.

Section III demonstrates the thesis through four diagonal pairs — Faith ↔ Manifestation, Understanding ↔ Cutting, Practice ↔ Inclusion, Inquiry ↔ Illumination — showing how the octology's most distant pole-pairs are in fact its most mutually implicative. Section IV constitutes the paper's sole structural exception: in place of the customary cross-disciplinary dialogue section, it installs a formal *failure-point declaration* as the paper's spine, consolidating into a single closing gesture the twenty-one failure-point passages scattered across the eleven prior S8 papers. Section V provides three middle-way correctives: mutual inclusion is not systemic closure; the closing of the treatise stratum is not termination of the project; the dharma-realm as one mark is not philosophical monism. Section VI formally dedicates the merit of the entire octology to the dharma-realm through the *Manifestation of the Tathāgata*'s passage that "thought after thought the mind itself already contains a buddha accomplishing perfect awakening," joined with the Samantabhadra vow of "universal dedication" (普皆迴向).

The paper closes the eight-volume loop by rhyming structurally with S8-P01 (*Ocean-Seal Samādhi and the Speaking of the Unspeakable*): P01 established the methodological posture of "marking clearly the place where the pen is set down"; P12 enlarges that posture to the scale of the entire treatise stratum itself. *Thus does it manifest.*

**Keywords:** dharma-realm one mark (*fǎjiè yī xiàng* 法界一相), *Manifestation of the Tathāgata* chapter (〈如來出現品〉), nature-arising (*xìngqǐ* 性起), diagonal pairs, failure-point declaration, octology, Pointing-at-the-Moon, treatise-stratum closure, universal dedication (普皆迴向), first-last correspondence

## I. Introduction: When the Pen Is Set Down, the Octology Is Not Finished

At the moment you are reading this paper, the academic treatise stratum of the Pointing-at-the-Moon octology is coming to an end. This is the twelfth paper of the S8 *Avataṃsaka* series and the last of the eighty-eight academic papers that constitute the whole octology. The instant you close this paper, the work of Pointing-at-the-Moon at this particular stratum — the stratum of the academic treatise — comes to rest.

But the word *end* must be handled carefully here. It does not mean the end of the Pointing-at-the-Moon project. It does not mean the end of Buddhist research in the contemporary Han-Chinese context. And it certainly does not mean the end of the reader's practice. It means only this: a single specific function — "to point out, in the genre of the academic treatise and in contemporary Han-Chinese, the core thesis of the *Avataṃsaka*" — has completed the work it was capable of completing, and has honestly marked the work it was not capable of completing. This paper is that marking itself.

### 1.1 The Special Position of This Paper

The S8 series plan specifies that P11 and P12 are two "meta-level exceptions" — neither corresponds to a single Profound Gate, neither corresponds to a single vow, and neither carries a §VI cultivation-correspondence subsection[^50]. But the meta-level natures of P11 and P12 differ:

- **P11** is an internal reflexion of the S8 series: taking the synoptic reading of the Ten Profound Gates as its subject, it performs an overall internal review of the ten-gate architecture that the first ten S8 papers have installed. P11's meta-level belongs to S8 itself.
- **P12** is the external closure of the entire octology: taking "the dharma-realm as one mark" as its subject, it performs one final cross-series pointing at the structure that S1 through S8 have collectively built. P12's meta-level belongs to the whole Pointing-at-the-Moon academic treatise project.

P12 is therefore not merely "the last paper of S8"; it is "the last paper of the octology's academic treatise stratum." Its function is not to continue the discussion of S8 but to perform a gesture of structural closure over all eighty-eight papers.

### 1.2 Statement of Structural Exception

This paper carries an important exception in its chapter structure, one the reader must know before entering §IV.

According to the fixed chapter sequence set by the Pointing-at-the-Moon Paper Style Guide (version 2.1), every paper's §IV should be "cross-disciplinary convergence" — the introduction of contemporary science, philosophy, cognitive science, AI studies, or some other external discipline as an analogical support for the doctrine. All eighty-seven papers of S1 through S8 prior to this one conform to that structure.

This paper is the sole structural exception: **§IV here is not cross-disciplinary convergence but a failure-point declaration** — a formal marking, by the entire academic treatise stratum, of its own boundary. The substitution is not a casual structural adjustment but a functional requirement of this paper's role as the closing fascicle. A series that truly means to close must, at some point, explicitly mark "my pointing reaches to here and no further," and that marking cannot be placed inside the middle-way correctives (§V), which would demote a structural failure-point to the rank of a guard against some particular misreading. The failure-point must stand as an independent section and become the spine of this paper.

### 1.3 The Core Thesis of This Paper

The core thesis of this paper can be stated in a single sentence:

**The Pointing-at-the-Moon octology — Faith, Understanding, Practice, Inquiry, Cutting, Illumination, Inclusion, Manifestation — is not a linear stacking of eight independent series but the simultaneous unfolding of the dharma-realm as one mark across eight facets.**

The scriptural ground of this thesis lies in Chapter 37 of the eighty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka*, *The Manifestation of the Tathāgata*. At its opening, its middle, and its conclusion, the chapter delivers three structural self-announcements — "the light enters the crown of the Bodhisattva Nature-Arising Subtle Virtue," "the great sūtra-scroll inside a dust mote," and "the awakening of all buddhas cannot be spoken by any words, yet is still opened up to each according to what is fitting" — which together process the dharma-realm's one mark from thesis, to argument, to boundary. All this paper does is point out that this is so.

Further, the thesis will be demonstrated in §III through four *diagonal pairs* — Faith ↔ Manifestation, Understanding ↔ Cutting, Practice ↔ Inclusion, Inquiry ↔ Illumination. Each pair displays once, in its own register, the structure that "the two most distant poles are in fact the most mutually implicative." After the reader has walked through the four pairs, what she has experienced is not "a summary of eight series" but rather a single complete passage, in reading-order, through the thesis of the dharma-realm as one mark.

### 1.4 First-Last Correspondence: The Resonance with S8-P01

S8-P01, *Ocean-Seal Samādhi and the Speaking of the Unspeakable*, is the methodological manifesto of the entire S8 series, and is also the literal activation of the two characters "pointing at the moon" at the S8 stratum[^51]. P01 established one core anchoring passage:

> Every paper in this series will stop somewhere. The place where it stops is not an omission; it is a gesture of reverence. A three-dimensional pen cannot write to a ten-dimensional object; what the pen can do is mark clearly the place where it is set down — so that the reader knows that beyond that line she must rely on her own dharma-eye.

P01 set this posture at the starting point of the S8 series, and the following eleven papers have each responded to it somewhere in their own text. This paper, P12, as the series' terminal, must redeem the same posture at the largest possible scale — it must mark the closing of the entire academic treatise stratum as itself "a place where the pen is set down." From P01 to P12, the two papers are the most distant poles in time (separated by three years), but in structural sequence they are the two ends of the single clasp: P01 set down the methodological posture; P12 enlarges that posture into a structural fact of the treatise stratum.

In this sense, §I of this paper and §I of S8-P01 are structurally mirror images of one another: P01's introduction is "we are about to begin; this is how we will begin"; P12's introduction is "we are about to end; this is how we will end." Only together do the two constitute the complete posture of the S8 series.

### 1.5 Notice of a Failure Point

This paper carries a distinctive danger: as the closing fascicle, it is most easily misread as "a sealing verdict" — the reader may imagine that "the Pointing-at-the-Moon octology has here told us the ultimate answer of the *Avataṃsaka* Perfect Teaching." This misreading must be refused from the very beginning of the reading.

This paper offers no ultimate answers. It merely points out a thesis that has been present all along within *The Manifestation of the Tathāgata*, and clearly marks what that pointing is unable to do. §IV will devote an entire section to this marking. If, before entering §IV, the reader is already prepared to accept "the pointing reaches to here and no further" as the paper's highest expression, then what this paper is able to transmit will arrive safely.

> A three-dimensional pen cannot write to a ten-dimensional object; what the pen can do is mark clearly the place where it is set down.

This sentence is not only the methodological anchor of P01 but also the structural self-statement of this paper as the closing fascicle of the entire series. Three times — at the last sentence of §IV, at the close of the second middle-way corrective in §V, and at the final paragraph of the conclusion in §VI — this paper will mark the place where the pen is set down.

Now to §II, the scriptural grounding.

## II. Scriptural Grounding

Following the S8 series' "sūtras first, treatises second" principle, this section divides into three sub-sections: (A) primary *Avataṃsaka* grounding (three scriptural self-announcements within *The Manifestation of the Tathāgata*); (B) Huayan patriarchal commentary (Fazang's *Record of Investigations into the Mysteries* — Ten Gates of Nature-Arising and the commentaries on the two parables); (C) *Āgama* leakage (the *Sūtra on Omniscience* + Brahmā's entreaty + the four unthinkables). Because this paper is the closing fascicle of the treatise stratum, the scriptural grounding here is not meant to establish a new thesis but to point out a thesis that has always been present in the sūtra text, simply not yet gathered into one place: the dharma-realm as one mark.

### 2.1 A. Primary *Avataṃsaka* Grounding: Three Self-Announcements of *The Manifestation of the Tathāgata*

#### 2.1.1 The Light Enters the Crown of Bodhisattva Nature-Arising Subtle Virtue: The Chapter Title's Self-Reference

At the opening of Chapter 37 of the eighty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka*, *The Manifestation of the Tathāgata*, the World-Honored One releases from the white hair-tuft between his brows a great radiance. The radiance is named *Manifestation of the Tathāgata*. It universally illuminates all worlds across the ten directions and the exhaustion of the empty-space dharma-realm. Having circled the assembly ten times to the right, it enters the crown of one specific bodhisattva. That bodhisattva's name is **Tathāgata-Nature-Arising Subtle Virtue** (*Rúlái xìngqǐ miàodé* 如來性起妙德)[^1].

This arrangement is not incidental. The radiance is named "Manifestation of the Tathāgata"; the crown it enters belongs to "Nature-Arising Subtle Virtue." The sūtra text itself has already announced, through this very configuration: **"Manifestation of the Tathāgata" and "nature-arising" (*xìngqǐ*) name the same reality under two descriptions.** The name of the radiance and the name of the bodhisattva gloss one another; the chapter title and the receiver of the light define one another. The "nature-arising teaching" (*xìngqǐ shuō* 性起說) later elaborated by the Huayan school is not a Tang-dynasty terminological invention by the patriarchs; it comes directly from this bodhisattva's own name in the opening lines of *The Manifestation of the Tathāgata*. The sūtra has already written its answer into its own structure.

Further: after entering the crown of Nature-Arising Subtle Virtue, the radiance then passes from that bodhisattva "into the mouth of Bodhisattva Samantabhadra"[^2]. Thus the one who raises the question is Bodhisattva Nature-Arising Subtle Virtue, and the one who answers is Samantabhadra. The structural shape of the entire three-fascicle prose of *The Manifestation of the Tathāgata* is "Nature-Arising asks, Samantabhadra answers." Samantabhadra is the "cause-person" (*yīn rén* 因人) of the Huayan tradition — as Zhiyan and Fazang both state explicitly[^3] — and the pairing of "nature-arising" with the "cause-person" is itself a structural announcement that "the Manifestation of the Tathāgata equals the Perfect Teaching cause-person's direct pointing at the fruition-position."

This passage is the anchoring scene of this paper. Walking through eighty-eight academic treatises across the octology, what this final paper has to point out is not any new doctrine but something that the opening two hundred characters of this chapter have already announced.

#### 2.1.2 The Great Sūtra-Scroll within a Dust Mote: The Tathāgata's Wisdom Reaches Everywhere

In the middle fascicle of *The Manifestation of the Tathāgata*, Samantabhadra expounds the "ten marks of the Tathāgata's heart-mind," the last of which is "the Tathāgata's wisdom reaches everywhere." The sūtra says:

> "Furthermore, O son of the Buddha! The Tathāgata's wisdom reaches everywhere. Why is this? There is no single sentient being that does not possess the Tathāgata's wisdom. Only by reason of deluded thinking, perverted clinging, have they failed to verify it. If they could depart from deluded thinking, then the knowledge of all things, the self-arising knowledge, and the unobstructed knowledge would at once appear. O son of the Buddha! Suppose there were a great sūtra-scroll, equal in extent to the great trichiliocosm, upon which the entire contents of the great trichiliocosm were written down, nothing omitted. … Though this great sūtra-scroll is equal in extent to the great trichiliocosm, it resides entirely within a single dust mote; as in one dust mote, so in every dust mote. Then there arises a person of discerning wisdom, possessed of the pure heavenly eye, who sees that this scroll lies within the dust mote without bringing any benefit to sentient beings, and who thinks: 'I shall, by the power of diligence, break open that dust mote and bring out this scroll so that it may benefit all beings.' … O son of the Buddha! The Tathāgata's wisdom is likewise: measureless, unobstructed, universally capable of benefiting all beings, and fully present within the body of every being. Only by the deluded clinging of ordinary foolishness are they unknowing, unaware, and not benefited. At that time, the Tathāgata, with his unobstructed pure wisdom-eye, universally observing all sentient beings throughout the dharma-realm, said: **'How extraordinary! How extraordinary! How is it that these beings possess the Tathāgata's wisdom, yet in foolishness and confusion do not know it, do not see it?** I shall teach them the noble path, so that they may forever depart from deluded thinking and clinging, and see within their own bodies the Tathāgata's vast wisdom, not different from the Buddha's.'"[^4]

This long passage is the original source of the famous line that the Chan tradition has quoted repeatedly for twelve centuries: "How extraordinary! How extraordinary! How is it that these beings possess the Tathāgata's wisdom." Samantabhadra then restates it in verse, whose closing lines say:

> "Just as there is a great sūtra-scroll, equal in extent to the triple-thousand realms, yet residing within a single dust mote, and as with one mote, so with every mote.
> There is a person of keen wisdom, pure eye beholding it completely, who breaks the mote and brings out the scroll, universally benefiting all beings.
> The Buddha's wisdom is likewise: pervading the minds of sentient beings, bound up by deluded thinking, unaware and unknowing.
> The great compassion of the buddhas causes them to remove their delusion — **thus does it manifest**, benefiting the bodhisattvas."[^5]

The five characters that close the verse — "thus does it manifest" (*rú shì nǎi chū xiàn* 如是乃出現) — clasp the great-sūtra-scroll-in-a-mote parable directly back onto the chapter title. **"Manifestation" is the breaking-of-delusion-to-reveal-the-nature.** The scriptural ground for the eighth movement of the Pointing-at-the-Moon octology, "Manifestation" (顯), lies precisely in this line. "Manifestation" here does not mean something emerging from a hidden place, or something arising from an existing place; it means the natural self-disclosure, upon the breaking of the dust mote (deluded thinking, clinging), of the already-complete great sūtra-scroll whose extent equals the great trichiliocosm.

This scriptural passage simultaneously answers the core thesis of this paper. "The octology's dharma-realm as one mark" does not mean that the eight series are pressed together by some overarching integrating concept. It means this: every doctrine the octology handles — Faith, Understanding, Practice, Inquiry, Cutting, Illumination, Inclusion, Manifestation — is already present, in the posture of a great sūtra-scroll inside a dust mote, within every mote and every sentient being's mind. What the octology performs is not "construction" but "breaking the mote and bringing out the scroll."

#### 2.1.3 Manifestation Not by a Single Cause, Not by a Single Event: The Scriptural Ground of Structural Isomorphism

In the first fascicle of *The Manifestation of the Tathāgata*, Samantabhadra answers the first of the ten marks of manifestation:

> "O son of the Buddha! This place is unthinkable — namely, the Tathāgata, Arhat, Perfectly Awakened One, manifests by means of measureless dharmas. Why? It is not by a single cause, not by a single event, that the Tathāgata's manifestation is accomplished; it is accomplished through ten measureless hundred-thousand *asaṃkhyeya* events. … O son of the Buddha! Consider the great trichiliocosm: it is not by a single cause, not by a single event, that it is accomplished — it is accomplished through measureless causes, measureless events. … Thus through these measureless causes and conditions the great trichiliocosm is accomplished; the nature of dharmas is such, with no maker, no actor, no knower, no one who accomplishes, and yet that world is accomplished. The Tathāgata's manifestation is likewise: not by a single cause, not by a single event, is it accomplished. … The Tathāgata's perfect awakening is accomplished in this manner; the nature of dharmas is such: unborn, unmade, and yet accomplished."[^6]

This passage is the underlying scriptural ground of the §III argument of four diagonal pairs. "The Tathāgata's manifestation is not by a single cause, not by a single event" corresponds structurally to "the octology is not derived by a linear movement from a single series." The two share a single dharma-nature structure: measureless causes and conditions simultaneously accomplishing, not a sequential stacking. The nature of dharmas is such, not by a maker made, not by a knower known, and yet accomplished. The eight series and eighty-eight papers of the Pointing-at-the-Moon octology are also "accomplished" in precisely this manner — not in a temporal order of "first comes Faith, then Understanding, then Practice…," but as eight facets simultaneously present in every thought of the writer.

#### 2.1.4 The Awakening of the Buddhas Cannot Be Spoken by Words: Reserving the Scriptural Ground for the Failure-Point in §IV

In the third fascicle of the same chapter, Samantabhadra expounds the chapter "The Tathāgata's Accomplishment of Perfect Awakening," in which one passage directly handles the relation between "speech" and "the object of speech":

> "O son of the Buddha! The awakening of the buddhas cannot be spoken by any words, cannot be reached by any voice, cannot be said by any language; yet it is still opened up to each according to what is fitting."[^7]

Immediately after, in the chapter "The Tathāgata's Turning of the Dharma Wheel," Samantabhadra proposes a parallel structure:

> "He turns the dharma wheel without any speech, for he knows that all dharmas cannot be spoken; he turns the dharma wheel in ultimate quiescence, for he knows that all dharmas are of the nature of *nirvāṇa*; he turns the dharma wheel through every word and every language, for the Tathāgata's voice reaches everywhere. … O son of the Buddha! The Tathāgata's dharma-wheel enters all languages and words without any dwelling. … The various languages of all sentient beings are never apart from the Tathāgata's dharma-wheel. Why? Because the true reality of voice is itself the dharma-wheel."[^8]

This parallel structure — "turning the wheel without speech" set beside "turning the wheel through all words" — is the scriptural ground for the failure-point declaration in §IV of this paper. Speech and the unspeakable are not opposites; they are the two faces of a single dharma-wheel. The closing of the treatise stratum (the silence), and the totality of the octology's eighty-eight papers (the myriad words), belong structurally to the same body under the posture "the true reality of voice is itself the dharma-wheel." This passage gives the forward notice of the thesis §IV will handle: **closing the pen is not the dharma ceasing to flow; silence is the other face of turning the wheel.**

### 2.2 B. Huayan Patriarchal Commentary: Fazang's *Tanxuan ji* on the Sixty-Fascicle *Avataṃsaka*

#### 2.2.1 The Chapter-Title Difference Between the Two Translations Is Already a Miniature Proof of the Dharma-Realm as One Mark

Before entering Fazang's *Record of Investigations into the Mysteries of the Avataṃsaka* (*Huāyán jīng tànxuán jì* 華嚴經探玄記, T1733), one fact at the philological stratum must be handled — a fact that is itself a miniature demonstration of the core thesis of this paper.

The *Tanxuan ji* is Fazang's commentary on the **sixty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka*** (Buddhabhadra's Eastern Jin translation, T0278). In the sixty-fascicle version, this chapter is titled **"Chapter 32: The Nature-Arising of the Jewel-King Tathāgata"** (〈寶王如來性起品第三十二〉), conventionally "The Nature-Arising Chapter" (性起品). In the eighty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* (Śikṣānanda's Tang translation, T0279), the same chapter is titled **"Chapter 37: The Manifestation of the Tathāgata"** (〈如來出現品第三十七〉). The two translations give different chapter names, different chapter numbers, and different lexical choices.

A conventional philological treatment would say: this is a translator-variance, and the reader should simply take note. But what this paper wishes to say is something else: **this very variance is already a miniature proof of the thesis of the dharma-realm as one mark.**

If the same chapter of the same sūtra generates, under two translators' brushes, two entirely different names — "nature-arising" and "manifestation of the Tathāgata" — and if each name stood for an independent doctrine, it would mean that the original teaching had been split in half. But if the two names point to the same reality, merely highlighting different facets within different language-structures, then: **the two facets of a single doctrine were present simultaneously from the start, and two different translators, entering from different angles, each pointed out one of them.** Buddhabhadra pointed out the "nature" (the arising of the Tathāgatagarbha's own nature); Śikṣānanda pointed out the "mark" (the Tathāgata's manifestation within the dharma-realm). Neither was wrong, neither omitted anything; only the two together constitute the complete chapter title. And this is exactly the ancient demonstration of "one is all, all is one": the same chapter automatically generates two names at the translation layer, proving that the object itself carries a structure of "one meaning, two faces."

The single character 顯 ("manifestation") in the title of this paper is, in essence, the Chinese-language fusion of those two translation-names. "Nature-arising" speaks from the cause-position; "Manifestation of the Tathāgata" speaks from the fruit-position; in the Perfect Teaching the two are non-dual, and the Chinese character 顯 happens to cover both "making the nature manifest" (the hidden-becoming-revealed *xiǎn*) and "appearing as a phenomenon" (the manifestation *xiǎn*). The reason that the eighth movement of the octology is named "Manifestation" is that within Chinese the character 顯 already fuses into a single glyph the two translator-perspectives of Buddhabhadra and Śikṣānanda.

In this sense, P12 is not performing a philological footnote. It is pointing out that **the Huayan literary history itself already contains an octology-shaped demonstration of mutual inclusion** — one chapter of sūtra has lived in Han-Chinese Buddhism for thirteen hundred years under two translation-names, each glossing the other, neither able to replace the other. This is what the dharma-realm as one mark looks like in the life of the literature.

When Fazang's *Tanxuan ji* is cited below, the annotation will note "Fazang, *Tanxuan ji*, on the Jewel-King Tathāgata Nature-Arising Chapter, i.e. the Manifestation of the Tathāgata Chapter of the eighty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka*," for the reader's convenience.

#### 2.2.2 The Ten Gates of Nature-Arising: Fazang's Judgment that "The Arising Has No Other Arising; It Takes the Nature Itself as Its Arising"

Fazang in *Tanxuan ji* fascicle 16, commenting on the Jewel-King Tathāgata Nature-Arising Chapter, opens the exposition in four gates: explaining the name, the reason for its coming, the purport, and the explanation of the text. Under "purport" he further opens ten gates: distinguishing the marks, reliance, mutual融 and fusion, nature-virtue, definition, defilement-and-purity, cause-and-effect, general-and-particular, extent, and establishment[^9]. These ten gates constitute the most systematic doctrinal unfolding of "nature-arising" in the whole *Tanxuan ji*. This paper draws only upon the gate of *definition* and the gate of *defilement-and-purity* as commentary support for §III.

**Gate of definition:**

> "Question: In the text below it says 'not by few causes does the Tathāgata accomplish perfect awakening' — this is precisely dependent arising, so why speak only of nature-arising? Answer: There are four meanings. First, the fruit-ocean itself, as unspeakable-unspeakable nature, responds to opportunity through the fullness of conditions; but once arisen, it turns back from conditions and conforms to its own nature — hence, setting aside conditions, we name it only 'nature-arising.' Second, the nature-body is unspeakable; once spoken, it is already 'arising.' Now speaking of arising in terms of conditions, **the arising has no other arising; it takes the nature itself as its arising**, hence the name 'nature-arising,' not 'dependent-arising.' Third, though the arising is through conditions, the conditions are necessarily without own-nature; the principle of no-own-nature becomes manifest through the place of conditions — hence, naming it from its manifestation, we speak only of nature-arising. As in 'all dharmas are established upon the non-abiding root.' Fourth, if what is arisen resembles the conditional mark, it belongs to dependent-arising; now, what is arisen is according to pure function, conforming to the verification of true-nature, so it belongs to nature-arising."[^10]

The key thesis of this passage is "**the arising has no other arising; it takes the nature itself as its arising**" — all dependent-arising phenomena ultimately return to the self-disclosure of the "nature" rather than pointing to some external condition. The theoretical ground for the "eight facets of the octology are mutually inclusive" which §III will demonstrate lies precisely here: **every arising of every movement ultimately takes the dharma-realm as one mark as the nature of its arising.** Faith is not an independent starting point; it is the dharma-realm as one mark disclosing itself under the facet "faith." Manifestation is likewise not an independent end-point; it is the dharma-realm as one mark disclosing itself under the facet "manifestation." Eight arisings, all taking one nature as their arising — this is Fazang's "the arising has no other arising, it takes the nature itself as its arising" realized at the structural level.

**Gate of defilement-and-purity:**

> "Question: Are sentient beings and their defilements also nature-arising? Answer: They are. Why? Because they are what is to be saved, what is to be cut off, what is to be known. Therefore everything without exception is nature-arising."[^11]

This passage is extremely important. Fazang states explicitly: **not only the pure dharmas belong to nature-arising; even the afflictions, ignorance, and perversions within sentient beings also belong to nature-arising** — because they are saved, cut off, and known by the Buddha's wisdom. This judgment extends "nature-arising" from a concept of "purity only" into a dharma-realm structure that "universally embraces defilement and purity." Its significance for this paper is that the failure-point declaration handled by §IV (language reaches to here and no further) is itself included within nature-arising. The failure of a treatise is not a gap in nature-arising; it is nature-arising disclosing itself under the facet "failure." §IV below will take this as its theoretical ground for its formal unfolding.

#### 2.2.3 Fazang's Commentary on the Dust-Mote Parable: In the Perfect Teaching, Cause and Effect Are Non-Dual

In *Tanxuan ji* fascicle 16, commenting on the great-sūtra-scroll-in-a-mote parable of the Nature-Arising Chapter, Fazang gives a passage of decisive importance in the history of Huayan doctrinal classification:

> "The tenth is the parable of the mote containing the scroll, which illustrates the universal equality-wisdom of buddha-nature. It has three parts: *dharma, simile, and union*. The first has two sub-parts: first, the thesis — namely, that **the buddha-fruit wisdom and the original-awareness cause-nature within sentient beings are without difference; hence the very cause under bondage already contains the fruit-dharma that is released from bondage. Because in the Perfect Teaching cause and effect are non-dual, and this meaning is not found in the other holy teachings**."[^12]

"The very cause under bondage already contains the fruit-dharma released from bondage; in the Perfect Teaching cause and effect are non-dual; this meaning is not found in the other holy teachings" — these three clauses constitute Fazang's ultimate verdict on the difference between the Huayan Perfect Teaching and all other holy teachings. He says: the original-awareness cause-nature within sentient beings is without difference from the buddha-fruit wisdom; therefore, even while sentient beings remain within the bondage of afflictions, their cause-nature already fully possesses the fruit-dharma of post-bondage; such a non-dual cause-and-effect is found only in the Perfect Teaching, and not in the other holy teachings (Hīnayāna, Inceptive, Final, or Sudden).

The significance for this paper: the eight movements of the octology — Faith, Understanding, Practice, Inquiry, Cutting, Illumination, Inclusion, Manifestation — are unfolded one by one under the writer's brush, yet according to Fazang's commentary, every movement at the very moment of its writing already fully contains the whole doctrine of the other seven. Faith from the beginning already contains Manifestation; Manifestation from the beginning already contains Faith; Understanding from the beginning already contains Cutting; Cutting from the beginning already contains Understanding. Not because the writer consciously makes them echo one another, but because the nature of dharmas is such: the cause-and-effect structure of the Perfect Teaching is originally non-dual. §III's four diagonal pairs take this commentary as their principal supporting text.

Fazang in the same section goes further:

> "The knowledge-of-all is inceptive-awareness wisdom; the unmastered knowledge is original-awareness wisdom; the unobstructed knowledge is the wisdom in which inceptive and original are non-dual."[^13]

These three clauses dissect the "if one departs from deluded thinking, the knowledge-of-all, the self-arising knowledge, and the unobstructed knowledge at once appear" of the ten marks of the Tathāgata's heart into inceptive-awareness, original-awareness, and inceptive-original non-duality. This is also the theoretical axis that §III's first diagonal pair "Faith ↔ Manifestation" will draw upon.

### 2.3 C. *Āgama* Leakage: Three Ancient-Stratum Anchors

The S8 methodological manifesto specifies that every paper must find one or more "leakages" in the *Āgama* literature — traces of Huayan doctrine already present at the *Āgama* stratum, used to prove that what Huayan teaches is not a late invention but something that "was always there, only not being broadcast at full power on that channel." This paper, as the closing fascicle of the treatise stratum, requires three *Āgama* anchors, corresponding to this paper's three core postures: the non-omniscience-in-a-single-moment of the knowledge-of-all, the reluctant opening of the Dharma's expression, and the marking of boundaries through the unthinkable.

#### 2.3.1 The *Sūtra on Omniscience*: The Buddha Denies "Knowing All in a Single Moment"

The *Madhyama-āgama* (*Zhōng ā-hán* 中阿含), fascicle 59, Chapter "Exemplars," sūtra 212, the *Sūtra on Omniscience* (〈一切智經〉), records a dialogue between the Buddha and King Pasenadi of Kosala. Pasenadi, on the basis of a second-hand rumor, had come to believe that the Buddha had claimed to be "one who knows all and sees all," and he came to the Buddha for confirmation. The Buddha's answer unfolds in two steps.

First, the Buddha expressly denies having ever said "I know all and see all" — "Great King! I do not recall having said any such thing."

Second, the Buddha acknowledges having said something else:

> "Great King! I recall having said thus: 'There has not been, there will not be, and there is not now, any other recluse or brahmin who **knows all at one time, sees all at one time**.' Great King! This I do recall saying."[^14]

Laying the two statements side by side, the Buddha's position is exceedingly clear: **he does not deny being "omniscient" (*sabbaññu*, in principle capable of knowing all); what he denies is "knowing all in a single moment" — all knowledge manifest at once in any single instant**. The completeness of omniscience is disclosed through temporality, through dependent arising, through what is asked — it is not a static totality already fully unfolded.

This passage is the ancient-stratum legitimation for the closing of the Pointing-at-the-Moon treatise stratum. Eighty-eight papers across eight series — Faith, Understanding, Practice, Inquiry, Cutting, Illumination, Inclusion, Manifestation — do not add up to "all of the Dharma," nor do they constitute a closed, omniscient system. For even the Buddha himself never claimed "knowing all at one time." The leakages of the octology (the system's boundaries, failure-points, incompletenesses) are not the writer's inadequacy; they are a faithfulness to the *Āgama* stratum's ancient principle that "omniscient does not equal omniscient in a single moment." The closing fascicle does not carry any obligation to "finish saying everything," because "finishing" was never, from the beginning, the Buddha's way of describing his own omniscience.

#### 2.3.2 Brahmā's Entreaty: From the Beginning, the Preaching of the Dharma Was Given with Reservation

Brahmā's entreaty is a key episode from the early period after the Buddha's awakening: having attained the Way, the Buddha sat in solitude reflecting that "the dharma I have attained is extremely profound, subtle, and difficult to see"; he resolved to remain silent and not to preach. The lord of the Sahā-world, Brahmā, knowing the Buddha's thought, descended and entreated him; only after three earnest entreaties did the Buddha resolve to open the Dharma.

The fullest version of this passage in the Chinese *Āgama* literature appears in the *Dīrgha-āgama* (*Cháng ā-hán* 長阿含), fascicle 1, the *Mahāvadāna-sūtra* (〈大本經〉):

> "The Buddha Vipaśyin, in a quiet place, again made this thought: 'I have now attained this supreme dharma; it is **profound and subtle, difficult to comprehend and difficult to see, stilled, pure**; it is known only to the wise, not to the common and foolish. … If I preach it to them, they will not understand, and it will only give rise to disturbance.' Having thought this, **he fell silent and did not preach the Dharma.**
>
> Then the king of Brahmā, knowing the thought of the Tathāgata Vipaśyin, considered: 'If the world is thus to be spoiled, it is deeply pitiable. The Buddha Vipaśyin has just attained this subtle profound dharma, and yet does not wish to preach it.' … He addressed the Buddha: 'May the World-Honored One preach the Dharma in season! These sentient beings now are lightly soiled, their faculties sharp, they bear a reverent mind and can easily be taught…'
>
> Then **the king of Brahmā entreated yet again, earnestly and reverently, up to three times**. …
>
> Then the World-Honored One, **having heard Brahmā's earnest entreaty thrice**, surveyed the world with the Buddha-eye: the dust on beings was now thick, now thin; their faculties now sharp, now dull. … Just as with the blue lotus, the pink lotus, the white lotus, and the white-lotus bloom: some had already come out of the mud but had not yet reached the water; some were now level with the water; some had come out of the water but had not yet blossomed. … So likewise with the beings of the world.
>
> Then the World-Honored One said to Brahmā: '**In pity for you all, I shall now open the door of the ambrosial Dharma.** It is a dharma profound and subtle, hard to comprehend; **today I preach for those who trust and delight in hearing, and not for those who would be disturbed to no benefit**.'"[^15]

Although this passage is formally ascribed to the Buddha Vipaśyin (the first of the seven past buddhas), the *Mahāvadāna-sūtra* itself explicitly states that this "is common to the seven buddhas" — Śākyamuni himself, upon his awakening, followed the same trajectory. Subsequent commentators have uniformly used this passage as the authoritative account of Śākyamuni's own entreaty.

Another Chinese version appears in the *Ekottarika-āgama* (*Zēng yī ā-hán* 增壹阿含), fascicle 10, *Chapter on Entreaty*, sūtra 1, set with Śākyamuni himself "not long after attaining the Way":

> "On one occasion the Buddha was at the Bodhi-tree seat in Magadha. At that time, the World-Honored One, **not long after attaining the Way**, gave rise to this thought: 'This profound dharma of mine is **hard to comprehend, hard to know, not to be thought out, stilled and subtle**, known only by the wise. … If I were to preach the subtle dharma to others, they would not trust and receive it, nor would they practice it; my effort would be in vain, and the loss would be great. **I ought now to fall silent; what need is there to preach?**'
>
> At that time, the king of Brahmā, in the Brahmā heavens, knew the Tathāgata's thought from afar. … He addressed the World-Honored One: '… **May the Tathāgata universally preach the profound dharma for sentient beings!**'
>
> … At that time the World-Honored One, knowing the thought in Brahmā's heart and also in his compassion for all beings, spoke this verse:
>
> '**Brahmā now has come to entreat, the Tathāgata opens the Dharma-door;** those who hear shall gain firm trust and discern the profound essentials of the dharma.
> As though upon a high mountain peak, one universally observes all kinds of beings — I now possess this dharma, I mount the hall, I manifest the dharma-eye.'"[^16]

Reading the two versions together — T1 for narrative completeness (including the four-lotus simile), T125 for the direct Śākyamuni setting (including the "Brahmā now has come to entreat" verse) — a single thesis can be established in common: **Śākyamuni's preaching of the Dharma was never, from the beginning, "speaking the whole for everyone"; it was "speaking for those who trust and delight in hearing, and not for those who would be disturbed to no benefit." Preaching is essentially selective, essentially reserved, essentially built with the internal limit "this is not the whole."**

The significance for this paper: the entire writing posture of the eighty-eight papers of the Pointing-at-the-Moon octology, from the first to the last, has an ancient legitimacy in the *Āgama* stratum. The addressee of the octology's writing has from the beginning been "those who trust and delight in hearing" — readers willing to stop at every middle-way corrective, willing to set down their demands at every failure-point declaration. The octology is not responsible for "those who would be disturbed to no benefit," which is not exclusion but the preaching condition already set at the very turning of the Buddha's first wheel.

Further: S7-P12, *From Stars to Black Holes*, closed the S7 series through the gate of the *Lotus Sūtra*'s inclusion, and S7-P12 itself had already used Brahmā's entreaty as its key parallel — the "profound, silent, thrice-entreated, opening-the-Dharma" of the first turning of the wheel, expanded in the *Lotus*'s age into "the five thousand withdrew, the Tathāgata remained silent." From *Āgama* to *Lotus* to *Avataṃsaka*, the dual track of "silence / opening" has been continuously present. S7-P12 used it to close S7; S8-P12 uses it now to close the entire academic treatise stratum — a first-to-last clasp: beginning with an entreaty, ending with a fall into silence.

#### 2.3.3 The Four Unthinkables: Marking Boundaries, Not Setting a Target to Break Through

*Ekottarika-āgama*, fascicle 21, *Chapter on Suffering and Joy*, sūtra 6:

> "At that time, the World-Honored One told the bhikṣus: 'There are four things which must never be thought upon. What four? **Sentient beings are unthinkable; the world is unthinkable; the realm of dragons is unthinkable; the realm of buddha-lands is unthinkable**. **Why so? Because one does not reach the extinction of nirvāṇa through thinking upon them.** … Thus, bhikṣus, these four places are unthinkable; they are not what ordinary people may think upon. **Moreover, these four matters have no root of goodness, nor do they lead one to cultivate the brahma-conduct, nor to the place of cessation, nor to the place of nirvāṇa — they serve only to drive one mad, to confuse the mind, and to raise nets of doubt**.'"[^17]

This passage is another ancient-stratum ground for the failure-point declaration in §IV. The Buddha expressly names four "unthinkables" and then adds a clause of decisive importance: "**one does not reach the extinction of nirvāṇa through them**" — that is, the "unthinkable" is marked as unthinkable not because it is a target one could cross to attain omniscience, but because it is in essence the boundary of knowing, and the act of crossing such a boundary is unprofitable for liberation.

This is exactly the thesis §IV of the present paper must handle: **the closing of the treatise stratum is not because there is still much left unsaid, but because at a certain point the further pushing of language transforms from "aid to the Way" into "the raising of nets of doubt."** The *Āgama* Buddha already told the bhikṣus where this boundary runs; the Huayan Fazang restated the same boundary in the language of "nature-arising"; Pointing-at-the-Moon, in the twenty-first century's academic treatise stratum, marks this boundary once more. The three are not three different versions but three disclosures of the same boundary in three language-channels.

### 2.4 §II Summary: The Sūtra Has Already Written the Answer into Its Structure

The three clusters of scriptural grounding in this section point to a single fact: **the "dharma-realm as one mark in the octology" that this paper wishes to state does not need to be established by Pointing-at-the-Moon on its own, because *The Manifestation of the Tathāgata* has already said it completely through its five structural announcements — the radiance released, the radiance entering the crown, the great sūtra-scroll, "not by a single cause," and "cannot be spoken by words"; Fazang's *Tanxuan ji* has already classified it completely through "the arising has no other arising, it takes the nature itself as its arising" and "in the Perfect Teaching cause and effect are non-dual"; and the *Āgama*, at the oldest stratum, has already marked its boundaries completely through "omniscience does not equal omniscience in one moment," "Brahmā's entreaty," and "the four unthinkables."**

Walking to the eighty-eighth paper, what the Pointing-at-the-Moon octology has to do is not to establish a new thesis but to point out: from *Āgama* to *Avataṃsaka*, from Śikṣānanda to Fazang, from Vipaśyin's silence to Śākyamuni's ambrosial dharma-door, this spine of "the dharma-realm as one mark" has always been in place. The octology is merely one twenty-first-century Han-Chinese academic-treatise-stratum disclosure of this spine, and even this disclosure is itself included within nature-arising — the nature of dharmas is such: unborn, unmade, and yet accomplished.

## III. The Octology's Mutual Inclusion: Four Diagonal Pairs

### 3.0 Why Diagonals Rather Than a Linear Review

The core thesis of this paper is that "the octology is not a linear stacking but the simultaneous unfolding of the dharma-realm as one mark across eight facets." If §III were to adopt a linear structure — "Faith, Understanding, Practice, Inquiry, Cutting, Illumination, Inclusion, Manifestation" reviewed one by one in sequence, each core thesis restated and then closed — then the form of §III would violate the very thesis it is trying to demonstrate: it would re-break "the dharma-realm as one mark" into a sequence of eight separate items, equivalent to a denial of what the thesis itself wishes to say.

The alternative is a diagonal demonstration: selecting the "most distant two poles" among the eight characters and pairing them, displaying how these apparently opposed extremes are in fact the most mutually implicative. This structure has two theoretical grounds. The first is Chengguan's classification, in his *Elaborated Exegesis of the Avataṃsaka Sūtra*, of the "one-totality, nine-differentiations" relation among the Ten Profound Gates: the compatibility between the most distant two gates is the strictest test of "one-is-all"[^18]. The second is Fazang's verdict in the *Tanxuan ji*: "in the Perfect Teaching cause and effect are non-dual, this meaning is not found in the other holy teachings" — the non-difference of cause-and-effect, far-and-near, beginning-and-end, purity-and-defilement in the Perfect Teaching is exactly the structural distinction of Huayan from all other holy teachings[^19].

On this principle, the eight series-characters of the Pointing-at-the-Moon octology — Faith, Understanding, Practice, Inquiry, Cutting, Illumination, Inclusion, Manifestation — can be grouped into four diagonal pairings:

- **3.1 Faith ↔ Manifestation** (S1 ↔ S8): the same disclosure at the temporal cause-position and at the fruit-position
- **3.2 Understanding ↔ Cutting** (S2 ↔ S5): the same occurrence at the theoretical layer and at the practical layer
- **3.3 Practice ↔ Inclusion** (S3 ↔ S7): the same action at the analytical layer and at the integrative layer
- **3.4 Inquiry ↔ Illumination** (S4 ↔ S6): inquiry in its incomplete and its consummated form

This section is not a review, but a demonstration. Having walked the four pairs, the reader will have experienced not "a summary of eight series" but a single complete pass, in reading-order, through the thesis of the dharma-realm as one mark.

### 3.1 Faith ↔ Manifestation: The Same Disclosure at Cause and Fruit

The first movement of the Pointing-at-the-Moon octology is "Faith" (S1, the Initial Arising of Bodhicitta series), and the eighth is "Manifestation" (S8, the *Avataṃsaka* series). In temporal sequence these are the two end-poles of the octology: S1's writing was started in 2023, S8 approaches completion in 2026; S1 handles "the single thought of initial bodhicitta," and S8 handles "the manifestation of the Tathāgata"; S1's reader is the beginner just stepping into the Buddhist gate, while S8's reader is a fellow traveler who has already walked through the preceding seven movements.

On the surface, there is nothing more distant than "initial bodhicitta" and "manifestation of the Tathāgata" — one is the beginning of time, the other the end; one is the very first stirring of the cause-position, the other the perfect accomplishment of the fruit-position. But what this paper wishes to say is: in the Perfect Teaching of the *Avataṃsaka*, these two most distant points are in fact the same event.

The scriptural ground lies in the *Worthy Leader Chapter* (〈賢首品〉). Fascicle 14 of the eighty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* records the Bodhisattva Worthy Leader's answer to Mañjuśrī's question about "pure conduct," which includes a passage that has been repeatedly quoted in Huayan doctrinal history:

> "Faith is the primordial source of the Way, the mother of virtues; it grows all good dharmas, cuts off the net of doubt, brings one out of the stream of craving, and opens the supreme path of nirvāṇa. … If one constantly trusts and reveres the buddhas, one is able to uphold precepts and train in the learnings; if one constantly upholds precepts and trains in the learnings, one is able to fulfill all merits. … The bodhisattva gives rise to the thought and seeks bodhi — it is not without cause, not without condition."[^20]

This is the scriptural ground of the "Completion of the Ten Faiths Stage." But the decisive passage is the next:

> "**In a single thought one knows completely the dharmas of the three times**, fully understands the faculties of all beings, and preaches for them all the dharmas of all the buddhas. … **All these sovereign and unthinkable powers come from the might of the Huayan samādhi**."[^21]

Fazang, in fascicle 2 of the *Treatise on the Five Teachings of the One Vehicle of Huayan*, commenting on this passage, puts forward the famous thesis of "becoming a buddha at the completion of faith" — at the moment of the completion of the bodhisattva's stage of faith, her mind-practice has already become identical to the buddha-fruit, without needing to wait for the sequential progression through the Ten Abodes, Ten Practices, Ten Dedications, and Ten Grounds. In Fazang's exact words: "**Because it is by faith that the accomplishment is had, therefore this is the practice-buddha, not the position-buddha**"[^22]. This thesis of "practice-buddha" thoroughly opens S1's "initial bodhicitta" and S8's "manifestation of the Tathāgata" into a single identity at the Perfect Teaching stratum: the moment of the completion of faith *is* the moment of the manifestation of the Tathāgata, not awaiting any temporal sequence.

Further, Fazang's *Tanxuan ji* on the Nature-Arising Chapter's mote-parable contains the passage (cited above in §2.2.3): "the knowledge-of-all is inceptive-awareness wisdom; the unmastered knowledge is original-awareness wisdom; the unobstructed knowledge is the wisdom in which inceptive and original are non-dual"[^23]. Placing this commentary back into the diagonal of "Faith ↔ Manifestation": the "faith" of initial bodhicitta belongs to inceptive-awareness (the first response of the cause-ground of sentient beings to original-awareness); the "manifestation" of the Tathāgata belongs to original-awareness (the awareness that has always been in place at the dharma-realm stratum); and what the Huayan Perfect Teaching truly wishes to point out is neither of these two alone but "the wisdom in which inceptive and original are non-dual" — the non-duality of Faith and Manifestation.

This is precisely the thesis already handled by S8-P05, *Rejoicing in Merit and the Gate of Host-and-Guest Perfect Clarity and Complete Virtue*: "completion of faith becoming buddha" does not mean "once faith has been cultivated to completion, one then becomes a buddha"; it means "the very structure of the completion of faith *is* the structure of the buddha"[^24]. The first paper of S1 was written on "the butterfly effect of initial bodhicitta" — how a single thought of initial bodhicitta may unfold, over measureless eons, into the fruit-position awakening; the last paper of S8 (the present paper) points out that, at the stratum of dharma-nature, this unfolding has no before-and-after, no cause-effect distance, no distance from Faith to Manifestation. The reason S1 and S8 may clasp so distantly head and tail is not that the writer has made a deliberate design; it is that "Faith" and "Manifestation" are, in the structure of the dharma-realm as one mark, two names for the same event — from the cause-position it is called faith, from the fruit-position it is called manifestation, from the nature-side it is called nature-arising, and from the mark-side it is called the manifestation of the Tathāgata.

### 3.2 Understanding ↔ Cutting: The Same Occurrence at Theory and Practice

The second movement "Understanding" (S2, *Prajñāpāramitā*) and the fifth "Cutting" (S5, the *Vajracchedikā*) are, on the surface, two series that belong to the same *prajñā* lineage — S2 handles the emptiness contemplation of the *Heart Sūtra*, S5 handles the *Vajracchedikā*'s "dwelling on nothing." The two share a single source, and readers very easily take them as "two consecutive steps": first understand emptiness in S2, then put it into practice in S5.

But this "theory first, practice second" reading is precisely the reading that misses the core structure of the *prajñā* literature. The *Vajracchedikā* says:

> "**One should not dwell on anything, yet let the mind arise**."[^25]

This is not "first not-dwelling, then letting the mind arise" — the two are not sequential steps in time. In classical Chinese, the character 應 ("should") here is not a temporal adverb but a simultaneity adverb: "not dwelling" and "letting the mind arise" are two faces of a single action. The very moment of dwelling is the very moment of mind-arising; the very moment of mind-arising is the very moment of not-dwelling. Understanding emptiness and cutting off clinging occur at the same time, not one before the other.

If this structure is mapped back onto the Pointing-at-the-Moon octology: the "Understanding" treated in S2 is not a stage of theoretical comprehension preceding the "Cutting" treated in S5; it is another name for the very same blade that S5 handles. The "Cutting" treated in S5 is not a stage of practice following upon the understanding attained in S2; it is the dynamic face of that very understanding itself. The *prajñā* literature has, from the beginning, refused the "first understand, then practice" structure — this is a thesis that the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-sūtra* and the *Vajracchedikā* together handle repeatedly in their account of cultivation-verification[^26].

This is precisely why the present paper places "Understanding" and "Cutting" as the two ends of a diagonal rather than as adjacent items. Adjacency implies "one after another" — and would mislead the reader into reading the two as a temporal sequence. The diagonal forces the reader to see that the distance between them is not a temporal distance but a difference of angle. The same "one should not dwell on anything, yet let the mind arise," viewed from the angle of static doctrine, is "Understanding"; viewed from the angle of dynamic cutting-off, is "Cutting." Not two actions, but two faces of a single action.

This structure has another formulation in Fazang's *Contemplation of the Profound Meaning of Huayan: Exhausting the Deluded and Returning to the Source* (*Xiū huāyán àozhǐ wàngjìn huányuán guān*), fascicle 1, in its definition of the ocean-seal samādhi: "**Ocean-seal is the original awareness of suchness. When delusions are exhausted and the mind is clear, all phenomena appear together**"[^27]. "Delusions exhausted" is the cutting; "all phenomena appearing together" is the understanding-disclosed — and the two occur in the same moment of "clarity of mind." The "Understanding" handled in S2-P01 and the "Cutting" handled in S5-P01 are non-dual at the dharma-nature stratum of "clarity of mind."

### 3.3 Practice ↔ Inclusion: The Same Action at the Analytical and Integrative Layers

The third movement, "Practice" (S3, the *Treatise on the Hundred Dharmas* series), and the seventh, "Inclusion" (S7, the *Lotus Sūtra* series), are the two series most easily misread as being in opposition. S3 installs the finest dharmic-analysis system in Buddhist history — the one hundred dharmas are classified, the eight consciousnesses sequenced, the five groups of one hundred dharmas set in place; S7, by contrast, handles the grandest inclusive thesis of Chinese Buddhism: the *Lotus Sūtra*'s "In the buddha-lands of the ten directions, there is only one vehicle of the Dharma"[^28], all beings will all become buddhas, the three vehicles all returning to the one buddha-vehicle.

On the surface, these are two opposite methodologies: S3 represents "analysis," breaking the dharma-realm into the finest grains; S7 represents "synthesis," gathering all dharmas into one. The former is a microscope, the latter a wide-angle lens; the former divides, the latter unifies.

But what this paper wishes to say is this: **analysis taken to its extreme *is* inclusion; inclusion taken to its extreme *is* analysis**.

The evidence lies at the frontier between Yogācāra and Huayan. The *Mahāyāna Hundred Dharmas Gate of Clarity Treatise* (*Dàshèng bǎi fǎ míngmén lùn* 大乘百法明門論) — the text handled in S3 — is itself a Yogācāra work, but its title already calls itself a "gate of clarity" (*míng mén* 明門): the reason that the one hundred dharmas are installed as one hundred is not to "divide them apart" but to allow each dharma's position to receive a clear *pointing-out*[^29]. If a dharma is truly "made clear" (its position is unambiguously pointed out), then its coordinate within the totality of the dharma-realm has been established, which is to say this dharma has already been *included* by that totality.

Look now in reverse at the *Lotus Sūtra*: "**The buddhas, the World-Honored Ones, manifest in the world solely for one great cause-and-condition**"[^30]. The reason the "one great matter" in this line is called "one" is not that it excludes multiplicity, but that every pathway of multiplicity is included by this "one." If beings could not be pointed out individually (each with her own path, her own faculties, her own affliction-structure), then "the one buddha-vehicle" would collapse into an abstract slogan; only if each path is individually seen does "the one buddha-vehicle" become a structure capable of truly including all.

This is why S3 and S7 are a diagonal pair rather than opposites. The analysis of the hundred dharmas and the inclusion of the one buddha-vehicle are not two actions in opposite directions, but two faces of a single action: one turns inward and makes a fine pointing-out of each dharma; the other turns outward and makes a comprehensive receiving of all dharmas. Taken inward to the extreme, the outward is already complete; taken outward to the extreme, the inward is already complete.

In Fazang's *Tanxuan ji*, commenting on the Nature-Arising Chapter's "gate of defilement-and-purity," he proposes: "Sentient beings and their afflictions are all nature-arising; why? Because they are what is to be saved, what is to be cut off, what is to be known"[^31] — these three clauses are in fact a dharma-nature classification of "inclusion (being saved) × analysis (being cut off, being known)." To save is to include, to cut off is to excise, to know is to analyze; the three belong together to nature-arising, not as three parallel actions but as three facets of the same nature-arising structure. S3 is the complete demonstration of the "known" layer (the one hundred dharmas are one hundred in order to be known down to the finest detail); S7 is the complete demonstration of the "saved" layer (the one buddha-vehicle is one in order to save in the widest extent). In Fazang's judgment, both are included within nature-arising and cannot be separated.

The Pointing-at-the-Moon octology places S3 at the third position and S7 at the seventh, with four movements in between. In the linear sequence this interval appears to be four steps; in the diagonal structure it is zero steps — they are, from the beginning, two names for the same event.

### 3.4 Inquiry ↔ Illumination: Inquiry in Its Incomplete and Consummated Form

The fourth movement, "Inquiry" (S4, *Inquiry: AI × Buddha-Dharma*), and the sixth, "Illumination" (S6, the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* series), are the most asymmetrical pair in the octology. S4 is the only original series in the octology (it takes no single sūtra as its foundation; its writing starts from "the questions raised by the appearance of contemporary AI for the Buddha-Dharma"), while S6 is the most classical and traditional series (the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* is one of the central mind-nature treatises of Chinese Buddhism). One is new, one is old; one is inquiry, one is answer; on the face of it the two cannot be placed together.

But the very structure of the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* itself is a complete demonstration of "inquiry → illumination." The sūtra opens with Ānanda's being ensnared by Mātaṅgī's daughter, and the Buddha asking Ānanda "where is the mind?" — this is the beginning of the *Śūraṅgama*'s "seven places searched for the mind"[^32]. Ānanda answers seven times where the mind is, and the Buddha refutes each answer — every "inquiry" points toward a deeper "illumination," until after the seventh time Ānanda discovers: the so-called "mind" cannot, in fact, be located anywhere he is able to answer. That moment of "nowhere to answer" is exactly the starting point of "illumination."

Further, the central argument of the *Śūraṅgama*, the "twenty-five perfect penetrations," is structurally twenty-five demonstrations of "inquiry → illumination" — each of twenty-five bodhisattvas starts from one sense-base (eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, or mind; or form, sound, smell, taste, touch, dharma), interrogates the essence of that base, and at the limit of that interrogation turns back to recognize the self-nature[^33]. The most famous line from Avalokiteśvara's "ear-base perfect penetration" is "**Turning the hearing to hear the self-nature; the nature becomes the supreme Way**"[^34] — this sentence is structurally the thesis that "when inquiry returns to inquiry itself, it *is* illumination." When the "inquirer, the inquired, and the act of inquiry" are all placed within the question mark, the structure of inquiry collapses into illumination.

Placing this structure back onto the diagonal of S4 and S6: the writing posture of S4 is "inquiry" — asking Buddha-Dharma questions of AI, asking AI questions of the Buddha-Dharma, asking, at this strange frontier, who one is oneself. In none of the eight papers of S4 does this "inquiry" give a closed answer; rather, each pushes the interrogation to a point "where language reaches no further," and there sets down the pen[^35]. The writing posture of S6 is "illumination" — not asking outward but pulling the inquirer herself onto the mirror-face of the *Śūraṅgama*'s observation. S4's inquiries all end up becoming S6-style illumination; S6's illuminations all begin as S4-style inquiry.

This is no coincidence. In the Han-Chinese Buddhist context, "inquiry" and "illumination" were originally two characters of the same source. Fascicle 2 of the *Śūraṅgama* records the Buddha saying to Ānanda, "**Look now upon this assembly — upon these knowings and seeings**"[^36]. "Looking" here is not a passive seeing but an active interrogation. The *Śūraṅgama*'s "illumination" is not the "silent illumination" of later Chan, but a seeing that carries a question with it; inquiry is the unconsummated form of illumination, illumination is the inquiry in which no question remains.

S4-P01 has this line: "The first thing we learn when we face AI is to set down all our assumed answers and let the question itself begin to speak." Placed into fascicle 4 of the *Śūraṅgama* and Avalokiteśvara's "turning the hearing to hear the self-nature," this line turns out to be the contemporary Han-Chinese expression of exactly the same thesis. "Turning the hearing" is not hearing a fainter sound; it is allowing the structure of hearing itself to speak. "Turning the inquiry" is not asking a more elegant question; it is allowing the structure of inquiry itself to disclose. S4 and S6 are the same action across two epochs.

### 3.5 §III Summary: Four Diagonals Point at a Single Event

Having walked through the four diagonal pairs, the reader should see one fact: the eight characters of the Pointing-at-the-Moon octology — Faith, Understanding, Practice, Inquiry, Cutting, Illumination, Inclusion, Manifestation — are not eight independent research fields but the simultaneous disclosure of the dharma-realm as one mark across four most-distant pairings.

- Faith = Manifestation at the beginning of the temporal cause-position; Manifestation = Faith at the completion of the temporal fruit-position.
- Understanding = Cutting in its action at the theoretical layer; Cutting = Understanding in its occurrence at the practical layer.
- Practice = Inclusion consummated at the analytical layer; Inclusion = Practice redeemed at the integrative layer.
- Inquiry = the unconsummated form of Illumination; Illumination = the inquiry in which no question remains.

By Fazang's thesis that "the arising has no other arising; it takes the nature itself as its arising," the "arising" pointed out by these four diagonal pairs takes a single "nature" as the nature from which all arise — a "nature" that is neither Faith nor Manifestation, neither Understanding nor Cutting, neither Practice nor Inclusion, neither Inquiry nor Illumination. It is simply the dharma-realm itself.

When the eighty-eight papers of the Pointing-at-the-Moon octology have been walked through, their significance lies not in "we have talked about eight things" but in "these eight things are eight facets of one thing." This is not modesty, not rhetoric — it is a structural fact of dharma-nature. The work of pointing out this fact is completed in this one section. What remains for the rest of this paper is to mark the boundary of that very pointing — the failure-point declaration of §IV.

## IV. Failure-Point Declaration: The Structural Honesty of the Academic Treatise Stratum

### 4.0 Statement of Structural Exception

**This section replaces the customary cross-disciplinary dialogue section.** It is a deliberate and explicit structural exception within the Pointing-at-the-Moon paper series. According to the fixed chapter sequence of the Pointing-at-the-Moon Paper Style Guide (version 2.1), §IV should be "cross-disciplinary convergence" — drawing in contemporary science, philosophy, cognitive science, AI studies, or similar external disciplines as analogical support for the doctrine. All eighty-seven papers of S1 through S8 prior to this one conform to that structure; in each of them, §IV is a cross-disciplinary dialogue.

This paper (S8-P12) is the sole exception. §IV here is not a cross-disciplinary convergence but a failure-point declaration — a formal marking, by the entire academic treatise stratum, of its own boundary. This substitution is not a casual structural adjustment but a functional requirement of this paper as the closing fascicle of the treatise stratum: a series that truly means to close must, at some point, explicitly mark "my pointing reaches to here and no further," and this marking cannot be placed inside the middle-way correctives of §V, which would demote a structural failure-point to the status of a guard against some particular misreading. The failure-point must stand as its own independent section and become the spine of this paper.

This structural exception must also be made clear in §I (Introduction), so that the reader enters this section with the appropriate structural expectation — as has been done above.

### 4.1 A Failure Point Is Not a Defect but Structural Honesty

Across the entire writing process of the Pointing-at-the-Moon octology, every paper contains, somewhere in §III or §V, a passage that marks a "language-reaches-to-here-and-no-further" moment. This is not rhetorical modesty, nor the customary "this study has its limitations" disclaimer of academic writing. It is a structural requirement set by Declaration III of the S8 series' methodological manifesto:

> Every paper in this series will stop somewhere. The place where it stops is not an omission; it is a gesture of reverence. A three-dimensional pen cannot write to a ten-dimensional object; what the pen can do is mark clearly the place where it is set down — so that the reader knows that beyond that line she must rely on her own dharma-eye.[^37]

This methodological anchoring passage was first established in S8-P01, *Ocean-Seal Samādhi and the Speaking of the Unspeakable*, and each of the following eleven S8 papers has responded to it somewhere in its own text. Up to the present paper, the "place where the pen is set down" has been marked in twenty-one different passages across the S8 series[^38] — each marking directed at a specific doctrinal boundary: the unspeakability of the ocean-seal samādhi, the unthinkability of nature-arising, the non-totalizability of the "one-totality, nine-differentiations" structure of the Ten Gates, the inexhaustibility of the Samantabhadra vows and practices, and so on.

What §IV of this paper has to do is not to add a twenty-second failure point, but to point out: **these twenty-one failure points are, structurally, the same failure point from the beginning.** They are merely repeated disclosures of the "unspeakable" face of the dharma-realm as one mark across different doctrinal contexts. Just as the *Manifestation of the Tathāgata*'s "the awakening of the buddhas goes beyond all words and speech" is not a doctrinal detail but a structural fact of the Dharma itself, so the failure points of the Pointing-at-the-Moon octology are not writerly shortcomings but the academic treatise stratum's faithfulness to its own functional boundary.

Further, the classification drawn from Fazang's *Tanxuan ji* gate of defilement-and-purity, cited in §2.2.2 — "sentient beings and their afflictions are all nature-arising" — applies here again: the failure of the treatise writing is also included within nature-arising. Failure is not a gap in nature-arising; it is nature-arising disclosing itself under the facet "failure." The Pointing-at-the-Moon octology stops somewhere in every one of its papers, and that stopping is itself a self-disclosure of the dharma-realm as one mark, not an insufficiency of academic ability. This classification promotes "the failure point" from a structure of self-depreciation into a doctrinal construction — failure is part of the paper's completeness, not its opposite.

### 4.2 The Common Silence of the Octology: A Specific Boundary of the Treatise Stratum

The Pointing-at-the-Moon octology comprises eighty-eight papers at the academic treatise stratum, totaling roughly eight hundred thousand Chinese characters. That quantity is not small for a three-year writing project, but placed within the two-thousand-five-hundred-year commentarial tradition of Buddhism — the eighty-five volumes and more than eight thousand texts of the Taishō canon, the ninety volumes of the Japanese continued canon, the Tibetan Kangyur and Tengyur … — eight hundred thousand characters is a drop in an ocean. The ratio itself already suggests something: Pointing-at-the-Moon is not trying to "cover" the Dharma; it is trying to point out one specific entry-angle needed in the contemporary Han-Chinese academic context.

More critical still: the eighty-eight papers of Pointing-at-the-Moon contain no single paper that attempts to answer the following questions —

- What is the essence of mind? (The true answer to the *Śūraṅgama*'s seven places searched for the mind can only appear inside the reader's own sitting.)
- What is seeing-the-nature? (Eighteen hundred years of Chan recorded sayings have been circling this term; Pointing-at-the-Moon has no standing to give a better definition.)
- What actually happens at the moment of awakening? (Every description in the sūtras and treatises is a marking, not the scene.)
- What is the single thought at the moment of death? (Pure Land's recollection of the Buddha, the *Śūraṅgama*'s hearing-and-reflecting, the Vajrayāna's *tummo* — the three traditions have their own paths; Pointing-at-the-Moon has no standing to adjudicate among them.)
- Does AI have buddha-nature? (S4 walks to its eighth paper, and the place where the pen is set down is right at the edge of this question.)

These five questions are not accidentally omitted; they are questions that the academic treatise stratum *should not*, in principle, write. The true answer to each must occur in "a place that is not a treatise" — in a meditation hall, on a deathbed, in a single instant of correspondence, on the day the reader's own dharma-eye ripens. The entire posture of the Pointing-at-the-Moon octology has been, across eight hundred thousand characters, to circle repeatedly around the edges of these five questions, draw a circle around them, and then point it out to the reader: "What is inside this circle is not in a place a treatise can reach."

This is the literal redemption of the two characters "pointing at the moon." The moon is inside the circle; the finger is outside. On the rim of the circle are written eight hundred thousand characters; inside the circle is written silence. The completion of the academic treatise stratum of the octology is not a matter of having filled the rim with characters; it is a matter of the circle itself having been drawn closed.

### 4.3 Speech and Non-Speech: Two Faces of a Single Dharma-Wheel

§II has already cited the parallel structure of the "Tathāgata Turning of the Dharma Wheel" chapter in *The Manifestation of the Tathāgata*:

> "He turns the dharma wheel without any speech, for he knows that all dharmas cannot be spoken; … he turns the dharma wheel through every word and every language, for the Tathāgata's voice reaches everywhere. … The true reality of voice is itself the dharma-wheel."[^39]

The key point of this passage is not the rhetorical dialectic of "the Buddha both speaks and does not speak." It is the structural revelation that: **"speech" and "non-speech" are not two options for one dharma-wheel; they are the two-faced structure that the single dharma-wheel inherently carries.** The very nature of the dharma-wheel is "the true reality of voice" — and the reason this is "true reality" is that it simultaneously carries the emptiness face of "entering all voices yet entering nothing."

The eight hundred thousand characters of the academic treatise stratum of the Pointing-at-the-Moon octology, and the silence that lies outside those eight hundred thousand characters, belong, under this structure, to the two faces of one and the same dharma-wheel. Closing the pen (the silence, the circle) is not the Dharma ceasing to flow; it is rather the official presentation of the other face of this dharma-wheel. What §IV of this paper declares — "the academic treatise stratum reaches here and no further" — is not "Pointing-at-the-Moon reaches here and no further." The Pointing-at-the-Moon YouTube channel is still unfolding; the sub-project "Sudhana's Fifty-Three Encounters" (S8.5) is still in planning; the offering platform and the readers' practice community are still being built; and the next generation of the treatise (the action the reader will take once she has finished reading) has not yet been born[^40]. What is being closed is only this one particular face of the dharma-wheel, the "academic treatise stratum," not the dharma-wheel itself.

The line of the *Sūtra on Omniscience* in which King Pasenadi praises the Buddha — "What the recluse Gautama says is like a teacher, what the recluse Gautama says is like a good teacher"[^41] — holds together only because the Buddha's preaching, from the beginning, carried the built-in reservation "I do not preach for those who would be disturbed to no benefit." The eight hundred thousand characters of the Pointing-at-the-Moon octology, from the first paper to the eighty-eighth, were never written for "everyone"; they were written for the reader willing to stop at every middle-way corrective, and to set down her demands at every failure-point declaration. This is not exclusion; it is structural honesty. Pointing-at-the-Moon takes no responsibility for "those who would be disturbed to no benefit" — this is not an arrogance of Pointing-at-the-Moon; it is the preaching condition set down at the very turning of the Buddha's first wheel.

### 4.4 Pointing-at-the-Moon as Pointing: The Pointing Reaches to Here

The core declaration of §IV of this paper can be gathered into a single sentence:

**The Pointing-at-the-Moon octology academic treatise stratum reaches to here and no further. Beyond this line, nothing is in the eighty-eight papers, nothing is in any subsequent Pointing-at-the-Moon treatise, nothing is in any Han-Chinese academic paper that may in the future respond to Pointing-at-the-Moon — what lies beyond is in the reader's own dharma-eye, in the instant of the reader's own homage, praise, offering, confession, rejoicing, requesting-the-turning-of-the-wheel, requesting-the-buddhas-to-remain, constant-learning, accommodating-beings, and dedication, and in the single breath drawn after the reader has closed this paper and sat down to recollect the Buddha.**

This declaration is not pessimism, not giving up, not "we cannot do it so we are stopping." On the contrary — this declaration is the most confident utterance of the entire Pointing-at-the-Moon octology: **it trusts that the reader's dharma-eye can do what the treatise cannot.** To mark clearly the place where the pen is set down is the highest honor a treatise can pay its reader.

Fazang's *Tanxuan ji*, commenting on the Nature-Arising Chapter, contains one more passage, which will be the final citation of this §IV:

> "**Since this true nature pervasively fuses everything, what arises from it likewise fully contains everything. The partial and the complete are without limit; therefore, at each partial place everything is complete, nothing not containing the entire inexhaustible dharma-realm; therefore it pervades all time, all place, all dharmas, like Indra's net, nothing lacking.**"[^42]

The meaning of this passage is that true-nature (dharma-nature, original awareness) pervades every place, so that whatever arises in accordance with nature-arising from any place already fully contains the whole. Every "place" is complete; no place is partial. The moment the reader closes this paper, the reader herself at that instant is a "place" — and that place already fully contains everything; it is not in any way "insufficient."

The pointing of Pointing-at-the-Moon reaches to here. The moon is at the reader's own position, and always has been.

### 4.5 §IV Summary

With §IV declared, what remains of the work of this paper is only three things:

First, §V Middle-Way Correctives — to install three corrective guardrails upon the declaration of §IV itself, so that the reader does not misread "the failure-point declaration" as "systemic closure" or "project termination."

Second, §VI Conclusion — to formally dedicate the merit of the entire academic treatise stratum of the octology to the dharma-realm, in the posture of universal dedication.

Third, §I Introduction and Abstract — as the opening of the whole paper, they are in fact to be written only after the later sections are complete, for an introduction must have already seen the ending before it can truly "lead" to anywhere.

Once these three tasks are complete, the writing of the Pointing-at-the-Moon academic treatise stratum is officially closed.

## V. Middle-Way Correctives

If the declarations of §III and §IV are read directly and inferred from directly, three misreadings are liable to arise. This section dismantles them one by one.

### 5.1 Mutual Inclusion ≠ Closed System: The Octology Has Not Exhausted the Buddha-Dharma

§III demonstrates "the octology's dharma-realm as one mark" by means of four diagonal pairs, and it is easy to misread this as "the Pointing-at-the-Moon octology has already covered every facet of the Buddha-Dharma." This reading must be expressly refused.

The eight series of the octology correspond to eight foundational groups of texts within Han-Chinese Buddhism: Faith (S1, Yogācāra fundamentals and the bodhicitta treatises), Understanding (S2, the *Heart Sūtra*), Practice (S3, the *Treatise on the Hundred Dharmas*), Inquiry (S4, the original AI × Dharma series), Cutting (S5, the *Vajracchedikā*), Illumination (S6, the *Śūraṅgama*), Inclusion (S7, the *Lotus*), Manifestation (S8, the *Avataṃsaka*). This is a finite selection. Within the Han-Chinese canon there remain vast bodies of literature Pointing-at-the-Moon has not touched: the Vinaya piṭaka (the extensive *vinayas*, the precept-sūtras, the bodhisattva-precept texts); the Pure Land three sūtras and one treatise; the Vajrayāna and *tantras*; the Madhyamaka treatises (Nāgārjuna, Bhāviveka, Candrakīrti); the Tathāgatagarbha literature (*Ratnagotravibhāga*, *Buddha-Nature Treatise*); the Chan transmission-of-the-lamp and recorded-sayings literature; the Tiantai *śamatha-vipaśyanā* literature; the Three-Treatise school; the *Mahāyāna-saṃgraha* (Vasubandhu, Asvabhāva, Paramārtha); the *Abhidharmakośa*; the *Abhidharma*; the Tibetan Five Great Treatises; the Theravāda *Abhidhamma* …

None of these untouched fields are "unimportant in the view of Pointing-at-the-Moon," and none are "already implicitly covered." They are domains that "the writer of the Pointing-at-the-Moon academic treatise stratum, together with her era, cannot cover." A twenty-first-century Han-Chinese academic treatise series cannot substitute for the whole Dharma of two and a half thousand years, three major traditions, and eighty-five volumes of canonical scripture.

What Pointing-at-the-Moon points out as "the dharma-realm as one mark" does not mean "these eight series are everything"; it means "the *thing* to which these eight series point is the same thing to which any series not touched by Pointing-at-the-Moon also points." The moon is one; the pointing fingers of Pointing-at-the-Moon can point from eight angles — yet there remain measureless fingers that can point at the same moon from measureless angles. The eight lines of pointing of the Pointing-at-the-Moon octology are only a small bundle among measureless fingers, not the only finger, and certainly not the moon itself.

This corrective corresponds to Fazang's statement in the *Tanxuan ji* on the gate of general-and-particular: "**In the Perfect Teaching, both buddha-nature and nature-arising pervade the supportive and the principal**"[^43] — nature-arising pervades the supportive (environment), the principal (body), and all places; one can enter the seeing of nature-arising from any place. The *Avataṃsaka* never claimed that nature-arising can be entered only through the Nature-Arising Chapter; correspondingly, Pointing-at-the-Moon does not claim that the dharma-realm as one mark can be entered only through the octology's eight entries.

### 5.2 Closure ≠ Termination: After the Treatise Stratum Is Sealed

§IV declares "the academic treatise stratum reaches here and no further," and this is easily misread as "the Pointing-at-the-Moon project is about to stop operating" or "Pointing-at-the-Moon will not write anything else." This reading must also be expressly refused.

The "academic treatise stratum" is one specific layer among Pointing-at-the-Moon's four content strata: the treatise stratum, the YouTube spoken stratum, the offering-practice platform stratum, and the S8.5 Sudhana's Fifty-Three Encounters sub-series. The closing declared in §IV is limited to the first layer (the treatise stratum). The other three layers are not closed — indeed, they receive more space precisely because of the closure of the treatise stratum. In concrete terms:

1. **The YouTube spoken stratum.** The Pointing-at-the-Moon YouTube channel 〈Pointing at the Moon〉 will continue to unfold in the form of spoken talks, Q&A, and dialogues. After the closing of the treatise stratum, the spoken stratum will take on the responsibility of "translating the results of the treatise stratum into an idiom accessible to the contemporary Han-Chinese public," and will continue for years to come.

2. **The offering-practice platform stratum.** The online offering-practice platform Pointing-at-the-Moon is currently building — a venue that provides regular joint-practice, learning paths, and dedication liturgies for readers — bears no direct relation to the treatise stratum and is unaffected by the closure declared in this paper.

3. **The S8.5 Sudhana sub-series.** As foretold in S8-P09, *Sudhana's Southern Pilgrimage Prologue*, the Fifty-Three Encounters of Sudhana will unfold as an independent sub-series of Pointing-at-the-Moon. This sub-series does not belong to "the octology's academic treatise stratum"; it belongs to "the further unfolding after the octology," structurally analogous to the *Gaṇḍavyūha* in relation to the main body of the eighty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* — both an extension and a new stratum[^44].

The "closure" declared in §IV is therefore the closing of one particular stratum, not the ending of the project as a whole. If, having finished this paper, the reader concludes that "Pointing-at-the-Moon has ended," that is a structural misreading of §IV. Quite the opposite: the closing of the treatise stratum is the very moment at which the other three strata truly begin — just as Samantabhadra, at the end of *The Samantabhadra-caryā-praṇidhāna*, having pronounced the ten great vows, did not "end" the *Avataṃsaka* but handed the whole dynamic of the *Avataṃsaka* back into the daily practice of every one of its readers. The closing of the pen is a handing-off, not a curtain fall.

### 5.3 Dharma-Realm as One Mark ≠ Monism: The Huayan "One" Does Not Extinguish Difference

This paper takes "the dharma-realm as one mark" as its core thesis, and it is easily misread as a form of monism — as reading the *Avataṃsaka* to say "everything is one, and difference is mere illusion." This reading is one of the most ancient and most tenacious misreadings of the Huayan Perfect Teaching, and it must be expressly refused in this section.

The "one-is-all, all-is-one" of Huayan is not an extinguishing thesis of "one and many non-dual," but a structural thesis of "one and many mutually identical." Fazang in *Tanxuan ji* fascicle 16, gate of extent, says:

> "Since this true nature pervasively fuses everything, what arises from it likewise fully contains everything. The partial and the complete are without limit; therefore, at each partial place everything is complete, nothing not containing the entire inexhaustible dharma-realm."[^45]

The key phrase is "**at each partial place everything is complete**." The "partial place" must be truly retained as a "partial place," and at the same time every partial place "must be everything complete." Not "the partial place disappears into the whole," but "the partial place is, precisely in being the partial place, simultaneously the whole." This structure does not exist in the Vedic Brahmanical doctrine of the unity of ātman and Brahman — the Brahmanical "thou art that" (*tat tvam asi*) dissolves the individual into the whole; Huayan's "one is all" requires the individual to be retained in its individuality and at the same time non-different from the whole.

The same is true of this paper's "dharma-realm as one mark" thesis. What the four diagonal pairs of §III demonstrate is not "Faith, Understanding, Practice, Inquiry, Cutting, Illumination, Inclusion, Manifestation are all in fact the same character" — on the contrary, what they demonstrate is "the greater the difference among these eight characters, the deeper their mutual inclusion." Faith is Faith, Manifestation is Manifestation; the two are never to be conflated. But precisely because each strictly holds its facet, they can be compatible at the dharma-nature stratum in the manner of "inceptive and original non-dual." A monism that extinguishes difference destroys the very core structure of the Huayan Perfect Teaching.

This corrective is especially important for the Han-Chinese reader, because the character 一 ("one") in the Han-Chinese cultural substratum naturally carries overtones of "unification, identification, merging," which easily leads the reader to read "the dharma-realm as one mark" as "the dharma-realm is one total entity." The correct reading is this: the "one" in "the dharma-realm as one mark" is the "one" of "mutual identity and mutual entry," the "one" of "one-totality, nine-differentiations," the "one" of "at each partial place everything is complete." This "one" does not exclude "the many"; it *is* the very existentiality of "the many."

If, having finished this paper, the reader persists in reading the *Avataṃsaka* under a monistic posture, that is not the success of this paper but its failure. The Perfect Teaching of Huayan is not a Buddhist version of Indian philosophy; it is Buddhism's most precise demonstration of the structure "mutual identity does not obstruct difference."

### 5.4 §V Summary

With the three middle-way correctives complete, the structural work of this paper is fully done. The Introduction (§I) and the Conclusion (§VI) are responsible for two further functions: §I brings the reader into this paper's writing posture, and §VI formally dedicates the merit of the whole octology to the dharma-realm. Neither section installs new theses; each only handles the entrance and the exit.

## VI. Conclusion: Universal Dedication

### 6.1 Returning to the Final Passage of *The Manifestation of the Tathāgata*

In the third fascicle of *The Manifestation of the Tathāgata*, Samantabhadra, closing the chapter on "The Tathāgata's Accomplishment of Perfect Awakening," delivers this passage:

> "O son of the Buddha! The bodhisattva-mahāsattva should know that within a single pore of the Tathāgata's body there are buddha-bodies equal in number to the assemblies of all sentient beings. … As with one pore pervading the dharma-realm, so with every pore; one should know that there is no place, however small, empty of the buddha-body. Why? Because when the Tathāgata accomplishes perfect awakening, there is no place to which he does not reach. … O son of the Buddha! The bodhisattva-mahāsattva should know that **the mind itself, thought after thought, always contains a buddha accomplishing perfect awakening**. Why? **Because all the buddha-tathāgatas do not accomplish perfect awakening apart from this very mind.** As is one's own mind, so are the minds of all sentient beings — each contains the Tathāgata's accomplishment of perfect awakening, vast and pervasive, with no place where it is absent, neither departing nor being cut off, without pause, entering the inconceivable gate of upāya."[^46]

This passage is the highest arrival-point of the entire Chapter. It gathers "the manifestation of the Tathāgata" — as chapter-title, as doctrine, as historical event — into four words: **"the mind, thought after thought" (*zì xīn niàn niàn* 自心念念)**. The Buddha's accomplishment of perfect awakening is nowhere else; it is in this very thought, at this very moment, in the reader herself; and the buddha-tathāgatas do not accomplish perfect awakening apart from this mind. This is not a metaphor, not a rhetorical figure; it is the structural statement the eighty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* makes to its reader.

Walking to the last section of the eighty-eighth paper of the Pointing-at-the-Moon octology, the last thing that can be done is to read this passage once more, and then set down the pen.

### 6.2 How Extraordinary! How Extraordinary!

§II has already cited the long prose of the great-sūtra-in-a-mote parable of *The Manifestation of the Tathāgata*, which contains the famous line that the Chan tradition has quoted repeatedly for twelve hundred years. At the end of this paper, the line must appear once more — not this time as scriptural citation but as pointing-out:

> "**How extraordinary! How extraordinary! How is it that these beings possess the Tathāgata's wisdom, yet in foolishness and confusion do not know it, do not see it? I shall teach them the noble path, so that they may forever depart from deluded thinking and clinging, and see within their own bodies the Tathāgata's vast wisdom, not different from the Buddha's.**"[^47]

The "How extraordinary!" in this line is the two words that rose from the Buddha's heart at the moment he universally observed all beings throughout the dharma-realm. It is not astonishment, not pity — it is the Buddha's direct pointing at a structural fact: *how is it that these beings possess the Tathāgata's wisdom*. The two characters "how is it" (*yún hé* 云何) are the key. They are not interrogative; they are declarative. The Buddha is saying: *you are already like this, you have always been like this — why do you still not know it?*

The entire writing posture of the eighty-eight papers of the Pointing-at-the-Moon octology is, in the end, aimed at this "how is it." Not the setting up of a new thesis for the reader to learn, but the pointing-out of a fact that has always been present and that only the reader has not yet seen. If, at the moment when the reader, having read this final section, closes the paper, these two characters "how is it" appear in her heart even for a single instant, then the eight hundred thousand characters of the Pointing-at-the-Moon academic treatise stratum have not been written in vain.

### 6.3 Universal Dedication

The tenth vow of the *Samantabhadra-caryā-praṇidhāna* — Universal Dedication — says:

> "Furthermore, O virtuous son! 'Universal dedication' means: from the first vow of Homage down through Accommodation, all the merits produced, I dedicate universally to all sentient beings of the boundless dharma-realm and empty-space realm. I vow that all beings shall constantly attain peace and ease, be free from all sickness and suffering… **To the limit of empty space, to the exhaustion of the realm of beings, to the exhaustion of the karma of beings, to the exhaustion of the afflictions of beings: my dedication shall be without limit.** Thought after thought continuing without interruption; in body, speech, and mind, without weariness."[^48]

Pointing-at-the-Moon Shi Huijing began writing the first paper, S1-P01, *The Butterfly Effect of Initial Bodhicitta*, in the summer of 2023; now, in April 2026, having completed S8-P12, the eighty-eight papers of the Pointing-at-the-Moon octology academic treatise stratum have come to their close. Over a period of roughly thirty-three months, through every single action of deliberation, consideration, writing, proofreading, organization, publication, response, and revision that accompanied these papers, whatever roots of goodness have arisen — all are here dedicated:

- Dedicated to all sentient beings throughout the boundless dharma-realm and empty-space realm: may they all attain peace and ease, all hear the true Dharma, all see within themselves the marks and qualities of the Tathāgata's wisdom that have always been their own.
- Dedicated to every reader of these eighty-eight papers: may wisdom arise in the moment she stops at each middle-way corrective, may the dharma-eye open in the moment she sets down her demands at each failure-point declaration, and may she find her home in the single breath drawn after she closes the paper and sits down to recollect the Buddha.
- Dedicated to the three other strata of Pointing-at-the-Moon beyond the treatise stratum (the YouTube spoken stratum, the offering-practice platform stratum, and the S8.5 Sudhana sub-series): may they continue to unfold after the closing of the treatise stratum, and take on the work that the treatise stratum cannot bear.
- Dedicated to the two and a half thousand years of good friends who have composed Buddhist commentary in every language — Chinese, Tibetan, Pāli, Sanskrit, Japanese, Korean, English, German, French, and all others: may every marking they set down be seen by those who come after, may every failure-point they left be honored by those who come after, and may every "the pointing reaches to here" they left be walked further by those who come after.
- Dedicated to Pointing-at-the-Moon herself (Shi Huijing): may she not, after the closing of the treatise stratum, become lost in the illusion that "I have finished writing," but may she continue, in the postures of spoken talk, of practice, of offering, and of the journeys of Sudhana-like encounters, to remain a perpetual beginner.

> To the limit of empty space, to the exhaustion of the realm of beings, to the exhaustion of the karma of beings, to the exhaustion of the afflictions of beings: this dedication shall be without limit. Thought after thought continuing without interruption; in body, speech, and mind, without weariness.

### 6.4 The Last Line

In the second fascicle of the eighty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka*'s *Manifestation of the Tathāgata*, the closing lines of the verse of the great-sūtra-in-a-mote parable read:

> "The great compassion of the buddhas causes them to remove their delusion — **thus does it manifest**, benefiting the bodhisattvas."[^49]

The Pointing-at-the-Moon academic treatise stratum reaches to here and no further.

*Thus does it manifest.*
