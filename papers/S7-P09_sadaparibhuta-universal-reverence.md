## Abstract

This is the ninth paper in the Pointing at the Moon *Lotus Sūtra* (*Saddharmapuṇḍarīka*) research series, treating Chapter Twenty, "The Bodhisattva Never-Disparaging" (常不輕菩薩品, *Sadāparibhūta-bodhisattva-parivarta*). The chain of escalating prediction (vyākaraṇa, 授記) traced across S7 reaches its terminus here: from the Buddha's prediction of the śrāvaka disciples beginning with Śāriputra, through the limit-case predictions of Devadatta and the Nāga princess in S7-P08, to the present chapter—**a bodhisattva's universal prediction of all sentient beings, issued by his own act**. The central claim of this paper is that the Sadāparibhūta chapter accomplishes a structural inversion of decisive importance: **prediction is elevated from a "speech-act of a Buddha" to a "dharma-gate that any practitioner can operate daily."** The phrase *"I dare not slight you, for you shall all become Buddhas"* is no longer merely a declarative utterance; it is a complete practice mobilizing the three karmas (body, speech, mind) in unison.

This paper argues three theses. **First**, the Sadāparibhūta chapter is the second clear demonstration of what the present author calls "Thesis I: the dual-track confluence." Following S7-P08, Kuiji's (窺基) commentary on this chapter in the *Fahua xuanzan* (法華玄贊, T1723) deploys *both* the Tathāgatagarbha-track language (報身/法身如來藏) *and* the Yogācāra-track language (種姓 *gotra*, 習性 *vāsanā*) on a single sentence of scripture—more concentrated and more explicit than the Devadatta–Nāga-princess witness of P08. **Second**, Sadāparibhūta's "universal reverence" (普敬, *pǔjìng*) is not a Mahāyāna innovation but the dramatized completion of Āgamic *mettā* (the radiance of loving-kindness pervading all beings); the *Ekottarāgama* fascicle 41 (the Buddha's autobiographical account of seven years cultivating *mettā*) and fascicle 47 (the eleven fruits of *mettā*-liberation, with its explicit injunction to "broadcast its meaning, expounding it for others") together form the twin pillars from which the Lotus action descends with structural fidelity. **Third**, the Sadāparibhūta chapter shifts the doctrine of prediction from the doctrinal register down into the practical register: the bodhisattva himself takes "you shall all become Buddhas" as *his own dharma-gate to perform*, and that performance is itself the bodhisattva path.

The paper also addresses three Middle-Way corrections (中道校正): (1) *Danger #7—the magical reading of "one sentence and one becomes a Buddha,"* corrected through the Yogācāra seed doctrine (śruta-vāsanā plants seeds, it does not produce instantaneous fruit) balanced against the *Ekottarāgama*'s "swiftly attaining nirvāṇa"; (2) *Danger #8—reading Sadāparibhūta as passive submission*, corrected by returning to the two active verbs of the scripture itself ("seeing the fourfold assembly from afar, he would still go up to them" and "fleeing to a safe distance, he would still call out aloud"), supported by the Āgamic two-track structure of "inward forbearance, outward withdrawal"; and (3) *Danger #2—the cheapening of universal Buddhahood*, corrected by a cross-series callback to the Diamond Sūtra's (S5) erasure of the four marks (四相), establishing the structural constraint that universal reverence must rest on selflessness (anātman) as its floor.

**Keywords**: Sadāparibhūta · Universal Reverence dharma-gate · operationalization of prediction · Tathāgatagarbha-track · Yogācāra-track · Thesis I dual-track confluence · *mettā* pervading all beings · *śruta-vāsanā* seed-planting · Middle-Way corrections

## I. Introduction: The Terminus of the Chain of Escalating Prediction

By the close of S7-P08, the *Lotus Sūtra* series has pushed the concept of prediction (vyākaraṇa) to its two extreme limits: the qualitative limit (Devadatta—the man who sought to murder the Buddha can become a Buddha) and the quantitative limit (the Nāga princess—a female body, a child, a dragon, can become a Buddha *in this very instant*). Both limits were sealed by the Buddha's own predictive utterance, demonstrating the thesis that *no condition whatsoever can obstruct Buddhahood*.

But P08 closed with one question unresolved. If the power of prediction is so immense, why must this power be wielded only by a Buddha? If sentient beings already possess Buddha-nature, why is it that only a Buddha may say "you shall become a Buddha"? This question cannot be answered from inside P08, because the protagonist of P08 is still the Buddha himself.

The Sadāparibhūta chapter supplies the answer. In this chapter, the agent who issues the prediction **is not a Buddha but a monk (bhikṣu)**. This monk has received no prediction, possesses no supernatural powers, and indeed "did not specialize in reciting the scriptures." His sole practice is to bow to whomever he meets and, while bowing, to speak a single sentence: *"I deeply revere you and dare not slight you. Why? Because you all walk the bodhisattva path, and you shall attain Buddhahood."*[^1] He spoke this sentence his entire life—when reviled, when struck, even when struck and forced to flee, he called it out from a distance.

The structural inversion accomplished by this chapter is far weightier than its surface narrative suggests. Sadāparibhūta accomplishes three things at once:

**First, the agent of prediction is broadened.** Prediction ceases to be the exclusive speech-act of a Buddha and becomes something a bodhisattva—indeed, any practitioner who has attained the contemplative insight that "the fourfold assembly all possess the cause of Buddha-nature"[^2]—may perform. This does not cheapen prediction; it relocates it from the register of "exceptional doctrinal events" to the register of "daily practice on the path."

**Second, the nature of prediction is operationalized.** In P08 and earlier chapters, prediction was essentially a *sentence*: the Buddha utters a phrase, and a being is destined for future Buddhahood. In the Sadāparibhūta chapter, prediction becomes an *action*: bowing is the karma of body, praising is the karma of speech, deep reverence is the karma of mind[^3]; the three karmas operate in unison, and the verbal phrase "you shall all become Buddhas" is merely the linguistic component of this action-chain. Zhiyi's *Fahua wenju* (法華文句, T1718) sees through to this layer: "Deep reverence is the karma of mind; the words of non-disparagement are the karma of speech; deliberately approaching to bow is the karma of body. These three, joined with compassion, constitute the Vow-and-Ease practice."[^4]

**Third, the object of prediction is universalized.** Sadāparibhūta does not face a single śrāvaka disciple selected by the Buddha, nor a dramatic limit-case figure, but rather "everyone he saw—monks, nuns, laymen, laywomen."[^5] The phrase "everyone he saw" pushes prediction to the breadth of unconditionality.

These three taken together constitute what I am calling the **Universal Reverence dharma-gate** (普敬法門): *a method of practice that any person, at any moment, with respect to any object, may perform—whose sole operational content is to bow with the body, to declare aloud "you shall become a Buddha" with speech, and to revere deeply with mind.*

The paper answers three questions. (1) What is the ontological warrant of universal reverence? On what grounds may the sentence "you shall all become Buddhas" be uttered? (2) Once uttered, what actually happens? How does the sentence avoid degenerating into a cheap slogan? (3) Is Sadāparibhūta's mode of action *passive forbearance* or *active practice*? These three questions correspond, respectively, to the three theses and three Middle-Way corrections of this paper.

Structurally, this paper closes the S7 prediction sub-arc (P07 *Robe-Jewel and Topknot-Jewel* → P08 *Devadatta and the Nāga Princess* → P09 *Sadāparibhūta*) and lays the groundwork for S7-P10 on Avalokiteśvara's Universal Gate (普門品). Sadāparibhūta is *the universal gate as inner stance*: a practitioner taking universal reverence as his interior practice. Avalokiteśvara is *the universal gate as outward responsive manifestation*: dharmadhātu compassion responding to all beings without condition. Read together, the two chapters yield the complete spectrum of the Lotus concept of *universal gate* (普門, *samantamukha*).

## II. Scriptural and Treatise Foundations

This section follows the standard methodology of the series, treating sūtra (甲) and treatise (乙) sources separately. Sūtras come first and ground the argument; treatises follow and refine it.

### II.A · Sūtra Sources

#### II.A.1 · The Āgamic Seed: From *Mettā* Pervading All Beings to the Universal Reverence Practice

Thesis III of the series holds that the seven parables and central teachings of the *Lotus Sūtra* are not Mahāyāna inventions ex nihilo but the dramatized completion of seeds already present in the Āgamas. The Āgamic root of the Sadāparibhūta chapter is *mettā*—loving-kindness pervading all beings. This seed-line rests on two pillars: a *vertical* pillar (the Buddha's own attestation) and a *horizontal* pillar (the method of practice).

**The vertical pillar** is the Buddha's autobiographical account in *Ekottarāgama* fascicle 41 (T0125). The Buddha addresses the bhikṣus: *"You should practice loving-kindness, broadcast loving-kindness; by practicing loving-kindness, all hatred in the mind will dissolve of itself."* He then recalls his own past: "In days past, for seven years I continually cultivated *mettā*; I passed through seven kalpas of formation and dissolution without returning to birth-and-death... Thirty-seven times I became Śakra, Lord of the Devas, and innumerable times a wheel-turning sage king."[^6] The *Madhyamāgama* "Sūtra on Merit" (福經, T0026) repeats the same content in verse: *"In times past I, a bhikṣu, for seven years cultivated mettā; through seven kalpas of formation and destruction, I did not return to this world."*[^7]

The importance of these passages is this: **the Buddha himself attests that his own bodhisattva path began with seven years of intensive practice in loving-kindness alone.** *Mettā* is not an auxiliary practice but a core practice capable of bearing the temporal span of seven kalpas of cosmic formation and dissolution, capable of holding the practitioner's direction unbroken across saṃsāra. Sadāparibhūta's "everyone he saw, he bowed to and praised"[^5] and the Buddha's "for seven years I continually cultivated *mettā*" share an identical temporal structure: both stand in an unconditional, uninterrupted posture toward all objects.

**The horizontal pillar** is the section on the *eleven fruits of mettā-liberation* in *Ekottarāgama* fascicle 47. The Buddha addresses the bhikṣus: "If sentient beings practice the liberation of loving-kindness, *broadcasting its meaning and expounding it for others*, they will obtain these eleven fruits. What are the eleven? Sleeping in peace, waking in peace, seeing no evil dreams, being protected by devas, beloved by humans, untouched by poison, untouched by weapons, untroubled by water, fire, or thieves. And when the body breaks up at death, they are reborn in the Brahma heavens."[^8] The verse appended to this passage adds: *"Practicing loving-kindness pervading all, never bearing a hateful mind... swiftly attaining nirvāṇa, eternally reaching the unconditioned."*[^9]

This passage contributes three things to P09. **First**, the eight-character phrase 廣布其義,與人演說 ("broadcast its meaning, expound it for others") is structurally identical to Sadāparibhūta's "even when he saw the fourfold assembly from afar, he would still go up to them and bow and praise"[^10]—both externalize an inner state of *mettā* / reverence into a deliberate verbal act addressed to others. This is the most direct lexical evidence for Thesis III's claim that the Lotus operationalizes Āgamic seeds. **Second**, "loving-kindness pervading all" prefigures Sadāparibhūta's "I dare not slight you"—the *you* in question is the universal-gate object admitting no exception. **Third**, "swiftly attaining nirvāṇa" pre-empts the misreading that *mettā* is the "slow path," and supplies the Middle-Way balance for §IV.1's treatment of Danger #7: seed-planting is neither instantaneous Buddhahood nor infinite deferral, but rather *swift attainment of nirvāṇa*.

Read together, the two pillars locate Sadāparibhūta's Universal Reverence practice with precision: **the all-pervading mental posture of *mettā*, joined with the externalizing act of "broadcasting its meaning," concentrated in a single person as a concrete practice mobilizing the three karmas in unison.**

This Āgamic seed-line requires one further dimension: *mettā* is not blind, undiscriminating accommodation. The *Saṃyuktāgama* fascicle 46 records that Śakra "by the power of loving-kindness, with majestic strength subdued the army of asuras"[^11]—loving-kindness runs in parallel with strength. The *Madhyamāgama* "Sūtra to Velāma" states explicitly that one who cultivates loving-kindness, "if able to contemplate all dharmas as impermanent, suffering, empty, and not-self, this contemplation surpasses all giving"[^12]—loving-kindness operates in tandem with the wisdom-eye (prajñā-cakṣu). These passages establish that Āgamic *mettā* was never "the principled niceness of a pushover" but always the integrated practice of loving-kindness, wisdom, and majesty together. Sadāparibhūta's universal reverence inherits precisely this *karuṇā-prajñā in tandem* tradition—a point §IV.2 will mobilize against the "passive forbearance" misreading.

Finally, the Āgamas supply a *two-track structure of "inward forbearance, outward withdrawal,"* which is the key to dismantling Danger #8. The *Ekottarāgama* "Sūtra to Mahā-Kauṣṭhila" (均頭經) in fascicle 43 explicitly juxtaposes two acts: "If others give rise to anger, we shall train in forbearance" (the inward act), and "If others harbor jealousy, we shall withdraw" (the outward act).[^13] Forbearance is a mental posture; withdrawal is an action; the two stand side by side without contradiction. The *Dīrghāgama* fascicle 9 lists "drawing near to good friends" as the first of the three constituent factors of attainment[^14]—the converse implication being that "withdrawing from evil friends" is itself a constituent of practice. This two-track structure provides full Āgamic warrant for Sadāparibhūta's "fleeing to a safe distance, he would still call out aloud"[^15]—as I will argue in §IV.2.

#### II.A.2 · The Lotus Sūtra Itself: The Five-Layer Narrative Structure of the Sadāparibhūta Chapter

The Sadāparibhūta chapter in Kumārajīva's translation (T0262) divides into five narrative layers, each contributing distinctly to P09's argument.

**Layer one: the opening frame.** The Buddha addresses Bodhisattva Mahāsthāmaprāpta (得大勢): countless asaṃkhyeya kalpas ago there was a Buddha named Bhīṣmagarjitasvara-rāja (威音王如來), in a kalpa called Vinirbhoga (離衰), in a land called Mahāsaṃbhava (大成).[^16] This layer establishes the temporal scale: Sadāparibhūta's story unfolds *after* a Buddha's parinirvāṇa, after the True Dharma has perished, during the age of the Semblance Dharma, when overweening monks (增上慢比丘, *abhimānika-bhikṣu*) wield great influence. This setting is doctrinally crucial—it corresponds not to a flourishing age of the True Dharma but to **a setting structurally analogous to the present era of Dharma decline**. The chapter is therefore not a remote myth but a direct response to the question: *how does one practice after the Dharma has withered?*

**Layer two: the declaration of the practice.** "At that time there was a bodhisattva-bhikṣu named Sadāparibhūta. Mahāsthāmaprāpta, why was he called Sadāparibhūta? Because everyone this monk saw—whether monk, nun, layman, or laywoman—he would bow to and praise, saying: 'I deeply revere you and dare not slight you. Why? Because you all walk the bodhisattva path and shall attain Buddhahood.'"[^5] This layer compresses Sadāparibhūta's entire practice into three actions: bowing (body), praising (speech), reverence (mind). Zhiyi reads this as the "four-fold unity" (人一/理一/行一/教一: one person, one principle, one practice, one teaching),[^17] and Jizang reads it as "spreading the scripture through the three karmas"[^18]—treatments treated below in §II.B.

**Layer three: the active practice of fleeing to a safe distance.** This is the central textual datum for §IV.2's treatment of the "passive forbearance" misreading and must be cited verbatim:

> And this monk did not specialize in reciting the scriptures, but only practiced making obeisance, and **even when he saw the fourfold assembly from afar, he would still go up to them and bow and praise** them, saying: "I dare not slight you, for you shall all become Buddhas." Among the fourfold assembly were those who gave rise to anger and to impure minds, and reviled him with harsh words... So it went for many years, constantly reviled, never giving rise to anger, always saying: "You shall become a Buddha." When he spoke these words, some among the people would strike him with sticks or hurl tiles and stones at him, and **fleeing to a safe distance, he would still call out aloud**: "I dare not slight you, for you shall all become Buddhas."[^19]

Two key actions in this passage must be highlighted. **"Seeing the fourfold assembly from afar, he would still go up to them"**—this is an active *approach*: Sadāparibhūta sees the fourfold assembly in the distance and *deliberately walks over* to bow and praise. **"Fleeing to a safe distance, he would still call out aloud"**—this is an active *withdrawal*: when people raised sticks, tiles, and stones to strike him, he fled, and from a safe distance continued to declare his message.

Read together, Sadāparibhūta's behavioral pattern is: **actively approach those of capacity, actively withdraw from scenes of violence, and from a safe distance continue to declare the message.** This is decidedly not the "stand-your-ground non-resistance" that the misreading projects. Dharmarakṣa's earlier translation (T0263, *Zhengfahua jing*) preserves this dynamic still more vividly: "Though he was reviled, he was not angry; his face did not change... When struck with tiles and stones, he continued to raise his voice from a distance, instructing them."[^20] The "raising of voice from a distance" matches Kumārajīva's "fleeing to a safe distance, he would still call out aloud" precisely. The third translation, Jñānagupta–Dharmagupta's *Tianpin miaofa lianhua jing* (T0264), likewise retains "fleeing to a safe distance, he would still call out aloud: 'I dare not slight you, for you shall all become Buddhas.'"[^21] Across all three translations, the key actions are preserved without exception. This proves that "active withdrawal + continuous declaration" is the most original and stable narrative core of the chapter, not the embellishment of any single translator.

**Layer four: purification of the six sense-faculties and the broad expounding of the Lotus.** At the end of his life, Sadāparibhūta hears in the air "the Lotus Sūtra previously spoken by Bhīṣmagarjitasvara Buddha, twenty thousand koṭi gāthās, all of which he was able to receive and hold; and he immediately attained, as above, the purification of the eye-faculty, and the purification of the ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind faculties."[^22] Thereafter "his lifespan was extended by two million koṭi nayuta years, and he expounded this Lotus Sūtra widely to others."[^22] This layer is decisive: Sadāparibhūta's attainment **was not achieved instantaneously during the period of universal reverence**; it unfolded across three distinct phases: (1) the universal reverence phase (during which he was reviled and beaten); (2) the deathbed phase of hearing the sūtra and attaining the six purifications; (3) the post-extension phase of widely expounding the Lotus, lasting two million koṭi nayuta years. The temporal structure is unambiguous: *seed-planting → maturation → manifest expounding*—not the magic of "one sentence and one becomes a Buddha." This is the direct scriptural evidence for §IV.1's correction of Danger #7.

**Layer five: identification and the karmic recompense of the fourfold assembly.** "The Bodhisattva Sadāparibhūta of that time—was he some other person? He was none other than my own self."[^23] Sadāparibhūta is the past-life of Śākyamuni. And the fourfold assembly that once reviled him: "Because of slighting me with minds of anger, for two hundred koṭi kalpas they constantly failed to meet a Buddha, hear the Dharma, or see the Saṅgha; for a thousand kalpas they suffered great agony in the Avīci hell. Having exhausted that retribution, they again encountered Bodhisattva Sadāparibhūta, who instructed them in *anuttarā samyaksaṃbodhi*"[^24]—and they are none other than the five hundred bodhisattvas led by Bhadrapāla in the present Lotus assembly. The significance of this layer: **the seeds planted all eventually manifested**, even those of the revilers, separated from the moment of planting only by the time-lag of two hundred koṭi kalpas plus a thousand kalpas in Avīci hell. This is the most dramatic confirmation of the Yogācāra doctrine that "every seed planted will have its day of manifestation," and simultaneously a counter-defense against cheapening: seed-planting is real, but the cost of manifestation can be appalling.

#### II.A.3 · The Tathāgatagarbha Ontological Substrate: *Śrīmālā*, *Laṅkāvatāra*, *Buddha-Nature Treatise*

What makes universal reverence sayable at all? The ontological warrant is the proposition that *all sentient beings possess Buddha-nature*. The early systematic literature on this proposition consists of three texts: the *Śrīmālādevī Sūtra*, the *Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra*, and the *Foxing lun* (佛性論, *Buddha-Nature Treatise*)—all three present in vault-唯識 and directly citable.

The *Śrīmālā Sūtra*'s Chapter Thirteen, "On Self-Nature Purity" (自性清淨章), is one of the earliest and most concise systematic statements in the history of Tathāgatagarbha thought: "The Tathāgatagarbha is the dharmadhātu-treasury, the dharmakāya-treasury, the supramundane-supreme-treasury, the self-nature-pure-treasury. This pure-nature Tathāgatagarbha is yet defiled by adventitious dust-defilements and upper defilements—an inconceivable Tathāgata-realm."[^25] This passage establishes the "four-treasury formula" (dharmadhātu / dharmakāya / supramundane-supreme / self-nature-pure) and identifies Tathāgatagarbha unambiguously with the dharmakāya inherent in sentient beings. The subsequent statement that "the self-nature-pure mind yet has defilement" establishes the core *two-layer structure* of Tathāgatagarbha thought: **a pure substance** (unchanging) and **adventitious dust** (removable).[^26] The Buddha himself certifies this statement as "difficult to comprehend." This two-layer structure is the direct ontological warrant for Sadāparibhūta's continuing to say "you shall become a Buddha" even to those reviling and beating him: defilements are adventitious dust; Buddha-nature is the substance; the dust can be removed; the substance cannot be lost.

The *Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra* fascicle 4 expresses the same structure in different language: "This Tathāgatagarbha-store-consciousness... though pure in self-nature, is yet covered by adventitious dust, and so appears impure—but this is not the case for the Tathāgatas."[^27] The *Laṅkāvatāra*'s contribution is to splice the *Śrīmālā* concept of "treasury" (garbha) with the Yogācāra concept of "consciousness" (vijñāna)—the "Tathāgatagarbha-store-consciousness" is at once *garbha* (dharmakāya) and *vijñāna* (ālayavijñāna). This is precisely the textual foundation that later allows Kuiji to deploy both tracks simultaneously in his commentary on the Sadāparibhūta chapter.

The *Foxing lun* (T1610, attributed to Vasubandhu, translated by Paramārtha) opens with the "five-fault / five-virtue" structure explaining why the Buddha taught that all sentient beings possess Buddha-nature: "The Tathāgata, in order to remove five faults and to generate five virtues, taught that all sentient beings possess Buddha-nature. The five faults to be removed are: first, to free beings from the mind of self-disparagement; second, to free them from arrogance toward inferior persons; third, to free them from attachment to falsehood; fourth, to free them from slandering the true Dharma; fifth, to free them from the grasping of self."[^28] The contribution of this passage to P09: **the very utterance "you shall all become Buddhas" is itself a therapeutic act**—it treats the listener's mind of self-disparagement, arrogance, false grasping, slander of true Dharma, and self-attachment. Sadāparibhūta saying "you shall become a Buddha" to the fourfold assembly is not a factual prediction; it is the performance of a therapeutic action. The efficacy of the therapeutic action does not depend on the present condition of the object but on the posture and frequency of the agent—a structure analogous (⚠️) to "unconditional positive regard" in modern psychotherapy, though irreducible to it (see §III.1's three-layer treatment of cross-disciplinary analogy).

The three texts together provide P09 not with a single proposition but with a **three-layer ontological substrate**: (1) the equality of dharmakāya (the *Śrīmālā* four-treasury formula); (2) the two-layer structure of purity and adventitious dust (*Śrīmālā* × *Laṅkāvatāra*); (3) the pragmatic mechanism of declaration-as-therapy (*Foxing lun*'s five-fault / five-virtue structure). All three layers will be mobilized in §III.1's argument for the dual-track confluence of Thesis I.

### II.B · Treatise Sources

With the three sūtra strands established (Āgamic seed × Lotus scripture × Tathāgatagarbha substrate), the task of the treatise section is to address *how these three strands converge, and how the convergence operates concretely*. This section treats the commentators in chronological order: Vasubandhu, Zhiyi, Jizang, and Kuiji—of which Kuiji's *Fahua xuanzan* is the most decisive convergence document for this chapter.

#### II.B.1 · Vasubandhu's *Treatise on the Lotus Sūtra*: The Six Predictions and Sadāparibhūta as the Paradigm of Bodhisattva-Prediction

Vasubandhu's *Saddharmapuṇḍarīkopadeśa* (*Miaofa lianhua jing youbotishe* 妙法蓮華經憂波提舍, T1519), in systematizing the Lotus doctrine of prediction, proposes a typology of "six kinds of prediction." When discussing the category of "bodhisattva-prediction" (菩薩記), Vasubandhu cites the Sadāparibhūta chapter directly as the paradigm: "As for bodhisattva-prediction, it should be understood as shown in the chapter on the Bodhisattva Never-Disparaging below. 'Bowing and praising and saying: I do not slight you, you shall all attain Buddhahood'—this **demonstrates that all sentient beings possess Buddha-nature**."[^29]

This passage is decisive. Vasubandhu, the founding Indic Yogācāra commentator on the Lotus, states explicitly that the substance of Sadāparibhūta's "you shall all attain Buddhahood" **is not prediction-as-prophecy but demonstration of inherent Buddha-nature**. The difference from a literal reading is fundamental: a literal reading takes "you shall all become Buddhas" as a future-tense prediction (at some future time and place this being will become a Buddha); Vasubandhu's reading takes it as a *present-tense act of showing* (at this very moment, displaying to the listener the fact that they already possess Buddha-nature).

In Vasubandhu's framework, "prediction" is not a statement about the future but a disclosure of inherent Buddha-nature. This translation is decisive because it directly defuses the literalist prophecy-misreading of *vyākaraṇa*. The semantic structure of Sadāparibhūta's utterance is: *"By means of the act of bowing and praising, I am showing you that you possess Buddha-nature, and that therefore, when conditions ripen, you must inevitably attain Buddhahood."* This is not prophecy; it is unveiling.

#### II.B.2 · Kuiji's *Fahua xuanzan*: The Second Witness of Thesis I's Dual-Track Confluence

Kuiji's *Miaofa lianhua jing xuanzan* (T1723) is the authoritative Tang-era Faxiang (Yogācāra) commentary on the *Lotus Sūtra*. In his commentary on the Sadāparibhūta chapter, Kuiji deploys *both* Tathāgatagarbha-track and Yogācāra-track language—the **second witness** of Thesis I's dual-track confluence in this series (the first having occurred in P08's Devadatta–Nāga-princess pairing).

Kuiji first defines the name *Sadāparibhūta* in Yogācāra-track language: "Contemplating that the fourfold assembly possess the cause of Buddha-nature, exhorting them to practice so that they must attain Buddhahood, with the three karmas constantly reverent—therefore he is called Never-Disparaging."[^30] Note the structure of this sentence: "possessing the cause of Buddha-nature" (the inherent cause) + "exhorting them to practice" (the contributory condition, *adhipati-pratyaya*) + "must attain Buddhahood" (the fruit). This is a complete cause-condition-fruit triad, not a prophecy. "Buddha-nature" here is the **cause**, not an already-realized fruit.

Kuiji then enters the most decisive convergence passage in this chapter's commentary:

> "I deeply revere you and dare not slight you"—because they possess the inherent nature of a Buddha and abide in the lineage (*gotra*); **this reveres the Tathāgatagarbha as saṃbhogakāya**. "You walk the bodhisattva path and shall attain Buddhahood"—because for those possessing the lineage, *if* they activate habituated nature (*vāsanā*) and arouse the mind to practice, Buddhahood is necessarily attained. Furthermore, on the basis of the **Tathāgatagarbha as dharmakāya**, all beings have my deep reverence; if they walk the bodhisattva path and arouse the cultivation of the saṃbhogakāya Tathāgatagarbha, they shall attain Buddhahood.[^31]

This single passage **deploys four distinct technical vocabularies simultaneously**: (1) "saṃbhogakāya Tathāgatagarbha" and "dharmakāya Tathāgatagarbha" from the Tathāgatagarbha-track; (2) "lineage" (*gotra* 種姓) from the Yogācāra-track lineage doctrine; (3) "habituated nature" (*vāsanā* 習性) from Yogācāra perfuming theory; (4) the conditional cause-effect structure ("for those possessing the lineage, *if* they activate habituated nature, *then* attaining Buddhahood is necessary") from Yogācāra causal reasoning. Four vocabularies operate simultaneously, none dissolved into the others—this is precisely what *confluence* (rather than fusion) means: the two tracks preserve their distinctions while being mobilized jointly within a single textual event.

The claim of this paper is: **if the Devadatta–Nāga-princess pairing of S7-P08 is the first witness of Thesis I's dual-track confluence, then Kuiji's commentary on Sadāparibhūta in S7-P09 is the second and more concentrated witness.** The significance of the second witness lies in its proof that confluence is not an isolated case prompted by the limit-case pairing of P08—Kuiji is not invoking dual-track machinery only when handling exceptional reversals (Devadatta) or compressed temporalities (the Nāga princess); he invokes it automatically and densely when commenting on the most "ordinary" of bodhisattva chapters. This strongly suggests: **the doctrinal structure of the *Lotus Sūtra* itself requires the dual track**; it is not the ad hoc improvisation of a commentator.

Kuiji then maps Sadāparibhūta's entire practice onto the Yogācāra path of cultivation: "The earlier period of bowing corresponds to the forty mind-stages; hearing the Dharma and the purification of the faculties to the four wholesome roots (*kuśalamūla*) stage. Through the extension of lifespan he attains great wholesome quiescence and gradually engages in principle-contemplation, contemplating the true principle... Departing from the previous five fears, he attains the four fearlessnesses and enters the first ground; later, entering the eighth ground, he departs from the cause of fear."[^32] This passage maps the five-layer narrative structure of the chapter (§II.A.2 above) onto the Yogācāra ten-grounds path: the bowing period = the forty minds (path of provisions through path of preparation); deathbed sūtra-hearing and purification = four wholesome roots leading into the first bodhisattva ground; the post-extension Lotus-expounding period = the first ground onward, up through the eighth ground. The significance of this mapping is decisive: **Sadāparibhūta's attainment was not stage-skipping**—he too proceeded step by step, from path of provisions through path of preparation, into the first ground and the eighth. This directly refutes the "one sentence and one becomes a Buddha" misreading: even Sadāparibhūta himself was not someone who "became a Buddha in a single sentence"—he became a Buddha "one sentence at a time, one step at a time."

#### II.B.3 · Zhiyi's *Fahua wenju*: The "Four-Fold Unity" Reading and the Three Karmas of Compassion

Zhiyi's *Miaofa lianhua jing wenju* (T1718) opens its commentary on the chapter with the "comprehension of non-disparagement": "Inwardly harboring the comprehension of non-disparagement, outwardly revering the objects of non-disparagement, in body establishing the practice of non-disparagement, in speech proclaiming the teaching of non-disparagement, among people earning the designation of non-disparagement."[^33] This four-layer structure—comprehension (insight), object, practice (body-karma), teaching (speech-karma)—is fully present in a single practitioner.

Zhiyi then splices in the five Buddha-natures of Vasubandhu's *Treatise on the Lotus Sūtra*: "The *Treatise on the Lotus Sūtra* says: 'This bodhisattva knows that sentient beings possess Buddha-nature and dares not slight them.' Buddha-nature is fivefold; the **direct-cause Buddha-nature pervades both root and consummation**; the contributory-cause and revealing-cause Buddha-natures—the seeds are originally present, not freshly arisen now. The fruit-nature and the fruit-of-fruit-nature are necessarily attained, never in vain. This is called the comprehension of non-disparagement."[^34] The seven characters 正因佛性通亘本當 ("the direct-cause Buddha-nature pervades both root and consummation") are the gnomon of the entire chapter: the direct-cause Buddha-nature spans both *root* (originally possessed) and *consummation* (yet to be attained), serving simultaneously as cause and fruit, present and future. This supplies the distinctively Tiantai temporal reading of "shall attain Buddhahood": the *shall* in "you shall attain Buddhahood" is not a remote future but **a future already pervaded by the originally-given root**.

Zhiyi's "four-fold unity" reading (四一) structures the chapter as: "Being named Sadāparibhūta is the **unity of person**; everyone he saw is the **unity of principle**; bowing to all is the **unity of practice**; and saying these words is the **unity of teaching**."[^35] One person, one principle, one practice, one teaching—the four collapsed into one. The point of "four-fold unity" is not that four things are tied together, but that **in this particular case, person, principle, practice, and teaching are intrinsically inseparable**. Sadāparibhūta the person = the principle of universal reverence = the practice of bowing = the teaching that "you shall all become Buddhas." This is the Tiantai version of the thesis "the action *is* the teaching" that I will develop in §III.3.

Finally, Zhiyi splices Sadāparibhūta's practice into the "three rules" of the *Lotus Sūtra*'s "Chapter on the Dharma Teacher" (the Tathāgata's room / robe / seat): "Non-disparagement and deep reverence is the seat of the Tathāgata; enduring beating and reviling is wearing the robe of the Tathāgata; with a mind of compassion to practice constantly without ceasing—this is the room of the Tathāgata. Furthermore, deep reverence is the karma of mind, the words of non-disparagement are the karma of speech, deliberately approaching to bow is the karma of body. These three, joined with compassion, are the Vow-and-Ease practice."[^36] Three karmas of body, speech, and mind × the mind of compassion = the Vow-and-Ease practice (*pūrvapraṇidhāna-sukhavihāra*)—this is the structural docking between the Sadāparibhūta chapter and the chapter on "Practice in Ease and Joy" (*Sukhavihāra-parivarta*). The Vow-and-Ease practice is one of the four ease-and-joy practices, designating any action arising from a bodhisattva's compassionate vow. Sadāparibhūta's "deliberate approach to bow" is itself the concrete enactment of the Vow-and-Ease practice.

#### II.B.4 · Jizang's *Fahua yishu*: Non-Duality and the Buddha-Nature of Evil Persons

Jizang's *Fahua yishu* (T1721) enters the chapter through the Sanlun (Madhyamaka) reading of non-duality: "As for *Sadāparibhūta*: all sentient beings constantly possess Buddha-nature; thus the saying 'all dharmas are originally and constantly of the mark of quiescence.' The great being attains this contemplative mind, and thereby constantly does not disparage anything."[^37] Note the logic of this sentence: Sadāparibhūta is able to be Sadāparibhūta not because he "has resolved to be a good person," but because he "has attained this contemplative mind"—he has *seen* the non-dual reality of "all dharmas originally and constantly of the mark of quiescence," and consequently does not disparage any sentient being. **Universal reverence is not the result of moral effort but the natural overflow of contemplative insight into reality.**

Jizang's second contribution pushes Buddha-nature to an extreme formulation: "This chapter rightly clarifies the doctrine that even evil persons possess Buddha-nature... Those who hold fast to the lesser vehicle and reject the greater are persons who slander the Vaipulya scriptures. And those who, attached to the lesser, do not believe in the greater—lacking faith in the great vehicle—are *icchantika*, that is, persons of utmost evil; the doctrine that they too possess Buddha-nature is no different from the *Nirvāṇa Sūtra*."[^38] Even an *icchantika* (the ultimate evil person) possesses Buddha-nature—this formulation pushes universal reverence to its logical limit: if even the *icchantika* possesses Buddha-nature, then "you shall all become Buddhas" admits no exception whatsoever. This provides the firmest possible ontological warrant for Sadāparibhūta's continuing to say "you shall become a Buddha" even to overweening members of the fourfold assembly.

Jizang's third contribution is the distinction between *covert* and *overt* exposition of the One Vehicle: "Sadāparibhūta's spreading of the scripture is of two kinds: first, covert; second, overt. The acts of reverence and praise constitute covert exposition of the One Vehicle; the later attainment of purified faculties and the wisdom and supernatural powers constitute overt exposition. The reason for covert first and overt later is that, in the age of the Latter Dharma, overweening persons are strong in evil and weak in good and cannot be addressed all at once; therefore at first he could only intimate the teaching obliquely."[^39] This distinction is decisive—the chapter itself contains two periods of teaching. The bowing period is *covert* exposition of the One Vehicle (intimating the One Vehicle by action); the post-deathbed period of widely expounding the Lotus is *overt* exposition (declaring the One Vehicle in language). One person, one teaching, one set of objects—first covert, then overt. This is a direct response to the question of *how to spread the One Vehicle in the age of Dharma decline*.

#### II.B.5 · Zhiyi's *Fahua xuanyi*: Direct-Cause Prediction and Prediction of Those Yet to Arouse the Mind

In the chapter on prediction in Zhiyi's *Fahua xuanyi* (T1716), Sadāparibhūta is positioned as the broadest form of "direct-cause prediction": "As for **direct-cause prediction, it is as in the Sadāparibhūta chapter**; contributory-cause prediction is as in the ten kinds of offering in the Dharma-Teacher chapter; revealing-cause prediction is as in the prediction of those of three roots. Direct-cause prediction is broad; contributory and revealing predictions are narrow."[^40] The three Buddha-nature causes (direct, contributory, revealing) correspond to three predictive modes. Sadāparibhūta corresponds to the broadest and most fundamental "direct-cause prediction"—issued directly upon the direct-cause Buddha-nature of sentient beings, without restriction to those who have aroused the mind, and without restriction to those of certain practical attainments.

More crucially: "The *Śūraṅgama-sūtra* speaks of four kinds of prediction; this scripture has them all: **prediction of those who have not yet aroused the mind, as in the Sadāparibhūta chapter**."[^41] *Prediction of those who have not yet aroused the mind*—this is the most distinctive class within the entire system of prediction: prediction issued to beings who have not yet given rise to the *bodhicitta*. Zhiyi's classification echoes Kuiji's lineage doctrine in §II.B.2 directly: "for those possessing the lineage, *if* they activate habituated nature, and arouse the mind to practice, Buddhahood is necessarily attained"[^31]—the lineage is already there, the habituated nature has not yet arisen, but prediction can be issued first, and through the prediction itself the activation of habituated nature can be triggered. This is the theoretical foundation of P09's §III.3 thesis on the operationalization of prediction.

## III. Central Argument: The Three Theses Unfolded

This section treats the paper's three central theses: (1) Thesis I—the second witness of dual-track confluence in the Sadāparibhūta chapter; (2) Thesis III—the dramatized completion of the Āgamic seed; (3) the operationalization of prediction—from declaration to method of practice.

### III.1 · Thesis I: The Second Witness of Dual-Track Confluence

S7-P08 established Thesis I—the first confluence of the Tathāgatagarbha-track and the Yogācāra-track in the *Lotus Sūtra*. The Devadatta wing and the Nāga-princess wing were borne respectively by the two tracks: the Tathāgatagarbha-track handled the directional reversal (the metaphysical warrant for an evil person attaining Buddhahood = the equality of dharmakāya); the Yogācāra-track handled the spatiotemporal compression (the pragmatic mechanism for the Nāga princess's instantaneous Buddhahood = the acceleration of seed-maturation). The two tracks first appear in unambiguous symmetry in the Devadatta chapter.

The Sadāparibhūta chapter supplies the second witness of Thesis I, and a more concentrated and clearer one. The decisive document is the convergence passage in Kuiji's *Fahua xuanzan* commentary cited in §II.B.2. This second confluence has three distinguishing features.

**First, the two tracks operate on a single sentence of scripture simultaneously.** In the dual-wing structure of P08, the two tracks corresponded to two distinct passages of the chapter (the Devadatta passage and the Nāga-princess passage). In P09, the two tracks act simultaneously upon **a single sentence**—"I deeply revere you and dare not slight you; you walk the bodhisattva path and shall attain Buddhahood." Kuiji parses the first half ("I deeply revere you and dare not slight you") as "reverencing the **saṃbhogakāya Tathāgatagarbha**," and the second half ("you walk the bodhisattva path and shall attain Buddhahood") as "for those possessing the **lineage**, if they activate **habituated nature** and arouse the mind to practice, Buddhahood is necessarily attained," then adds: "Furthermore, on the basis of the **dharmakāya Tathāgatagarbha**, all beings have my deep reverence." A single scriptural sentence carries both tracks simultaneously—this is not the parallelism of two tracks but their *simultaneous operation*.

**Second, the two tracks divide labor functionally without canceling one another.** A comparative table:

| Track | Linguistic Resources | Question Borne | Answer Supplied |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Tathāgatagarbha-track** | saṃbhogakāya Tathāgatagarbha; dharmakāya Tathāgatagarbha; the Buddha's inherent nature | Why may Sadāparibhūta say "you shall become a Buddha" to all sentient beings? | Because all sentient beings inherently possess the Tathāgatagarbha (*Śrīmālā* four-treasury, *Laṅkāvatāra* on adventitious dust, *Foxing lun* five-fault / five-virtue) |
| **Yogācāra-track** | *gotra*; *vāsanā*; perfuming; the ten bodhisattva grounds | Once the sentence is uttered, what happens to the listener? | Hearing-perfuming (*śruta-vāsanā*) arises, activating the uncontaminated seeds within the lineage; the seed is planted and awaits *vipāka*-maturation (*Cheng weishi lun* on inherent + newly-perfumed seeds; *Saṃdhinirmocana* on the *ādāna* torrent) |

The two tracks address different questions and supply different answers, but point to the same object (Sadāparibhūta's universal reverence practice). The Tathāgatagarbha-track answers *on what grounds*; the Yogācāra-track answers *what then occurs*. Without the Tathāgatagarbha-track, universal reverence would be challenged as "groundless effusion"; without the Yogācāra-track, it would be misread as "the magic of one sentence and one becomes a Buddha." Only when both tracks operate simultaneously can the Universal Reverence dharma-gate stand.

**Third, the pivot of confluence is *paratantra-svabhāva* (the dependent nature), and in this chapter that pivot is concretized as *śruta-vāsanā* (hearing-perfuming).** In P08, I identified *paratantra-svabhāva* as the common pivot of the two-track confluence—at once the dependent-arising manifestation of the Yogācāra-track and the conventional-truth display of the Tathāgatagarbha-track. In P09, this abstract pivot is concretized as a performable action: **hearing-perfuming**. *Cheng weishi lun* fascicle 2 states explicitly: "The arising of uncontaminated seeds is also by perfuming; this is called **hearing-perfuming**, perfumed up through hearing the orthodox Dharma flowing equally from the pure dharmadhātu, since this is the seed-nature of the supramundane mind."[^42] Sadāparibhūta's single sentence "you shall all become Buddhas" is, viewed from the Yogācāra-track, hearing-perfuming (planting an uncontaminated seed in the listener); viewed from the Tathāgatagarbha-track, it is the disclosure of inherent Buddha-nature (activating the pure mind beneath the adventitious dust). The two tracks converge on the concrete act of hearing-perfuming—no further argument is required at the abstract level of *paratantra-svabhāva*; a single concrete act of bowing and praising actualizes both tracks at once.

The conclusion: **if P08 demonstrated the existence of dual-track confluence, P09 demonstrates its normality.** Confluence is not the *Lotus Sūtra*'s ad hoc device for handling limit cases (the evil one, the female body); it is the standard structure by which the *Lotus Sūtra* handles any utterance of universal Buddhahood. P11 (the chapter on the Tathāgata's lifespan) will mobilize this same structure once more at the level of the *root teaching*—dharmakāya as ever-abiding (Tathāgatagarbha-track) and the phenomenological unfolding of the long-since-realized (Yogācāra-track). Thesis I will reach three witnesses by P11, forming one main axis of the S7 series.

### III.2 · Thesis III: The Dramatized Completion of Āgamic *Mettā*

Thesis III holds that the central teachings of the *Lotus Sūtra* are the dramatized completion of seeds already present in the Āgamas. The Āgamic seed corresponding to the Sadāparibhūta chapter is *mettā*. This section establishes not "resemblance" but three stronger relations of *structural identity*.

**Identity One: identity of temporal structure.** The Buddha says of himself, "for seven years I continually cultivated *mettā*, passing through seven kalpas of formation and dissolution"[^6]; Sadāparibhūta "passed in this way through many years, constantly reviled, never giving rise to anger, always saying 'you shall become a Buddha'"[^19]. Both share a temporal structure of **uninterrupted continuity across spans far exceeding ordinary time**. Seven years is not seven days; many years is not a few occasions; the temporal scale itself is the argument—loving-kindness / universal reverence is not a transient emotional response but a core practice able to bear the test of long duration.

**Identity Two: identity of object structure.** The standard Āgamic formulation of *mettā* is "practicing loving-kindness pervading all"[^9]; Sadāparibhūta's formulation is "everyone he saw... he bowed to and praised"[^5]. "Pervading all" and "everyone he saw" are logically equivalent—both are universal quantifications admitting no exception. In particular, Āgamic *mettā* requires no specific qualification of the object (does not require that the recipient be virtuous, fortunate, or karmically connected); Sadāparibhūta likewise places no qualification on the objects of his bowing (he even bowed to those about to revile and strike him).

**Identity Three: identity of externalization structure.** The *Ekottarāgama* fascicle 47—"those who practice the liberation of loving-kindness, **broadcasting its meaning, expounding it for others**"[^8]—and Sadāparibhūta's "even seeing the fourfold assembly **from afar, he would still go up to them and bow and praise**"[^10] correspond exactly in structure: both externalize the inner state of loving-kindness / reverence into a deliberate verbal act addressed to others. This is the strongest evidence for Thesis III—not merely conceptual resemblance but **literal identity of action structure**.

Read together, Sadāparibhūta's universal reverence practice can be precisely described as: **the externalization, concentration, and operationalization of Āgamic *mettā***. *Externalization*: from a mental dharma to body and speech. *Concentration*: from the broad pervasion of loving-kindness to the single sentence "I dare not slight you." *Operationalization*: from inner feeling to a concrete act that can be seen, recorded, and imitated.

This seed-line requires a Middle-Way balance: Thesis III must not be pushed to the extreme that "the *Lotus Sūtra* is merely the Āgamas writ large." The Sadāparibhūta chapter has something Āgamic *mettā* lacks: **the ontological substrate of prediction**. Āgamic *mettā* says "cultivating loving-kindness one is reborn in the Brahma heavens"—this is the register of karmic recompense; Sadāparibhūta says "you shall all become Buddhas"—this is the register of ontological assertion. The former depends on the karma-recompense system; the latter depends on the Tathāgatagarbha ontology. The leap from Āgama to *Lotus* is not mere expansion but the splicing of a new ontological layer onto the externalization structure of *mettā*—and that new ontological layer is precisely what Thesis I's dual-track confluence handles.

Thesis I and Thesis III are therefore complementary within P09: Thesis III explains where Sadāparibhūta's **practice structure** comes from (Āgamic *mettā*); Thesis I explains the grounds on which his **assertive content** stands (dual-track confluence). Without either, the Sadāparibhūta chapter becomes unintelligible.

### III.3 · The Operationalization of Prediction: From Declaration to Method of Practice

This is P09's most central contribution and the terminus of the entire S7 prediction sub-arc.

In the arguments of S7-P01 through P08, prediction was treated essentially as *declaration*—a verbal act issued by the Buddha toward a specific object, the content of which concerned the inherent possibility of future Buddhahood. S7-P08 had already clarified prediction from "prophecy" to "seed-statement," dissolving the literalist prophecy-misreading. Even so, the agent of prediction remained the Buddha and its form remained the sentence.

The Sadāparibhūta chapter completes the final inversion: **the agent of prediction is broadened to include any bodhisattva (indeed any practitioner taking universal reverence as his dharma-gate), and the form of prediction is elevated from a sentence to a practice mobilizing the three karmas of body, speech, and mind in unison**. The significance of this inversion can be approached from three angles.

**Angle one: the democratization of the agent.** Before the Sadāparibhūta chapter, the right to predict was essentially the Buddha's exclusive prerogative. Sadāparibhūta is an ordinary "bodhisattva-bhikṣu" who "did not specialize in reciting the scriptures,"[^10] possessing no supernatural powers and no special qualification. His sole credential is the contemplative insight that "the fourfold assembly possess the cause of Buddha-nature."[^30] This contemplative insight is something any practitioner with a correct understanding of Tathāgatagarbha ontology can establish. Therefore, in principle, *anyone* may perform prediction as Sadāparibhūta did—though most will not perform it with such purity and uninterruptedness.

**Angle two: the operationalization of form.** Zhiyi's "four-fold unity" reading (person / principle / practice / teaching) reaches its full meaning here: in this case, one person equals one principle, one principle equals one practice, one practice equals one teaching. "You shall all become Buddhas" is not merely a sentence on Sadāparibhūta's lips—**his entire existence is this sentence**. The bowing is this sentence; the praising is this sentence; the deep reverence is this sentence; deliberately approaching the assembly seen from afar is this sentence; fleeing to a safe distance and calling out aloud is also this sentence. Jizang's "covert exposition of the One Vehicle"[^39] sees through to this layer: in the bowing period, Sadāparibhūta's One-Vehicle teaching is transmitted not by language but by action itself. Language is merely one component of the action, not the totality.

**Angle three: prediction as practice itself.** This is the deepest layer of the inversion. In the framework of P08 and earlier, prediction was the Buddha's pedagogical act and practice was the sentient being's enactment—two acts belonging to two different agents. In the Sadāparibhūta chapter, **the act of prediction is itself the bodhisattva's practice**. Sadāparibhūta's bodhisattva path *is* the act of "predicting all sentient beings"—he has no other "real practice" behind this; bowing and praising are his entire practice. This corresponds to Zhiyi's "these three, joined with compassion, are the Vow-and-Ease practice"[^36]—the three karmas in unison × the mind of compassion = the Vow-and-Ease practice = the core practice of a bodhisattva.

The direct contemporary lesson of this inversion: **universal reverence is something to be *done*, not merely something to be *thought***. Many readers, encountering "all sentient beings possess Buddha-nature," treat it as a concept to be understood, agreed with, internalized. The Sadāparibhūta chapter points to another road: take it as a performable action. Walk toward another person; in your mind, bow to them deeply; silently think *"you shall become a Buddha."* That is universal reverence; that is prediction; that is one act of the bodhisattva. You do not need to have read all of Yogācāra first; you do not need to have established a perfect view first; you only need to do this action, and the doing itself is the practice.

⚠️ This operationalized path requires one careful contemporary qualification. Sadāparibhūta's "bowing" reproduced literally in contemporary society could cause distress to its objects—a stranger suddenly bowing to you and announcing "you shall become a Buddha" would unsettle most people rather than benefit them. The contemporary execution of the Universal Reverence dharma-gate **must preserve the integrity of the inner stance while making appropriate adjustments to outer form**. The body-karma need not be a literal bow; it can be reverence in one's gaze, respect in one's manner of address, the basic posture of refusing to objectify the other. The speech-karma need not be the literal phrase "you shall become a Buddha"; it can be any verbal act that does not demean, does not insult, and acknowledges the other's fundamental dignity. The mind-karma, however, must be preserved in full—the inner contemplation that "you possess Buddha-nature" cannot be discounted. This is the necessary adjustment in transposing the Sadāparibhūta dharma-gate from its ancient setting to the present.

## IV. Three Middle-Way Corrections

### ❌ IV.1 · Danger #7: The Magical Reading of "One Sentence and One Becomes a Buddha"

**The misreading**: Hearing Sadāparibhūta's single phrase "you shall become a Buddha" produces instantaneous Buddhahood, or uttering "you shall all become Buddhas" itself constitutes the completion of an act of Buddhahood. This misreading construes prediction as a magical incantation with immediate efficacy.

**Correction one: seeds ≠ manifest activity.** *Cheng weishi lun* fascicle 2 defines *bīja* (seed) as "that within the root consciousness which directly produces its own fruit, a distinct functional capacity"[^43]—seeds are *potentialities*, not *manifest activities*. The arising of uncontaminated seeds does indeed depend on "hearing-perfuming, perfumed up through hearing the orthodox Dharma flowing equally from the pure dharmadhātu,"[^42] but once arisen, the seed must still pass through a long *vipāka*-maturation period before manifesting. The *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra* fascicle 1 likens seeds to a torrent: "The *ādāna* consciousness is profound and subtle; all seeds flow like a torrent."[^44] The torrent is constant; the waves arise in their own time. Sadāparibhūta's "you shall become a Buddha" plants an uncontaminated seed in its object—it does not transform the object into a Buddha on the spot.

**Correction two: the scripture itself supplies the time-lag.** Layer five of the chapter (§II.A.2) states explicitly that the fourfold assembly who reviled Sadāparibhūta "because of slighting me with minds of anger, for two hundred koṭi kalpas constantly failed to meet a Buddha, hear the Dharma, or see the Saṅgha; for a thousand kalpas they suffered great agony in the Avīci hell. Having exhausted that retribution, they again encountered Bodhisattva Sadāparibhūta, who instructed them in *anuttarā samyaksaṃbodhi*."[^24] From the planting of the seed (being bowed to) to its manifestation (encountering Sadāparibhūta again and arousing the mind), the interval was two hundred koṭi kalpas plus a thousand kalpas in Avīci. The time-lag itself is scriptural evidence: every seed planted will manifest, but the manifestation can be vastly distant. The *Lotus Sūtra* itself never claims instantaneous Buddhahood by a single phrase.

**Correction three: Sadāparibhūta himself is not "one sentence and a Buddha."** Kuiji's mapping of Sadāparibhūta's path-stages—"the bowing period = the forty minds; hearing the Dharma and pure faculties = the four wholesome roots stage; the extension of lifespan and the attainment of great wholesome quiescence = gradual principle-contemplation; departing from the five fears and attaining the four fearlessnesses = the first ground; later, entering the eighth ground, departing from the cause of fear"[^32]—spans the path of provisions, the path of preparation, the first ground, and the eighth ground. Even Sadāparibhūta, the founder of the Universal Reverence dharma-gate, ascended step by step; the objects of his seed-planting cannot possibly skip these stages either.

**The Middle-Way balance**: dissolving "instantaneous Buddhahood" does not entail asserting "infinite deferral." The verse from *Ekottarāgama* fascicle 47—"swiftly attaining nirvāṇa, eternally reaching the unconditioned"[^9]—supplies the balance: the path of Buddhahood after seed-planting can be significantly accelerated, but acceleration is not abolition of the process. The actual efficacy of the Universal Reverence dharma-gate is this: **it furnishes a definite acceleration to the object's path, without abolishing the path itself**. The practical meaning: today, when you bow inwardly to a stranger, the act will not make them a Buddha tomorrow, but it does in truth deposit a real uncontaminated seed in their continuum.

### ❌ IV.2 · Danger #8: Sadāparibhūta Misread as Weakness / Passive Forbearance

**The misreading**: Sadāparibhūta is the figure of one who passively endures being beaten; the Universal Reverence dharma-gate is equivalent to non-resistance toward any evil; practitioners ought to stand still and accept blows as a display of forbearance. This misreading turns Sadāparibhūta into a paradigm of moral weakness.

**Correction one: the two active verbs of the scripture.** Layer three of the chapter (§II.A.2) attests two actions. The first is *"seeing the fourfold assembly from afar, he would still go up to them and bow and praise"*[^10]—an active *approach*. This is not the passive "bow to whomever I happen to meet"; it is the active "seeing people in the distance, walk over to bow to them." The second is *"fleeing to a safe distance, he would still call out aloud"*[^19]—an active *withdrawal*. When people raised sticks, tiles, and stones to strike him, he fled, and from a safe distance continued to declare. Read together: Sadāparibhūta's behavioral pattern is **actively to approach those of capacity, actively to withdraw from scenes of violence, and to continue declaring from a safe distance**. All three translations (T0262, T0263, T0264) preserve this dynamic, proving it the original narrative core.

**Correction two: the Āgamic two-track of "inward forbearance, outward withdrawal."** The *Ekottarāgama* "Sūtra to Mahā-Kauṣṭhila" in fascicle 43 explicitly juxtaposes two acts: "If others give rise to anger, we shall **train in forbearance**; if others harbor jealousy, we shall **withdraw**."[^13] Forbearance is inward (a mental posture); withdrawal is outward (an action); the two stand side by side without contradiction. The *Dīrghāgama* fascicle 9 lists "drawing near to good friends" as the first of the three constituents of attainment.[^14] This two-track structure proves: Buddhism never demanded that disciples unconditionally endure evil persons—inward forbearance and outward withdrawal are a normal practice combination. Sadāparibhūta's "fleeing to a safe distance" conforms exactly to this two-track structure—mentally he gives no rise to anger (forbearance track), in action he withdraws from violence (withdrawal track), and he does not abandon the proclamation of universal reverence.

**Correction three: Zhiyi's "three rules" reading.** "Non-disparagement and deep reverence is the seat of the Tathāgata; enduring beating and reviling is wearing the robe of the Tathāgata; with a mind of compassion to practice constantly without ceasing is the room of the Tathāgata."[^36] Zhiyi locates "enduring beating and reviling" as "wearing the robe of the Tathāgata"—the *robe of gentle forbearance* (柔和忍辱衣). The robe of gentle forbearance is the bodhisattva's inner adornment, not the outward act of "standing still and waiting to be struck." A bodhisattva wearing the robe of gentle forbearance may flee or advance; what matters is the unretreating gentleness of mind, not the immobility of the body. Zhiyi's framing aligns precisely with the scriptural "fleeing to a safe distance, he would still call out aloud."

**Correction four: the Āgamic *karuṇā-prajñā* in tandem.** Loving-kindness in the Āgamas is never blind, undiscriminating accommodation. The *Saṃyuktāgama* fascicle 46 records that Śakra "by the power of loving-kindness, with majestic strength subdued the army of asuras"[^11]; the *Madhyamāgama* "Sūtra to Velāma" requires that one cultivating loving-kindness must "contemplate all dharmas as impermanent, suffering, empty, and not-self."[^12] Loving-kindness operates jointly with majesty and wisdom. Sadāparibhūta's universal reverence inherits precisely this *karuṇā-prajñā in tandem* tradition—his universal reverence toward the fourfold assembly and his wise judgment of scenes of violence (flee when one must) are two faces of a single practice structure.

**Redefinition**: universal reverence is an **inner stance**, not an **outer constraint**. This distinction is the key to understanding the Sadāparibhūta dharma-gate. The content of universal reverence is "to bow inwardly and deeply to all sentient beings, deeply trusting that they possess Buddha-nature." Its outward expression must be adjusted to circumstance—it can be a face-to-face bow, it can be a verbal declaration, or it can be "fleeing to a safe distance and calling out aloud." But **the inner stance itself is non-negotiable**. While Sadāparibhūta was running, his mind was still revering the fourfold assembly—this is the core of the dharma-gate; where his body was at the moment is a secondary question.

### ❌ IV.3 · Danger #2: The Cheapening of Universal Buddhahood (Cross-Series Callback to S5 Diamond Sūtra)

**The misreading**: If all sentient beings possess Buddha-nature and shall all become Buddhas, then Buddhahood becomes cheap—since everyone gets there anyway, why practice seriously? Or, conversely: to say "you shall become a Buddha" to anyone is to say it to no one, since the phrase contains no specific content.

**The correction: the four-marks-erased structure of the Diamond Sūtra.** The *Vajracchedikā*'s treatment of compassion supplies the standard answer: "Thus liberating immeasurable, innumerable, boundless sentient beings, in truth there is no sentient being liberated. Why? Subhūti, if a bodhisattva has the mark of self, the mark of person, the mark of sentient being, the mark of life-span, then he is not a bodhisattva."[^45] True salvific action must rest on the empty floor of "in truth there is no sentient being liberated."

This structure applies directly to Sadāparibhūta's universal reverence. When Sadāparibhūta says "you shall all become Buddhas," **the force of the sentence rests not on some measurable potential in the object but on the speaker's own state of selflessness (anātman)**. If Sadāparibhūta's mind harbored an objectifying structure of "I am revering him; he is a sentient being; he shall become a Buddha," he would have fallen into the four marks the Diamond Sūtra dismantles (mark of self, of person, of sentient being, of life-span), and universal reverence would immediately become a self-satisfying performance—"see how compassionate I am, how willing to revere every sentient being." This is not universal reverence but the costume of self-conceit.

True universal reverence requires that, **while uttering "you shall become a Buddha," Sadāparibhūta have neither a revering self nor a sentient being to be revered**. This is structurally identical to the Diamond Sūtra's "no sentient being is in truth liberated"[^46]—both take the absence of marks as the prerequisite of compassion. The reason Sadāparibhūta could persist for many years, never retreating in the face of revilement and violence, is not that he possessed enormous moral willpower; it is that his universal reverence rested from the start on the floor of selflessness. A selfless person has no "offended self," and therefore no need to retreat.

This callback also establishes a concrete argumentative line between P09 and the S5 series: **Sadāparibhūta is the dramatized demonstration of the Diamond Sūtra's "give rise to a mind that does not abide anywhere."** The Diamond Sūtra dismantles the four marks at the level of language; Sadāparibhūta displays at the level of action what action without the four marks looks like. The two scriptures are mutually inside and outside—the Diamond Sūtra supplies the linguistic apparatus for breaking grasping; the *Lotus Sūtra* supplies the personal exemplar without grasping.

The callback also explains why "you shall all become Buddhas" does not degenerate into a cheap slogan. The root of cheapening is objectification—treating "Buddhahood" as something that can be issued, received, compared. Once objectified, it can be devalued. The four-marks-erased structure of the Diamond Sūtra severs the possibility of objectification: there is no predicting self, no predicted sentient being, only the markless act of prediction itself. A markless act cannot be cheap, because from the outset it was never within the dimension of "value calculation."

## V. Cross-Series Callbacks and Closing Remarks

This section offers three cross-series callbacks, locating P09 within the architecture of the Pointing at the Moon project as a whole.

**First, the callback to S5-P01 through P06 (Diamond Sūtra series)** (developed in §IV.3). Sadāparibhūta is the dramatized human exemplar of the Diamond Sūtra's "four marks erased." The significance of this callback: S5 and S7 are exact opposites in pedagogical posture (S5 *cuts*, 切; S7 *gathers*, 攝), but on the ultimate view they are non-dual. Sadāparibhūta's universal reverence can stand precisely because S5 has already cut away every possible "object" that universal reverence could grasp at. Without S5's cutting, S7's gathering would degenerate into grasping; with S5's cutting, S7's gathering can preserve marklessness.

**Second, the callback to S7-P08 (Devadatta and the Nāga princess).** S7-P08 is the first witness of Thesis I's dual-track confluence; S7-P09 is the second. The difference: P08's confluence was triggered by a *limit case* (an evil person, a female body), and a reader might suppose that confluence is merely an ad hoc device for handling exceptional circumstances; P09's confluence appears in the most "ordinary" of bodhisattva chapters—Kuiji, commenting on an ordinary monk's ordinary practice, automatically and densely deploys both tracks. This proves that dual-track confluence is the *Lotus Sūtra*'s standard structure for handling any utterance of universal Buddhahood, not a special device. P08 + P09 read together is what allows Thesis I to truly stand.

**Third, the callback to S7-P10 (the chapter on Avalokiteśvara's Universal Gate)—forward-laying.** The Sadāparibhūta chapter and the Avalokiteśvara chapter constitute the two faces of the concept of *universal gate*: **the universal gate as inner stance** vs. **the universal gate as outward responsive manifestation**. Sadāparibhūta is one person taking universal reverence as his own inner practice—the agent of the universal gate is the practitioner; the direction is outward, from the practitioner. Avalokiteśvara is dharmadhātu compassion responding unconditionally to all sentient beings—the agent is the dharmadhātu; the direction is from the dharmadhātu inward toward sentient beings. Read together, the spectrum of *universal gate* is complete: from "I universally revere all sentient beings" to "the dharmadhātu universally responds to all sentient beings." The latter requires the former as preparation, because only when a practitioner has first established the complete inner stance of universal reverence within himself can he correctly understand that the compassionate structure of Avalokiteśvara's universal gate is not an "external divine power" but the dharmadhātu-level completion of the very universal reverence he has been practicing.

**Closing remark: the Universal Reverence dharma-gate as one application of the Pointing at the Moon manifesto.** The fundamental posture of this project is "the true mark of all dharmas is just thus, originally so" (諸法實相,本來如是). This posture takes different expressive forms in different series: in the S2 *Heart Sūtra* series as "contemplating one's own freedom" (觀自在); in the S5 *Diamond Sūtra* series as "non-abiding" (無住); in the S6 *Śūraṅgama* series as "discerning the true from the false" (辨真妄); in the S7 *Lotus* series as "gathering" (攝). The Sadāparibhūta chapter offers one of the purest cases of "gathering": a single person, with his entire existence, says to all sentient beings, *"You are originally a Buddha."* The reason this sentence is not arrogant is that it states only what reality is originally so; the reason it is not cheap is that it rests on selflessness as its floor; the reason it is not illusory is that its form is the concrete three karmas of body, speech, and mind. These three taken together constitute one concrete grounding of "the true mark of all dharmas, originally so."

## VI. Conclusion

The narrative of the Sadāparibhūta chapter looks simple: one monk, one sentence, one lifetime. Yet this simple structure carries the most central inversion of the S7 series—**prediction is elevated from "the speech-act of a Buddha" to "a dharma-gate that sentient beings can operate daily."** The three layers of this inversion have been argued separately above: the democratization of the agent (§III.3), the operationalization of form (§III.3), and the universalization of the object (§II.A.2).

The contemporary significance of this inversion deserves to be stated directly in closing. The Universal Reverence dharma-gate does not require its reader first to master Yogācāra, first to understand Tathāgatagarbha, first to establish a perfect view. It requires only one action: walk toward any other person, bow to them deeply in your mind, silently think *"you shall become a Buddha."* If this action can be performed every day, performed for a lifetime, then the entire path of Sadāparibhūta is contained in this action. The bodhisattva path of the three karmas in unison is not in some distant place—it is in the next thought you have when you next encounter another human being.

The Sadāparibhūta chapter is not the endpoint. The next chapter, the Avalokiteśvara Universal Gate chapter, addresses the question: once the inner stance of universal reverence has been established, how does it unfold outward into the dharmadhātu-level compassionate manifestation? That is the work of S7-P10. Sadāparibhūta lays the path for Avalokiteśvara, just as Avalokiteśvara lays the path for the chapter on the Tathāgata's lifespan (S7-P11)—inner stance → outward manifestation → dharmakāya as ever-abiding. This is the progressive line from P09 to P11 in the S7 series.

A final return to the three questions of the Introduction. **What is the ontological warrant of universal reverence?** The Tathāgatagarbha ontological substrate of *Śrīmālā* / *Laṅkāvatāra* / *Foxing lun*, joined with Vasubandhu's reading of Sadāparibhūta's sentence as "demonstrating that all sentient beings possess Buddha-nature." **How does the single sentence "you shall all become Buddhas" not degenerate into cheapness?** The selfless floor of the Diamond Sūtra's four-marks-erased structure, joined with the Yogācāra hearing-perfuming seed doctrine's precise account of the mechanism of efficacy. **Is Sadāparibhūta passive endurance or active practice?** The two active verbs of the scripture—"seeing the fourfold assembly from afar, he would still go up to them" and "fleeing to a safe distance, he would still call out aloud"—have already answered. The three answers taken together yield the complete structure of the Sadāparibhūta dharma-gate: **a bodhisattva practice grounded in the Tathāgatagarbha as substance, in selflessness as posture, in the three karmas in unison as form, and in long-time persistence as rhythm.**

This dharma-gate, anyone, beginning today, can perform.
