## Abstract

The Diamond Sūtra is not a philosophical treatise awaiting comprehension. It is a mirror. A mirror teaches you nothing, does nothing to you — it simply reflects you as you are. You either see it or you don't. There is no middle ground, no way to fake it. This paper argues that the Diamond Sūtra's overall structure exhibits the defining characteristic of a Chan (Zen) kōan — mirror reflection rather than knowledge transmission — and takes the Sixth Patriarch Huineng's instantaneous awakening upon hearing "give rise to a mind that abides nowhere" as its central case study, demonstrating how the same mirror reveals different people. On this basis, the paper presents the Pointing-at-the-Moon project's methodological declaration: reverse-engineering the pedagogy — asking not "what does the sūtra say?" but "what does the sūtra do to its reader?" It further argues that each paper in the project is itself a jífēi structure (§III Core Argument = X; §V Middle Way Correction = is not X; the reader's post-reading cognition = is called X), and that the project's eight-series arc is a macro-scale Diamond Sūtra. The Middle Way Correction section frankly acknowledges: a paper that uses conceptual analysis on an anti-conceptual text is itself exactly the kind of behavior the Diamond Sūtra is designed to illuminate — but that is no reason not to write it. The raft analogy teaches: tools may be used, then set down.

## I. Introduction: The Mirror Does Not Speak

### 1.1 The Problem

The scholarly literature on the Diamond Sūtra is vast, yet virtually all of it answers the same question: what does the Diamond Sūtra say?

Line-by-line commentators ask "what is the meaning of each passage?" Structural analysts ask "what is the logical architecture of the whole text?" Philologists ask "what are the historical origins of this sūtra?" These are all valuable inquiries, but they share a presupposition: the Diamond Sūtra is a text containing content, and the researcher's task is to extract that content.

This paper asks a different question: **what does the Diamond Sūtra do to its reader?**

Or more precisely: the Diamond Sūtra does nothing. It is simply a mirror. But what the reader sees in the mirror — their own confusion, grasping, the illusion of understanding, the genuine release — that is the Diamond Sūtra's teaching.

### 1.2 Relationship to S5-P01

The companion paper S5-P01 established the doctrinal framework: the jífēi formula maps onto trisvabhāva. That was analysis — a map.

This paper addresses the pedagogical effect: what does this structure do to the reader? This is experience — the road.

S5-P01 gives you a map; S5-P02 tells you: the map is not the road. But without the map, you cannot even understand the sentence "the map is not the road" — which is why P01 had to be written first. And once you have read P01, P02 must immediately tell you: set the map down.

The relationship between the two papers mirrors the first and second layers of vajra (emptiness/structure and illumination/mirror), and also mirrors the relationship between Kuiji's mapping (conceptual analysis) and the mirror mapping (direct illumination).

## II. Scriptural Foundations

### 2.1 The Sixth Patriarch and the Diamond Sūtra: One Mirror, Different People

The most famous Diamond Sūtra teaching event in Chan history was not a lecture. It was a mirror test.

The Fifth Patriarch Hongren asked his assembly to compose a verse to demonstrate their realization. The head monk Shenxiu wrote:

> "The body is a bodhi tree, the mind a bright mirror's stand. Diligently polish it always — let no dust alight upon it."[^1]

[^1]: *The Platform Sūtra of the Sixth Patriarch* (六祖大師法寶壇經), Chapter 1 (行由品), T48, No. 2008. All Platform Sūtra citations in this section are from this chapter unless otherwise noted.

The Fifth Patriarch's assessment was pointed: "You have arrived at the gate but have not yet entered." Shenxiu's verse was not wrong — "diligently polish it always" represents authentic practice, a stage most practitioners must pass through. But from the mirror's perspective, Shenxiu saw a mirror that needed wiping. He was still wrestling with the dust on the glass.

Huineng, upon hearing Shenxiu's verse, asked someone to write his own:

> "Bodhi is fundamentally without a tree; the bright mirror is also not a stand. Originally there is not a single thing — where could dust alight?"

Same mirror. Same prompt. Two entirely different people revealed. Shenxiu saw cultivation — sustained effort, gradual purification. Huineng saw original nature — not that the mirror had been cleaned, but that the mirror was always clean; there was never any dust to begin with.

The Fifth Patriarch publicly declared "this also has not yet seen the nature" to protect Huineng, but the next day he secretly visited the threshing room and struck the mortar three times with his staff as a signal. Huineng understood. At the third watch, he entered the patriarch's chamber. The Fifth Patriarch did not explain the Diamond Sūtra to him — he read the Diamond Sūtra to him. Upon reaching "give rise to a mind that abides nowhere" (應無所住而生其心):

> Huineng was instantly and completely awakened — all the ten thousand dharmas do not depart from one's own nature. He said to the Patriarch: "How unexpected that self-nature is originally pure! How unexpected that self-nature is originally neither born nor destroyed! How unexpected that self-nature is originally complete in itself! How unexpected that self-nature is originally without agitation! How unexpected that self-nature can give rise to the ten thousand dharmas!"

The Fifth Patriarch's response cuts to the heart of mirror teaching:

> "If one does not recognize one's original mind, studying the dharma is of no benefit. If one recognizes one's own original mind, sees one's own original nature — that is what is called a great person, teacher of gods and humans, a Buddha."

"If one does not recognize one's original mind, studying the dharma is of no benefit" — if you have not seen your own original face, all the studying in the world merely pastes labels onto the mirror's surface. The Diamond Sūtra's role in this scene is not that of a textbook but of a verification instrument. The Fifth Patriarch was not teaching Huineng about emptiness — Huineng's practice had already brought him to the threshold. The Diamond Sūtra was simply a mirror, confirming that he had arrived.

### 2.2 "Give Rise to a Mind That Abides Nowhere" — Why This Line?

Why did Huineng awaken at "give rise to a mind that abides nowhere" and not at some other passage?

The answer does not lie in this line being more profound than others — the Diamond Sūtra is saturated with jífēi structures; every line could serve as a kōan. The answer lies in Huineng himself. His practice had already brought him to that point; any sufficiently clear mirror could have completed the final confirmation. "Give rise to a mind that abides nowhere" happened to be the line the Fifth Patriarch reached on that particular night.

The same sentence, heard by Shenxiu, would have registered as a practice instruction: "I should abide nowhere" — and then he would have set about cultivating "non-abiding," just as he cultivated "diligent polishing." Heard by Huineng, it registered as original nature revealed — not that one "should" abide nowhere, but that one's own nature has always abided nowhere.

This is the complete definition of the kōan mirror: the same mirror reveals different people. The difference is not in the mirror but in whoever stands before it.

### 2.3 Diamond Sūtra Echoes in the Platform Sūtra

After his awakening, the Diamond Sūtra's teaching became the substrate of Huineng's entire pedagogical system. In the "Prajñā" chapter of the Platform Sūtra, Huineng directly cites the Diamond Sūtra to elucidate "formlessness":

> "Good friends, what is called formlessness? Formlessness means: within forms, to be free of forms. ... All characteristics are illusory. If one sees that all characteristics are not characteristics, then one sees the Tathāgata."[^2]

[^2]: *The Platform Sūtra of the Sixth Patriarch*, Prajñā Chapter (般若品), T48, No. 2008.

"Within forms, to be free of forms" (於相而離相) — four characters that precisely articulate the lived operation of the jífēi formula: not the absence of forms, but freedom from bondage to forms while in their midst. This echoes S5-P01 §3.3's Pointing-at-the-Moon mapping — "is called X" is not the absence of X but the faithful knowing of X as a name while fully engaged with it.

Huineng also cites the Diamond Sūtra to explain "non-abiding":

> "Good friends, in thought after thought, do not dwell on previous states. If past thought, present thought, and future thought follow each other in unbroken succession ... the past mind cannot be grasped, the present mind cannot be grasped, the future mind cannot be grasped."

The three minds that cannot be grasped — this is the jífēi formula deployed across the temporal dimension. Past mind, present mind, future mind: each is an object of "is not." The mind you thought you could seize is simply not there.

Huineng further cites "If you seek me through form, if you seek me through sound, that person walks a deviant path and cannot see the Tathāgata" to dismantle the attachment of seeking the Buddha through appearances. In each of these citations, Huineng is not "explaining" the Diamond Sūtra — he is using it as a mirror to reflect back his listeners' attachments.

### 2.4 The Diamond Sūtra's Own Mirror Structure

The Diamond Sūtra's textual architecture is itself a mirror device.

§17 is the pivot of the entire text: Subhūti re-asks, "If good men and good women give rise to the mind of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, how should they abide? How should they subdue their minds?" — nearly identical to the question posed in §2.[^3] Same question, deeper answer.

[^3]: *Vajracchedikā Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra* §2 and §17, T08, No. 235.

This folding structure is itself a mirror: the first half reflects the second, the second reflects the first. In §10, the reader believes they have grasped "the adornment of buddha-lands; it is not adornment; it is called adornment." After §17, they encounter "the Tathāgata speaks of the foremost pāramitā; it is not the foremost pāramitā; it is called the foremost pāramitā" — the object has escalated from "buddha-lands" to "pāramitā itself." The "emptiness" you thought you understood in the first half gets mirrored again in the second: your understanding itself becomes the object of "is not."

S5-P01 §3.6 already analyzed the functional difference between the first and second halves in terms of the Yogācāra two-obstruction theory (kleśāvaraṇa vs. jñeyāvaraṇa). From the kōan mirror perspective, this structure produces an even more direct pedagogical effect: the first half is the first mirror — revealing your attachment to external objects; the second half is the second mirror — revealing your attachment to your own understanding. The second mirror is always harder to face than the first.

### 2.5 Historical Approaches in Review

The interpretive history of the Diamond Sūtra, viewed through the kōan mirror lens, falls into four paths:

Line-by-line commentary — Kuiji, Wŏnch'ŭk, Zongmi, and other masters match doctrinal analysis to every passage, comprehensive and meticulous. But this approach carries a risk: the reader receives knowledge while the mirror surface gets ground down. Once the kōan has been translated into answers, the reader no longer needs to see themselves — they only need to remember the answers.

Structural analysis — Asaṅga's seven-topic framework, Vasubandhu's twenty-seven doubts: the Diamond Sūtra decomposed into logical units. The framework is clear, but the Diamond Sūtra's recursive self-negation (it is not prajñāpāramitā — even the tool gets negated) cannot be contained by linear logic.

Direct Chan pointing — the Sixth Patriarch, Huangbo, Deshan: no explanation, just deployment. Shouts, blows, burning scriptures, a single phrase that cuts off thinking. Maximally effective for those with sufficient capacity, but nothing is transmitted in writing — unhelpful for beginners.

Western scholarship — Conze, Schopen, Harrison: philology, linguistics, historical criticism. Rigorous and precise, but untouched by pedagogical effect — the Diamond Sūtra treated as a text to study, not a mirror to face.

This paper's position: none of the above, and all of the above. The Pointing-at-the-Moon methodology is not a fifth path — it asks a different question. The first four paths all ask "what does the Diamond Sūtra say?" Pointing at the Moon asks "what does the Diamond Sūtra do to its reader?"

## III. Core Argument: The Kōan Mirror

### 3.1 Mirror, Not Trap

A clarification must come first: to call the Diamond Sūtra a kōan is not to call it a cognitive trap. "Trap" implies a designer's intent — someone laying a snare, waiting for you to fall in. A mirror is entirely different.

A mirror has three characteristics:

**Passivity.** A mirror does nothing. It does not teach you, attack you, or guide you. You stand before it; it reflects you. You walk away; it is empty. The Diamond Sūtra, as mirror, does not "give" you emptiness — it simply reflects the distance between you and emptiness.

**Impossibility of pretense.** You can fake awakening before other people; you cannot fake it before a mirror. When you read "give rise to a mind that abides nowhere," whether you truly abide nowhere or are merely performing non-abiding is perfectly clear in the mirror. This is why the Fifth Patriarch chose the Diamond Sūtra to test realization, rather than a debate: debates can be won on knowledge alone; mirrors cannot.

**Binary nature.** You either see it or you don't. There is no seventy-percent awakening, no "roughly understanding emptiness." The mirror is binary — illuminated or not. This sounds harsh, but it is in fact the most compassionate form of teaching: it does not allow you to settle into a false "almost got it" — and that false comfort is the real obstacle.

### 3.2 Every Response Is a Self-Portrait

Any response to the Diamond Sūtra — including "I understand emptiness" — is a self-portrait handed to the mirror.

The beginner asks "what does this mean?" — the mirror reveals: you are on the conceptual level, still treating the Diamond Sūtra as a knowledge object to be comprehended. The scholar asks "what is the structure?" — the mirror reveals: you are on the analytical level, deeper than mere concepts, but still approaching with analytical tools something that transcends analysis. The practitioner is silent — the mirror reveals: perhaps arrived, perhaps performing arrival. The mirror does not judge for you, but you yourself know.

There is no "correct response." The Diamond Sūtra's teaching lies not in guiding you toward the right answer but in letting you see the answer you gave. Every response is a reflection — the difference is not between right and wrong, but in how honestly you face your own reflection.

### 3.3 The Ultimate Distance Between Knowledge and Realization

The "knowledge vs. realization" distinction marked throughout S1–S4 is pushed to its limit in the Diamond Sūtra.

You can flawlessly explain "all characteristics are illusory" — from a Mādhyamaka perspective, a Yogācāra perspective, a philosophy-of-language perspective. All of that is knowledge. Whether you can, in the very moment of being insulted, not generate a reactive characteristic; whether you can, in the very moment of losing someone you love, not give rise to self-grasping — that is realization.

The Diamond Sūtra as mirror reflects precisely the distance between knowledge and realization. You read "it is not prajñāpāramitā" and feel you have understood — good. Now see whether, in your daily life, you can "is not" your understanding of prajñā itself.

The distance itself is not a bad thing. Seeing the distance is where practice begins. Not seeing the distance is the real problem — it means you have mistaken knowledge for realization, the map for the road, the finger for the moon.

### 3.4 Three Barriers and the Two Mappings

S5-P01 discovered that Kuiji (T1702) and Pointing at the Moon offer two different trisvabhāva mappings of the jífēi formula. Kuiji maps "speaks of → parikalpita, is not → paratantra, is called → pariniṣpanna"; Pointing at the Moon maps "X → paratantra, is not → negation of parikalpita, is called → pariniṣpanna."[^4] Why the difference?

[^4]: See Shi Huijing, "Diamond Prajñā: The Jífēi Tripartite Formula as Trisvabhāva Mapping," Pointing at the Moon S5-P01, §3.2–§3.4.

Because they speak to people at different levels.

The Chan tradition marks three stages of practice with the framework of Three Barriers (三關): the Initial Barrier (breaking through initial delusions), the Deeper Barrier (profound illumination), and the Final Barrier (ultimate liberation). Borrowing this framework (without reifying it):

**The Initial Barrier** corresponds to Kuiji's mapping. At this stage, the practitioner needs conceptual analysis — being told "speaks of = parikalpita" so they can identify what they are clinging to. Kuiji's mapping is a precise pedagogical tool: it proceeds from the listener's cognitive process, step by step dismantling the transformation from parikalpita through paratantra to pariniṣpanna. This is the path of "thinking it through."

**The Deeper Barrier** corresponds to mirror illumination. At this stage, the practitioner no longer needs conceptual analysis. They stand before the mirror and see directly — they do not need anyone to tell them "you are clinging"; they can see it themselves. The Pointing-at-the-Moon mapping proceeds from the object's ontological status not because it is "more correct," but because it no longer starts from the listener's confusion — it presupposes that you have already transcended the conceptual level and can see directly.

**The Final Barrier** is the Diamond Sūtra itself. At this stage, even the mirror is set down. You do not need the "jífēi formula = trisvabhāva" framework, because you are illumination itself. At this level, the Diamond Sūtra is no longer a text — it is the direct manifestation of prajñā.

The key insight: Kuiji's mapping is not "wrong"; the Pointing-at-the-Moon mapping is not "more right." They are images cast by the same mirror at different distances. The Initial Barrier needs Kuiji; the Deeper Barrier needs the mirror; the Final Barrier needs nothing — you are the mirror.

Here, too, the jífēi structure appears: Kuiji's mapping is X; Pointing at the Moon says "is not" Kuiji's mapping — not negating Kuiji, but transcending the conceptual level; both are valid teachings: "is called" X.

## IV. Core Argument: The Pointing-at-the-Moon Method

### 4.1 Methodological Declaration: Reverse-Engineering the Pedagogy

What Pointing at the Moon does is not commentary, not structural analysis, not the shout-and-strike of Chan masters, not philology. It is reverse-engineering the pedagogy.

The core question is not "what does the Diamond Sūtra say?" but "what does the Diamond Sūtra do to its reader?"

This way of framing the question is itself a methodological stance: it presupposes that a Buddhist sūtra is not merely a vessel carrying doctrine but a precision pedagogical device — whose structure, sequence, repetitions, variants, and omissions each carry a teaching function. S5-P01 already demonstrated this method at the doctrinal level (variant classification of the jífēi formula and functional differences between the first and second halves); the present paper extends the same method to the level of pedagogical effect.

"What does it do to the reader" — this question has a subtlety: its answer varies by person. The same line, "give rise to a mind that abides nowhere," did entirely different things to Shenxiu and to Huineng. This is not a flaw in the methodology but its central insight: the sūtra is a mirror, and the image it reflects depends on who stands before it. What the researcher can do is identify the mirror's structure — curvature, angle, material — but not predict what any given person will see.

### 4.2 Each Paper Is Itself a Jífēi Structure

Every paper in the Pointing-at-the-Moon project replicates the jífēi structure at the level of composition:

§III "Core Argument" = X — establishing a clear doctrinal framework. The reader acquires a map here: trisvabhāva mapping, kōan mirror, pedagogical reverse-engineering. This framework is useful, is real — it is not false.

§V "Middle Way Correction" = is not X — severing the reader's reification of the framework. "The trisvabhāva mapping is not strict equivalence." "Do not use the framework to domesticate the Diamond Sūtra." "A paper that analyzes kōans is itself a form of attachment." Each correction is an "is not" applied to §III.

The reader's cognitive state after finishing the paper = is called X — the framework remains, but the reader's relationship to it has changed. You still know that the jífēi formula can be mapped onto trisvabhāva, but you no longer treat this knowledge as "the answer to emptiness." The map is still in your hands, but you know it is not the road.

This is why the Middle Way Correction is not an appendix, not a formality, but the paper's core teaching moment — it is the mirror turned toward the reader. §III gives you a mirror aimed at the Diamond Sūtra; §V turns the same mirror around, aimed at you and the understanding you just acquired.

### 4.3 The Eight-Series Arc as Macro-Scale Diamond Sūtra

The overall architecture of the Pointing-at-the-Moon eight-series project replicates the Diamond Sūtra's jífēi structure at the macro level:

S1–S3 = X (construction). S1 on generating bodhicitta, S2 on the Heart Sūtra, S3 on the *Treatise on the Doors of the Hundred Dharmas* — three series building a complete Buddhist cognitive toolkit. Here the reader acquires seed theory, emptiness, Yogācāra classification; all conceptual frameworks are carefully erected.

S5 = is not X (the cut). The Diamond Sūtra series tells you: everything S1–S3 gave you is paratantra. A hundred dharmas? Is not a hundred dharmas. Prajñāpāramitā? Is not prajñāpāramitā. Even "is not" itself — the very tool — must be set down.

S6–S8 = is called X (re-establishment). The Śūraṅgama Sūtra for verification, the Lotus Sūtra for recollection, the Avataṃsaka Sūtra for manifestation — the toolkit has not vanished, but your relationship to it has fundamentally changed. You still use the Hundred Dharmas classification, but you no longer mistake it for reality. You still practice, but you no longer treat practice as "acquiring" something.

S4 is the questioning — standing between S3 and S5, the tension point between construction and the cut.

If the reader stops after S1–S3, what they have is knowledge — precise, useful, but potentially generating attachment. Only after reading S5 does the loosening begin. Only upon reaching S8 might the moon be seen. The entire eight-series arc is a single large-scale "is not X, is called X" — first pointing you toward the moon, then telling you the finger is not the moon, and then... what comes after is not in the text.

### 4.4 Not Crossing the River for the Reader

Pointing at the Moon provides the raft, not the far shore.

This is a deliberate pedagogical design choice. The papers articulate doctrinal structures clearly — how the trisvabhāva mapping operates, what variants the jífēi formula takes, what functional differences exist between the first and second halves — but they deliberately refrain from saying "therefore, here is what you should do."

The Diamond Sūtra's power as a kōan lies in its being a mirror — the reader sees themselves in the reflection, rather than being handed an answer. If a Pointing-at-the-Moon paper concluded with "here are three practice steps," it would be swapping the mirror for an instruction manual — the reader would gain directions but lose the opportunity to see themselves.

### 4.5 The Honest Boundary of Knowledge and Realization

Every paper in the Pointing-at-the-Moon project operates within the domain of knowledge. What academic papers can do is limited to conceptual analysis, structural identification, and doctrinal comparison — all of which are knowledge.

But every paper must also mark the existence of realization and then acknowledge that it cannot reach there.

S5-P01's Middle Way Correction states: "The trisvabhāva mapping is a tool, not the moon." This paper's Middle Way Correction states: "A paper that analyzes kōans is itself a form of attachment." These acknowledgments are not modesty — they are core components of the kōan method. A finger that points at the moon must simultaneously acknowledge that it is not the moon. A finger that does not acknowledge it is a finger will trap the reader's gaze upon itself, forever preventing them from seeing the moon.

## V. Cross-Disciplinary Dialogue

### 5.1 Cognitive Science: Mirror Cognition and Metacognition ⚠️ Heuristic Analogy

Metacognition in cognitive psychology — awareness of one's own thinking processes — shares a structural resemblance with the kōan mirror.[^5] Metacognitive training requires the learner not only to perform a cognitive task but simultaneously to monitor how they perform it. This parallels the functional dimension of the jífēi formula: you do not merely read "the adornment of buddha-lands"; you simultaneously observe what arises in your mind upon reading those words.

[^5]: Flavell, John H. "Metacognition and Cognitive Monitoring: A New Area of Cognitive-Developmental Inquiry." *American Psychologist* 34.10 (1979): 906–911.

However, the kōan mirror goes further than metacognition. Metacognition still operates at the conceptual level — it uses thinking to observe thinking; the observer itself is never questioned. The terminus of the kōan mirror is the de-reification of the observer itself — not merely awareness of your thinking, but illumination that "the one who is aware" is also an object of "is not." Metacognition is a mirror reflecting your behavior; the kōan mirror ultimately reflects the mirror itself.

### 5.2 Instructional Design: Desirable Difficulties ⚠️ Heuristic Analogy

Robert Bjork's theory of "desirable difficulties" demonstrates that deliberately increasing the difficulty of the learning process paradoxically enhances long-term retention and deep understanding.[^6] Reducing fluency, increasing spacing, interleaving practice — these strategies prevent learners from relying on surface cues, forcing deeper processing.

[^6]: Bjork, Robert A. "Memory and Metamemory Considerations in the Training of Human Beings." *Metacognition: Knowing about Knowing*, ed. Janet Metcalfe and Arthur P. Shimamura, MIT Press, 1994, pp. 185–205.

The Diamond Sūtra's jífēi formula is a form of desirable difficulty. It does not let you easily "learn" — just when you think you have grasped the formula in §10, §13 escalates the object (from buddha-lands to prajñā itself); just when you think you have grasped the full form, §13 gives you an abbreviated form omitting "is called." Every time you establish a stable understanding, the text disrupts it. The mirror does not give you answers; it simply reflects you — and being reflected is itself the most desirable difficulty.

The limits of this analogy: Bjork's framework remains within the domain of conceptual learning — desirable difficulties aim at better remembering and understanding concepts. The kōan mirror's aim transcends conceptual learning itself — it is not about better understanding emptiness, but about illuminating the very act of "understanding emptiness" and its inherent limitations.

### 5.3 Systems Theory: Logical Levels ⚠️ Heuristic Analogy

Gregory Bateson's theory of logical levels of learning distinguishes multiple orders:[^7] zero learning (fixed response), Learning I (behavioral change), Learning II (learning how to learn), and Learning III (transformation of the entire learning framework).

[^7]: Bateson, Gregory. *Steps to an Ecology of Mind*. University of Chicago Press, 1972.

Line-by-line commentary is Learning I — you change your understanding of the Diamond Sūtra. Structural analysis is Learning II — you change how you understand the Diamond Sūtra. The kōan mirror is Learning III — you change your relationship to understanding itself. In Learning III, it is not that you learn something new, but that the learner is transformed.

The jífēi formula at the §17 pivot executes precisely the jump from Learning II to Learning III: the first half teaches you to dissolve attachment to external objects (I → II); the second half teaches you to dissolve attachment to dissolving itself (II → III).

## VI. Middle Way Correction

### 6.1 ❌ A Paper Analyzing Kōans Is Itself a Form of Attachment

This paper must squarely acknowledge its meta-level contradiction: an academic paper using conceptual analysis on an anti-conceptual text — this is precisely the kind of behavior the Diamond Sūtra is designed to illuminate. You are using analytical tools to illuminate "the limits of analytical tools"; you are using words to point out "the boundary of words."

But this is no reason not to write it. The raft analogy of §6 teaches:[^8] the dharma is like a raft — used for crossing the river, then set down after crossing. This paper is a raft — the reader uses it to cross (to understand how the kōan mirror operates), then sets it down.

[^8]: *Vajracchedikā Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra* §6: "You should know that my teaching of the dharma is like the analogy of a raft — even the dharma should be abandoned, how much more so what is not dharma." T08, No. 235.

If you refuse to analyze kōans because "analyzing kōans is attachment," that is equivalent to refusing to cross the river because "the raft must also be set down." You must first cross before you have the right to talk about setting down.

### 6.2 ❌ "Kōan" Does Not Mean "Irrational"

To call the Diamond Sūtra a kōan mirror is not to say it defies reason or resists analysis. Quite the contrary — S5-P01 has already demonstrated that the jífēi formula possesses a precise doctrinal structure analyzable through the trisvabhāva framework with full scholarly rigor.

The structure of a mirror is precise — optical principles, curvature calculations, material selection — but this precision serves a purpose that transcends technique: letting you see yourself. The Diamond Sūtra's doctrinal structure is precise, but its precision serves a purpose that transcends doctrine: letting you see your relationship to doctrine.

Chan's "not relying on words and letters" is not anti-intellectualism — it is pointing out the boundary of words. Within that boundary, words are extraordinarily useful (every word you are currently reading is evidence). Beyond it, something else is needed — not more words, not the absence of words, but the kind of direct illumination exemplified by the Sixth Patriarch.

### 6.3 ❌ A Mirror Cannot Replace a Living Teacher

A mirror reflects but does not guide. A good teacher (kalyāṇamitra / 善知識) both reflects and guides.

The Diamond Sūtra as mirror can show you where you are, but it cannot tell you how to take the next step. You see the distance between knowledge and realization — and then what? The mirror does not answer this question.

This is why, in the Chan kōan tradition, the master is indispensable. The kōan is the mirror, but the mirror requires a living person to adjust the angle — which mirror, at what moment, aimed at which student. That is the teacher's work. The Fifth Patriarch read the Diamond Sūtra to Huineng at the third watch, privately, rather than lecturing publicly in the dharma hall — the choice of timing, audience, and method was the teacher's judgment, not something the mirror can do on its own.

Academic papers are even less capable of replacing a living teacher. What Pointing at the Moon can provide is a precision mirror (doctrinal analysis) and a clear user's guide (methodological declaration), but the road of practice must be walked by the reader, and the teacher must be sought by the reader.

### 6.4 ❌ The Three Barriers Are Not a Practice KPI

The Initial Barrier / Deeper Barrier / Final Barrier framework in §3.4 is an explanatory tool, not a scorecard for self-assessment.

"I have passed the Initial Barrier, so I no longer need Kuiji's conceptual analysis" — this very judgment is a fresh product of parikalpita. You are using practice stages to reinforce self-identity — which is precisely the "mark of self" (ātma-lakṣaṇa) that the Diamond Sūtra's first half is designed to dismantle.

Using the Three Barriers to locate your own practice stage is as dangerous as using the merit comparisons to measure your own practice score — both turn a pedagogical device into a scoreboard (see S5-P05).

The mirror does not tell you which barrier you are at. Where you are is perfectly clear in the mirror — but that clarity is for you to see, not for you to compare with others. If you need to ask yourself which barrier you are at, you are still at the first.

## VII. Conclusion

### 7.1 Core Findings

The argument of this paper can be summarized in five points.

First, the Diamond Sūtra is a mirror — you either see it or you don't. The defining characteristic of a kōan is mirror reflection, not cognitive entrapment. Passivity, impossibility of pretense, and binary nature — these three characteristics together constitute the Diamond Sūtra's unique function as a pedagogical device.

Second, the Sixth Patriarch's instantaneous awakening upon hearing "give rise to a mind that abides nowhere" occurred not because this line possesses magical power, but because his practice had already brought him to the threshold. Shenxiu's and Huineng's different responses to the same mirror — "diligently polish it always" versus "originally there is not a single thing" — constitute the most complete historical case study of the kōan mirror: the same mirror reveals different people.

Third, the Pointing-at-the-Moon project's methodology is reverse-engineering the pedagogy: asking not "what does the sūtra say?" but "what does the sūtra do to its reader?" The answer to this question varies by person — just as the image in a mirror varies by person — but the mirror's structure can be identified.

Fourth, each paper in the project is itself a jífēi structure: §III Core Argument = X; §V Middle Way Correction = is not X; the reader's post-reading cognition = is called X. The eight-series macro-architecture — S1–S3 construction, S5 the cut, S6–S8 re-establishment — is a macro-scale Diamond Sūtra.

Fifth, this paper is a mirror. What you see after reading it is not the meaning of the Diamond Sūtra. It is yourself.

### 7.2 Open Questions

The unfolding of the jífēi formula across specific sūtra objects — from "all characteristics are illusory" (S5-P03) to "all conditioned phenomena are like dreams" (S5-P09): what different practice challenges does each object reflect in the mirror?

The reward paradox of the merit comparisons — using a reward mechanism to dismantle reward mechanisms: is the merit comparison itself also a large-scale kōan? See S5-P05.

AI as the kōan mirror of the digital age — AI holds all knowledge but possesses no realization, constituting a perfect mirror: the way you ask reveals your level. This topic will be developed in the S4 series.
