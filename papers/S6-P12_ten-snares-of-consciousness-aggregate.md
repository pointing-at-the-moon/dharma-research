## Abstract

This paper continues from S6-P11 (*The Ten Wrong Views of the Formation Aggregate: The Mirror-Student and the Net of Brahmajāla*), entering the final station of the demon-detection mirror — the diagnostic of the ten *graspings* (執) of the consciousness aggregate (*vijñāna-skandha* 識陰). The central thesis of this paper is that the ten graspings of *vijñāna-skandha* are not ten new errors, but **ten topological forms of "the last grasping at a most subtle object."** At the transitional position described by the *Śūraṅgama* as "having exhausted [the realm of] formations as empty, having returned to consciousness at its source, with arising-and-passing already extinguished, but having not yet rounded out the subtle marvel of cessation" (窮諸行空,於識還元,已滅生滅,而於寂滅精妙未圓), any "establishment of a superior view" (立勝解) freezes this unfinished transitional state and mistakes it for the ultimate. The first eight graspings mistake some subtle object disclosed in *vijñāna-skandha* — its causal source, its unified body, its pervasive knowing — for an absolute, and thereby fall into eight types of non-Buddhist (*tīrthika*) seed-natures. The final two are stranger and more dangerous: they are not non-Buddhist at all but **prematurely capped Two Vehicles** (二乘) — practitioners who have already approached the gate of *yuántōng* 圓通 (perfect penetration) but who, "taking what is not yet complete to be complete," seal off the apex of *bodhi* on top of an authentic but partial attainment.

The Ming-dynasty commentator Jiāoguāng Zhēnjiàn 交光真鑒, in his *Lengyan jing zhengmai shu* 楞嚴經正脈疏, gives the position a precise structural name: "The formation aggregate is the front pillar; the consciousness aggregate is the rear pillar; and the locus where the ten graspings arise is precisely between these two — which is why all ten graspings are placed under this same heading."[^1] This image of "between the two pillars" (兩楹之間) is the hermeneutic key to *vijñāna-skandha*. The ten graspings are not ten different errors that occur *inside* the consciousness aggregate; they are ten topological forms of "establishing a superior view *while standing on the threshold* between the formation aggregate (already broken) and the consciousness aggregate (not yet rounded out)." Each of the ten passages in the sūtra opens with the same formulaic phrase, marking the same threshold position; only the second half — *what* the practitioner takes as ultimate — varies along ten lines of divergence.

The Song-dynasty commentator Zǐxuán 子璿, in his *Shoulengyan yishu zhujing* 首楞嚴義疏注經, supplies the most penetrating diagnosis of the consciousness aggregate's intrinsic nature: although the *vijñāna-skandha* presents itself as "lucent and unmoving" (湛不搖), as "apparently unified and apparently permanent" (似一似常, in Jiāoguāng's phrase), it is in fact "**entirely the shadow of consciousness, not yet [true] cessation**" (此之境界全是識影,未為寂滅).[^2] The deepest danger of *vijñāna-skandha* is not that it *obviously fails* to be the ultimate — it is that it is *so refined that it looks exactly like the ultimate*. The "as if true" character of the *vijñāna* essence is the most subtle mask the demon-detection mirror is asked to expose.

The structural contribution of this paper lies in distinguishing the first eight graspings from the last two as **two topologically distinct types of error**. The first eight are "graspings at the intrinsic nature of a subtle object"; the last two are "premature ceilings on the height of attainment." The former corresponds to non-Buddhist seed-natures, the latter to Two-Vehicle seed-natures — and the question of why two Vehicles legitimately recognized within the Buddhist tradition appear in a list of *māras* is the *Śūraṅgama's* deepest verdict on "taking what is not yet complete to be complete." That verdict, as we will see, is also the direct bridge from the S6 *Śūraṅgama* series to the S7 *Lotus Sūtra* series, whose central theme — "opening the provisional to disclose the real, the One Buddha Vehicle" (開權顯實一佛乘) — addresses precisely the same structural error from the doctrinal rather than the phenomenological side.

The paper proceeds in four movements. First, it establishes the topological foundation of "between the two pillars" through Jiāoguāng's three core images: the threshold position, "the cock's second crowing," and "apparent unity and apparent permanence." Second, it grounds the analysis in the canonical sources: the *Śūraṅgama*'s tenth fascicle, Āgama definitions of *vijñāna*, the early sūtra account of *adhimāna* (overweening pride), and the foundational treatments by Zǐxuán and Jiāoguāng. Third, it develops the original framework of this paper — **the two-segment topology of the ten graspings** — through seven layers of argument, culminating in the demonstration that the first eight graspings exhaust the structural possibilities of "grasping a subtle object as ultimate" while the last two exhaust the structural possibilities of "ceiling at a real but partial attainment." Fourth, it follows the bridge through *vijñāna-skandha*'s last two graspings into the *Lotus Sūtra*'s parable of the conjured city (*ṛddhi-nagara*), thereby establishing the formal interface between the *Śūraṅgama* and *Lotus* series.

## §1 Introduction

### 1.1 Between the Two Pillars: Jiāoguāng's Topological Naming

Around 1610, the Ming-dynasty Buddhist commentator Jiāoguāng Zhēnjiàn was writing his *Zhengmai shu* on the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* at the Longquan monastery in Jingzhou. Reaching the section on the consciousness aggregate in fascicle ten, he encountered a structurally peculiar feature of the source text: each of the ten passages on the *vijñāna-skandha* graspings begins with the *same* opening formula — "Ānanda, you should know that this good son, having exhausted [the realm of] formations as empty, having returned to consciousness at its source, with arising-and-passing already extinguished, but having not yet rounded out the subtle marvel of cessation…"[^3] Ten passages share an identical opening; only the second half — what the practitioner does *next* — diverges into ten paths.

Jiāoguāng's response was a structural naming that would become canonical for all subsequent commentaries:

> "The formation aggregate is the front pillar; the consciousness aggregate is the rear pillar; and the locus where the ten graspings arise is precisely between these two — which is why all ten graspings are placed under this same heading."

The classical Chinese term *yíng* 楹 refers to the columns flanking a hall's entryway. The traditional ritual of entering a formal hall takes "between the two pillars" (兩楹之間) as a structurally crucial position — neither outside the hall nor inside it, but on the very threshold of crossing. Jiāoguāng's image is precise: **the position where the ten graspings of *vijñāna-skandha* arise is not "inside the consciousness aggregate" but at the threshold position between "the formation aggregate already broken" and "the consciousness aggregate not yet rounded out."** The front pillar (formation aggregate) has already been crossed; the back pillar (consciousness aggregate) has not yet been crossed; the practitioner stands on the threshold between them.

This position is metaphysically delicate. On the one hand, the formation aggregate has been broken — the eight characters "having exhausted formations as empty, with arising-and-passing already extinguished" mark that the ten *māras* of P11 (the sixty-two views of the *Brahmajāla*) have been entirely circumvented; the practitioner no longer attempts to make any totalizing pronouncement about the intrinsic nature of the streaming awareness. On the other hand, "having not yet rounded out the subtle marvel of cessation" — true cessation has not yet been completed; what the practitioner perceives as "subtle stillness" is in fact the imaging of the consciousness aggregate, not yet the unconditioned. Zǐxuán's gloss makes this position's danger explicit:

> "Although the formation aggregate is exhausted and arising-and-passing already extinguished, returning to consciousness and tracing its source, [the practitioner] has not yet returned to cessation. This is why [the sūtra] says 'the subtle marvel is not yet rounded out' — because consciousness itself has not yet been broken; it is precisely a *subtle* arising-and-passing… This entire experiential field is the shadow of consciousness, not yet [true] cessation."[^4]

"**Entirely the shadow of consciousness, not yet cessation**" — these eight characters are the most precise diagnostic of the *vijñāna-skandha* position. *Vijñāna-skandha* looks very much like cessation: "lucent and unmoving" (湛不搖), "apparently unified and apparently permanent" (似一似常, in Jiāoguāng's phrase), "the six sense-faculties empty and still" (六根虛靜), pervaded by the awareness of all twelve classes of beings — every one of these phenomenological signs is *very nearly identical* with the descriptions of authentic cessation found in the same sūtras. But it is still the consciousness aggregate, still the imaging of the eighth consciousness (*ālaya-vijñāna*), still subtle arising-and-passing rather than non-arising-and-passing. **The deepest danger of the *vijñāna-skandha* position is not that it obviously fails to be the ultimate, but that it is so refined that it appears to be exactly the ultimate.**

The image of "between the two pillars" simultaneously discloses why the ten graspings are structurally exhaustive. If the practitioner stands on the threshold between two pillars, the only structural mistakes available are: misjudging the front pillar he has just crossed (the *constancy/non-constancy* and *attached-craving/non-craving* graspings), misjudging the back pillar he has not yet crossed (the *cause/caused* and *capable/incapable* graspings), misjudging the threshold position itself (the *returning/no-returning*, *generating/non-generating*, *knowing/non-knowing*, and *real/non-real* graspings), or simply stopping at the threshold and refusing to step forward (definite-nature *śrāvaka* and definite-nature *pratyekabuddha*). The ten graspings are not arbitrary — they are the structurally complete enumeration of "establishing a superior view" at this topological position.

### 1.2 Central Thesis: The Ten Graspings as Topological Forms of "Last Grasping"

From P09 (feeling aggregate) through P10 (perception aggregate) through P11 (formation aggregate), the progressive refinement of the fifty *skandha-māras* follows a clear arc:

- **Feeling-aggregate stage** (P09): the demon's form is the **second arrow** — the first arrow is the bare sensation; the second is the reactive grasping at that sensation. The corresponding antidote is "non-clinging" (不取著) to feeling.
- **Perception-aggregate stage** (P10): the demon's form is the **mirroring other** — flying spirits attaching themselves to mediums (飛精附人), using external possessing entities (rough demons, vexing demons, even *Iśvara*-class deities) as vehicles for "ascending the seat and expounding the teaching" to deceive the practitioner. The antidote is "to seek the great teacher" (求大師), the genuinely realized *kalyāṇamitra*.
- **Formation-aggregate stage** (P11): the demon's form is the **mirror-student** — observer and observed are now identical, and the reflexive structure causes external referents to fail. The antidote is "to refrain from taking [appearances as] holy" (不作聖解), to make no totalizing pronouncement on the intrinsic nature of the field of awareness.
- **Consciousness-aggregate stage** (P12, the present paper): the demon's form is **last grasping** — the final act of "establishing a superior view" upon a most subtle object. The antidote is *not establishing a superior view* (不立勝解) for the first eight, and, for the last two, *turning the mind toward the Great* (迴心向大) — no longer treating an authentic but partial attainment as the apex.

The specific structure of "the demon" varies at each stage, but the underlying refinement curve is highly consistent: the object becomes ever more subtle, the act ever more interior, and the danger ever closer to authentic attainment. From "grasping at sensation" through "longing for an external mark of validation" through "totalizing the nature of the streaming field," we arrive finally at "the last grasping at a subtle object" — the entire structure of the fifty *skandha-māras* is precisely this curve of refinement.

The unique feature of the *vijñāna-skandha* stage is that **it is the endpoint of this curve, but it is also the most dangerous station**. As endpoint: beyond *vijñāna-skandha* lies the exhaustion of the five aggregates altogether — what the *Śūraṅgama* calls "rounding out *bodhi*, returning to no-attainment" (圓滿菩提,歸無所得). If *vijñāna-skandha* is crossed, the practitioner immediately enters the position of the first abode (初住) of the bodhisattva path with full *yuántōng* realization (so Jiāoguāng), from which the leap to the equivalent-enlightenment stage (等覺) becomes possible. As most dangerous: precisely *because* it is the endpoint, precisely because the "subtle marvel not yet rounded out" experiential field is so close to authentic cessation, **any "establishment of a superior view" at this position will mistake this nearly-complete state for completion itself, thereby losing the final step**.

The sūtra's own description of this terminal position is luminous:

> "At the heaven of *nirvāṇa*, [the practitioner] is about to realize great brightness — like the cock crowing for the second time, gazing eastward and already seeing essential color [in the dawn sky]. The six sense-faculties are empty and still, no longer galloping outward; inwardly and outwardly lucent, [the practitioner] enters where there is nothing to enter."[^5]

Jiāoguāng unfolds the image of "the cock's second crowing" with rare precision:

> "The heaven of *nirvāṇa*-nature is covered by the five aggregates — dark as a long night. When the first three aggregates are exhausted, it is like the cock's first crowing: a glimmer of dawn, but two aggregates still cloud the sky, and essential color has not yet emerged. When the formation aggregate is exhausted, it is like the cock's second crowing: only one aggregate remains, and so [the practitioner] is *about* to realize great brightness."[^6]

The first three aggregates exhausted is the cock's first crowing; the formation aggregate exhausted is the cock's second crowing — the *vijñāna-skandha* position is the period when "the day is about to dawn but has not yet dawned." The east is colored, but the sky is not yet fully bright. **This is what "the subtle marvel is not yet rounded out" means** — the light has come, but the rounded fullness of the light has not yet been completed. To "establish a superior view" at this position is to declare "it is dawn" before the dawn is finished — the loss is not the dawn (which will come in any case), but the *correct cognition* of the dawn (which can only stabilize once the dawning is actually complete).

The central thesis of this paper is then: **the ten graspings of *vijñāna-skandha* are ten topological forms of "establishing a superior view between the two pillars." Each grasping is the practitioner's verdict, at the threshold position of "subtle marvel not yet rounded out," that some subtle object "is the ultimate." The first eight graspings mistake this object for the ultimate cause, ultimate body, or ultimate knowing; the last two graspings mistake the practitioner's current attainment-altitude for the ultimate fruit-position.** The structural exhaustiveness of the ten graspings parallels that of the formation-aggregate stage's ten *māras* (P11) — the latter exhaust the possibilities of "totalizing the intrinsic nature of streaming awareness," the former exhaust the possibilities of "last grasping at a subtle object."

### 1.3 Why the Last Two Graspings Are the Bridge from S6 to S7: Mahāyāna's Deepest Verdict on the Two Vehicles

The first eight graspings of *vijñāna-skandha* correspond to identifiable non-Buddhist (*tīrthika*) traditions: the *cause/caused* grasping corresponds to the *prakṛti* (root substance) of the Sāṃkhya school; the *capable/incapable* grasping corresponds to the *Maheśvara* cosmology; the *constancy/non-constancy* grasping corresponds to the *Īśvara*-creator schools; the *knowing/non-knowing* grasping corresponds to the pan-cognitivist *Vaṭa-śaṇika* school; the *generating/non-generating* grasping corresponds to the Brahminical fire-and-water rituals of the Kāśyapa lineages; the *returning/no-returning* grasping corresponds to the *asaṃjñi-sattva* (perception-less) heaven schools; the *attached-craving/non-craving* grasping corresponds to the *Asita* school of seekers of long life; and the *real/non-real* grasping corresponds to the *Ṭakkirāja* (sense-pleasure-king) demonic school. The structural correspondence between these eight and the eight non-Buddhist schools mirrors the parallel between the formation-aggregate stage's ten *māras* and the *Brahmajāla*'s sixty-two views (P11 §3.3) — it is "the structural enumeration of non-Buddhist views as it appears within meditative phenomenology."

But the final two graspings (the ninth and tenth) correspond to something entirely different:

- **Ninth grasping: definite-nature *śrāvaka*** → corresponds to "monks of no-learning (*aśrutavān*) afflicted by overweening pride" (*adhimāna*). *Śrāvaka* is the *arhat* — one of the Buddhist tradition's own *correct vehicles*, not a non-Buddhist position.
- **Tenth grasping: definite-nature *pratyekabuddha*** → corresponds to "those of solitary lineage who do not turn back the mind." *Pratyekabuddha* is also one of Buddhism's own *correct vehicles*.

**Two of the proper fruits within Buddhism — the *arhat* fruit and the *pratyekabuddha* fruit — are listed in the *vijñāna-skandha* stage as *māras*.** This structural anomaly demands an explanation. Why would Buddhism's own attainment-fruits appear as "demons" within Buddhism's own meditation map?

Jiāoguāng's response to precisely this objection is the most precise verdict in the entire fifty-*skandha-māras* discussion:

> "Question: *Śrāvaka* and *pratyekabuddha* are the proper vehicles of the inner teaching, named as 'world-transcending lesser sages' — why are they here listed in the count of demons? Answer: 'Demon' (*māra*) is the designation **'that which destroys the right'**. In the great *samādhi* of this sūtra, that which advances *yuántōng* and approaches *nirvāṇa* is benefit; that which violates *yuántōng* and turns away from *nirvāṇa* is loss. And the Two Vehicles, [in their definite-nature form], precisely violate and turn away — branching off, blunting and stagnating, equally injurious to the right [path]. **How should they not be called demons?** However, qualified by 'definite-nature,' [the verdict] applies only to those who do not turn back the mind. For those who can turn back, this verdict does not fall upon them."[^7]

Jiāoguāng's argument proceeds in three steps:

*First*, the definition of "demon" — "the designation 'that which destroys the right' (*māra* < *māraṇa*, killing/destroying)." A demon is not necessarily an external monstrous figure; it is anything whatsoever that obstructs the right path, regardless of what costume it wears. Anything that obstructs *yuántōng* and *nirvāṇa* is a demon by the *Śūraṅgama*'s functional definition.

*Second*, why the Two Vehicles are listed among demons — "branching off, blunting and stagnating, equally injurious." The Two Vehicles' problem is not that *their teachings are wrong* (the teachings themselves are spoken by the Buddha) but that *they stop at a particular position and refuse to continue forward*. The direction of their stopping is wrong (turning away from the apex of *nirvāṇa*), and so even though their stopping-position looks like a holy position ("world-transcending lesser sage"), it constitutes an obstruction to the practitioner's full realization of *yuántōng*. To call that which obstructs the right path "a demon" is the *Śūraṅgama*'s technical definition.

*Third*, the qualifying restriction — "definite-nature." Not all *śrāvakas* and *pratyekabuddhas* are listed as demons; only those of "definite-nature" who "do not turn back the mind." The phrase "definite-nature" precisely means "not turning the mind toward the Great." Practitioners who can turn the mind toward Mahāyāna, even if they have temporarily rested at the *arhat* fruit, are not classified as demons by the *Śūraṅgama*. Zǐxuán's gloss is even more generous: "'Definite-nature' simply means that during one period of seeking quiescence the disposition does not change — but in the duration of *kalpas* they will eventually turn back to the higher vehicle."[^8] In other words, even practitioners labeled "definite-nature *śrāvaka*" will, in the long timescale of *kalpas*, eventually turn the mind. "Definite-nature" describes a *stage*, not a permanent ontological judgment.

These three steps together yield Mahāyāna's deepest verdict on the Two Vehicles: **the fruits of the Two Vehicles, *as a destination*, are erroneous (the structural error of "taking what is not yet complete to be complete"); but *as a stage*, they are legitimate; and the eventual turning of the mind toward the Great is the final realization of that legitimacy.** This verdict is structurally identical to the central teaching of the *Lotus Sūtra*'s *upāya-kauśalya* (*fāngbiàn pǐn* 方便品): the Lotus's verdict on the Two Vehicles is not "you are wrong" but "you have not yet reached the ultimate; the ultimate is one only — the Buddha Vehicle."

**This is precisely the bridge from S6 to S7.** The S6 series on the *Śūraṅgama* takes the fifty *skandha-māras* as its central axis; in its final paper on *vijñāna-skandha*, the discussion encounters the structure of the "definite-nature Two Vehicles" and immediately interfaces with the *Lotus*'s "opening the provisional to disclose the real, the One Buddha Vehicle" (S7 series). The final section of this paper (§6.2) develops this bridge in detail.

## §2 Canonical Sources

### 2.1 The *Śūraṅgama*'s Tenth Fascicle: "About to Realize Great Brightness" and "Not Yet Rounded Out"

The *Śūraṅgama* opens its treatment of *vijñāna-skandha* with a passage of remarkable phenomenological density:

> "Ānanda! When this good son cultivating *samādhi* has exhausted the formation aggregate, the subtle disturbing motion of the worldly nature, the common ground of life-mechanism, suddenly tears apart; the deep pulse of *pudgala*-karma-recompense, the threading and binding [of the mind] to the cycle of arising — its responsive linkage is severed. At the heaven of *nirvāṇa*, [the practitioner] is about to realize great brightness — like the cock crowing for the second time, gazing eastward and already seeing essential color. The six sense-faculties are empty and still, no longer galloping outward; inwardly and outwardly lucent, [the practitioner] enters where there is nothing to enter, deeply reaching the original ground of life-rebirth for the twelve classes of beings throughout the ten directions… This is named the realm of the consciousness aggregate (*vijñāna-skandha-viṣaya*). If, within the assembled bonds already attained as one, the six gates are smelted clear and their union and separation perfected, hearing, seeing, and the rest of the senses pierce through to one another; the ten directions of the world together with body-and-mind are like *vaiḍūrya*, inwardly and outwardly perfectly clear — this is named the exhaustion of the consciousness aggregate, and this person can transcend the turbidity of life-itself. The root of all this, examined, is *罔象虛無* — the inverted thought of phantom forms and emptiness — taken as its origin."[^9]

The density of this passage requires unpacking layer by layer:

- **"Having exhausted the formation aggregate… the responsive linkage severed"**: the marking of formation-aggregate exhaustion. The formation aggregate is "the deep pulse by which sentient beings repay karma" — the subtle channel of *saṃsāric* continuity — and at its exhaustion this channel "has its responsive linkage severed." Zǐxuán glosses: "When the formation aggregate is exhausted, the deep pulse is broken; thus segmentary birth-and-death, with cause lost and fruit dissipated, no longer issues in further birth."[^10]
- **"At the heaven of *nirvāṇa*, about to realize great brightness — like the cock's second crowing"**: the topological naming of the *vijñāna-skandha* position. *Nirvāṇa* is like the sky; the five aggregates are like night; the exhaustion of the first three aggregates is like the cock's first crowing; the exhaustion of the formation aggregate is like the cock's second crowing — the day is not yet bright, but the east is colored.
- **"The six sense-faculties empty and still… entering where there is nothing to enter"**: the entry-mark of the *vijñāna-skandha* experiential field. The six sense-faculties no longer gallop outward; inwardly and outwardly the field is lucent; the practitioner enters "a place of no-entering" — which marks both the dissolving of the six gates back into consciousness and *vijñāna-skandha*'s own "apparent unity and apparent permanence."
- **"Deeply reaching the original ground of life-rebirth for the twelve classes of beings"**: the practitioner *sees* what Zǐxuán identifies as the *ālaya-vijñāna* itself — the eighth consciousness in its role as the substrate of the *gati*-realm continua.
- **"This is named the realm of the consciousness aggregate"**: the formal identification of the *vijñāna-skandha-viṣaya*.
- **"… inwardly and outwardly perfectly clear like *vaiḍūrya*"**: the marking of *vijñāna-skandha* exhaustion. The opening and closing of the six gates is fully smelted; body and mind become like *vaiḍūrya* (lapis lazuli or beryl) crystal, inwardly and outwardly transparent — this is the experiential mark of the consciousness aggregate having been crossed.
- **"*罔象虛無顛倒妄想* taken as its origin"**: the final diagnosis of *vijñāna-skandha*'s nature. *Wǎngxiàng* (罔象) means "having form yet as if without form"; *xūwú* (虛無) means "appearing to exist yet not truly existing"; *diāndǎo wàngxiǎng* (顛倒妄想) means "taking the apparently-true as the truly-true." Together these three terms identify *vijñāna-skandha* as **the most subtle of all the deluded thinkings**.

Note the internal contrast within this passage: the *realm* of *vijñāna-skandha* is "essential color not yet sunk, the hidden mystery beginning to show"; the *exhaustion* of *vijñāna-skandha* is "like *vaiḍūrya*, inwardly and outwardly perfectly clear." The transition from "hidden mystery" (the realm) to "perfectly clear" (the exhaustion) is the entire task of the *vijñāna-skandha* stage. **And the entire problem of the ten graspings is that they mistake the "essential color not yet sunk" of the realm for the "inwardly and outwardly perfectly clear" of the exhaustion** — the freezing of "subtle marvel not yet rounded out" into "establishing a superior view."

### 2.2 The Structure of the Ten Graspings: Common Premise and Ten Diverging Paths

The ten graspings of *vijñāna-skandha* exhibit a peculiar structural feature, unique within the entire fifty-*skandha-māras* discussion: **every one of the ten passages opens with the same formula**.

> "…this good son, having exhausted [the realm of] formations as empty, with arising-and-passing already extinguished, but having not yet rounded out the subtle marvel of cessation…"

(The first grasping additionally includes "having returned to consciousness at its source"; the remaining nine compress this to the standard form above.)

Compare this with the other aggregate sections:

- **Form aggregate** (P08): each of the ten objective states begins independently, with no shared premise.
- **Feeling aggregate** (P09): each of the ten *māras* opens independently.
- **Perception aggregate** (P10): the openings are slightly more uniform — each grasping has some inner motivation of "longing-and-seeking" — but the syntactic premise varies.
- **Formation aggregate** (P11): the openings vary (e.g., "in the round origin generating reckoning…," "in the round constancy generating reckoning…," "in the position-divisions generating reckoning…"); the inner structure of the sixty-two views determines a corresponding diversity of premise.
- **Consciousness aggregate** (P12): this is the *only* section in which all ten passages share an identical opening syntax.

This structural feature is not stylistic accident — it precisely reflects the topology of "between the two pillars." **All ten graspings occur at the same position — the position of "having exhausted formations as empty, having extinguished arising-and-passing, but having not yet rounded out the subtle marvel of cessation."** The first eight characters mark "formation aggregate broken"; the middle four characters mark "consciousness aggregate emerging"; the final eight characters mark "consciousness aggregate not yet exhausted." The ten graspings are not "ten different errors at ten different locations"; they are "ten diverging paths at one and the same location."

Zǐxuán's commentary makes the point with striking concision: "Although the formation aggregate is exhausted and arising-and-passing already extinguished, returning to consciousness and tracing its source, [the practitioner] has not yet returned to cessation; this is why [the sūtra] says 'the subtle marvel is not yet rounded out' — **because consciousness itself has not been broken; it is precisely a *subtle* arising-and-passing**."[^11] The phrase "precisely a subtle arising-and-passing" is the root of all the ten graspings: *vijñāna-skandha* is itself "subtle arising-and-passing"; it is not "non-arising-and-passing." Any verdict that takes it for "non-arising-and-passing" is "establishing a superior view" — and this manufactures, at this single threshold position, one of the ten graspings.

The ten graspings, with their corresponding non-Buddhist or Two-Vehicle classifications, can be tabulated as follows[^12]:

| # | Grasping | What is taken as ultimate | Corresponding tradition | Resulting seed-nature |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Cause/caused (因所因) | The "round origin" (圓元) as a true permanent cause | *Kapila*'s *prakṛti* (Sāṃkhya) | Non-Buddhist seed |
| 2 | Capable/incapable (能非能) | The "round origin" as one's own self-body, generating all beings | *Maheśvara* | Self-pervading-the-round seed of the great-pride heaven |
| 3 | Constancy/non-constancy (常非常) | The flow-source of body-mind as a true permanent body | *Īśvara* | Inverted-roundness seed |
| 4 | Knowing/non-knowing (知無知) | The pervasive knowing as inherent to all things, even grass and trees | *Vaṭa-Śaṇika* (pan-knowing) | Inverted-knowing seed |
| 5 | Generating/non-generating (生無生) | The collected dust-elements as the eternal causal agent of creation | *Kāśyapa* fire-and-water Brahmins | Inverted-transformation seed |
| 6 | Returning/no-returning (歸無歸) | The "permanent extinction-support" as the place of return | *Asaṃjñi-sattva* heaven (*śūnyatā*) | Annihilationist seed |
| 7 | Attached-craving/non-craving (貪非貪) | The "round constant solidification of body" as eternal abiding | *Asita* longevity-seekers | Falsely-extended-life seed |
| 8 | Real/non-real (真無真) | The mutual penetration of life as license for indulgence | *Ṭakkirāja* | Heavenly-demon seed |
| 9 | Definite-nature *śrāvaka* | "Already at cessation, ceases — no further advance" | *Aśrutavān* monks of overweening pride | Bound-emptiness seed |
| 10 | Definite-nature *pratyekabuddha* | "Establishes *nirvāṇa*, no further advance" | "Solitary-lineage non-turning" | Round-bright but un-transformed-roundness seed |

The two-segment structure of the ten graspings is visible in this table:

- **Graspings 1–8** take some *object* — the round origin, pervasive knowing, the dust-elements, the extinction-support — as ultimate. Each is a "verdict on the intrinsic nature of a subtle object." The corresponding destination is a non-Buddhist seed-nature, because "establishing a subtle object as the ultimate intrinsic nature" is precisely the structural definition of non-Buddhist views.
- **Graspings 9–10** are different in kind. They take no specific object as ultimate; they "stop at the current attainment-altitude" — "rests at cessation and does not advance," "establishes *nirvāṇa* and does not advance." The corresponding destinations are the Two-Vehicle fruit-natures (bound-emptiness, round-bright but un-transformed), because "self-sealing at a particular attainment-altitude" is precisely the structural definition of the Two Vehicles.

The detailed analysis of this two-segment topology occupies §3.4 below.

### 2.3 Āgama Background: The Layered Definition of *Vijñāna*

The Āgama treatments of *vijñāna* operate at multiple layers, providing the early-canonical ground for the *Śūraṅgama*'s sophisticated phenomenology. The most foundational is the twelvefold dependent arising's "consciousness conditioned by formations" (*saṃskāra-paccayā viññāṇaṃ*) — *vijñāna* is dependent upon *saṃskāra*, not an independent substance. The *Saṃyukta Āgama* sūtra 298 makes this explicit: "What is *vijñāna*? It refers to the six bodies of consciousness — eye-consciousness, ear-consciousness, nose-consciousness, tongue-consciousness, body-consciousness, mind-consciousness."[^13] *Vijñāna* is the six-fold operation of consciousness arising in dependence on the matching of sense-faculty and sense-object — not some independent "soul" or "self."

The later development in the *Laṅkāvatāra* introduces the *ālaya-vijñāna* — the storehouse consciousness — and identifies the "lucent and unmoving" experiential field encountered at the *vijñāna-skandha* stage as the eighth consciousness. Zǐxuán's commentary explicitly takes this position: "How could the consciousness aggregate transcend seeing-hearing-knowing? … In the deluded position the consciousness aggregate gathers up all eight consciousnesses; but in the position of cultivation and severance, when the first four aggregates are exhausted, there is no longer the gross floating motion; how then can we be talking of the first seven? It must be the case that this refers exclusively to the eighth."[^14] The first seven consciousnesses are progressively traversed in the earlier aggregate stages; what remains at the *vijñāna-skandha* stage is the eighth consciousness in its imaging operation.

This explains why the *vijñāna-skandha* experiential field is "apparently unified and apparently permanent" (*sì-yī sì-cháng* 似一似常). The eighth consciousness has, by its nature, a *surface appearance* of "unified abiding" relative to the streaming of the prior seven — but this "apparent unity" is only relative to those streamings, not absolute. To take it as absolute is the first grasping (cause/caused).

### 2.4 *Adhimāna* (Overweening Pride) and the Structural Definition of "Insufficient Taken as Sufficient"

The classical Buddhist concept of *adhimāna* (Pāli: *adhimāna*; Chinese: *zēngshàng-màn* 增上慢) refers to the structural error of "claiming attainment one has not in fact reached." The *Madhyama Āgama* gives the standard definition: "claiming realization where there is no realization; claiming attainment where there is no attainment; claiming arrival where there is no arrival."[^15] The core of this definition is "taking the insufficient as sufficient" — proclaiming completeness at a position one has not yet completed.

In the *vijñāna-skandha* context, *adhimāna* takes a particularly subtle form: **it is no longer the empty pretension to a position one has not reached, but the genuine arrival at a real partial attainment which is then mistaken for the ultimate**. The ninth grasping's "rests at cessation and does not advance" and the tenth's "establishes *nirvāṇa* and does not advance" both describe this refined form. The practitioner has indeed achieved real stage-attainment ("realizing cessation" is the actual content of *arhat* fruition; "round and clear bright awareness" is the actual content of the *pratyekabuddha* fruit). These are not empty pretensions; they are genuine achievements. But they are then **mistaken for ultimate finality** — and that mistaking is the deepest structural form of *adhimāna*.

This explains why the definite-nature Two Vehicles are "the highest grade of overweening pride" — they are not the low-grade error of empty pretension, but the high-grade error of "self-sealing the apex of *bodhi* on the basis of a real but partial attainment." From the vulgar deluded man's empty pretension to the Two Vehicle's genuine partial-attainment-mistaken-for-completion, the structural arc of "taking the insufficient as sufficient" reaches its final station.

### 2.5 Zǐxuán's *Yìshū*: The Diagnostic of "Entirely the Shadow of Consciousness, Not Yet Cessation"

The Song-dynasty *Shoulengyan yishu zhujing* of Zǐxuán is the foundational commentary for the entire Song-Yuan-Ming-Qing tradition of *Śūraṅgama* exegesis. Its tenth fascicle, on *vijñāna-skandha*, organizes its diagnosis around three structural propositions which together constitute the doctrinal foundation for §3 of the present paper.

The first diagnostic concerns the intrinsic nature of *vijñāna-skandha*. Glossing the first grasping's experiential description ("able to cause his body's sense-gates to open and close, and through awareness to penetrate to all the classes of beings, this awareness fully entering the round origin"), Zǐxuán writes:

> "[This is] only a momentary experience within contemplation. In contemplation [the practitioner] *seems* to have his sense-gates already open, but they are not yet fully interpenetrating. 'Awareness pervading all classes' means that within this contemplation he has already seen that the beings of the ten directions and his own body share the one awareness-nature, mutually fusing without distinction of knower from known — this is what 'entering the round origin' refers to. **This entire experiential field is the shadow of consciousness, not yet [true] cessation.** Thus the prior text says 'the subtle marvel of cessation has not yet been rounded out.'"[^16]

"**Entirely the shadow of consciousness, not yet cessation**" — these eight characters constitute the most decisive diagnostic of the *vijñāna-skandha* position. Every "marvelous experiential mark" of the *vijñāna-skandha* stage — sense-gates opening, awareness penetrating all beings, entering the round origin, seeing the ground of life-rebirth — falls within the category of "shadow of consciousness," i.e., the imaging activity of the eighth consciousness. They look very much like descriptions of the ultimate; they are still in the position of "subtle marvel not yet rounded out." "Entirely the shadow of consciousness" directly extinguishes the possibility of mistaking these experiential marks for the ultimate.

The second diagnostic concerns the *quality* of *vijñāna-skandha*'s "lucent immobility." Glossing the passage on the fifth subtle inverted thought ("the so-called 'essential brightness, lucent and unmoving, named permanent…'"), Zǐxuán writes:

> "What practitioners take to be 'subtly clear, separated from formation-aggregate's arising-and-passing, blandly unmoving, marked as constant abiding' — this is the consciousness aggregate. … *This lucence is not true*: like a swiftly flowing stream, viewed from afar appearing tranquil, the very speed of its flow concealing the flow itself, not at all the absence of flow. … The ordinary practitioner and the Two-Vehicle adept do not perceive [this flow] at all; before the tenth bodhisattva-stage one perceives but cannot exhaust, and so it is said 'the flow is too swift to see.'"[^17]

"This lucence is not true" combined with the "swift stream" image constitutes the most surgical critique of *vijñāna-skandha*: it *appears* "lucent and unmoving" but is in fact "flowing too swiftly to be seen." It is not "not flowing"; it is "flowing at a speed that makes it look like not flowing." The point of the image is sharp: **any "lucent and unmoving" meditative state should be suspected of "flowing too swiftly to be seen,"** even if it is identical, in description, with the descriptions of true cessation, even if it betrays no perceptible trace of motion. **"The ordinary practitioner and the Two-Vehicle adept do not perceive this at all"** — this is not merely saying that the ordinary man is fooled; even the *arhat* of the Two Vehicles is fooled — it is only the bodhisattva before the tenth stage who begins to perceive the subtle stream, and only after the tenth stage who can fully cross it.

The third diagnostic is the qualifying restriction on definite-nature *śrāvakas*. Zǐxuán writes:

> "'Definite-nature' here means: simply during one period of seeking quiescence the disposition does not change — this is provisionally judged as 'definite.' But there are real *kalpas* still ahead, and they will eventually turn back to the higher vehicle."[^18]

This is Mahāyāna's most generous treatment of the Two Vehicles — "definite-nature" is not a permanent ontological judgment but a stage-description. Even practitioners labeled "definite-nature *śrāvaka*" will, in the duration of *kalpas*, eventually turn the mind toward Mahāyāna. The judgment here is structurally identical with the *Lotus*'s "expedient-means" treatment of the Two Vehicles in the *Upāyakauśalya* chapter: the *Lotus*'s verdict is not "you are wrong" but "you have not yet reached the ultimate; the ultimate is one only." Zǐxuán's gloss directly opens the bridge from P12 to S7.

### 2.6 Jiāoguāng's *Zhèngmài Shū*: "Between the Two Pillars" and "The Designation 'That Which Destroys the Right'"

The Ming-dynasty *Lengyan jing zhengmai shu* of Jiāoguāng Zhēnjiàn is the second principal doctrinal anchor for P12. Its tenth fascicle's treatment of *vijñāna-skandha* makes three structural contributions.

The first contribution is the topological naming "between the two pillars," already discussed in §1.1 above and serving as the central image of the entire paper.

The second contribution is the precise phenomenological characterization of *vijñāna-skandha*'s nature — "apparently unified, apparently permanent." Jiāoguāng writes:

> "'Essential brightness, lucent and unmoving' — this refers to the eighth consciousness. Pure unity unmixed, with horizontal and vertical penetrating illumination, called 'essential brightness' — this is what is meant by *apparently unified*. Floating thoughts already exhausted, and the disturbances all stilled, called 'lucent and unmoving' — this is what is meant by *apparently permanent*."[^19]

"Apparently unified and apparently permanent" (*sì-yī sì-cháng*) is the most precise four-character description of *vijñāna-skandha* in the entire commentarial tradition. It is *apparently* unified (it looks like "the one" — the unified non-dual true mind) and *apparently* permanent (it looks like "the constant" — the unchanging substantive ground). But each adjective is qualified by *apparent* (*sì* 似) — these are *resemblances*, not realities. The deepest danger of *vijñāna-skandha* lies precisely in the refinement of this resemblance — refined to the point that any unprepared meditator will mistake it for the real.

Jiāoguāng then unfolds the "swift stream" image with fresh precision:

> "'A swiftly flowing stream' must be taken as a swift waterless-of-waves flowing water. 'Viewed from afar appearing tranquil' — because there is no parallax of waves, no jets of foam or surge. The next two phrases clarify that precisely *because* the flow is swift it cannot be seen — not because there is no flow. If you sit beside this water and quietly test its current by tossing a leaf onto its surface, the leaf is swept rapidly away — only then do you realize how very swift the current is, that it is not no-flow. So the Buddha had us test it by means of a recognizable object [in the *Śūraṅgama*'s earlier section], like tossing a leaf."[^20]

The image's precision lies in its provision of a *test method* — toss a leaf on the water, and through the leaf's swift carrying-away you know the water is still flowing. In the cultivation of the *vijñāna-skandha* stage, "tossing a leaf" corresponds to subjecting the apparent stillness to some form of *comparative test* in order to verify its authenticity. Jiāoguāng leaves the precise nature of this test to subsequent passages, but the image is already clear: **any meditative state that *appears* "lucent and unmoving" should be tested by "tossing a leaf"; it cannot be trusted directly**.

The third contribution is the argument for the inclusion of the Two Vehicles in the demon-count — "the designation 'that which destroys the right'" — already discussed in §1.3. To this we add Jiāoguāng's secondary observation:

> "'Bound by emptiness' is called the bound-emptiness seed. For the *nirvāṇa-with-remainder* [of the *arhat*] is positioned only at the seventh stage of faith — covered by the consciousness aggregate, not yet reaching the cause-ground of *yuántōng*. How could it reach the fruit-ground of [*nirvāṇa*]-without-remainder? Therefore both equally violate *yuántōng* and turn from *nirvāṇa*."[^21]

"Positioned only at the seventh stage of faith" is the key — Jiāoguāng's claim is that the *arhat*'s attainment, on the Mahāyāna fifty-two-stage map, corresponds to the seventh of the ten stages of faith (七信位), and that to enter *yuántōng* requires the first abode (初住位) — there are still the remaining stages of faith plus the entry into the abode-stages between the *arhat* and the threshold of *yuántōng*. The *arhat*, in other words, has not yet reached even the *cause-ground* of *yuántōng* on the Mahāyāna map, let alone its *fruit-ground*. The problem of "definite-nature *śrāvaka*" is the self-sealing of completeness at the seventh stage of faith — and this is the most refined form of "taking the insufficient as sufficient."

### 2.7 The Five-Aggregate Water Image: Jiāoguāng's Final Positioning of *Vijñāna-skandha*

Jiāoguāng deploys throughout fascicle ten a recurring water image to position the relations among the five aggregates, which reaches its completed form in the *vijñāna-skandha* section:

> "[The perception aggregate] is like great waves; the formation aggregate is like fine waves; **the consciousness aggregate is like a wave-less flowing water**. The body of true awareness should be like a still and unmoving water."[^22]

The image's pivotal contrast is in the final pair: "consciousness aggregate as wave-less flowing water" versus "true awareness as still unmoving water." **The consciousness aggregate looks almost identical to true awareness** — both "still," both "unmoving," both without visible waves. The difference lies only in the extremely subtle distinction between "wave-less *flowing* water" and "truly *un-flowing* water": the former is still flow, only without waves; the latter is genuinely not flowing. No observation at the perceptual level can distinguish these two states — they are phenomenally identical. **This is the deepest danger of the *vijñāna-skandha* stage** — the resemblance at the level of object reaches its limit, to the point where cognitive discrimination becomes nearly impossible.

The image complements Zǐxuán's diagnostics: where Zǐxuán dismantles *vijñāna-skandha* at the level of intrinsic nature ("entirely shadow, not yet cessation"), Jiāoguāng dismantles it at the level of phenomenology ("appears wave-less, in fact still flowing"). Together they constitute the most complete doctrinal critique of the consciousness aggregate available in the tradition.

## §3 Core Argument

### 3.1 The Topology of "Between the Two Pillars": Why the Ten Graspings Share One Premise

The structural feature noted in §2.2 — that all ten graspings share an identical opening formula — is, within the entire fifty-*skandha-māras* section, unique to *vijñāna-skandha*. This is not stylistic accident; it precisely reflects the topology of "between the two pillars." **All ten graspings occur at one and the same position — "having exhausted formations as empty, having extinguished arising-and-passing, but having not yet rounded out the subtle marvel of cessation."** The ten are not "ten different errors at ten different positions" but "ten diverging paths from one and the same threshold."

"Between the two pillars" is a topologically fragile position. On one hand, the practitioner has completed the entire cultivation of P08–P11: he is no longer disturbed by external demons (form aggregate), no longer misjudges his interior sensations (feeling aggregate), no longer deceived by the "mirroring other" (perception aggregate), no longer locked by his own reflexive judgment (formation aggregate). On the other hand, he has not yet completed the final step — *vijñāna-skandha* has not yet been crossed; true cessation has not yet been rounded out. **He stands on the threshold; all the prior difficulties have been resolved, but the last step remains.** Any "establishment of a superior view" at this position — any movement of declaring "I have arrived" — freezes the unfinished transitional state and thereby loses the last step.

Zǐxuán's gloss is one piercing sentence: "Although the formation aggregate is exhausted and arising-and-passing already extinguished, returning to consciousness and tracing its source, [the practitioner] has not yet returned to cessation. This is why [the sūtra] says 'the subtle marvel is not yet rounded out.' **Because consciousness itself has not been broken; it is precisely a *subtle* arising-and-passing**."[^23] The phrase "precisely a subtle arising-and-passing" is the root of all ten graspings: *vijñāna-skandha* is *itself* "subtle arising-and-passing"; it is not "non-arising-and-passing." Any verdict that takes it for "non-arising-and-passing" is "establishing a superior view," and this manufactures, at this single threshold position, one of the ten graspings.

### 3.2 From "Totalizing the Streaming Field" to "Last Grasping at a Subtle Object": The P11 → P12 Refinement

The relation between the ten *māras* of the formation aggregate (P11) and the ten graspings of the consciousness aggregate (P12) is best characterized as a *structural shift* — and this shift is more significant than their similarity. On the surface both are "mind-demons" (心魔, in Zǐxuán's terminology); both involve the practitioner's own misjudgment of the experiential field; neither requires an external possessing entity. But on closer examination the *target of misjudgment* is entirely different:

- **The target of the formation-aggregate *māras***: the streaming nature of awareness itself (*saṃskāra*). The error is "totalizing a streaming object" — using the conceptual framework of "permanence," "impermanence," "existence," "non-existence," "annihilation," "present-life-*nirvāṇa*," and so on, to *pin down* the streaming. The antidote is "do not take [it] as holy" (不作聖解) — make no totalizing pronouncement on the intrinsic nature of the experiential field.
- **The target of the consciousness-aggregate graspings**: a subtle object refined to "apparent unity, apparent permanence" (*vijñāna-skandha* itself). The error is "the last grasping at a subtle object" — establishing this refined object as "the true permanent cause," "the true permanent body," or "the true permanent knowing." The antidote is "do not establish a superior view" (不立勝解) — make no ultimate-grasping verdict on a subtle object.

The two antidotes seem similar but are subtly distinct: "do not take as holy" forbids *totalizing pronouncement*; "do not establish a superior view" forbids *ultimate grasping*. The former operates on the act of *making a verdict* (over any state whatsoever — permanent, impermanent, etc.); the latter operates on the act of *grasping* (which arises only when the object is *very nearly identical* with the true). Verdicts can be made on any state; *graspings* arise only when "the object resembles the real to a degree that any further verdict would freeze it as ultimate." **From "verdict" to "grasping," the refinement of the act has advanced one level.**

The deepest implication of this refinement is: **the danger of the *vijñāna-skandha* stage arises from the *resemblance* of the object, not from its *falsity***. In the formation-aggregate stage, the object that is misjudged (the formation streaming) is "subtle but still manifestly streaming" — the practitioner is merely trying to deny the streaming. In the consciousness-aggregate stage, the object that is misgrasped (*vijñāna-skandha*) "phenomenally appears not to be streaming at all" — it has actually achieved the experiential marks of "wave-less," "lucent," "unmoving," and these marks are nearly identical with the descriptions of true cessation. **The consciousness-aggregate stage does not require the practitioner to "deny some manifest fact" — only to "take half a step further at a position of extreme resemblance"** — and that half-step is "establishing a superior view," "grasping," "taking *vijñāna-skandha* as ultimate."

The progressive curve from "mirroring other" (P10) → "mirror-student" (P11) → "between the two pillars" (P12) is best summarized in tabular form:

| Stage | Object of mirroring | Mediation requirement | Availability of comparative tools |
|---|---|---|---|
| P10 perception | An external *kalyāṇamitra* (the mirroring other) | External medium required (flying spirits attached to mediums) | Comparative tools fully available |
| P11 formation | The practitioner's own observing act (mirror-student) | No medium, but reflexive structure | Comparative tools largely fail |
| P12 consciousness | A subtle object refined to *vijñāna-skandha*'s "apparent unity" (the threshold) | No medium, no reflexivity, only resemblance | Comparative tools fail completely — object and reality are phenomenally indistinguishable |

At each successive stage, the "availability of comparative tools" further deteriorates. P10's comparative tool is "the verdict of the genuine *kalyāṇamitra*" — fully available; just find the right person. P11's comparative tool is "the practitioner's own act of awareness" — partially failing, because the act of observation is itself caught in the reflexive structure. P12's comparative tool is "the mark of true cessation" — completely failing, because *vijñāna-skandha* phenomenally looks identical with true cessation, and any attempt to discriminate by *comparison* would itself fall into "establishing a superior view" (since "comparing and concluding" already constitutes a verdict).

This explains why the *vijñāna-skandha* stage is the final station of the fifty *skandha-māras*, and also the deepest. **Not because the content of this stage is more complex, but because its comparative tools have completely failed.** No "external test" can tell the practitioner "you are presently in *vijñāna-skandha*-not-yet-exhausted" — *vijñāna-skandha*'s "apparent unity, apparent permanence" is precisely the experiential field that is *immune* to all tests.

### 3.3 "Apparent Unity, Apparent Permanence": Why *Vijñāna-skandha* Is More Dangerous Than the Formation Aggregate

Jiāoguāng's characterization of *vijñāna-skandha*'s nature as "apparently unified, apparently permanent" (§2.6) is the most precise dismantling of the consciousness aggregate available in the commentarial tradition. "Apparently unified" dismantles the concept of "the unified non-dual true mind"; "apparently permanent" dismantles the concept of "the unchanging substantive ground" — and these two concepts are precisely the two most common Mahāyāna formulations for describing *tathatā*, *buddha-nature*, and the true mind. *Vijñāna-skandha*'s "apparent unity, apparent permanence" is precisely "**it has phenomenally achieved every mark that the true mind should have, lacking only one final essence**."

What is this "one final essence"? It is "non-arising-non-passing" — the difference between true cessation and *vijñāna-skandha* lies only at this point, and this point is invisible at the perceptual level. Zǐxuán's swift-stream image makes this invisibility crystal clear:

> "This lucence is not true: like a swiftly flowing stream, viewed from afar appearing tranquil, the very speed of the flow concealing the flow itself — not at all the absence of flow. … **The ordinary man and the Two-Vehicle adept do not perceive this at all**; before the tenth bodhisattva-stage one perceives but cannot exhaust, and so it is said 'the flow is too swift to see.'"[^24]

"**The ordinary man and the Two-Vehicle adept do not perceive this at all**" — these eight characters are the most precise statement of the *vijñāna-skandha* stage's danger level. *Vijñāna-skandha*'s "subtle arising-and-passing" is completely invisible to the ordinary man (who cannot even see gross arising-and-passing); it is completely invisible to the Two-Vehicle *arhat* (who has crossed the prior four aggregates but stops short of *vijñāna-skandha*). **Only the bodhisattva before the tenth stage begins to perceive the subtle stream; only after the tenth stage can it be fully crossed.**

The sharpness of this verdict lies in its setting of the *vijñāna-skandha* stage's danger level at "*arhat* level" — that is, **even practitioners who have already attained *arhat* fruition will be deceived by *vijñāna-skandha*'s "apparent unity, apparent permanence."** This is not to say that the *arhat* "has not practiced enough" (he has completed the entire cultivation of personal liberation); it is to say that "*vijñāna-skandha*'s subtlety has already surpassed the discriminative capacity of the *arhat* fruit-position." To cross *vijñāna-skandha* requires not "more vigorous meditation" (the *arhat*'s meditation is already very deep) but "the specific structure of the bodhisattva path of Mahāyāna" — particularly the bodhisattva's structure of *bodhicitta*, "turning the mind toward the Great" and entering *yuántōng*.

The difference between "apparent unity, apparent permanence" and true cessation is essentially a "last mile" problem. The first ninety-nine miles of cultivation (form, feeling, perception, formation aggregates) have all been completed; the remaining final mile (*vijñāna-skandha*) is *almost identical* in landscape with the destination — but it is still "on the road" rather than "arrived." **Any movement of declaring "I have arrived" mistakes this "on the road" position for "arrived" and thereby loses the possibility of the final mile.** The ten graspings of *vijñāna-skandha* are precisely the ten topological forms of "declaring arrival."

More subtly: the danger of the "last mile" is not "running insufficiently far" but "thinking one has already finished running." If the practitioner honestly admits "I have not yet arrived," he will continue the final mile; but once he declares "I have already arrived," he stops, and the final mile is forever incomplete. **The *vijñāna-skandha* stage's antidote is not "run faster" but "do not declare arrival"** — this is the precise content of "not establishing a superior view."

### 3.4 The Two-Segment Topology of the Ten Graspings (Original Contribution of P12)

The ten graspings of *vijñāna-skandha* are not ten parallel errors but are divided by a structural pivot into two segments. The first eight and the last two are *topologically distinct types of error* — and this distinction is the central structural contribution of the present paper.

**The first eight graspings: the last grasping at the intrinsic nature of a subtle object**

The shared structure of the first eight graspings (cause/caused, capable/incapable, constancy/non-constancy, knowing/non-knowing, generating/non-generating, returning/no-returning, attached-craving/non-craving, real/non-real) is: **establishing a superior view on some side of *vijñāna-skandha*, taking that side as ultimate intrinsic nature**.

| Grasping | Object of superior view | Content of the verdict |
|---|---|---|
| Cause/caused | *Vijñāna-skandha* as the "place of return" | Established as "true permanent cause" (cause of all dharmas) |
| Capable/incapable | *Vijñāna-skandha* as "self-body" | Established as "capable of generating all beings of the ten directions" |
| Constancy/non-constancy | *Vijñāna-skandha* as "source from which body-mind flows" | Established as "true permanent body" |
| Knowing/non-knowing | *Vijñāna-skandha*'s "round pervasive knowing" | Established as "even insentient things have knowing" |
| Generating/non-generating | The "collected dust-elements" disclosed through *vijñāna-skandha* | Established as "the eternal causal agent of creation" |
| Returning/no-returning | The "permanent extinction-support" within *vijñāna-skandha* | Established as "annihilationist place of return" |
| Attached-craving/non-craving | *Vijñāna-skandha*'s "round-constant-solidifying-of-body" | Established as "essential round long-abiding" |
| Real/non-real | The "mutual penetration of life" within *vijñāna-skandha* | Established as the realm of "indulging the mind" |

Each grasping corresponds to an identifiable non-Buddhist position — and this is not because the *Śūraṅgama* borrows from non-Buddhist sources, but because **the structural enumeration of "establishing a superior view on a subtle object" exactly corresponds to the structural enumeration of non-Buddhist views**. The first eight graspings are "the final mask of Indian non-Buddhist views appearing within meditative phenomenology" — they are not the *coarse* non-Buddhist views (those have already been processed in the formation-aggregate stage's sixty-two views) but extremely refined non-Buddhist views, refined to the point that they only arise at the "between the two pillars" position.

Zǐxuán's gloss on the first grasping (cause/caused) makes the structure explicit:

> "Because [the practitioner] takes the consciousness aggregate as the *caused* and grasps it as the true permanent — as the cause of all dharmas — it is therefore called 'cause/caused grasping.' All dharmas are without cause, deludedly arising and passing; now to reckon there *is* a cause that is true permanent nature, this is no different from the non-Buddhist position which holds the *prakṛti* (root substance) to be permanent and capable of generating all things. **This is to reckon the *ālaya-vijñāna* in its still-formless 'dim beginning' as the *prakṛti*.**"[^25]

"Reckoning the *ālaya-vijñāna* in its still-formless 'dim beginning' as the *prakṛti*" exactly clarifies the inner structure of the cause/caused grasping. The Sāṃkhya school's *prakṛti* is the most refined of the non-Buddhist conjectures about "the cause of all dharmas," and the first grasping of the consciousness-aggregate stage is "having directly seen, in deep meditation, *something extraordinarily similar to* *prakṛti*, and established it as ultimate." **The first grasping is not "a non-Buddhist entering the meditator from outside" but "the meditator, in the consciousness-aggregate stage, having directly seen an experiential field extraordinarily similar to *prakṛti* and established it as ultimate"** — the same generative path as in the formation-aggregate stage's "wrong wisdom suddenly arising, called the unfolding of views, not that there really exist non-Buddhists from outside" (P11 §3.3), only with a more refined object.

**The last two graspings: the premature ceiling on attainment-altitude**

The shared structure of the last two graspings (definite-nature *śrāvaka*, definite-nature *pratyekabuddha*) is entirely different. They do not establish a superior view on any *object* — they establish it on *the height of their own attainment*.

| Grasping | Object of superior view | Content of the verdict |
|---|---|---|
| Definite-nature *śrāvaka* | The achieved position of "seeing suffering, severing the cause, realizing cessation, cultivating the path" | Established as "rests at cessation, no further advance" |
| Definite-nature *pratyekabuddha* | The achieved position of "round and clear bright awareness" | Established as "establishes *nirvāṇa*, no further advance" |

Note the structural difference between these two and the first eight:

- The error of the first eight is "having established the wrong thing" — having taken something which is not ultimate as ultimate. The corresponding antidote is "to recognize that this thing is not ultimate" (and through further meditation cross this object).
- The error of the last two is "having stopped at a correct position" — the "realization-of-cessation," "round and clear bright awareness" they have attained are real stage-attainments, not deluded experiences. The corresponding antidote is *not* "to recognize that this thing is not ultimate" (because it actually *is* a real stage-attainment) but "to recognize that one should not stop at this position."

**The antidote for the first eight is *breaking the grasping* (recognizing the falsity of the object); the antidote for the last two is *turning back the mind* (recognizing the non-ultimacy of the stage).** These are two structurally different antidotes.

Jiāoguāng's verdict on this structural difference is the most precise in the entire fifty-*skandha-māras* discussion (already cited at §1.3, repeated here for emphasis):

> "'Demon' is the designation 'that which destroys the right.' In the great *samādhi* of this sūtra, that which advances *yuántōng* and approaches *nirvāṇa* is benefit; that which violates *yuántōng* and turns away from *nirvāṇa* is loss. And the Two Vehicles, [in their definite-nature form], precisely violate and turn away — branching off, blunting and stagnating, equally injurious to the right [path]. **How should they not be called demons?** However, qualified by 'definite-nature,' [the verdict] applies only to those who do not turn back the mind."[^26]

"How should they not be called demons?" combined with "qualified by 'definite-nature'" together constitute Mahāyāna's most complete verdict on the Two Vehicles: **the Two Vehicles are not wrong but stopped; the direction of the stopping is wrong (turning from *nirvāṇa*), so it is a demon; but the position of the stopping can be re-departed from (turning the mind), so it is not a permanent demon.** The refinement of this verdict far exceeds that for the first eight graspings — for the first eight the dismantling is "the object is not ultimate," for the last two the verdict is "the position should not be stopped at."

**The ultimate significance of the two-segment topology**

The division of the ten graspings into "first eight" and "last two" is not a post-hoc scholarly classification; it is determined by the structure of the *Śūraṅgama* text itself. The closing verdict on each of the first eight graspings ends with "deluding *bodhi*, losing knowing-and-seeing" plus "generating the X seed" (where X is some non-Buddhist seed-nature, pride seed, inverted-roundness seed, etc.); the closing verdict on the last two has the same syntax but the *content* of the "seed" is "bound-emptiness seed" and "round-bright-but-untransformed-roundness seed" — both clearly belonging to a different category from the first eight's "non-Buddhist seeds."

The closing verdict of the entire *vijñāna-skandha* section also explicitly distinguishes the two segments:

> "… non-Buddhists and demonic-māras whose karma is brought to fruition fall into the *avīci* hell. *Śrāvaka* and *pratyekabuddha* fail to advance further."[^27]

"Non-Buddhists and demonic-māras" and "*śrāvaka* and *pratyekabuddha*" are listed separately; "falling into *avīci* hell" and "failing to advance further" are two consequences of entirely different character and severity. The former is hell (a severe negative consequence), the latter is "failing to advance" (a neutral stagnation consequence). **This is the *Śūraṅgama*'s own explicit marking of the two-segment distinction.**

From this distinction the deeper structural meaning emerges: **the first eight are *active errors* (establishing a superior view); the last two are *passive stoppages* (taking-as-sufficient).** Active errors fall into non-Buddhist seeds; passive stoppages remain in the Two Vehicles — both are diverging paths at the "between the two pillars" position, but the *direction* of the divergence is entirely different.

### 3.5 Why the First Eight Correspond to Non-Buddhist Schools: The *Vijñāna-skandha* Version of Structural Isomorphism

P11's central argument that "the parallel between the *Śūraṅgama*'s formation-aggregate ten *māras* and the *Brahmajāla*'s sixty-two views is *structural isomorphism* rather than literary borrowing" (§3.3 of P11) is repeated in even more refined form in the consciousness-aggregate stage. The correspondence between the first eight graspings and the eight non-Buddhist schools (*prakṛti*, *Maheśvara*, *Īśvara*, *Vaṭa-śaṇika*, the Kāśyapa Brahmins, the *asaṃjñi-sattva* heaven schools, the Asita longevity-seekers, the *Ṭakkirāja* heavenly demons) is not because the *Śūraṅgama* borrows from non-Buddhist sources, but because **the structural enumeration of "the last grasping at a subtle object" exactly corresponds to the structural enumeration of Indian non-Buddhist views**.

The "structural isomorphism" of the consciousness-aggregate stage is even more refined than that of the formation-aggregate stage. The isomorphic object of the formation-aggregate stage is "totalizing the intrinsic nature of streaming awareness" — an act common in philosophy (every metaphysical system performs this), so the sixty-two views can also be found in philosophical texts. The isomorphic object of the consciousness-aggregate stage is "the last grasping at a subtle object" — an act that occurs only in extremely deep meditation, so the first eight graspings' corresponding objects are not "everyday philosophers" (whose meditative depth is insufficient) but "the highest stratum of Indian non-Buddhist traditions" — Sāṃkhya, Yoga, *Maheśvara* worship, the long-life sects, and so on. **These are the deepest meditative traditions of Indian religious history**, the traditions whose practice could approach the *vijñāna-skandha* position.

In other words, the corresponding objects of the first eight graspings are "all the traditions in the history of Indian religion that could enter the consciousness-aggregate stage position" — they are not random non-Buddhist views, but "non-Buddhist views whose meditative depth was sufficient to approach the *vijñāna-skandha* position." This range is extremely limited — in two and a half millennia of Indian religious history, the traditions that could really reach this depth can be counted on one hand. The *Śūraṅgama*'s verdict on them is severe, but this severity is not aimed at their "non-Buddhist identity" — it is aimed at their structural error of "establishing a superior view at the last step within the consciousness-aggregate stage."

**Put differently: the *Śūraṅgama*'s critique of the first eight graspings is Mahāyāna's final verdict on the highest stratum of Indian meditative traditions.** Mahāyāna does not deny that these traditions could attain extremely deep meditation — they reached the "between the two pillars" position, which is already an extremely high attainment. Mahāyāna only points out that **at this position "establishing a superior view" is wrong; the correct act is "not establishing a superior view" and continuing the last step**.

The sharpness of this verdict lies in its clear separation of "meditative depth" from "correct attainment" — meditative depth can reach an extremely refined position, but if at that position one "establishes a superior view," all the meditative depth becomes an obstacle rather than a help. **Depth itself is not the ultimate; "not establishing a superior view" upon depth is the ultimate.**

### 3.6 Why the Last Two Correspond to the Two Vehicles: The Most Refined Form of "Taking the Insufficient as Sufficient"

The structural significance of the last two graspings (definite-nature *śrāvaka* and definite-nature *pratyekabuddha*) corresponding to Two-Vehicle fruit-positions has been discussed in §1.3 and §3.4. Here we develop further the special form that "taking the insufficient as sufficient" takes in the *vijñāna-skandha* stage.

"Taking the insufficient as sufficient" is the standard definition of *adhimāna* (overweening pride), but it takes different forms at different stages:

- **The vulgar form** (ordinary practitioners): empty pretension to a position one has not reached. The antidote is basic right-view training.
- **The novice meditator's form**: mistaking meditative experience for actual attainment. Many of the form-aggregate and feeling-aggregate stage's *māras* are of this kind, e.g., "claiming to have attained supreme *nirvāṇa*," "claiming to have become an *arhat*." The antidote is training in the discernment of meditative experiences (which is precisely the content of P08–P11).
- **The perception-aggregate form**: being misled by a "mirroring other" into self-claiming holiness. The antidote is exposing the flying-spirit-attached-to-medium structure (P10).
- **The formation-aggregate form**: making a totalizing verdict on the streaming nature and taking the verdict as attainment. The antidote is "not taking as holy" (P11).
- **The first eight graspings of the consciousness-aggregate**: establishing a refined object of *vijñāna-skandha* as the ultimate intrinsic nature and self-claiming arrival. The antidote is "not establishing a superior view" (this paper §3.4 first half).
- **The last two graspings of the consciousness-aggregate**: **having reached a real stage-attainment and mistaking the stage for the ultimate** — this is the most refined form, because the "attained content is real" makes it different from all earlier forms.

The refinement of the last two graspings' "taking the insufficient as sufficient" lies in this: **it is not delusion, but a real stage-attainment mistaken for the ultimate.** The definite-nature *śrāvaka* has reached a real "seeing suffering, severing the cause, realizing cessation, cultivating the path" (this is the actual content of the *arhat* fruit); the definite-nature *pratyekabuddha* has reached a real "round and clear bright awareness" (this is the actual content of the *pratyekabuddha* fruit). These are not empty pretensions but actual stage-attainments.

But even a real stage-attainment, if treated as the "ultimate fruit," becomes a "demon" — because it obstructs the possibility of the more ultimate attainment. Jiāoguāng's argument (§1.3) at its core is precisely this point: the term "demon" in the *Śūraṅgama* context does not mean "an external monster" but "that which obstructs the practitioner's realization of *yuántōng*." Anything that obstructs *yuántōng* is a demon, even if its content is a real stage-attainment of holiness.

The deepest implication of this verdict is: **a real stage-attainment, if treated as ultimate, becomes more dangerous than an empty pretension**. An empty pretension is easily disposed of — the practitioner need only honestly examine his own mind and he will discover that he has not really reached the position he claims. But a real stage-attainment cannot be disposed of in this way — the practitioner who honestly examines his own mind will indeed find that he has reached "seeing suffering, severing the cause, realizing cessation, cultivating the path"; he will indeed find that he has reached "round and clear bright awareness." **The antidote cannot be "denying this attainment" (because the attainment is real); it can only be "not treating this attainment as the destination."**

This brings us to Mahāyāna's most generous and most severe verdict on the Two Vehicles — "qualified by 'definite-nature,' [the verdict] applies only to those who do not turn back the mind." **The generous side**: the *Śūraṅgama* does not deny the real attainment of the *arhat* and *pratyekabuddha*; it does not deny that they have realized a real stage-fruit. **The severe side**: in the Mahāyāna context, any "no-further-advance" posture is a demon, even if it is built on a real attainment.

### 3.7 Antidotes: "Not Establishing a Superior View" and "Turning the Mind Toward the Great"

From the two-segment analysis of §3.4 we can derive two antidotal principles for the *vijñāna-skandha* stage, addressed respectively to the first eight graspings and to the last two:

**Antidote for the first eight: do not establish a superior view**

The core of the antidote for the first eight graspings is "do not establish an ultimate-grasping verdict on a subtle object." This unfolds into four operational principles:

1. **Any state of "essential brightness, lucent and unmoving" should be suspected of "flowing too swiftly to be seen."** The closer the experiential mark is to true cessation, the higher the alertness should be — not the lower.
2. **Every experiential mark of "the body's sense-gates opening and closing," "all classes of beings pervaded by awareness," "entering the round origin" is a shadow of consciousness.** Even if these experiential marks are identical, in description, with the descriptions of the Mahāyāna true mind, they are still in the position of "subtle marvel not yet rounded out."
3. **Do not declare to any state "this is the ultimate."** In the consciousness-aggregate stage, any "establishment of a superior view" is wrong — even when the *content* of the superior view appears entirely correct. "Not establishing a superior view" is not "incorrect verdict" but "the not-doing of the act of verdict-making."
4. **Continue forward; let the threshold remain a threshold.** "Between the two pillars" is a transitional position; the practitioner's task is to *cross* it, not to *stop* upon it. The concrete method for crossing is to continue meditation without establishing a superior view, allowing *vijñāna-skandha* to disclose its "subtle arising-and-passing" nature spontaneously.

These four operational principles have an extraordinarily concise summary in fascicle ten of the *Śūraṅgama* — "**If [you] make a holy interpretation, you will receive the host of evils; if [you] make no holy interpretation, this is named a good experiential field**."[^28] This sentence appears repeatedly in the prior four aggregate stages, but its meaning is most refined in the consciousness-aggregate stage — **"not making a holy interpretation" is not "not knowing the content of the experiential field" but "knowing the content of the experiential field but not establishing it as the ultimate."**

**Antidote for the last two: turning the mind toward the Great**

The core of the antidote for the last two graspings is not "breaking the grasping" (because there is no "grasping" to break — the object of the last two is real stage-attainment), but **turning the mind** — taking what one has already attained as a transitional point rather than as the destination. This unfolds into three principles:

1. **Acknowledge the reality of the stage-attainment.** The first step in the antidote for the last two is to acknowledge that the *arhat* fruit and the *pratyekabuddha* fruit are real stage-attainments, not delusory experiences. To deny the reality of these attainments would only multiply the practitioner's confusion, because he would begin to doubt whether he has attained "anything real at all."
2. **Acknowledge that the stage-attainment is not the ultimate.** The second step is to acknowledge that on the Mahāyāna stage map these attainments are "positioned only at the seventh stage of faith" (Jiāoguāng) — early stages within the bodhisattva path's fifty-two positions, with considerable distance still to the ultimate. This acknowledgment is not a denial of the Lesser Vehicle but an acceptance of the Mahāyāna map.
3. **Generate the aspiration of turning the mind toward the Great.** The third step is to generate the *bodhicitta* of the bodhisattva, transforming personal-liberation attainment into the resource of benefiting beings. Zǐxuán's verdict — "definite-nature… in the duration of *kalpas*, will eventually turn back to the higher vehicle" — explicitly states that even those labeled "definite-nature *śrāvaka*" will eventually turn the mind. **The question of cultivation is not "whether one will turn the mind" but "whether one turns the mind now or only after *kalpas*."**

The relation between the two antidotal principles: **"not establishing a superior view" addresses the basic structure at the "between the two pillars" position, while "turning the mind toward the Great" addresses the advanced structure of "taking stage-attainment as sufficient" at that same position.** The former is the antidote for the first eight of the ten graspings; the latter is the antidote for the last two. Together they constitute the entirety of the antidote method for the consciousness-aggregate stage — that is, the final antidote of the fifty *skandha-māras*.

From P09's "do not cling" to the second arrow, through P10's "seek the great teacher" to the mirroring other, through P11's "do not take as holy" to the mirror-student, to P12's "do not establish a superior view + turn the mind toward the Great" at the threshold position — this is the refinement curve of the entire antidotal logic of the fifty *skandha-māras*. Each successive stage's antidotal principle is more interior, more subtle, and closer to the ultimate principle of "not grasping at any position." **At the consciousness-aggregate stage, the refinement of the antidote matches the refinement of *vijñāna-skandha* itself — "not establishing a superior view" is precisely the act of letting *vijñāna-skandha* return to its native position (subtle arising-and-passing) without performing any "freezing" upon it.**

## §4 Interdisciplinary Resonance

### 4.1 ⚠️ A Minimal Contemporary Analogy: Premature Convergence and Local Optima

The "between the two pillars" structure of the *vijñāna-skandha* stage has a far-from-perfect but structurally resonant parallel in contemporary computer science and cognitive science: **premature convergence** and **local optima**. These two concepts were first named in optimization algorithms (and have since been encountered repeatedly in machine learning, neuroscience, and organizational behavior). The core insight: **any process attempting to approach a global optimum through iterative search will tend to halt at some position that *appears* to be the optimum from within its local neighborhood, while missing a deeper position that is actually more optimal**.

The standard form of premature convergence is this: an algorithm, in the course of iteration, finds a "local optimum" — a position such that all neighboring positions are worse than it, so from the local viewpoint it appears to be the best. But if one steps outside this local neighborhood, there is another "global optimum" position that is far deeper. The algorithm's problem is that it does not know about the existence of the global optimum, and so it halts at the local optimum because it cannot find any direction "better than the current position."

⚠️ This analogy resonates with the *vijñāna-skandha* stage's "between the two pillars" structure on three points:

*First*, the difference between "appears to be optimal" and "actually is optimal." In the *vijñāna-skandha* stage, the "subtle marvel not yet rounded out" experiential field looks identical to true cessation (essential brightness, lucent and unmoving, apparently unified, apparently permanent), but it is in fact still "subtle arising-and-passing" — the shadow of consciousness. In optimization algorithms, the local optimum and the global optimum are completely indistinguishable from the local viewpoint as well — only by jumping outside the local neighborhood can the difference be seen.

*Second*, the choice of "stopping" versus "continuing." The error of the last two graspings of the consciousness-aggregate (definite-nature *śrāvaka* and definite-nature *pratyekabuddha*) is not "having reached the wrong position" but "having stopped at the reached position and refusing to advance further." In optimization algorithms, the standard method for "escaping the local optimum" is *simulated annealing* or *mutation* in genetic algorithms — these methods deliberately introduce perturbation at the position currently appearing optimal, giving farther positions a chance. This corresponds structurally to "turning the mind toward the Great" — refusing to take one's current stage-attainment as the destination, continuing the search for a deeper position.

*Third*, the cognitive difficulty of "no external verdict." One of the greatest difficulties in optimization algorithms is that they have no "global observer" to tell them "your current position is local-optimum or global-optimum." The algorithm can only infer its position from "local information around the current position," and this local information is insufficient for distinguishing local from global optima. This corresponds structurally to the failure of comparative tools at the "between the two pillars" position — the practitioner has no external judgment tool to tell him "the current state is true cessation or shadow of consciousness."

**The limit of this analogy must be explicitly marked**: premature convergence and local optima are concepts of computer science; they handle optimization problems in which the "objective function" is explicitly defined — the goal can be quantified, compared, and tested. The "true cessation" of the *vijñāna-skandha* stage is not an "objective function"; it cannot be quantified, cannot be tested before it is "reached," cannot be verified by any external standard. **The position of meditative phenomenology is not an "optimization problem"; no "optimization model" can substitute for the concrete experience of meditation**. The value of this analogy lies solely in helping the reader intuitively understand the structural error of "stopping at a position that appears optimal" — it cannot become a scientific explanation of the *vijñāna-skandha* stage, much less a methodological tool for the antidote.

The greatest danger in pushing this analogy too far is that the reader might mistakenly believe that "the *vijñāna-skandha* stage can be crossed by some computational method." The crossing of the *vijñāna-skandha* stage in fact depends entirely on the meditative posture of "not establishing a superior view" — this is a phenomenological act, not an algorithmic step. **Any attempt to translate it into an algorithm would change it into something it is not.** No epistemological tool can substitute for the concrete experience of meditative phenomenology — this is the limit that §5 will repeatedly emphasize.

## §5 Middle-Way Corrections

### 5.1 ❌ The Ten Graspings Are Not a Historical Catalog of Eight Non-Buddhists Plus Two Vehicles

The first misreading to avoid: treating the ten graspings as "a historical catalog of eight non-Buddhist schools and two Vehicle fruits" and attempting to use the methods of religious history to verify each grasping's correspondence to some specific historical school. The problem with this misreading is that it transforms the *Śūraṅgama*'s tenth fascicle into a religious-history text and thereby completely misses its character as meditative phenomenology. **The ten graspings of *vijñāna-skandha* are not "a historical catalog" but "ten topological forms of 'establishing a superior view' at the 'between the two pillars' position."** Their object is not the historical schools that religious-history scholars verify in their studies, but the concrete experience of meditators at the *vijñāna-skandha* position. Any attempt to extract them from this meditative context will change them into something they are not — and Zǐxuán's verdict from the formation-aggregate stage, "all of these are practitioners reaching this point, where wrong wisdom suddenly arises, called 'the unfolding of views' — not that there really exist non-Buddhists from outside" (P11 §3.3), applies equally in the consciousness-aggregate stage.

### 5.2 ❌ The First Eight Graspings' Correspondence to Non-Buddhist Schools Is Not a "Re-Importation" of Non-Buddhist Views

The second misreading to avoid: since the first eight graspings have explicit correspondences to Indian non-Buddhist schools, the *Śūraṅgama* must be "re-importing" non-Buddhist views into its meditation map. This misreading conflates "structural isomorphism" with "literary borrowing" (the same misreading from P11 §3.3, in a more refined form for the consciousness-aggregate stage). In fact, the correspondence between the first eight graspings and the non-Buddhist schools is not because the *Śūraṅgama* borrows from non-Buddhist sources, but because **the structural enumeration of "the last grasping at a subtle object" exactly corresponds to the structural enumeration of Indian non-Buddhist views**. Zǐxuán's verdict "wrong wisdom suddenly arising, called the unfolding of views" applies equally in the consciousness-aggregate stage — the first eight graspings are the meditator's own internal product of "wrong wisdom suddenly arising" at the *vijñāna-skandha* position, not non-Buddhist views imported from outside into the meditator's mind. **Structural isomorphism arises because both are describing the same structural pitfall, not because one borrows from the other.**

### 5.3 ❌ "Definite-Nature Two Vehicles" Is Not a Wholesale Negation of the Two Vehicles

The third misreading to avoid — and the one most likely to be misunderstood in P12: since definite-nature *śrāvaka* and definite-nature *pratyekabuddha* are listed as demons, the *Śūraṅgama*'s attitude toward the Two Vehicles is one of wholesale negation. The problem with this misreading is that it ignores Jiāoguāng's explicit qualifying restriction "qualified by 'definite-nature,' [the verdict] applies only to those who do not turn back the mind." **The *Śūraṅgama* does not list all *śrāvakas* and *pratyekabuddhas* as demons; it only lists those of "definite-nature, not turning back the mind."** Two-Vehicle practitioners who can turn the mind toward Mahāyāna, even if they have temporarily rested at the *arhat* fruit or the *pratyekabuddha* fruit, are not classified as demons by the *Śūraṅgama*. Zǐxuán's further explanation — "definite-nature simply means that during one period of seeking quiescence the disposition does not change; provisionally judged as 'definite,' but there are real *kalpas* still ahead, and they will eventually turn back to the higher vehicle" — is even more generous: even practitioners labeled "definite-nature" will eventually turn the mind in the duration of *kalpas*. **"Definite-nature" is not a permanent ontological judgment but a stage-description.** To read the *Śūraṅgama*'s verdict on definite-nature Two Vehicles as "wholesale negation of the Two Vehicles" is the deepest misreading of this verdict.

### 5.4 ❌ "Taking the Insufficient as Sufficient" Does Not Mean the Cultivation Stages Themselves Are Wrong

The fourth misreading to avoid: since the core error of the last two graspings is "taking the insufficient as sufficient" (self-sealing on a stage-attainment), the cultivation stages themselves should be eliminated — every "stage-attainment" risks engendering "taking the insufficient as sufficient," so it is best not to establish any stages. The problem with this misreading is that it conflates "establishing stages" with "treating a stage as the endpoint" — two distinct things. **Cultivation stages themselves are necessary** — the Mahāyāna bodhisattva path has explicit positions of faith, abode, action, dedication, ground, equivalent-enlightenment, marvelous-enlightenment; the Lesser Vehicle has explicit positions of the four directions and four fruits. The existence of stages is not wrong; treating some position within a stage as "ultimate endpoint" is. The antidote for the last two graspings is not "to abolish the stages" but "to acknowledge each stage as a *stage* and not as an *endpoint*." Zǐxuán's verdict "definite-nature simply means that during one period of seeking quiescence the disposition does not change" makes this clear — "definite-nature" is a description of "one period of seeking quiescence the disposition does not change," not a negation of the cultivation stages themselves.

### 5.5 ❌ "Between the Two Pillars" Is Not Some Abstract Dyadic Mediation

The fifth misreading to avoid: reading Jiāoguāng's "between the two pillars" image as some abstract "concept of dyadic mediation" and then attempting to use philosophical concepts of "mediality," "interstice," "liminality," and so on, to explain it. The problem with this misreading is that it abstracts an extremely concrete meditative-phenomenological position, thereby losing the entire cultivational meaning of the image. **"Between the two pillars" is not an abstract concept but an extremely concrete meditative position — the position of "formation aggregate already broken, consciousness aggregate not yet rounded out" in actual practice corresponds to very specific experiential marks**: the six sense-faculties empty and still, entering where there is nothing to enter, deeply reaching the original ground of the twelve classes' rebirth, seeing all beings sharing the same ground of life, and so on. Any attempt to abstract this concrete position into a "concept of mediality" would lose its meditative-phenomenological meaning. "Between the two pillars" is "a position actually reached by meditators," not "a concept constructed by philosophers."

### 5.6 ❌ "Turning the Mind Toward the Great" Is Not Contempt for the Lesser Vehicle's Practice

The sixth misreading to avoid — a misreading that has appeared repeatedly in the history of Mahāyāna Buddhism: since Mahāyāna's antidotal principle is "turning the mind toward the Great," and since the Lesser Vehicle's fruit-positions are listed as demons, the Lesser Vehicle's practice itself should be contemned or abolished. This misreading is wholly contrary to the *Śūraṅgama*'s own position — the *Śūraṅgama* maintains an extraordinarily solemn respect for the Āgamas, for the Lesser Vehicle's practices, and for the *arhat*'s attainment. The verdict on the ninth grasping explicitly identifies the object of the problem as "monks of no-learning afflicted by overweening pride" — not the *arhat* itself. **The genuine *arhat* is not "definite-nature, not turning back the mind"** — historically, the great *arhats* mostly turned the mind toward Mahāyāna, reappearing as bodhisattvas in Mahāyāna sūtras (this is the central structure of the *Lotus*'s "Expedient Means" chapter). What is listed as a demon is not the *arhat*'s practice or attainment but the specific posture of "stopping at the *arhat* fruit and refusing to advance further." The antidote for the last two graspings is not "to contemn the Lesser Vehicle" but "to acknowledge the Lesser Vehicle's real attainment while not treating it as sufficient." Any attempt to read the *Śūraṅgama*'s verdict as "contempt for the Lesser Vehicle" is the deepest misreading of the *Śūraṅgama*, and the deepest misunderstanding of Mahāyāna's own tradition.

## §6 Conclusion

### 6.1 P12 Summary: Between the Two Pillars and the Last Grasping

The central thesis of this paper is: **the ten graspings of the consciousness aggregate are not ten new errors but ten topological forms of "the last grasping at a subtle object." At the transitional position of "having exhausted formations as empty, having returned to consciousness at its source, with arising-and-passing already extinguished, but having not yet rounded out the subtle marvel of cessation" (Jiāoguāng's "between the two pillars"), any "establishment of a superior view" freezes the unfinished transitional state and mistakes it for the ultimate. The first eight graspings mistake some subtle object disclosed in *vijñāna-skandha* for ultimate cause, ultimate body, or ultimate knowing, and fall into eight non-Buddhist seed-natures; the last two graspings are stranger and more dangerous — they are not non-Buddhist at all but prematurely capped Two Vehicles, sealing off the apex of *bodhi* on the basis of an authentic but partial attainment.**

Zǐxuán and Jiāoguāng converge on this verdict: Zǐxuán says "although the formation aggregate is exhausted, returning to consciousness, [the practitioner] has not yet returned to cessation, this is why the subtle marvel is not yet rounded out… this entire experiential field is the shadow of consciousness, not yet cessation"; Jiāoguāng says "the formation aggregate is the front pillar; the consciousness aggregate is the rear pillar; and the locus where the ten graspings arise is precisely between these two." Both commentators explicitly identify the position of the consciousness-aggregate stage as **not "inside the consciousness aggregate" but the transitional position "formation aggregate already broken, consciousness aggregate not yet rounded out" — the threshold "between the two pillars."** The entire problem of the ten graspings lies in the act of "establishing a superior view *on the threshold*," not in the specific content of any superior view.

The paper has unfolded seven layers of argument to clarify this thesis: (1) the topological foundation of "between the two pillars" — the ten graspings sharing one premise formula shows they occur at one and the same position; (2) the P11 → P12 refinement — from "totalizing the streaming object" to "the last grasping at a subtle object"; (3) the danger level of "apparent unity, apparent permanence" — *vijñāna-skandha* is phenomenally indistinguishable from true cessation, differing only at the point of "non-arising-non-passing"; (4) ⭐ the two-segment topology of the ten graspings — the first eight establish superior views on subtle objects; the last two seal a ceiling on attainment-altitude; (5) why the first eight correspond to non-Buddhists — the *vijñāna-skandha* version of structural isomorphism, the first eight being the final mask of the highest stratum of Indian meditative traditions; (6) why the last two correspond to the Two Vehicles — the most refined form of "taking the insufficient as sufficient," real stage-attainment mistaken for the ultimate; (7) the two-pronged unfolding of the antidotal principles — "not establishing a superior view" (for the first eight) and "turning the mind toward the Great" (for the last two).

**The "two-segment topology" is the most important original contribution of this paper.** The first eight graspings and the last two are not parallel ten errors but two structurally different types of error — the first eight being "active superior-view-establishing" (grasping at an object), the last two being "passive halting" (sealing on a stage). The directions of antidote also differ structurally — for the first eight, the antidote is "breaking the grasping" (recognizing the falsity of the object); for the last two, the antidote is "turning the mind" (recognizing the non-ultimacy of the stage). Treating these two segments separately is the only way to understand the internal structure of the ten graspings — and the only way to understand why the last two are the bridge from S6 to S7, since "turning the mind toward the Great" as an antidotal principle directly interfaces with the entire structure of the *Lotus Sūtra*'s "opening the provisional to disclose the real, the One Buddha Vehicle."

From P09's "do not cling" to the second arrow, through P10's "seek the great teacher" to the mirroring other, through P11's "do not take as holy" to the mirror-student, to P12's "do not establish a superior view + turn the mind toward the Great" at the threshold position — this is the complete refinement curve of all the antidotal principles of the fifty *skandha-māras*. Each successive stage's antidotal principle is more interior, more subtle, and closer to the ultimate principle of "not grasping at any position." **At the consciousness-aggregate stage, the antidotal principle reaches refinement-parity with *vijñāna-skandha* itself — "not establishing a superior view" is precisely the act of letting *vijñāna-skandha* spontaneously disclose its 'subtle arising-and-passing' nature without performing any 'freezing' upon it.** The entire antidote of the consciousness-aggregate stage is collected within the structure of "not establishing a superior view + turning the mind toward the Great."

### 6.2 The Bridge from S6 to S7: The Entry to the One Buddha Vehicle

The verdict of the *Śūraṅgama*'s tenth fascicle on the last two graspings of the consciousness aggregate (definite-nature *śrāvaka*, definite-nature *pratyekabuddha*) directly interfaces with Mahāyāna Buddhism's deepest historical verdict on the Two Vehicles — the *Lotus Sūtra*'s "opening the provisional to disclose the real, the One Buddha Vehicle" (開權顯實一佛乘).

The core structures of the *Lotus*'s verdict and the *Śūraṅgama*'s verdict on the last two graspings are perfectly parallel:

- **The *Lotus*'s verdict on the Two Vehicles**: not "your practice is wrong" but "you have already reached an authentic stage-attainment (the conjured city, *ṛddhi-nagara*); but this is only a halting station, not the ultimate destination (the treasure-place, *ratna-dvīpa*)." The direction of antidote is to turn the mind toward the Great, departing once more from the conjured city, heading toward the Buddha Vehicle as the unique ultimate destination.
- **The *Śūraṅgama*'s verdict on the last two graspings**: not "your practice is wrong" but "you have reached an authentic stage-attainment (cessation realized, clear bright awareness); but this is only the position of 'the subtle marvel not yet rounded out,' not true cessation." The direction of antidote is to *not establish a superior view* and continue forward, crossing from the consciousness aggregate into the rounded-out position of "the five aggregates exhausted."

The structures of the two verdicts are entirely parallel — **acknowledging the reality of the stage-attainment + denying the ultimacy of the stage-attainment + pointing toward the possibility of a deeper position**. The *Lotus* unfolds this verdict through the narrative structure of "opening the provisional to disclose the real, the One Buddha Vehicle"; the *Śūraṅgama* unfolds it through the meditative phenomenology of the "ten graspings of the consciousness aggregate." The two are two expressions of the same structure.

More subtly, the *Lotus* and the *Śūraṅgama* are entirely consistent in their attitude toward the Two Vehicles — **extraordinarily generous and extraordinarily severe at once**. The generous side: neither sūtra denies the real attainment of the Two Vehicles; both acknowledge the *arhat* and the *pratyekabuddha* as real stage-fruits. In the *Lotus*, Śāriputra, Maudgalyāyana, Mahā-Kāśyapa, and Subhūti — the great *arhats* — are personally given prediction by the Buddha to attain Buddhahood; their *arhat* fruits are not abolished but are repositioned as stage-attainments leading toward the Buddha Vehicle. The severe side: both sūtras decisively point out that "treating the *arhat* fruit as the ultimate" is wrong — and this wrong is not because the *arhat* fruit itself is wrong (it is a real stage-attainment) but because the act of "treating a stage as the endpoint" is wrong (this is precisely the structural error of "taking the insufficient as sufficient").

**The significance of this verdict in the history of Mahāyāna Buddhism cannot be overstated.** It defines the entire relation of Mahāyāna to the Lesser Vehicle — not opposition, not absorption, not transcendence, but *repositioning*. The Lesser Vehicle's attainments have their real position on the Mahāyāna map (the seventh stage of faith, the tenth stage of faith), but this position is not the endpoint — the endpoint is one only, the Buddha Vehicle. From this position, the direction "forward" is "turning the mind toward the Great"; the direction "backward" is "definite-nature not turning the mind" (judged a demon by the *Śūraṅgama*). Mahāyāna's verdict on the Lesser Vehicle is essentially a verdict of *direction-choice*.

**The S6 *Śūraṅgama* series reaches its internal turning point at this verdict, and there directly interfaces with the S7 *Lotus* series.** If the S6 *Śūraṅgama* series is read as "the complete unfolding of the fifty *skandha-māras*," then the ninth and tenth graspings of the consciousness-aggregate stage are the final two positions of this unfolding; and the antidotal principle for these two positions (turning the mind toward the Great) directly becomes the entire theme of the S7 *Lotus* series. **S6 ends at "not establishing a superior view"; S7 begins at "turning the mind toward the Great"** — this is the most precise junction between the two series.

The deeper implication: the entire theme of the S6 *Śūraṅgama* series (the discrimination and antidote of the fifty *skandha-māras*) reaches its internal limit at the consciousness-aggregate stage — it tells the practitioner "do not establish a superior view at this position," "do not treat a stage as the endpoint," but it does not positively unfold "what the true endpoint is." "The true endpoint" (the One Buddha Vehicle) requires the S7 *Lotus* series for its positive unfolding. **In this sense, S6 is a series of "breaking" (breaking down all wrong positions); S7 is a series of "establishing" (establishing the unique correct position).** Together, breaking and establishing constitute the complete Mahāyāna meditation map.

### 6.3 A Final Word to the Reader: The Final Chapter of the Fifty *Skandha-māras*

If you have read P08 (form aggregate), P09 (feeling aggregate), P10 (perception aggregate), P11 (formation aggregate), P12 (consciousness aggregate), you have walked the entire map of the fifty *skandha-māras*. A final word to those who practice meditation seriously: **the true use of this map is not to make you afraid, but to enable you to no longer be afraid.**

*First*: **a map is for identifying positions, not for positing traps.** The entire structure of the fifty *skandha-māras* is not "fifty inevitable disasters" but "fifty structurally possible positions" — each position has its entry condition, and if you have not reached that condition, you will not fall into the corresponding trap. The form-aggregate stage's condition is the meditative depth of "the four great elements no longer interweaving"; the feeling-aggregate stage's is the inner-feeling clarity of "manifestly clear in the present"; the perception-aggregate stage's is the motivational transparency of "the perception aggregate not yet exhausted"; the formation-aggregate stage's is the reflexive clarity of "the perception aggregate exhausted, the formation aggregate manifestly emerging"; the consciousness-aggregate stage's is the threshold position of "having exhausted formations as empty, having returned to consciousness at its source." **A practitioner who has not reached the entry condition will not fall into the corresponding trap** — and this structural fact is itself the greatest reassurance.

*Second*: **the map tells you a *direction*, not a *specific test method*.** For the consciousness-aggregate stage, this final position, no "external test method" can tell you "you are now in the position of *vijñāna-skandha*-not-yet-exhausted" — because *vijñāna-skandha*'s "apparent unity, apparent permanence" is immune to all tests. But the map tells you a direction: **any impulse to declare "I have arrived" is an alarm bell**. If you find yourself with a strong impulse of "I have arrived," the alarm has already sounded — this is the precursor of "establishing a superior view" at the "between the two pillars" position. The antidote is not to give a more refined judgment, but to notice the impulse itself and let it dissolve.

*Third*: **for beginners, what matters most is not the consciousness-aggregate stage but the basic training of right view.** If you have not yet entered deep meditation to the level of "the perception aggregate exhausted," then the ten graspings of the consciousness aggregate are for you only a future map — your present task is the basic three trainings of *śīla*, *samādhi*, and *prajñā*, not the attempt to rehearse the consciousness-aggregate position. Attempting to rehearse will produce the opposite result — it will translate the refined structure of the consciousness-aggregate stage into a conceptual game in everyday thinking, thereby losing its cultivational significance. **The entire significance of the map is "you can find it when you need it," not "you must enter the final station of the map right now."**

*Fourth*: **for practitioners who go deep, the entire antidote of the fifty *skandha-māras* is collected in a single sentence.** This sentence appears repeatedly in the prior four aggregate stages, but its deepest meaning is fully unfolded only at the consciousness-aggregate stage — "**if you make a holy interpretation, you receive the host of evils; if you make no holy interpretation, this is named a good experiential field**." From form aggregate to consciousness aggregate, the meaning of "do not make a holy interpretation" continually refines: in the form aggregate, it is "do not take light marks as attainment"; in the feeling aggregate, it is "do not take changes of feeling as attainment"; in the perception aggregate, it is "do not be deceived by the mirroring other into thinking yourself a sage"; in the formation aggregate, it is "do not make any totalizing pronouncement on the intrinsic nature of the experiential field"; at the consciousness aggregate, it is "do not establish a superior view on the subtle objects of *vijñāna-skandha*, and do not seal off the apex of *bodhi* on a stage-attainment." **'Do not make a holy interpretation' runs through the entire structure of the fifty *skandha-māras*, reaching its completed form at the consciousness-aggregate stage.**

If a single sentence can summarize P12, it is the one given by the *Śūraṅgama*'s tenth fascicle: "**If the consciousness aggregate is exhausted, then your present sense-faculties will interpenetrate one another. From this interpenetration, you can enter the bodhisattva's vajra dry-wisdom**."[^29] The mark of *vijñāna-skandha* exhaustion is "the interpenetration of the sense-faculties" (the functions of the six faculties become mutually substitutable, that is, the manifestation of "the true use of the faculties"), and from this interpenetration one can "enter the bodhisattva's vajra dry-wisdom" — that is, enter the *kṣānti* of dry-wisdom (*śuṣka-vidarśanā*) at the entrance of the bodhisattva path (the threshold of the first abode). **From the "between the two pillars" position, walking the final step, the practitioner enters directly into the genuine entrance of the Mahāyāna bodhisattva path.** This is the complete cultivational meaning of the consciousness-aggregate stage, and the final destination of the entire map of the fifty *skandha-māras*.

From form aggregate to consciousness aggregate, the entire map of the fifty *skandha-māras* points to one destination only — **the genuine entrance of the Mahāyāna bodhisattva path**. The meaning of the map is not "fifty traps" but "fifty diverging paths to be avoided on the road to the entrance." Walking this road to its end, the practitioner stands at the gate of the Mahāyāna bodhisattva path, and from there begins the entire unfolding of the S7 *Lotus* series' "opening the provisional to disclose the real, the One Buddha Vehicle."

**The final chapter of the fifty *skandha-māras* is the opening chapter of the Mahāyāna bodhisattva path.**

The separate listing of "non-Buddhists and demonic-māras" and "*śrāvaka* and *pratyekabuddha*," with their differing consequences ("falling into *avīci* hell" versus "failing to advance further"), is the *Śūraṅgama*'s explicit marking of the two-segment distinction.

[^28]: *Śūraṅgama*, fascicles 9–10, the recurring formula of the fifty *skandha-māras*: "若作聖解,即受群邪;不作聖解,名善境界."

[^29]: *Śūraṅgama*, fasc. 10, conclusion of the consciousness-aggregate section. The sentence "If the consciousness aggregate is exhausted, then your present sense-faculties will interpenetrate one another. From this interpenetration, you can enter the bodhisattva's vajra dry-wisdom" (識陰若盡,則汝現前諸根互用,從互用中,能入菩薩金剛乾慧) marks the entrance into the bodhisattva path's first abode.
