## Abstract

The final passage of the Heart Sūtra — from "all Buddhas of the three times, relying on prajñāpāramitā, attain anuttara-samyak-saṃbodhi" through "gate gate pāragate pārasaṃgate bodhi svāhā" — accomplishes a threefold closure. First, it elevates from the bodhisattva-fruit (mind without obstruction → ultimate nirvāṇa) to the buddha-fruit (anuttara-samyak-saṃbodhi), revealing prajñā as the common path of all awakening. Second, it transitions from the discursive analysis of exoteric teaching into the mantric dhāraṇī of esoteric practice, embodying the unity of exoteric and esoteric — one principle, different entries. Third, the same 260-character sūtra functions differently for three types of practitioners: the beginner plants seeds, the intermediate practitioner unties mental knots, and the advanced practitioner liberates while reciting — three practitioners, one sūtra.

This paper argues that "prajñā as the mother of all Buddhas" is not rhetoric but a doctrinal judgment. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitāśāstra* reasons that "among parents, the mother's merit is greatest" because prajñā alone can "comprehensively contemplate the true marks of all dharmas, accomplishing everything without exception." The four mantra epithets (great spirit mantra, great illumination mantra, unsurpassed mantra, unequalled mantra) are not four different mantras but four dimensions of the same prajñā's efficacy — overcoming obstacles, illuminating darkness, surpassing all, and equaling the unequalled. The gate-gate mantra is the esoteric dhāraṇī of prajñā's exoteric meaning; Fazang acknowledges it as "the secret speech of all Buddhas, beyond the understanding of those at the causal stage," yet proceeds to "forcibly interpret" it word by word — this tension itself embodies the dialectic between exoteric and esoteric.

The doctrinal basis for the three practitioners lies in the Yogācāra theory of śruta-vāsanā (hearing-perfuming). The *Mahāyānasaṃgrahabhāṣya* (Paramārtha's translation) defines it precisely: "The ear-consciousness hears sounds, triggering the mental consciousness to arise; relying on words and sentences, it discriminates their meaning; through repeated practice, a capacity is generated; holding and not forgetting — this is called hearing-perfuming." Each recitation of the Heart Sūtra by a beginner plants a dharmakāya seed in the ālaya-vijñāna. The *Cheng Weishi Lun* adds that "when hearing the correct Dharma, it also perfumes the innate uncontaminated seeds, causing them to gradually increase until they ultimately give rise to the supramundane mind."

The dharma-eye metaphor threads throughout: you possess eyes to see the world but do not know their value until they fail — then you would pay anything to restore them. The Diamond Sūtra's repeated comparisons between seven-treasure offerings and four-line verses make exactly this point: seven treasures improve your circumstances, but circumstances change; four lines of verse cultivate the dharma-eye itself, and the dharma-eye is indestructible. Vasubandhu's criterion of "progressive meaning" (*pravṛtti-artha*) versus "accumulative meaning" (*saṃcaya-artha*) captures it precisely: treasures can only accumulate, but the dharma-eye propels you forward.

Cross-disciplinary dialogue engages Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development as a heuristic analogy. Five Middle Way corrections are provided. This paper concludes the S2 series' textual exegesis of the Heart Sūtra's 260 characters.

## I. Introduction

### 1.1 From Personal Awakening to the Universal Path

S2-P10 closed the Heart Sūtra's awakening arc — from S2-P02's Avalokiteśvara (the causal longing for freedom) to S2-P10's mind without obstruction (the fruition of freedom), the bodhisattva traversed terror through prajñā and arrived at ultimate nirvāṇa. The individual path of awakening reached its conclusion.

Then the Heart Sūtra did not end. It posed a larger question:

> All Buddhas of the three times, relying on prajñāpāramitā, attain anuttara-samyak-saṃbodhi.[^1]

A bodhisattva relying on prajñā to attain nirvāṇa is one person's awakening. All Buddhas of the three times relying on prajñā to attain supreme, perfect awakening is the shared testimony of every being who has ever become fully enlightened. In a single sentence, the Heart Sūtra expanded the horizon from "one bodhisattva's journey" to "the path of all Buddhas." Then it did something unexpected — it shifted from doctrinal analysis into mantra:

> Therefore know that prajñāpāramitā is the great spirit mantra, the great illumination mantra, the unsurpassed mantra, the unequalled mantra, which removes all suffering — true, not false. Therefore the prajñāpāramitā mantra is spoken. The mantra is: gate gate pāragate pārasaṃgate bodhi svāhā.[^2]

From S2-P01 through S2-P10, ten papers unfolded prajñā's doctrinal content — the five aggregates, the twelve sense fields, the eighteen elements, the twelve links of dependent origination, the four noble truths, emptiness, terror, nirvāṇa. Now the Heart Sūtra compressed it all back into a single line of mantra. This is not regression but completion — the same prajñā, unfolded discursively by exoteric teaching and compressed mantically by esoteric practice.

### 1.2 The Dharma-Eye Metaphor

You have a pair of eyes. Every morning you open them and the world is there — light, color, depth, the faces of those you love. You never think this is remarkable, because you have never lost it.

Now imagine: your eyes fail. You know what the world looks like, you remember every face and every road, but you cannot see. At that moment, how much would you pay to restore your sight? Everything you have. Because suddenly you know: eyes are priceless.

Prajñā is the eye of the mind — the dharma-eye. In ordinary times, you do not sense its value. When suffering arrives — aging, illness, loss, confrontation with death — the deep terror in your heart cannot be escaped, and you realize: what you lack is not external protection but the capacity to see reality as it is.

The Diamond Sūtra makes this point repeatedly through a single structure: if someone filled the entire trichiliocosm with seven treasures and gave them in charity, is the merit great? Immense. Yet if someone receives and retains even four lines of this sūtra and explains them to others, "that merit surpasses the former."[^3] Why? Because seven-treasure charity cultivates externals — better circumstances, a safer environment. Four lines of verse cultivate the dharma-eye itself — the capacity to see where suffering originates and where the path to liberation lies. Circumstances change. The dharma-eye does not decay.

Vasubandhu, in the *Vajracchedikā-prajñāpāramitā-śāstra*, delivers the precise verdict: seven-treasure merit "does not progress toward bodhi" (*phala na bodhi-gāmin*), while receiving and expounding the teaching "can progress toward bodhi" (*dvayaṃ bodhi-gāmin*).[^4] Conditioned merit possesses "accumulative meaning" but not "progressive meaning" — however much treasure you pile up, the pile does not walk itself toward awakening. The dharma-eye has progressive meaning — it moves you forward.

The dharma-eye metaphor will thread throughout this paper. The three practitioners see to different depths, but each act of seeing polishes the same dharma-eye.

### 1.3 Central Questions

This paper addresses three core questions.

First, why does the same 260-character sūtra produce entirely different effects in the hands of different practitioners? Beginners feel peace after reciting it, intermediate practitioners use it to deconstruct their attachments, and advanced practitioners achieve real-time prajñā illumination while reciting. What is the doctrinal basis for these differences?

Second, why does the Heart Sūtra shift from doctrinal analysis to mantra at the very end? Having devoted substantial space to "empty," "without," and "neither arising nor ceasing," why does it conclude not with a summary but with "gate gate"? How do exoteric and esoteric teachings achieve unity here?

Third, what is the relationship between the bodhisattva-fruit and the buddha-fruit? S2-P10 demonstrated that bodhisattvas rely on prajñā to attain ultimate nirvāṇa; now the Heart Sūtra says all Buddhas of the three times also rely on prajñā to attain anuttara-samyak-saṃbodhi. Are nirvāṇa and bodhi the same thing? If not, where does the difference lie?

### 1.4 Paper Structure

Section II presents scriptural foundations in six subsections: prajñā as mother of all Buddhas, the distinction between bodhisattva-fruit and buddha-fruit, the four mantra epithets, word-by-word analysis of the gate-gate mantra, the theory of hearing-perfuming, and the Diamond Sūtra's merit comparisons. ## II. Scriptural Foundations

### 2.1 Prajñā as Mother of All Buddhas

"All Buddhas of the three times, relying on prajñāpāramitā, attain anuttara-samyak-saṃbodhi" — the doctrinal foundation for this passage receives its fullest treatment in the *Mahāprajñāpāramitāśāstra* (*Dàzhìdù Lùn*). Nāgārjuna does not merely call prajñā a "mother"; he explains why this metaphor is chosen.

Fascicle 34 directly answers the challenge "Why teach only prajñā?":

> "Prajñāpāramitā is the mother of all Buddhas. Among parents, the mother's merit is greatest. Therefore the Buddha takes prajñā as the mother and the pratyutpanna-samādhi as the father. Samādhi can only collect the scattered mind and enable wisdom to mature, but it cannot contemplate the true marks of all dharmas. Prajñāpāramitā can comprehensively contemplate all dharmas and discern their true marks — there is nothing it cannot reach, nothing it cannot accomplish. Because its merit is the greatest, it is called 'the mother.'"[^5]

The logic here is functional, not sentimental. Samādhi (concentration) is the "father" — it focuses the mind but cannot independently perceive reality. Prajñā is the "mother" — it sees everything as it truly is. In the project of awakening, prajñā's contribution is greater; hence it bears the title "mother."

The verse in fascicle 18 extends the metaphor further:

> "All Buddhas and bodhisattvas benefit all beings; prajñā is their mother, giving birth and nourishing. The Buddha is the father of sentient beings; prajñā gives birth to the Buddha — thus it is the grandmother of all sentient beings."[^6]

Prajñā gives birth to Buddhas; Buddhas benefit beings. If there were no prajñā, there would be no Buddhas; if there were no Buddhas, beings would have no opportunity to hear the Dharma. "Grandmother" is not a kinship metaphor but a causal chain.

At a deeper level, fascicle 58 states that prajñā is not only the mother but also the teacher of all Buddhas:

> "Prajñāpāramitā is the mother of all Buddhas in the ten directions, and it is also the teacher of all Buddhas."[^7]

The mother gives birth; the teacher guides. Prajñā both generates awakening and orients it.

The closing fascicle (fascicle 100) delivers the ultimate verdict:

> "When prajñā exists in the world, it is as if the Buddha exists. ... Prajñāpāramitā is the mother of all Buddhas, and all Buddhas take the Dharma as their teacher. The Dharma is prajñāpāramitā. When the teacher is present and the mother is present, one cannot say that benefit is lost. Why? Because the root of benefit is present."[^8]

When prajñā is present, the Buddha is present — because prajñā is the root of all benefit. The Buddha's physical body enters parinirvāṇa, but prajñā does not cease. As long as someone hears, retains, and reflects on it with right attention, prajñā remains in the world.

### 2.2 Bodhisattva-Fruit and Buddha-Fruit: Kuiji's Causal and Fruition Reliance

Kuiji, in the *Yōuzàn* (Profound Praise of the Heart Sūtra), divides "Bodhisattvas relying on prajñā ... ultimate nirvāṇa" and "All Buddhas of the three times relying on prajñā ... anuttara-samyak-saṃbodhi" into two distinct levels of reliance:

> "The above praises the causal reliance — the benefit of severing obstructive defilements. Below it praises the fruition reliance — the benefit of attaining bodhi."[^9]

Bodhisattvas rely on prajñā to sever the two obstructions (kleśāvaraṇa and jñeyāvaraṇa) and attain nirvāṇa — this is "causal reliance," the benefit at the causal stage. All Buddhas rely on prajñā to attain bodhi — this is "fruition reliance," the benefit at the fruition stage. Nirvāṇa is the achievement of severance (disconnective merit); bodhi is the achievement of realization (connective wisdom-merit). The two do not contradict — severance enables realization, and realization encompasses severance.

Kuiji then analyzes the content of bodhi through the five dharmas:

> "This manifests the five dharmas: first, the pure dharma-realm, which is the Buddha's dharmakāya, tathatā, and nirvāṇa, endowed with the subtle merits of true nature and marks, attained through contemplating the principle of emptiness; the remaining four wisdoms are conditioned merits, the saṃbhogakāya attained through cultivating the causes of self-benefit."[^10]

Bodhi = the pure dharma-realm (tathatā, dharmakāya, nirvāṇa) + the four wisdoms (ādarśa-jñāna, samatā-jñāna, pratyavekṣaṇā-jñāna, kṛtyānuṣṭhāna-jñāna). Nirvāṇa is one component of the five dharmas; bodhi encompasses nirvāṇa plus the full operation of the four wisdoms. The bodhisattva attains nirvāṇa through prajñā; the Buddha attains nirvāṇa plus the four wisdoms — the latter includes the former.

Kuiji further distinguishes four levels of awakened beings: "Ordinary beings have deviant wisdom — hence the qualifier 'right' awakening. Śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas have partial wisdom — hence 'equal' awakening. Bodhisattvas have incomplete wisdom — hence again 'right' awakening. Only the Buddha has perfect realization and alone bears the complete name."[^42] The full title "unsurpassed, right and equal, right awakening" is reserved for the Buddha alone, because only the Buddha's prajñā is complete.

Kuiji cites the Diamond Sūtra as proof: "All Buddhas emerge from this sūtra; all Tathāgatas are born from this sūtra."[^11] Prajñā gives birth to Buddhas — the Heart Sūtra says it in one sentence.

### 2.3 The Four Mantra Epithets

The Heart Sūtra calls prajñā "the great spirit mantra, the great illumination mantra, the unsurpassed mantra, the unequalled mantra." Kuiji's *Yōuzàn* provides two layers of interpretation.

The first layer (Dharmapāla lineage) offers functional descriptions:

> "Marvelous functioning without limit is called 'spirit'; illuminating even the most hidden is called 'bright'; supreme and first is called 'unsurpassed'; nothing comparable to compare with is called 'unequalled.'"[^12]

The great spirit mantra — prajñā's function is inconceivable and boundless. The great illumination mantra — prajñā's seeing penetrates even the most concealed. The unsurpassed mantra — nothing surpasses prajñā. The unequalled mantra — nothing can even be compared with it.

The second layer (dhāraṇī classification) carries greater scholastic weight. Kuiji notes that the Sanskrit "dhāraṇī" means "total retention" and comes in four kinds: dharma-dhāraṇī (condensing extensive teaching into brief formulas), artha-dhāraṇī (condensing extensive meaning into brief formulas), pratibhāna-dhāraṇī (attaining the bodhisattva's patience of non-arising), and mantra-dhāraṇī (secret speech).[^13] The Heart Sūtra's transition from prose prajñā (dharma-retention and meaning-retention) to the gate-gate mantra (mantra-retention) completes the full spectrum of dhāraṇī.

Kuiji also offers an alternative reading: prajñā is not "called" the great spirit mantra, etc., but rather is the mantra "spoken by" the great spirit, the great bright one, the unsurpassed one, the unequalled one — i.e., the Buddha.[^14] This reading shifts emphasis from prajñā's nature to the authority of the speaker.

Woncheuk (*Yuáncè*) in his *Heart Sūtra Commentary* adds the soteriological dimension: "Unfathomable spiritual function is the great spirit mantra; dispelling darkness and removing delusion is the great illumination mantra; surpassing the two vehicles is 'unsurpassed'; transcending even bodhisattvas, equal to the Buddha's wisdom — thus the repeated word 'unequalled.'"[^15] "Surpassing the two vehicles" — this is not the prajñā of śrāvakas or pratyekabuddhas; it is the prajñā of the buddha-vehicle. "Equal to the Buddha's wisdom" — prajñā stands equal with the Buddha's own realization, hence "un-equalled-equalling."

The *Heart Sūtra Commentary* (T1714) summarizes from the practitioner's perspective: the great spirit mantra overcomes māra-obstacles, the great illumination mantra extinguishes the darkness of ignorance, the unsurpassed mantra reveals ultimate principle, and the unequalled mantra is the supremely wondrous fruit of awakening with nothing its equal.[^16] These four layers — from counteracting (overcoming obstacles) to realization (awakening's fruit) — parallel the practitioner's progression from cause to fruition.

### 2.4 Word-by-Word Analysis of the Gate-Gate Mantra

"Gate gate pāragate pārasaṃgate bodhi svāhā" — the Heart Sūtra's final utterance is not doctrinal analysis but a Sanskrit mantra.

The commentarial tradition's responses to this passage create a fascinating tension. Fazang's *Brief Commentary on the Heart Sūtra* first establishes the "non-interpretable" position:

> "This has two meanings. First, it cannot be interpreted, for this is the secret speech of all Buddhas, beyond the understanding of those at the causal stage. One should simply recite and retain it, removing obstacles and increasing merit — there is no need to forcibly interpret it."[^17]

He then proceeds to "forcibly interpret" it:

> "If one insists on interpreting: *gate* means 'go' or 'cross' — the function of profound wisdom. The repetition *gate gate* means crossing oneself and crossing others. *Pāragate*: *pāra* means 'the other shore' — the destination of crossing. *Pārasaṃgate*: *saṃ* means 'together' or 'universally' — self and others universally crossing, all arriving at the other shore. *Bodhi*: what kind of shore? The shore of great bodhi. *Svāhā* means 'swiftly' — may the foregoing be swiftly accomplished."[^18]

Fazang's word-by-word rendering is transparent: gate = cross (the function of deep wisdom) → gate gate = cross oneself and others → pāragate = cross to the other shore → pārasaṃgate = all universally crossing to the other shore together → bodhi = the shore of great awakening → svāhā = swift accomplishment.

Woncheuk's *Heart Sūtra Commentary* is richer still, providing three interpretive systems.[^19]

The first system (crossing meaning): gate gate = "cross, cross" (prajñā crossing oneself and others) → pāragate = "arriving at the other shore" (nirvāṇa) → pārasaṃgate = "completed the crossing" (totally arriving) → bodhi = "the substance of the other shore" → svāhā = "swiftly arriving at the shore of bodhi."

The second system (excellence meaning): gate gate = "excellent, excellent" (prajñā's twofold excellence of self-benefit and other-benefit at the causal stage) → pāragate = "the other shore's excellence" (praising the dharma) → pārasaṃgate = "the saṅgha's excellence at the other shore" (praising the causal-stage ekayāna bodhisattva) → bodhi svāhā = "awakening completed" (praising the fruition-stage trikāya buddha).

The third system (Three Jewels meaning): gate gate + pāragate = praising the Dharma Jewel → pārasaṃgate = praising the Saṅgha Jewel → bodhi svāhā = praising the Buddha Jewel.

Woncheuk also records the "non-translatable" position: "This mantra is the authentic sound of the Western regions, a secret formula — translating it would destroy its efficacy, so the Sanskrit is preserved."[^20] The tension between the two camps itself embodies the exoteric-esoteric dialectic — doctrinally it can be translated (exoteric), functionally it should not be (esoteric).

Kuiji's own commentary on the mantra is remarkably brief: "The Buddha, through kalpas of wisdom-and-compassion practice difficult to cultivate, compressed them into abbreviated words whose meaning is profound and whose teaching is vast — not easy to comment on in detail."[^21] Countless kalpas of wisdom and compassion condensed into a few syllables; the meaning too deep and the teaching too vast for detailed commentary — Kuiji openly acknowledges "not easy to elaborate."

The variations across five Chinese translations are also noteworthy: Kumārajīva used 竭帝 (*jié dì*), Xuanzang used 揭帝 (*jiē dì*), Dharmacandra used 揭諦 (*jiē dì*), and Prajñā & Riguang used 峩帝 (*é dì*), while Prajñācakra used 誐帝 (*é dì*) — the last being closest to the Sanskrit pronunciation of *gate*, adding the syllable "oṃ" at the beginning and using "mantra" (*zhēnyán*) rather than "spell" (*zhòu*) throughout, giving the text its strongest esoteric character.[^22]

### 2.5 Hearing-Perfuming: Right Hearing as Seed-Planting

The doctrinal framework for the beginner among the three practitioners — "recitation itself is merit; recitation itself plants seeds" — requires rigorous justification. This justification lies in the Yogācāra theory of śruta-vāsanā (hearing-perfuming).

The *Mahāyānasaṃgraha* (Paramārtha's translation) establishes the core thesis: seeds for the supramundane mind cannot arise from the contaminated ālaya-vijñāna; they require "hearing-perfuming from the outflow of the supremely pure dharma-realm":

> "From the outflow of the supremely pure dharma-realm, the seeds of right hearing-perfuming give rise to the supramundane mind."[^23]

"Outflow of the supremely pure dharma-realm" — the dharma-realm realized by the awakened. "Right hearing" — hearing without distortion. "Perfuming" — repeated impressioning. The supramundane mind — the mind that transcends saṃsāra. Dharma-realm → scripture → hearing → perfuming → supramundane mind: a complete causal chain.

Paramārtha's *Mahāyānasaṃgrahabhāṣya* provides the clearest definition:

> "The ear-consciousness hears sounds, triggering the mental consciousness to arise; relying on words and sentences, it discriminates their meaning; through repeated practice, a capacity is generated; holding and not forgetting — this is called hearing-perfuming."[^24]

The ear hears sound (ear-consciousness), triggering comprehension (mental consciousness), which processes meaning through words and sentences; repeated engagement generates a functional capacity that is retained without loss — this is hearing-perfuming. When a beginner recites the Heart Sūtra, even without "understanding" it, the ear-consciousness is already hearing, the mental consciousness is already engaging, and seeds are already being planted.

More critically, the *Mahāyānasaṃgraha* uses the "water-and-milk" analogy to describe hearing-perfuming's nature: it resides within the ālaya-vijñāna, "co-turning" with it but "not of the same nature" — like water and milk mixed together, appearing unified but differing in essence.[^25] Hearing-perfuming is the outflow of the dharma-realm, a seed of supramundane dharma; the ālaya-vijñāna is a storehouse of contaminated dharma. The two occupy the same consciousness, but hearing-perfuming is precisely the force that counteracts the ālaya-vijñāna.

Hearing-perfuming has three grades: lower (hearing-wisdom), middle (thinking-wisdom), upper (cultivation-wisdom). All three grades are dharmakāya seeds:

> "The hearing-perfuming of lower, middle, and upper grades should be known as dharmakāya seeds."[^26]

A beginner's hearing — even at the lower grade — is already a dharmakāya seed. Paramārtha goes further: the hearing-perfuming attained by "a bodhisattva who is just beginning practice," though it is a "worldly dharma," belongs to the dharmakāya "because it serves as the cause for attaining the dharmakāya."[^27] A supramundane seed growing within a mundane context — because its cause (dharma-realm outflow) and its fruit (dharmakāya) are both supramundane.

The *Cheng Weishi Lun* offers Dharmapāla's synthetic position: innate uncontaminated seeds exist from beginningless time, but hearing-perfuming is needed to strengthen them:

> "When hearing the correct Dharma, it also perfumes the innate uncontaminated seeds, causing them to gradually increase until they ultimately give rise to the supramundane mind."[^28]

Each instance of right hearing irrigates the innate uncontaminated seeds. The seeds already exist; hearing-perfuming causes them to grow. When a beginner recites the Heart Sūtra — the sound of correct Dharma vibrating through the ear-consciousness, engaging the mental consciousness — irrigation is occurring.

Asvabhāva's *Mahāyānasaṃgrahabhāṣya* cites a moving precedent: when Rāhula asked the Buddha to teach him direct realization, the Buddha replied, "You should first receive and retain the Dharma treasury."[^29] Hear first, then realize. Asvabhāva adds: "One who has heard little has no capacity to enter direct realization." Without sufficient hearing, contemplative insight is simply impossible.

Asvabhāva also delivers a crucial judgment: "Although the listener may have distorted views, because hearing-perfuming perfumes their mental continuum, they gradually and progressively become free of distortion."[^30] Even if the one who hears still holds inverted views, hearing-perfuming will work on their continuum, gradually leading them away from inversion. You do not need to understand at the outset. The hearing-perfuming itself is already doing its work.

### 2.6 The Dharma-Eye's Scriptural Anchor: The Diamond Sūtra's Merit Comparisons

The Diamond Sūtra contains eight progressively escalating merit comparisons that constitute the most robust scriptural foundation for the dharma-eye metaphor.[^31]

The scale of escalation is staggering: seven treasures filling the trichiliocosm → seven treasures filling Ganges-sand-number trichiliocosms → bodily sacrifice equal to the sands of the Ganges → bodily sacrifice three times daily for immeasurable hundreds of thousands of millions of billions of kalpas → seven-treasure piles the size of all Mount Sumerus → seven treasures filling immeasurable asaṃkhyeya worlds. Each comparison reaches the same conclusion: it does not match receiving and retaining even four lines of this sūtra and explaining them to others.

The pivotal turn comes in the fifth comparison, where the Buddha reveals the empty nature of merit itself:

> "If merit had substantial reality, the Tathāgata would not say that merit is great. It is because merit is without [substantial reality] that the Tathāgata says merit is great."[^32]

Merit is "without" (empty of inherent existence), and therefore "great." Because no substantial entity called "merit" is being possessed, merit is inexhaustible. The merit of seven-treasure charity is finite and depletable; the merit of prajñā is empty of inherent existence and therefore inexhaustible.

Vasubandhu provides the most precise criterion in his *Vajracchedikā-śāstra*: merit-accumulation has two kinds of meaning — "accumulative meaning" and "progressive meaning."[^33] Seven-treasure charity possesses only accumulative meaning — wealth can be piled up, but piling up does not, of itself, move you toward awakening. Receiving and retaining the sūtra possesses both accumulative and progressive meaning — it not only gathers but propels forward.

The *Vajracchedikā-ṭīkā* (Diamond Immortal Commentary) offers a notable definition of "four lines of verse": "Whether verse or prose, anything that adequately expresses the principle of the dharmakāya constitutes a 'four-line verse.'"[^34] Not limited to metered quatrains. Any expression that points toward the dharmakāya qualifies. This means every passage of the Heart Sūtra — "the five aggregates are all empty," "form is emptiness," "neither arising nor ceasing," "gate gate" — can serve as a "four-line verse." Every line of the Heart Sūtra cultivates the dharma-eye.

Guṇamati's verse provides the most radical formulation:

> "If one upholds the correct Dharma and even generates the mind of awakening, it does not equal understanding emptiness — not even one-sixteenth."[^35]

Upholding the Dharma and generating bodhicitta — these are already advanced practices. Yet without "understanding emptiness" (penetrating śūnyatā), they amount to less than one-sixteenth. Because Dharma-practice and aspiration without prajñā remain conditioned — operating with an unopened dharma-eye.

## III. Core Argument

### 3.1 Three Practitioners, One Sūtra

The same Heart Sūtra, 260 characters, recited by three types of practitioners produces three fundamentally different effects from the same text.

**The beginner: conditions met, seeds planted.**

That someone picks up the Heart Sūtra and recites it is already the meeting of conditions — the karmic fortune to encounter this teaching, the disposition to engage with it. Regardless of whether the person understands while reciting, regardless of whether the motivation is seeking peace or simple curiosity, with each recitation hearing-perfuming is occurring. The ear-consciousness hears, the mental consciousness engages, the sounds of the words enter the ālaya-vijñāna — seeds have been planted.

This is not rhetoric. The hearing-perfuming doctrine laid out in §2.5 provides the precise mechanism: the outflow of the supremely pure dharma-realm (the Heart Sūtra) enters through the ear-consciousness, engages the mental consciousness, and produces perfuming capacity. Even lower-grade hearing is a dharmakāya seed.[^36] Asvabhāva states that "although the listener may have distorted views, hearing-perfuming perfumes their continuum, gradually leading them away from distortion." Understanding is not required; the seeds work on their own.

Therefore, no threshold can be set for the beginner. Each recitation removes a measure of fear and establishes a measure of right mindfulness. Recitation itself is merit. Recitation itself plants seeds.

**The intermediate practitioner: untying one's own knots, illuminating one's own life.**

The intermediate practitioner begins to contemplate the meaning of the text. "Form is emptiness" — are the things I cling to really so solid? "Feeling, perception, volitional formations, consciousness are also like this" — are my emotions, my thoughts, my habitual reactions also just conditions assembled? "Without eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, mind" — is the sensory world I took to be permanent also empty of intrinsic marks?

The intermediate practitioner uses the sūtra's meaning to illuminate their own life. The five aggregates, the twelve sense fields, the eighteen elements, the twelve links of dependent origination — the entire S2-P01 through S2-P10 series was paved for this practitioner. But the path is the path; walking it is one's own business. The knots were tied by oneself and must be untied by oneself. The Heart Sūtra's doctrinal analysis gives direction, but mental knots can only be opened by the person who tied them. No one else can see the emptiness of the five aggregates for you.

The intermediate practitioner's hearing-perfuming has progressed from the lower grade (hearing-wisdom) to the middle grade (thinking-wisdom). They are not merely hearing — they are reflecting, comparing, using the text to examine their own experience. Transformation at the level of perception-aggregate (saṃjñā-skandha) is underway.

**The advanced practitioner: liberating while reciting, Avalokiteśvara in operation.**

The advanced practitioner no longer "uses" the Heart Sūtra but "lives" it. When they recite "Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva," contemplation is already happening. When they recite "practicing deep prajñāpāramitā," prajñā is already operating within the formations-aggregate. When they recite "illuminated the emptiness of the five aggregates," illumination is occurring in that very moment. Each recitation is a clearing of dust from the mirror's surface — not removing something foreign, but allowing what has always been pure to appear.

The advanced practitioner's hearing-perfuming is of the upper grade (cultivation-wisdom). Paramārtha's commentary describes the progression: "As hearing-perfuming of the lower, middle, and upper grades progressively increases, so the resultant-maturation consciousness progressively decreases — this is the turning of the basis."[^37] Hearing-perfuming increases, the contaminated consciousness decreases — pure forces grow, defiled forces recede. This is āśraya-parāvṛtti, the turning of the basis. The advanced practitioner is in the process of turning.

What is the common starting point for all three practitioners? Generating the mind of awakening (bodhicitta). S1-P01 demonstrated the butterfly effect of initial aspiration — when the first thought at the causal ground is correctly directed, the Dharma-stream will find you.[^38] S1-P02 demonstrated the dharmakāya's trans-temporal blessing — the one who generates the aspiration is received by the Dharma-stream, not through one's own power but through prajñā itself extending its welcome.[^39] From beginner to advanced practitioner, the seed of prajñā and the fruit of prajñā are the same thing — only the degree of purity differs.

### 3.2 From Bodhisattva-Fruit to Buddha-Fruit: Two Milestones on the Same Path

S2-P10 demonstrated that bodhisattvas rely on prajñā to attain ultimate nirvāṇa. P11's passage states that all Buddhas of the three times rely on prajñā to attain anuttara-samyak-saṃbodhi. These are not two paths but two milestones on the same path.

Kuiji's "causal reliance / fruition reliance" distinction provides the precise framework.[^40] Bodhisattvas rely on prajñā to sever the two obstructions and attain nirvāṇa — "causal reliance, the benefit of severing obstructive defilements." All Buddhas rely on prajñā to attain bodhi — "fruition reliance, the benefit of attaining bodhi." Nirvāṇa is the achievement of disconnection (severance-merit); bodhi is the achievement of realization (wisdom-merit). Severance is for the sake of realization; realization encompasses severance.

What is the content of bodhi? Kuiji analyzes it through the five dharmas: the pure dharma-realm (tathatā, dharmakāya, nirvāṇa) + the four wisdoms (ādarśa-jñāna, samatā-jñāna, pratyavekṣaṇā-jñāna, kṛtyānuṣṭhāna-jñāna).[^41] Nirvāṇa is one of the five; bodhi includes nirvāṇa plus the fully operational four wisdoms. The bodhisattva attains nirvāṇa through prajñā; the Buddha attains nirvāṇa plus the four wisdoms through prajñā. The latter contains the former.

Prajñā is the sole guide on this path from beginning to end. Fazang captures it in a single phrase: "All Buddhas of the three times have no other path — only this single gate."[^43] All Buddhas, past, present, and future, walked no other road. This is what the Heart Sūtra accomplishes in a single sentence: elevating prajñā from one person's practice to the common foundation of all awakening.

### 3.3 The Four-Fold Efficacy of Prajñā: The Progression of the Four Mantras

"The great spirit mantra, the great illumination mantra, the unsurpassed mantra, the unequalled mantra" — these are not four different mantras but four dimensions of the same prajñā's efficacy. Kuiji's four characterizations form a progression from function to essence:

**The great spirit mantra: marvelous functioning without limit.** Prajñā's efficacy is inconceivable and boundless. It is not a tool for solving one particular problem — it comprehensively contemplates all dharmas, and nothing escapes it. Ask prajñā about the five aggregates, and prajñā illuminates their emptiness. Ask about the twelve links, and prajñā illuminates their emptiness. Ask about the four noble truths, and prajñā illuminates their emptiness. Ask prajñā about prajñā itself, and prajñā illuminates its own emptiness — "without wisdom and without attainment." This marvelous functioning knows no boundary; hence "great spirit."

**The great illumination mantra: illuminating even the most hidden.** Prajñā is light. No darkness escapes its reach. Avidyā (ignorance) is literally "non-seeing." Prajñā is the precise antidote — it shines into the deepest recesses of ignorance. S2-P08 demonstrated that ignorance is not a thing but a perceptual inversion. Prajñā illuminates the inversion itself, bringing light to the darkest place. Hence "great illumination."

**The unsurpassed mantra: supreme, nothing above it.** No dharma surpasses prajñā. Giving, morality, patience, vigor, and meditation — the other five pāramitās all require prajñā to govern them. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitāśāstra* states that the five pāramitās without prajñā are merely conditioned merit that cannot progress toward bodhi.[^44] Prajñā is the chief of the six pāramitās, the mother of the other five; hence "unsurpassed."

**The unequalled mantra: nothing to compare with.** Prajñā equals the Buddha. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitāśāstra*, fascicle 40, states: "Buddhas are called 'the unequalled'; prajñāpāramitā gives birth to the Buddhas, and therefore it is called 'the unequalled-equalling pāramitā.'"[^45] The Buddhas are the peerless ones (unequalled) in the world; prajñā gives birth to the Buddhas, and thus equals the peerless — "un-equalled-equalling." This is the ontological verdict on prajñā's nature.

The four mantras progress from outward function (overcoming obstacles) to inward illumination (dispelling darkness) to transcendence (surpassing everything) to identity with the source (equaling the Buddha). They are prajñā's four dimensions of self-recognition. Readers who have followed S2-P01 through S2-P10 will note that the great spirit mantra encapsulates S2-P04 through S2-P09's comprehensive contemplation, the great illumination mantra encapsulates S2-P08's illumination of ignorance, the unsurpassed mantra encapsulates S2-P09's "without wisdom and without attainment," and the unequalled mantra encapsulates S2-P10's ultimate nirvāṇa and the present passage's buddha-bodhi. The four mantras compress ten papers into four phrases.

### 3.4 The Unity of Exoteric and Esoteric: One Principle, Two Modes

At the very end, the Heart Sūtra shifts from prose prajñā into the gate-gate mantra. Fazang divides the sūtra into two sections:

> "First, the explicit prajñā; afterward, the secret prajñā. Why distinguish these two? Explicit and clear speech generates wisdom-understanding and extinguishes the obstruction of afflictions. The secret words of the mantra, through recitation, generate merit and extinguish the obstruction of karmic offenses. To extinguish the two obstructions and accomplish the two adornments [of merit and wisdom], these two sections are taught."[^46]

Explicit prajñā — all the preceding doctrinal analysis — generates wisdom-understanding and addresses the kleśāvaraṇa (affliction-obstruction). Secret prajñā — the mantra — generates merit through recitation and addresses the karmāvaraṇa (karmic-offense-obstruction). Together, they accomplish the twofold adornment of merit (puṇya-vyūha) and wisdom (jñāna-vyūha).

This division is precise and practical: exoteric teaching cultivates wisdom (understanding), esoteric practice cultivates merit (action). This is not a hierarchy but a complementarity.

The *Heart Sūtra Commentary* (T1714) explains from the perspective of practitioners' capacities: "The former is the explicit teaching of prajñā; the latter is the secret teaching of prajñā. Since it was already taught explicitly, why also teach it secretly? Because sentient beings differ in their faculties, and what they can enter differs."[^16] Some enter through doctrine; some enter through mantra recitation. Same prajñā, different gates.

Kuiji's four kinds of dhāraṇī unify the entire sūtra: the preceding text constitutes dharma-dhāraṇī (condensing extensive teaching) and artha-dhāraṇī (condensing extensive meaning), while the gate-gate mantra constitutes mantra-dhāraṇī (secret total retention).[^13] Three kinds of dhāraṇī together express prajñā completely — expanded doctrinally, contracted mantrically.

The relationship between exoteric and esoteric is not sequential, hierarchical, or oppositional; it is one principle expressed in two modes. Just as water can be liquid or solid — the form differs, but H₂O is the same. Prajñā's "principle-body" is emptiness, reality, the way things actually are. Exoteric teaching uses conceptual language to unfold this principle into comprehensible doctrine. Esoteric teaching uses mantra to compress this principle into recitable sound. Kuiji says the mantra's meaning is "not easy to elaborate" — not because it lacks doctrine, but because its doctrine is too dense; kalpas of wisdom and compassion compressed into a few syllables.[^21] If unfolded, it would be even more extensive than the preceding prose prajñā.

### 3.5 The Dharma-Eye: Why Wisdom Is More Precious Than Treasures

Returning to the dharma-eye metaphor.

Why do four lines of verse surpass immeasurable asaṃkhyeya worlds of seven treasures? Because treasures give you environment (better karmic fruits), while the dharma-eye gives you capacity (seeing reality as it is). Environment changes — treasures are eventually exhausted, favorable rebirths eventually end. Capacity does not — once you have seen the emptiness of the five aggregates, that seeing itself is irreversible.

The Diamond Sūtra's fourth comparison is the most extreme: sacrificing Ganges-sand bodies three times daily for immeasurable kalpas — yet "if someone hears this sūtra and generates irreversible faith, that merit surpasses the former."[^47] Note that what surpasses is not "understanding this sūtra" but "irreversible faith" — the mere generation of unshakable trust in prajñā already exceeds immeasurable kalpas of bodily sacrifice.

Why? Because irreversible faith is the dharma-eye beginning to open. Trusting that there exists a path to seeing reality as it is means your eyes are beginning to focus. This trust is not blind faith — its precondition is having "heard this sūtra," meaning hearing-perfuming is already at work. This aligns perfectly with the beginner framework among the three practitioners — recitation plants seeds, faith is the beginning.

The *Vajracchedikā-ṭīkā* says it most directly: "Seven-treasure merit is the cause of affliction-stained [karma], because it can bring about affliction-laden results."[^48] Seven-treasure charity, if accompanied by the mark-grasping mind — "I am doing a good deed; I have merit" — has that very "I" as an affliction-cause. Mark-grasping merit is conditioned; it circulates within saṃsāra and does not lead out. Prajñā's four-line verse is "merit without mark-grasping" — it does not accumulate within saṃsāra but points directly to the exit.

The dharma-eye metaphor ultimately returns to the *Mahāprajñāpāramitāśāstra*'s judgment: when prajñā is present, the Buddha is present.[^8] Because prajñā is the dharma-eye itself. As long as someone is reciting the Heart Sūtra, hearing prajñā, contemplating emptiness — the dharma-eye remains in the world. The three practitioners — beginner, intermediate, advanced — are all bearers of the dharma-eye. They see to different depths, but the direction of their seeing is the same: from suffering toward liberation, from darkness toward illumination, from saṃsāra toward nirvāṇa, and from nirvāṇa toward the bodhi that does not abide in nirvāṇa.

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Dialogue

### ⚠️ Heuristic Analogy, Not Theoretical Equivalence

The Soviet psychologist Lev Vygotsky's theory of the Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD) provides a modern educational analogy for the three practitioners.

Vygotsky observed that for any given task, learners occupy three zones: what they can already accomplish independently (the comfort zone), what they can accomplish with guidance (the zone of proximal development), and what they currently cannot accomplish at all (the zone beyond reach). Effective teaching does not leave learners in the comfort zone or throw them into the zone beyond reach; it operates precisely within the ZPD by providing "scaffolding" — structural support enabling learners to achieve what they could not achieve alone.[^49]

The three practitioners correspond to three different zones of proximal development.

The beginner's ZPD: independently comprehending the emptiness of the five aggregates or dependent origination is in the zone beyond reach. But the beginner can recite. The Heart Sūtra's sounds, rhythm, and fixed structure of 260 characters serve as scaffolding. Understanding is not required; the scaffold holds. Hearing-perfuming operates without the beginner's awareness — Vygotsky would say that social interaction (hearing the Buddha's teaching) occurs externally before it is internalized as individual cognition.

The intermediate practitioner's ZPD: this practitioner can already independently reflect on "What is suffering?" and "What is attachment?" The ZPD lies in "applying the sūtra's text to one's own life experience" — guidance is needed (teachers, the paper series), but once provided, the practitioner can walk on their own. The S2 series of ten papers is scaffolding built for the intermediate practitioner.

The advanced practitioner's ZPD: this practitioner can already contemplate, but between contemplation and irreversibility (non-retrogressive contemplation) there remains a distance. What scaffolds the advanced practitioner? No longer external teaching, but prajñā itself in operation — each recitation is a rehearsal, a calibration, a clearing of the mirror. The scaffold has been internalized as structure.

Another key element of Vygotsky's theory — the "more knowledgeable other" (MKO) — maps in the Buddhist context to the Buddha and the kalyāṇa-mitra (spiritual friend). The dharma-realm outflow of right hearing-perfuming flows from the "more knowledgeable other" (the Buddha's teaching, the spiritual friend's guidance) to the learner. This is not unidirectional transmission — the learner must actively engage within their ZPD (reciting, reflecting, contemplating) for the scaffold to function.

A critical limitation must be noted: this is a heuristic analogy. Vygotsky's theory concerns mundane cognitive development; the Buddhist three practitioners concern supramundane āśraya-parāvṛtti. The ZPD's "development" moves from "cannot do" to "can do"; Buddhist "awakening" is not about acquiring a new ability but about removing obstruction — the innate purity was never absent, only concealed by ignorance. After scaffolding is removed, Vygotsky's learner has gained a new skill; after prajñā illuminates, the practitioner discovers not something new but their original face. The analogy ends here.

## V. Middle Way Corrections

### ❌ Correction 1: Recitation does not automatically lead to Buddhahood

The hearing-perfuming doctrine does affirm that "recitation plants seeds," but seeds require conditions to sprout. The *Mahāyānasaṃgraha* specifies "right hearing" — hearing that is undistorted, with focused reverence.[^50] If someone recites the Heart Sūtra while simultaneously creating unwholesome karma, hearing-perfuming still plants seeds — but unwholesome karma is simultaneously planting its own seeds, and both sets operate within the ālaya-vijñāna. Hearing-perfuming is not a magical immunity; it is a force that requires the cooperation of right faith and right conduct to fully manifest. The beginner needs no prerequisites, but "recitation plants seeds" cannot be conflated with "recitation regardless of anything else leads to Buddhahood."

### ❌ Correction 2: Exoteric and esoteric are not hierarchically ranked

Fazang states that "explicit prajñā extinguishes the affliction-obstruction; secret prajñā extinguishes the karmic-offense-obstruction" — this is functional complementarity, not a ranking that places esoteric above exoteric. Some readers, upon encountering the mantra section, conclude that "esoteric practice is the real practice" and that the preceding doctrinal analysis was merely preliminary. This is a misreading. Kuiji's four kinds of dhāraṇī clearly show that dharma-retention, meaning-retention, and mantra-retention are different faces of the same prajñā. Without the preceding doctrine (exoteric), the mantra (esoteric) becomes groundless sound. Without the mantra's total retention, doctrine remains at the conceptual level, unable to penetrate deeper into the mind. The two condition each other.

### ❌ Correction 3: Mantras are not magic

"Removing all suffering" does not mean that reciting a mantra causes suffering to automatically vanish. Kuiji's commentary on this phrase cites the "ten dharma practices" — copying, making offerings, giving to others, listening, reading, retaining, correctly expounding, chanting, reflecting, and cultivating.[^51] The mantra's power lies in its compression of these ten practices into a single function. "Removing all suffering" presupposes "faith, learning, realization, and teaching." Kuiji further specifies: "How much more so when one is deeply sincere, the cause planted and the fruit ripened." Treating mantras as "recite and it works" incantations is a thorough misunderstanding of prajñā.

### ❌ Correction 4: The three practitioners are not fixed identities

The beginner, intermediate practitioner, and advanced practitioner described in this paper are not three types of people but three states. The same person at different times and under different conditions can move among the three states. A longtime practitioner who encounters intense affliction may temporarily return to the "beginner" state — reciting for comfort rather than contemplation. A newcomer to the Heart Sūtra, if conditions are exceptionally favorable, may in a single instant enter the advanced practitioner's direct contemplation. The three grades of hearing-perfuming (lower, middle, upper) are not fixed ranks but dynamically flowing states.

### ❌ Correction 5: "Prajñā as mother of Buddhas" is not a gender or birth metaphor

The *Mahāprajñāpāramitāśāstra*, fascicle 34, explicitly explains the metaphorical meaning of "mother": "Because its merit is the greatest, it is called the mother."[^5] "Mother" here is a functional judgment — the one whose contribution is greatest in the awakening causal chain — not a metaphor involving gender or biological reproduction. The pratyutpanna-samādhi (concentration) is called the "father" because of its specific function (collecting the scattered mind to enable wisdom), not because it is "male." Reading "prajñā as mother of all Buddhas" through frameworks of gender or procreation obscures the text's actual doctrinal content.

## VI. Conclusion

### 6.1 Threefold Closure

This paper accomplishes a threefold closure.

First, from bodhisattva-fruit to buddha-fruit. S2-P10's bodhisattva relying on prajñā to attain ultimate nirvāṇa is the benefit of severance at the causal stage. P11's all Buddhas relying on prajñā to attain anuttara-samyak-saṃbodhi is the benefit of realization at the fruition stage. Nirvāṇa is contained within bodhi — Kuiji's five dharmas unify them: the pure dharma-realm (nirvāṇa is here) + the four wisdoms = bodhi. From nirvāṇa to bodhi is not a change of path but the same path of prajñā walked to greater depth.

Second, from exoteric to esoteric. S2-P01 through S2-P10 constituted the doctrinal unfolding of exoteric prajñā — the five aggregates, emptiness, ignorance, nirvāṇa. P11's gate-gate mantra is the mantra-retention of esoteric prajñā — the same principle, compressed into sound. Fazang says the exoteric extinguishes the affliction-obstruction and the esoteric extinguishes the karmic-offense-obstruction; together they accomplish the twofold adornment of merit and wisdom. The Heart Sūtra is, in itself, a complete teaching of exoteric-esoteric unity.

Third, from one person to all persons. Awakening is not exclusively the business of advanced practitioners. When a beginner recites the Heart Sūtra once, a seed enters the ālaya-vijñāna — a dharmakāya seed. When an intermediate practitioner uses the sūtra to illuminate their own mental knots — thinking-wisdom is at work. When an advanced practitioner liberates while reciting — cultivation-wisdom is turning the basis. Three practitioners, one sūtra. The starting points differ; the direction is the same.

### 6.2 The Value of the Dharma-Eye

The dharma-eye metaphor has threaded through this paper. We possess eyes but do not know their value until we lose them — only then do we know they are priceless. Prajñā is the eye of the mind. The Diamond Sūtra repeatedly uses seven-treasure comparisons to make the point: circumstances can be improved, but circumstances change; the dharma-eye can be opened, and once opened, does not decay. Vasubandhu's criterion of "progressive meaning" captures it in a word: treasures can only accumulate; the dharma-eye carries you forward.

All three practitioners are polishing the same dharma-eye. The beginner plants the dharma-eye's seed in the ground. The intermediate practitioner lets the seed sprout — beginning to see. The advanced practitioner opens the dharma-eye fully — seeing and liberating in the same moment.

### 6.3 Retrospective and Prospective

From S2-P01 (*Prajñā-Hṛdaya-Sūtra: The Title as Teaching*) to S2-P11 (*Three Practitioners, One Sūtra: The Unity of Exoteric and Esoteric*), eleven papers have traversed all 260 characters of the Heart Sūtra.

S2-P01 decoded the title: prajñā is wisdom, pāramitā is reaching the other shore, hṛdaya is the essence, sūtra is the path. S2-P02 entered the gate: Avalokiteśvara is the causal longing for freedom. S2-P03 went deep: "practicing deeply" means turning inward to the formations-aggregate. S2-P04 illuminated: the emptiness of the five aggregates is an inherent endowment, not an acquisition. S2-P05 deconstructed: "form is emptiness" reveals the assembly line of suffering. S2-P06 revealed the dharma-nature: "neither arising nor ceasing" is the true mark of this very mind. S2-P07 contemplated the faculties: "in emptiness there is no form" is the awareness of sense-bases, objects, and consciousness. S2-P08 overturned perception: ignorance is not a thing but a perceptual inversion. S2-P09 released the tools: "without suffering, origination, cessation, or path" means even the instruments of practice are released. S2-P10 traversed terror: "mind without obstruction" is the traversal and transformation of existential fear. S2-P11 achieved unity: three practitioners, exoteric-esoteric unity, from seed to Buddhahood.

What is the starting point of this entire arc? Generating the mind of awakening. S1-P01 demonstrated the butterfly effect of initial aspiration; S1-P02 demonstrated the Dharma-stream's reception. The beginning lies not in ability but in aspiration — are you willing to recite this one Heart Sūtra? The moment of willingness is the moment conditions are met, seeds enter the ground, and the dharma-eye begins to focus.

Ahead lie S3 (the *Śatadharmaprakāśamukha-śāstra*, the *Saṃdhinirmocana-sūtra*, and the *Bāshí Guījǔ Sòng* — the body, teaching, and function of Yogācāra) and S5 (the Diamond Sūtra — the recursive deconstruction of "is called / is not / is named"). S2's prajñā illuminated emptiness; S3 will provide the precision map that follows emptiness — how the five universal mental factors operate, how the eight consciousnesses transform, how the hundred dharmas find their places. S5's Diamond Sūtra will push S2's prajñā to its limit — "what is called X is not X; it is named X" — and even the Heart Sūtra itself is a finger pointing at the moon; the moon is not on the finger.

But that lies ahead. For now —

> Gate gate pāragate pārasaṃgate bodhi svāhā.

Cross. Ferry others across. To the other shore. Everyone together to the other shore. Awakening. Accomplished.
