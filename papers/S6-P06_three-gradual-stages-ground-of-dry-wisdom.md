## Abstract

The previous paper (S6-P05) identified the door to practice through Mañjuśrī's selection of ear-faculty perfect penetration (耳根圓通, ěrgēn yuántōng). Finding the door, however, is not entering it. Rather than proceeding immediately to practice methodology, the Śūraṅgama Sūtra interposes an entire fascicle (fascicle 7) devoted to establishing the practice sanctuary and expounding the Śūraṅgama dhāraṇī before formally introducing the Three Gradual Stages (三種漸次, sān zhǒng jiàncì) in fascicle 8: removing auxiliary causes (除其助因), excising root nature (刳其正性), and reversing present karma (違其現業). These stages constitute not practice itself but preparation for practice — a "purification protocol" the Buddha illustrates through the metaphor of removing poisoned honey from a vessel before it can hold amṛta. Upon completion, the practitioner enters the Ground of Dry Wisdom (乾慧地, gān huì dì): wisdom-observation has formed but the water of meditative stability has not yet moistened it — a starting line, not a destination. The sūtra's most architecturally significant feature emerges at the conclusion of the fifty-five stages, when the equal-enlightenment bodhisattva returns to the "initial Ground of Dry Wisdom within the vajra mind" — the same name as the beginner's ground, disclosing the circular rather than linear structure of the Śūraṅgama's stage system. This paper further argues that the Śūraṅgama's Four Preparatory Stages (四加行, sì jiāxíng) — sharing names but not content with Yogācāra's four wholesome roots — describe not a single stage in a linear path but a recursive pattern of threshold-experience implanted at every transition within the fifty-five stages.

**Keywords:** Three Gradual Stages, removing auxiliary causes, excising root nature, reversing present karma, Ground of Dry Wisdom (śuṣka-vidarśanā-bhūmi), fifty-five stages, Four Preparatory Stages (catvāri kuśala-mūlāni), Śūraṅgama Sūtra, ear-faculty perfect penetration, circular stage structure

## I. Introduction: Amṛta Cannot Be Poured into a Poisoned Cup

The last sentence of the previous paper was: "That which hears has always been there."[^1]

If it has always been there, why can we not hear it?

The Śūraṅgama's answer is neither "because you are not intelligent enough" nor "because your karmic obstructions are too heavy." It is remarkably concrete: because there is poison in your cup.

> "As one removes poisoned honey from a clean vessel, washes it thoroughly with water, ash, and fragrant herbs, and only then stores amṛta within it."[^2]

The amṛta has always been available. The problem lies not with the amṛta but with the vessel. Residual poisoned honey clings to the walls — invisible to the naked eye, yet sufficient to contaminate anything poured in. The Three Gradual Stages constitute the process of washing that vessel clean.

One further observation merits attention: after Mañjuśrī's selection of the ear-faculty at the end of fascicle 6, the sūtra does not proceed directly to the Three Gradual Stages. An entire fascicle intervenes — fascicle 7 is devoted entirely to establishing the practice sanctuary and expounding the Śūraṅgama dhāraṇī. Why does the Buddha not move straight to the practice stages?

Because preparation itself requires preparation. The boundary-setting of the sanctuary, the protection of body and mind, the empowerment of the dhāraṇī — these constitute environmental preparation before the purification protocol can begin. Fascicle 7 prepares the space; fascicle 8's Three Gradual Stages prepare the mind.

From the Twenty-Five Perfect Penetrations (P05, "choosing the door") to the Three Gradual Stages (P06, "purification"), the sūtra's pedagogical logic is clear: you now know which door to enter, but before crossing the threshold, first wash yourself clean.

## II. Textual Foundations

### A. Sūtra Sources

#### 2.1 The Three Gradual Stages: Overview and the Poisoned Honey Metaphor

The Buddha introduces the Three Gradual Stages against the backdrop of the twelve categories of beings and their inversions, identifying all delusory proliferations as arising from the "inverted, wondrously perfect, truly pure bright mind" (顛倒妙圓真淨明心):

> "You now cultivate and realize the Buddha's samādhi. With regard to the fundamental causes of these original delusory thoughts, establish three gradual stages to achieve their elimination. ... What are these three? First, through practice, remove the auxiliary causes. Second, through true cultivation, excise the root nature. Third, through advancement, reverse present karma."[^3]

The architecture is precise: each stage employs paired verbs — the upper clause specifying the mode of cultivation, the lower specifying what must be eliminated. The three verbs of elimination — *remove* (除, chú), *excise* (刳, kū), *reverse* (違, wéi) — escalate in intensity, mapping the deepening logic of purification.

#### 2.2 First Stage: Removing Auxiliary Causes — The Five Pungent Herbs

The first stage targets the most external and identifiable category of obstruction: dietary stimulants that destabilize the practitioner's subtle mental states.

> "Those who seek samādhi should eliminate the five pungent herbs of the world. These five, when cooked, stimulate lust; when eaten raw, increase anger."[^4]

The sūtra details a consequence chain of remarkable specificity: consuming the five pungent herbs → celestial beings withdraw → hungry ghosts draw near → protective deities do not guard → Māra gains opportunity. This is not moral judgment but causal description — auxiliary causes left unremoved create chain reactions at deeper levels of practice.

#### 2.3 Second Stage: Excising Root Nature — Strict Observance of Precepts

> "Those entering samādhi must first strictly maintain pure precepts, permanently sever lustful thoughts, abstain from alcohol and meat, and eat only fire-purified food. ... First maintain the śrāvaka precepts of the four and eight grave prohibitions — holding the body motionless. Then practice the bodhisattva's pure discipline — holding the mind unmoving."[^5]

The verb *excise* (刳) — literally "to gouge out," as one might remove a tumor — is more radical than the *remove* (除) of the first stage. The first stage clears external stimulants; the second gouges out internal root tendencies (淫盜殺妄, lust-theft-killing-falsehood).

The passage's most significant structural feature is its two-sentence progression: "First hold the body motionless (執身不動) through śrāvaka precepts; then hold the mind unmoving (執心不起) through bodhisattva discipline." This encodes the principle of gradual internalization — from bodily conduct to mental activity, from behavioral compliance to the cessation of the impulse itself.

#### 2.4 Third Stage: Reversing Present Karma — Redirecting Habitual Momentum

> "What is present karma? ... The pure precept-keeper, mind free of lustful craving, does not flow outward toward the six sense-objects. Because of this non-outflowing, one returns to the source. Since objects are no longer engaged, the roots have no counterparts. Reversing the flow into unity, the six functions cease to operate. ... This person immediately attains the patience of the non-arising of dharmas."[^6]

The third stage represents the deepest tier of purification. *Reverse* (違) — to go against, to counter — targets not any specific behavior or substance but the default direction of mental operation: the habitual momentum by which the six sense faculties chase their six objects (流逸奔趣). This habitual outward flow operates automatically, without requiring conscious initiation.

Four characters capture the core operation: *reverse the flow into unity* (反流全一, fǎn liú quán yī). This precisely parallels the ear-faculty perfect penetration's "entering the flow, losing the object" (入流亡所) from P05 — not suppressing the faculties but reversing their outward momentum into inward return.

The result is remarkable: "This person immediately attains the patience of the non-arising of dharmas" (無生法忍, anutpattika-dharma-kṣānti) — a major milestone on the bodhisattva path.

#### 2.5 The Ground of Dry Wisdom: Starting Line and Finish Line

Upon completing the Three Gradual Stages, the practitioner enters the first named stage:

> "The good man's desire-love has dried up, roots and objects no longer paired, present residual form no longer continuing to arise. The held mind is empty-bright, purely wisdom. The wisdom-nature, bright and complete, illuminates the ten directions. Dry in its wisdom — this is called the Ground of Dry Wisdom."[^7]

"Dry in its wisdom" (乾有其慧) — the paradox is precise. Wisdom-observation has been established ("purely wisdom, wisdom-nature bright and complete"), yet something essential is missing: "Not yet connected with the Tathāgata's dharma-flow water."[^8]

The vessel has been thoroughly cleaned, but a clean vessel does not mean the vessel contains water. Purification is a necessary condition, not a sufficient one.

The sūtra's most architecturally significant move, however, occurs at the conclusion of the fifty-five stages. After the full journey from the ten faiths through equal enlightenment, the Buddha declares:

> "Ānanda, from the Dry Wisdom mind to equal enlightenment — this enlightenment first obtains the initial Ground of Dry Wisdom within the vajra mind. Thus, layer upon layer, single and compound twelve times, one exhausts wondrous enlightenment and achieves the unsurpassed Way."[^9]

The Ground of Dry Wisdom appears twice: first as the beginner's starting line ("not yet connected with the dharma-flow water"), then as the equal-enlightenment bodhisattva's final return ("the initial Ground of Dry Wisdom within the vajra mind"). Same name, utterly different depth. This circular structure — beginning and end sharing the same name — is the defining characteristic of the Śūraṅgama's stage system and marks its divergence from linear models.

#### 2.6 Overview of the Fifty-Five Stages

Following the Ground of Dry Wisdom, the sūtra unfolds the complete stage structure in fascicle 8[^10]:

| Stage Group | Count | Core Characteristic |
|-------------|-------|-------------------|
| Ground of Dry Wisdom | 1 | Wisdom formed, samādhi-water not yet received |
| Ten Faiths | 10 | Establishing stable roots of faith |
| Ten Abodes | 10 | Entering the Tathāgata's family |
| Ten Practices | 10 | Benefiting sentient beings |
| Ten Dedications | 10 | Redirecting merit toward bodhi |
| Four Preparatory Stages | 4 | Warmth, summit, patience, supreme worldly dharma |
| Ten Grounds | 10 | Partial realization of the dharma-body |
| Equal Enlightenment | 1 | One lifetime until Buddhahood |

#### 2.7 The Śūraṅgama's Four Preparatory Stages: Same Name, Different Teaching

The Śūraṅgama's Four Preparatory Stages share identical names with those of the Yogācāra tradition (warmth, summit, patience, supreme worldly dharma) but describe fundamentally different experiential content. This is not merely two languages describing the same thing but two doctrinal systems approaching the same territory from different directions.[^11]

**Warmth (煖地):** "As if emerging yet not yet emerged — like drilling for fire when the wood is about to ignite."

Yogācāra specifies the technical operation: "Relying on the light-obtaining samādhi, generating the lower investigative contemplation, observing that there is nothing apprehended." The Śūraṅgama offers only an image: fire *about to emerge but not yet emerged*. This captures a threshold experience — the practitioner senses the precursor of qualitative change without yet achieving definitive breakthrough. Yogācāra tells you what you are observing; the Śūraṅgama tells you what it feels like.

**Summit (頂地):** "Below, a subtle obstruction remains."

Four characters only: "below, subtle obstruction" (下有微礙). Not the gross obstructions cleared by the Three Gradual Stages, but the final residual, almost imperceptible resistance. The practitioner believes full transparency has been achieved, yet something — barely detectable — remains. Yogācāra describes the same position as "the extreme of investigative contemplation" (尋思位極); the Śūraṅgama uses the language of bodily sensation to convey the experience of *almost but not quite*.

**Patience (忍地):** "Like one enduring something that can neither be held within nor released."

This stage most sharply distinguishes the two systems. Yogācāra's patience is epistemological: "Definitively confirming and sustaining the non-apprehension of the apprehended" — a cognitive operation, the irrevocable confirmation that objects are empty. The Śūraṅgama's patience is existential: like someone bearing something that cannot be possessed (非懷, fēi huái) nor expressed (非出, fēi chū). The practitioner has touched something real, but this reality transcends the subject-object relationship — it cannot be turned into a possession, nor can it be externalized as an object of knowledge.

**Supreme Worldly Dharma (世第一地):** "In the middle way between delusion and enlightenment, neither can be targeted."

The Śūraṅgama's most kōan-like formulation: "Delusion-enlightenment middle way, the two having nothing to target" (迷覺中道，二無所目). "Delusion" and "enlightenment" constitute the practitioner's fundamental reference frame — *I am deluded, I must become enlightened*. The breakthrough of supreme worldly dharma is that this very frame dissolves. Not "no longer deluded" (which still implies enlightenment as counterpart), not "enlightened" (which still implies delusion as what was overcome), but the middle way between them in which even "targeting" (目) — the cognitive act of objectification, of aiming — ceases to exist. Yogācāra's "stamping the emptiness of both apprehended and apprehender" (印二取空) dissolves the subject-object structure. The Śūraṅgama's "neither can be targeted" dissolves the practice coordinate system itself.

**The Four Preparatory Stages as Recursive Pattern**

Within the fifty-five stages, these four are positioned between the ten dedications and the ten grounds, consistent with Yogācāra's structural placement. But the sūtra's circular architecture — "layer upon layer, single and compound twelve times" (重重單複十二) — implies something far beyond a linear reading.

If the entire fifty-five stages are traversed not once but recursively at different depths, then the four experiential qualities described here — threshold-sensing (warmth), residual-obstruction (summit), existential-bearing (patience), frame-dissolution (supreme worldly dharma) — constitute not a single stage but a recurring pattern at every transitional threshold. The crossing from ten faiths to ten abodes has its own warmth, its own subtle obstruction, its own bearing of what can neither be held nor released, its own dissolution of the previous framework. Every tier-crossing repeats the same four-beat rhythm: threshold → obstruction → bearing → dissolution.

This is the fundamental divergence between the Śūraṅgama's and Yogācāra's Four Preparatory Stages. Yogācāra's version is a specific stage on a linear path, with precise prerequisites, precise tools, and a precise destination.[^12] The Śūraṅgama's version is a transformation-pattern realizable at different depths — its imagistic rather than analytical language exists precisely because it describes not a single specific operation but the universal structure of every threshold-crossing. The extreme condensation of each formulation (none exceeds a dozen characters) is not due to unimportance but to reusability: each must be compact enough to be recognized as a pattern at whatever depth the practitioner encounters it.

### B. Commentarial Sources

#### 2.8 Zǐxuán: Remove, Excise, Reverse — Escalating Force of Three Verbs

Zǐxuán (子璿, *Commentary on the Śūraṅgama*, T1799) offers a precise taxonomy of the three stages:

> "The five pungent herbs are called auxiliary causes because they assist afflictions and karma. Lust, theft, killing, and falsehood are called root nature because they are the fundamental enemies of liberation from saṃsāra. The pairing of faculties and objects, flowing outward in pursuit — this is precisely the functional activity of avidyā's present karma. Therefore: remove and discard them, excise and empty them, reverse and turn away from them."[^13]

The three verbs constitute a perfect escalation: *remove* (除) targets what is external and visible; *excise* (刳) targets what is internal and deep-rooted; *reverse* (違) targets what is most hidden — not a thing but a direction, not a behavior but a default orientation of consciousness.

Zǐxuán further identifies the completion of the third stage with the culminating experience of Avalokiteśvara's perfect penetration:

> "The sole perfectly permanent, wondrously awakened bright essence — this is identical to Avalokiteśvara's perfect penetration: 'arising-and-ceasing having ceased, quiescent cessation manifests.'"[^14]

This judgment is structurally significant: it directly links P05 (the Avalokiteśvara method) with P06 (the Three Gradual Stages), confirming that choosing the door (P05) and preparing to enter it (P06) are two aspects of a single practice process.

#### 2.9 Jiāoguāng: Three Stages Mapped to Ear-Faculty Penetration

Jiāoguāng Zhēnjiàn (交光真鑑, *Orthodox Commentary on the Śūraṅgama*, X0275) proposes a structurally innovative correspondence, mapping each gradual stage to a phase of the ear-faculty perfect penetration[^15]:

| Gradual Stage | Ear-Faculty Phase | Characteristic |
|---------------|-------------------|---------------|
| First: Remove auxiliary causes | "Entering the flow, losing the object" | Initial practice, intermittent, "not yet unified" |
| Second: Excise root nature | "Movement and stillness both cease" | Practice "becoming unified," forgetting even stillness |
| Third: Reverse present karma | "Hearing and what is heard exhausted" | Faculty and object both dissolved, the outward flow completely reversed |

This correspondence rests on a deep structural insight: the Three Gradual Stages and the ear-faculty penetration describe the same practice process in two registers. The stages speak from the perspective of disciplinary purification; the ear-faculty method speaks from the perspective of contemplative deepening. Mañjuśrī in fascicle 6 selected the door (the ear); the Buddha in fascicle 8 teaches how to prepare for entry (the three stages) — and the mode of entry turns out to be the very practice of "reversing hearing" (反聞) that Mañjuśrī prescribed.

Jiāoguāng also advances a significant doctrinal observation: the Śūraṅgama places the precept against lust first (whereas most sūtras place killing first), because "the energy of desire is coarse and turbid, contaminating the wondrous brightness"[^16] — for samādhi cultivation specifically, lust constitutes the coarsest obstruction.

The Śūraṅgama's description of the ten grounds represents the most condensed version in the entire bodhisattva-stage literature. Each ground receives only a single phrase, yet each points toward the full doctrinal elaboration found across five fascicles of the Avataṃsaka Sūtra[^17]:

#### 2.11 Yogācāra and Śūraṅgama Four Preparatory Stages: Comparative Table

Section 2.7 above developed the Śūraṅgama's distinctive treatment in detail. The following table highlights the core divergence between the two systems[^18]:

| Stage | Yogācāra (*Cheng Weishi Lun* fasc. 9) | Śūraṅgama (T0945 fasc. 8) | Point of Divergence |
|-------|---------------------------------------|---------------------------|-------------------|
| Warmth | Relying on light-obtaining samādhi, generating lower contemplation, observing non-apprehension | "As if emerging yet not emerged, like drilling fire about to ignite the wood" | Analytical operation vs. threshold experience |
| Summit | Relying on light-increasing samādhi, generating upper contemplation | "Below, a subtle obstruction remains" | Growth of wisdom-light vs. awareness of residual resistance |
| Patience | Definitively confirming non-apprehension of the apprehended | "Like one enduring something — neither held within nor released" | Epistemological confirmation vs. existential bearing |
| Supreme | Stamping the emptiness of both apprehended and apprehender | "Delusion-enlightenment middle way — neither can be targeted" | Dissolving subject-object structure vs. dissolving the practice coordinate system |

Yogācāra's Four Preparatory Stages are linear: the Path of Accumulation must be completed → the four wholesome roots follow → uninterrupted entry into the Path of Seeing. The Śūraṅgama's are recursive: the same four-beat pattern recurs at every threshold within the fifty-five stages. For the complete Yogācāra treatment — four investigative contemplations, four realizations of actuality, the four supporting samādhis, and the precise sequence of emptying the apprehended and apprehender — see S3-SUP01.

#### 2.12 Saṃdhinirmocana Śamatha-Vipaśyanā Framework: Heuristic Parallels

The Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra's *Chapter on the Analysis of Yoga* provides a śamatha-vipaśyanā framework that can serve as a heuristic reference for understanding the Three Gradual Stages[^19]:

The three stages can be loosely mapped onto the contemplative trajectory: removing auxiliary causes → preliminary purification (moral discipline as the basis of śamatha-vipaśyanā); excising root nature → śamatha achievement (the mind no longer flowing outward); reversing present karma → vipaśyanā achievement (deepening from images with characteristics to the limit of phenomena).

⚠️ This mapping is a heuristic analogy (假說比喻), not a direct scriptural teaching. The Śūraṅgama's Three Gradual Stages belong to the tathāgatagarbha doctrinal system; the Saṃdhinirmocana's śamatha-vipaśyanā theory belongs to the Yogācāra system. The two operate from different doctrinal architectures.

## III. Core Argument

### 3.1 The Three-Tiered Logic of Purification: Body → Morality → Habit

The central thesis: ✅ Doctrinally sound

The Three Gradual Stages do not constitute three parallel methods but a single deepening sequence of purification, each tier building upon the previous.

**First tier (physical/material): Remove auxiliary causes.** Targets external, visible, easily identifiable obstructions. The five pungent herbs are material — what you consume directly affects your body. This is the shallowest purification, but also the most immediately actionable. Practice begins with what is achievable right now.

**Second tier (moral/volitional): Excise root nature.** Targets behavioral patterns and moral choices rooted in deep mental tendencies. The progression from "holding the body motionless" (śrāvaka precepts) to "holding the mind unmoving" (bodhisattva discipline) demands continuous deepening.

**Third tier (habitual/directional): Reverse present karma.** Targets the most concealed obstacle — not any specific behavior or substance but the default direction of consciousness. The six faculties chasing their six objects operates as a background process, running without conscious initiation. The third stage does not shut down a single program but reverses the entire operating system's default direction.

The pedagogical implication: no skipping tiers. One cannot achieve "reversing the flow into unity" while still consuming the five pungent herbs — not because garlic is morally grave, but because physical-level stimulation, if not first cleared, deprives the mind of the sensitivity required to address deeper habitual patterns.

### 3.2 The Poisoned Honey Metaphor: Vessel, Poison, and Amṛta

The Buddha's choice of *poisoned honey* rather than *poison* merits contemplation. The defining characteristic of poisoned honey is that it is *honey* — sweet, viscous, pleasant. Its difficulty of removal lies not only in its toxicity but in its sweetness. The practitioner knows it is poisoned but hesitates to discard it because the sweetness is hard to relinquish. The five pungent herbs are listed first not only because they are stimulating but because humans are attached to sensory stimulation. Lust heads the "root nature" list not only because it obstructs the path but because it commands the strongest adhesion.

"Washing with water, ash, and fragrant herbs" — three cleaning agents for three depths: water (rinsing the surface), ash (cutting through grease), fragrance (purifying residual odor). The three stages correspond: eliminating herbs is the surface rinse; maintaining precepts is the deep-cleansing ash; reversing habitual momentum is the final fragrance that removes what cannot be seen or touched.

"Then store amṛta" — the amṛta has always existed; it is not manufactured by the practitioner. The entire work of the Three Gradual Stages is not to *produce* anything new but to *remove* what prevents the amṛta from entering. This is fully consistent with the Śūraṅgama's tathāgatagarbha position: buddha-nature is inherently complete; practice removes obscurations rather than creating enlightenment.

### 3.3 The Paradox of Dry Wisdom: Having Wisdom Without Water

"Dry in its wisdom" — a precise paradox. Wisdom *has been* established: "purely wisdom, wisdom-nature bright and complete." But it is *dry*: "not yet connected with the Tathāgata's dharma-flow water."

One can wash a cup thoroughly clean, but a clean cup does not thereby contain water. Purification is necessary but not sufficient. This addresses a common practice frustration: "I have worked so diligently — why has breakthrough not come?" The Ground of Dry Wisdom responds: completing preparation does not equal arriving at the destination. From Dry Wisdom to the ten faiths, what is needed is not more effort but a qualitative shift — from the effortful state of "I am practicing" to the responsive state of "the dharma-flow water connects."

### 3.4 Circular Structure: Beginning and End at Different Depths

The Ground of Dry Wisdom's dual appearance constitutes the key to understanding the Śūraṅgama's stage-theory.

The beginner's Dry Wisdom: hope and uncertainty — "not yet connected with the dharma-flow water." The equal-enlightenment bodhisattva's return to "the initial Ground of Dry Wisdom within the vajra mind": full circle at infinite depth — the same name, the same primordial clarity, but now as consummate return rather than tentative beginning.

This contrasts with the linear path of Yogācāra's five stages (Accumulation → Preparation → Seeing → Cultivation → Ultimate). Yogācāra describes a clear line; each stage has irreversible forward progression. The Śūraṅgama equally maintains strict stage-ordering, but its circular structure implies a perspective that transcends sequentiality: from the vantage of wondrous enlightenment, the entire fifty-five stages are "layer upon layer, single and compound twelve times" — always unfolding at different depths upon the same mind-essence.

### 3.5 Three Gradual Stages and Four Preparatory Stages as Recursive Architecture

If the Four Preparatory Stages constitute a recurring transformation-pattern (§2.7), do the Three Gradual Stages operate similarly?

The answer is embedded in the sūtra's structure. The Three Gradual Stages are positioned before the fifty-five stages, ostensibly as one-time preliminary preparation. But read in conjunction with the circular structure — the equal-enlightenment bodhisattva returning to the initial Ground of Dry Wisdom — each return to a deeper "Dry Wisdom" would require deeper "purification."

The crossing from ten faiths to ten abodes requires removing that level's "auxiliary causes" (subtle wavering of faith), excising that level's "root nature" (attachment to faith itself), and reversing that level's "present karma" (the habitual patterns of the faith-stage). Each new stage has its own "poisoned honey" — what was pure at the previous depth becomes obstruction at the new one.

Three Gradual Stages (purification pattern) + Four Preparatory Stages (breakthrough pattern) = the complete recursive architecture of Śūraṅgama practice. Before each door, purify; at each threshold, break through. Each breakthrough opens a new space, and each new space contains new honey to be cleaned. This recursion is not a hopeless endless cycle — it spirals upward, because each iteration of the same pattern occurs at a different depth. The beginner's "as if emerging yet not emerged" and the eighth-ground bodhisattva's "as if emerging yet not emerged" differ not in pattern but in the depth of consciousness bearing that pattern.

### 3.6 Jiāoguāng's Correspondence: Structural Continuity from P05 to P06

P05 (Twenty-Five Perfect Penetrations) concluded: Mañjuśrī selects the ear-faculty — "reverse hearing to hear the self-nature; the nature achieves the unsurpassed Way." P06 (Three Gradual Stages) concludes: "reversing the flow into unity, the six functions cease." The same verb — *reverse* (反) — appears in both.

P05 answers *what method* to use: the ear-faculty, reverse hearing. P06 answers *how to prepare* for reverse hearing: remove interference, excise root obstructions, reverse the outward flow — and the reversal itself *is* the beginning of reverse hearing.

Jiāoguāng's correspondence reveals that the Śūraṅgama's sections do not speak independently but constitute different facets of a single practice structure. Perfect penetration is *what to do*; the gradual stages are *how to become capable of doing it*; the Ground of Dry Wisdom is *the first step after becoming capable*.

## IV. Interdisciplinary Engagement

⚠️ **The following constitutes a heuristic analogy, not an equivalence claim. Buddhist practice purification is a transformation at the level of consciousness that cannot be reduced to any modern professional protocol.**

### 4.1 The Sterile Protocol: Pre-Surgical Purification Is Not Surgery

Modern surgery requires a strict sterile protocol before any incision. Three tiers of purification must be completed:

**First tier: Environmental sterilization.** Removing external contamination sources from the operating theater — dust, microorganisms, unclean instruments. The most basic step, performed first.

**Second tier: Pre-operative preparation.** The surgical team changes clothes, scrubs hands, dons sterile gowns; the patient fasts, undergoes site sterilization. This goes deeper than environmental cleaning — it concerns the participants' own state.

**Third tier: Time-out reversal.** Immediately before the incision, the entire team pauses — confirming patient identity, surgical site, and procedure, reversing any potential autopilot errors. This does not remove a specific contaminant but interrupts the team's habitual automatic mode, ensuring every step is conscious.

The structural parallel holds: removing auxiliary causes (environmental sterilization) → excising root nature (self-preparation) → reversing present karma (interrupting autopilot).

**But the differences are equally important.** Surgical sterility is a single event; the Three Gradual Stages are life-transformative and non-reversible. Surgical sterility is physical; the Three Gradual Stages address consciousness. Surgery treats the body's illness; the Three Gradual Stages create conditions for consciousness's fundamental reorientation.

**Irreducibility statement:** This analogy captures only the structural insight that high-precision operations require thorough prior purification and that purification itself is not the operation. Buddhist practice purification exceeds any material analogy: sterile protocols address external microorganisms verifiable by instruments; the Three Gradual Stages address internal afflictive seeds verifiable only by the practitioner's own awareness.

## V. Middle Way Corrections

### ❌ Misreading 1: The Three Gradual Stages as Buddhist Vegetarian Advocacy

Eliminating the five pungent herbs is not vegetarianism. The Śūraṅgama's emphasis lies not in the moral properties of food but in its effect on the practitioner's subtle mental states. The five herbs are singled out because they "stimulate lust when cooked, increase anger when eaten raw" — they directly destabilize the conditions for deep meditation. This is a practice-technical guideline about what interferes with samādhi, not a dietary-ethical argument about what one should eat. Reducing the Three Gradual Stages to vegetarian advocacy substitutes a moral framework for a practice framework.

### ❌ Misreading 2: The Ground of Dry Wisdom Equals Enlightenment

"Wisdom-nature bright and complete, illuminating the ten directions" sounds impressive, but the Ground of Dry Wisdom is the starting line, not the finish line. "Not yet connected with the Tathāgata's dharma-flow water" explicitly states that establishing wisdom-observation does not constitute entry into sainthood. Equating Dry Wisdom with enlightenment mistakes the completion of preparation for the completion of the task.

### ❌ Misreading 3: The Fifty-Five Stages Constitute a Fixed Linear Ladder

While the Śūraṅgama arranges its fifty-five stages in strict sequence, the circular structure ("from the Dry Wisdom mind to equal enlightenment — this enlightenment first obtains the initial Ground of Dry Wisdom within the vajra mind") implies a perspective transcending linearity. Moreover, different scriptural traditions employ significantly different stage systems — the Śūraṅgama's fifty-five, the Avataṃsaka's fifty-two, Yogācāra's five stages — describing the same practice trajectory through different classificatory frameworks. Absolutizing any single system is itself a form of attachment to dharma (法執).

### ⚠️ Caution 1: Jiāoguāng's Ear-Faculty Correspondence Is One Reading, Not the Only One

Jiāoguāng's mapping of the Three Gradual Stages to the ear-faculty penetration is an insightful interpretation, but it remains his reading, not a direct scriptural statement. Zǐxuán's reading (emphasizing the Three Gradual Stages' disciplinary axis) has equal doctrinal grounding. The two approaches complement rather than exclude each other.

### ⚠️ Caution 2: Precepts Are Not External Moral Constraints

Within the Śūraṅgama's context, maintaining precepts does not mean "following rules" in a social-moral sense. The progression from "holding the body motionless" to "holding the mind unmoving" makes clear that precepts serve as tools of internal purification, not behavioral policing. Externalizing precepts — caring only about behavioral compliance while ignoring the state of consciousness — is a fundamental misunderstanding of "excising root nature."

## VI. Conclusion: Are You Ready?

The Śūraṅgama uses the Three Gradual Stages to answer a question that is simple on its surface and profound in its depths: after finding the door, are you ready?

The preparation is three-tiered: remove external interference, excise internal root obstructions, reverse the default direction of consciousness. Each tier builds on the previous; none can be skipped.

After preparation, you stand at the Ground of Dry Wisdom — the starting line. Wisdom-observation has formed, but the dharma-flow water has not yet connected. You have done everything within your power; what follows requires not more effort but a qualitative resonance.

Reading this far, you may feel that this path is impossibly long. The scriptures speak of three incalculable great eons (三大阿僧祇劫) of practice to achieve Buddhahood. The first eon alone — from the Path of Accumulation to the first ground — already exceeds ordinary conception. Some monastics devote an entire lifetime of intensive practice and traverse only a small segment of this path. This is not the curriculum of a ten-day meditation retreat.

Yet the scriptures simultaneously tell you something else: that you have read this far, and that it resonates, is itself no accident. The doctrines of the Four Preparatory Stages do not casually create resonance — the four characters "neither held nor released" (非懷非出) remain merely four characters to someone who has not accumulated sufficient merit and wisdom. If reading "the middle way between delusion and enlightenment, neither can be targeted" produced even a faint stirring in your mind — even an inchoate sense that "this is saying something important" — that stirring itself constitutes evidence of practice on the Path of Accumulation. The *Cheng Weishi Lun* states that bodhisattvas at the accumulation stage "though deeply believing and understanding the meaning of consciousness-only" have not yet directly realized emptiness. Your faith-understanding of these doctrines did not begin in this lifetime.

So do not be discouraged — but do not be cavalier either. Do not refuse to walk because the road is long: every step counts, every moment of recognizing "this name is just a name" accumulates provision, every fleeting experience of "neither held nor released" prepares conditions for future breakthrough. But equally, do not read a few sūtra passages and imagine you have arrived at the preparatory stages — knowing the road is long is itself a sign of being on the road; believing it is short signals you have not yet begun.

The Śūraṅgama's deepest teaching is here: this practice-path is not a straight line. The equal-enlightenment bodhisattva, having walked to the very end, returns to the "initial Ground of Dry Wisdom" — but that "initial" is no longer the parched beginning of old; it is consummation within the vajra mind. The sliver of clarity you glimpsed at the starting point and the sliver of clarity you face again at the end are the same thing. The difference is: having walked the entire path, you know it was always there.

The next paper (P07) will return to the Four Clear and Decisive Instructions on Purity (四種清淨明誨) in the latter half of fascicle 6. The sequence here is worth pausing on: the Four Instructions sit in the latter half of fascicle 6, the *maṇḍala* platform and the Śūraṅgama mantra in fascicle 7, and the Three Gradual Stages in fascicle 8 — the sūtra erects moral guardrails *first*, spatial protection *second*, and mental purification *last*. So P07 is not about "protection while walking"; it is about **the outermost perimeter raised before one even enters**. The present paper (P06) has described the innermost layer — the purification of mind; P07 will describe the outermost layer — the moral guardrails. All three protective layers together — moral, spatial, mental — constitute the actual preparatory package (加行) for the Ear-Faculty Practice.
