## Abstract

The Heart Sūtra passage "no suffering, no origination, no cessation, no path; no wisdom and no attainment, with nothing to be attained" represents prajñā's sharpest cut — directed not at external dharma-marks but at the very tools in the practitioner's hands. S2-P08 illuminated the emptiness of the twelve links of dependent origination (the causal chain of suffering is empty); this paper goes further: the prescription the Buddha wrote for you — the Four Noble Truths — is also empty; the tool you used to see all this — prajñā wisdom itself — is also empty. Beginning from the Buddha's first turning of the dharma wheel at Deer Park (the three turnings and twelve aspects in *Saṃyuktāgama* fascicle 15, Sūtra 379), the paper first establishes the conventional validity and pedagogical necessity of the Four Noble Truths. It then employs Nāgārjuna's prajñā contemplation in *Mahāprajñāpāramitopadeśa* fascicle 94 — "the equality of the Four Noble Truths is itself nirvāṇa" — as the method to reveal the doctrinal foundation of the Truths' lack of self-nature. Using Kuiji's dual-track dialectic between the "supreme emptiness" (*shengkong*) and "as-appropriate" (*ruying*) interpreters in his *Bore boluomiduo xinjing youzhan* fascicle 2, together with his trisvabhāva analysis, the paper unfolds the precise meaning of the emptiness of the Four Noble Truths — the imagined is negated, the dependent is not fixed, the perfected is without distinction — and elucidates the doctrinal significance of the bodhisattva path-stages: even at the fifth bhūmi, practitioners still cling to coarse marks of defilement and purity; it is only at the sixth bhūmi that this obstacle is removed. The paper then argues that "no wisdom and no attainment" constitutes the final step of "dispelling marks to realize the nature" (*qianxiang zhengxing*): all preceding negations emptied the objects of contemplation (dharma-marks), while this passage empties the instrument of contemplation itself (prajñā wisdom) — Kuiji concludes with "truly no grasping at marks, for it does not take marks." The five characters 以無所得故 ("with nothing to be attained") form the Heart Sūtra's passcode, retroactively explaining every "no" from "in emptiness there is no form" through "no wisdom and no attainment," while simultaneously serving as the pivot from practice to the bodhisattva's fruit (S2-P10, "mind without hindrance"). The paper includes a philological finding: the phrase "wisdom and the domain of wisdom are both called prajñā" (說智及智處俱名般若), widely cited by commentators and attributed to the *Mahāprajñāpāramitopadeśa*, does not appear in the Taishō XML full-text of T1509 and is actually a paraphrase by later commentators including Kuiji. Cross-disciplinary dialogue employs general semantics' "the map is not the territory" as a heuristic analogy. Five Middle Way corrections guard against common misreadings including "denying causation," "anti-intellectualism," and "passive inaction."

## I. Introduction

### 1.1 Textual Position

S2-P08 investigated why the structure of the eighteen dhātus traps you — the answer was avidyā, a perceptual inversion. At that paper's close, the origination and cessation of the twelve links were simultaneously negated by prajñā contemplation: "no ignorance, and no ending of ignorance." The causal chain of suffering is empty; even the path of eliminating the cause of suffering is empty.

The Heart Sūtra's next move is more radical still:

> 無苦集滅道，無智亦無得，以無所得故。
> No suffering, no origination, no cessation, no path; no wisdom and no attainment, with nothing to be attained.[^1]

This passage crosses a doctrinal watershed. The preceding negations targeted the *objects* of contemplation — the five aggregates, twelve āyatanas, eighteen dhātus, twelve links of dependent origination — all contemplated dharma-marks. What is now negated is the *method* of contemplation and the *fruit* claimed by the one who contemplates. The Four Noble Truths are the practice path the Buddha taught you; prajñā wisdom is the tool you carry while walking it; "attainment" is the thing you believe awaits you at the destination. The Heart Sūtra empties the path, the tool, and the destination all at once.

Within the Guide's five-layer architecture, this passage enters Layer Four. The first three layers emptied "what you are looking at" (Layer One: establishing the awakening loop; Layer Two: sequential deconstruction of dharma-marks; Layer Three: thusness as unborn and unceasing). Layer Four empties "how you look" — your method of practice, your wisdom in practice, and what you thought practice would bring you. The shift from emptying the other to emptying the self — this is prajñā's sharpest edge.

### 1.2 Research Questions

This paper addresses three core questions.

First, the Buddha turned the dharma wheel at Deer Park with his own voice: "this is the Noble Truth of suffering… one should correctly contemplate it." Three turnings, twelve aspects, every word directly transmitted. Why then does the Heart Sūtra say "no suffering, no origination, no cessation, no path"? Is this denying the Buddha's teaching, or transcending it? The difference is fundamental — the former is slander; the latter is the Buddha's own intent.

Second, "no wisdom and no attainment" — prajñā wisdom itself is emptied. If wisdom is also empty and the tool of practice is gone, what is there left to practice? This question touches the deepest paradox in the Dharma: you must use prajñā to see that prajñā, too, is empty — after climbing to the rooftop with a ladder, you must release the ladder as well.

Third, "with nothing to be attained" — if nothing can be gained, how do these five characters turn around to become the foundation for what follows: "the bodhisattva, relying on prajñāpāramitā, has a mind without hindrance"? "Nothing to be attained" is not a concluding negation but an originating affirmation — the logic of this reversal is the Heart Sūtra's most precise passcode.

### 1.3 Paper Structure

The paper unfolds in the sūtra → treatise → commentary layering. §II Scriptural Foundations establishes the Four Noble Truths through the Buddha's first turning (*Saṃyuktāgama*), Nāgārjuna's prajñā contemplation of the emptiness of the Truths (*Mahāprajñāpāramitopadeśa*), Kuiji's dual-track dialectic as the commentary core, and the *Vijñaptimātratāsiddhi*'s doctrine of *āśrayaparāvṛtti* (transformation of the basis) as the deeper doctrinal stratum. §III Core Argument develops six layers: the Four Noble Truths are addressed to a "self," the raft simile and dharma-attachment, Kuiji's trisvabhāva analysis of the emptiness of the Four Truths, "no wisdom and no attainment" as the final step of dispelling marks, "with nothing to be attained" as the key to all the negations, and the convergence of practice-attitude and fruit-reality. §IV Cross-Disciplinary Dialogue uses "the map is not the territory." §V Middle Way Corrections. §VI Conclusion and forward-looking.

## II. Scriptural Foundations

### 2.1 The Buddha's First Turning: Establishing the Four Noble Truths

The Four Noble Truths constitute the Buddha's very first teaching after attaining awakening. *Saṃyuktāgama* fascicle 15, Sūtra 379 records this discourse delivered to the five bhikṣus at Deer Park:

> 此苦聖諦，本所未曾聞法，當正思惟。時，生眼、智、明、覺。此苦集、此苦滅、此苦滅道跡聖諦，本所未曾聞法，當正思惟。時，生眼、智、明、覺。
> "This is the Noble Truth of suffering — a dharma not previously heard — which should be correctly contemplated. Then there arise vision, knowledge, clarity, and awakening. This is the Noble Truth of the origination of suffering, of the cessation of suffering, and of the path leading to the cessation of suffering — dharmas not previously heard — which should be correctly contemplated. Then there arise vision, knowledge, clarity, and awakening."[^2]

The Buddha delivered three turnings of the Four Noble Truths, with four aspects in each turning, forming the complete teaching of "three turnings and twelve aspects." The first turning is the *demonstration*: this is suffering, this is origination, this is cessation, this is the path — enabling recognition. The second is the *exhortation*: suffering should be known, origination should be abandoned, cessation should be realized, the path should be cultivated — urging action. The third is the *attestation*: suffering I have fully known, origination I have abandoned, cessation I have realized, the path I have cultivated — the Buddha testifying from his own experience.

The sūtra concludes by directly linking the Four Noble Truths to awakening:

> 我已於四聖諦三轉十二行生眼、智、明、覺，故於諸天、魔、梵、沙門、婆羅門聞法眾中，得出、得脫，自證得成阿耨多羅三藐三菩提。
> "Having generated vision, knowledge, clarity, and awakening regarding the Four Noble Truths through three turnings and twelve aspects, I attained liberation among the assembly of devas, māras, brahmās, śramaṇas, and brāhmaṇas, and realized anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi by my own verification."[^2]

"Vision, knowledge, clarity, and awakening" — these four words are the Buddha's self-attestation of his awakening. The Four Noble Truths are not abstract philosophical analysis — they are the path the Buddha walked to awakening and the path he pointed out for you. This must be established first before one can understand why the Heart Sūtra says "no." If "no suffering, no origination, no cessation, no path" were a denial of the Four Truths themselves, it would be a denial of the Buddha's awakening — and that is clearly not what prajñā intends.

### 2.2 Nāgārjuna's Prajñā Contemplation: The Emptiness of the Truths as Nirvāṇa

Nāgārjuna elucidates the relationship between prajñā and the Four Noble Truths on three levels in the *Mahāprajñāpāramitopadeśa*.

*First level: The equality of the Four Truths is itself nirvāṇa.* Fascicle 94 records the Buddha's instruction to Subhūti:

> 非苦聖諦得度，亦非苦智得度；乃至非道聖諦得度，亦非道智得度。須菩提！是四聖諦平等故，我說即是涅槃。
> "It is not through the Noble Truth of suffering that one crosses over, nor through the wisdom of suffering… up to not through the Noble Truth of the path, nor through the wisdom of the path. Subhūti! Because the Four Noble Truths are equal, I declare this to be nirvāṇa."[^3]

What is negated here is not the conventional utility of the Four Noble Truths but the approach of treating them as "real dharmas" through which nirvāṇa is pursued. When Subhūti asks what "equality of the Four Noble Truths" means, the Buddha answers: "When there is no suffering, no wisdom-of-suffering, no origination, no wisdom-of-origination, no cessation, no wisdom-of-cessation, no path, no wisdom-of-path — this is called the equality of the Four Noble Truths."[^3] All eight — the four Truths and their corresponding wisdoms — must be "no" before one can speak of equality. Here "equality" does not mean equivalence but single-flavor (*ekarasa*): under prajñā's illumination, all four Truths equally lack self-nature.

*Second level: The Four Truths lack self-nature.* The commentary in the same fascicle explains why the Truths are empty:

> 何以故？是苦等四法皆從緣生，虛妄不實，無有自性故不名為實，不實故云何能滅？
> "Why? These four dharmas of suffering and so forth all arise from conditions; they are illusory and unreal, lacking self-nature and therefore not called real. Being unreal, how could they be extinguished?"[^4]

The logic is rigorous: the Four Truths arise dependently → what arises dependently has no self-nature → what has no self-nature is not truly real → what is not truly real cannot be truly extinguished. The phrase "being unreal, how could they be extinguished" is particularly sharp: if suffering had substantial existence, it could never be extinguished (substances are unchanging); it is precisely because suffering lacks self-nature that "cessation of suffering" is possible. Yet simultaneously, since suffering lacks self-nature, "cessation of suffering" is itself a provisional designation — you are not destroying an entity but seeing through an illusion that never substantially existed.

Nāgārjuna further states:

> 知是四諦藥病相對，亦不著是四諦，但觀諸法如實相，不作四種分別觀。
> "Knowing that the Four Truths are medicine corresponding to illness, one does not attach to these Four Truths but simply contemplates all dharmas in their true mark, without making four kinds of discriminating contemplation."[^4]

The Four Noble Truths are medicine; suffering is the illness. Medicine and illness stand in mutual opposition — there is medicine only because there is illness; medicine is established for the sake of illness. But prajñā contemplation transcends the medicine-illness duality: it is not "using medicine to cure illness" but "contemplating all dharmas in their true mark." In this contemplation, reality is no longer partitioned into four compartments (suffering, origination, cessation, path) for separate treatment — because partitioning itself is a subtle form of attachment.

*Third level: The Four Truths as skillful means.* Fascicle 19 makes explicit the pedagogical nature of the Four Truths:

> 以有眾生厭苦著樂，為是眾生故說四諦。
> "Because there are sentient beings who loathe suffering and crave pleasure, the Four Truths are taught for the sake of these beings."[^5]

"For the sake of these beings the Four Truths are taught" — the Four Noble Truths were taught because sentient beings have the tendency to loathe suffering and crave pleasure, and the Buddha taught in accord with that tendency. This does not deny the value of the Truths but identifies their nature: they are *upāya* (skillful means), not *tattva* (reality-as-such). Skillful means are like a raft for crossing a river — you need it to get across, but at the far shore it must be released.

### 2.3 Nāgārjuna Revisited: Two Senses of "Nothing to Be Attained"

The Heart Sūtra's "no wisdom and no attainment" occupies a precise position in Nāgārjuna's prajñā system. *Mahāprajñāpāramitopadeśa* fascicle 18 distinguishes two dimensions of "nothing to be attained":

> 無所得有二種：一者、世間欲有所求，不如意，是無所得。二者、諸法實相中，受決定相不可得故名無所得，非無有福德智慧增益善根。
> "'Nothing to be attained' has two kinds: first, in the worldly sense, one desires and seeks but does not obtain — this is 'nothing attained.' Second, in the true mark of all dharmas, no determinate characteristic can be obtained, hence 'nothing to be attained' — but this does not mean there is no increase of merit, wisdom, and wholesome roots."[^6]

The first kind is the worldly meaning of "seeking but not getting" — you want something and cannot obtain it. The second is prajñā's meaning: in the true mark of all dharmas, there simply is no determinate entity that can be "obtained." But Nāgārjuna immediately adds: "this does not mean there is no increase of merit, wisdom, and wholesome roots." Prajñā's "nothing to be attained" does not deny the existence of merit and wisdom but denies that they possess a self-nature of "being possessed." You cannot put prajñā in your pocket like a jewel — it is not that kind of thing.

Fascicle 83 further equates "nothing to be attained" with prajñā itself:

> 佛言：不從有所得中無所得，不從無所得中無所得。須菩提！有所得、無所得平等，是名無所得。
> "The Buddha said: 'Nothing to be attained' does not come from 'something to be attained,' nor does it come from 'nothing to be attained.' Subhūti! The equality of 'something to be attained' and 'nothing to be attained' — this is called 'nothing to be attained.'"[^7]

> 無所得是般若波羅蜜相，無所得是阿耨多羅三藐三菩提相，無所得亦是行般若波羅蜜者相。
> "'Nothing to be attained' is the mark of prajñāpāramitā; 'nothing to be attained' is the mark of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi; 'nothing to be attained' is also the mark of the one who practices prajñāpāramitā."[^7]

Three instances of "nothing to be attained": prajñā's mark is nothing-to-be-attained, the fruit of Buddhahood's mark is nothing-to-be-attained, and the practitioner's mark is nothing-to-be-attained. "Nothing to be attained" is not a byproduct of prajñā — it *is* prajñā itself. It is not that you practice to the end and discover "actually there's nothing to get"; rather, the attitude of "nothing to be attained" is itself the sign that prajñā is present.

### 2.4 Kuiji's Trisvabhāva Analysis: "No Suffering, No Origination, No Cessation, No Path"

In fascicle 2 of the *Bore boluomiduo xinjing youzhan*, Kuiji unfolds the dual-track dialectic of the *shengkong* (supreme emptiness) and *ruying* (as-appropriate) interpreters on "no suffering, no origination, no cessation, no path."

The supreme emptiness interpreter is direct:

> 勝空者言，前無獨覺近觀，此無聲聞近觀。故契經言，為求聲聞者說應四諦法。又說，四諦唯有假名，自性空故。
> "The supreme emptiness interpreter says: previously the proximate contemplation of the pratyekabuddha was negated; here the proximate contemplation of the śrāvaka is negated. As the sūtras say, 'For those who seek the śrāvaka vehicle, the Dharma of the Four Truths should be taught.' It further says, 'The Four Truths possess only conventional names, being empty of self-nature.'"[^8]

For Kuiji, the Heart Sūtra has a sequential structure: first emptying what the pratyekabuddha contemplates (twelve links), then emptying what the śrāvaka contemplates (Four Noble Truths), then emptying what the bodhisattva contemplates (wisdom and attainment). The supreme emptiness interpreter judges directly: the Four Truths possess only conventional names; they are empty of self-nature.

The as-appropriate interpreter is more refined, introducing the distinction between established (*anli*) and non-established (*fei anli*) truths:

> 如應者言，《勝鬘經》說安立四聖諦、非安立四聖諦……分段生死名苦，煩惱及有漏業名集，擇滅名滅，生空智品名道，麁顯施設，淺智所知，名安立諦；變易生死名苦，所知障及無漏有分別業名集，自性清淨、無住涅槃名滅，法空智品名道，微隱難知，非麁淺境，名非安立諦。
> "The as-appropriate interpreter says: the *Śrīmālā Sūtra* speaks of established Four Noble Truths and non-established Four Noble Truths… Segmented birth-and-death is suffering, afflictions and contaminated karma are origination, analytical cessation is cessation, wisdom-of-personal-emptiness is the path — coarsely manifest and instituted, knowable by shallow wisdom, called the established truths. Transformational birth-and-death is suffering, cognitive obstructions and uncontaminated discriminative karma are origination, intrinsically pure and non-abiding nirvāṇa is cessation, wisdom-of-dharma-emptiness is the path — subtle, hidden, difficult to know, not a coarse or shallow domain, called the non-established truths."[^8]

The established Four Truths are the coarse level known by the śrāvaka; the non-established Four Truths are the subtle level realized by the bodhisattva. These are not two sets of Four Truths but the same Four Truths manifesting at different depths of realization. "No suffering, no origination, no cessation, no path" negates the reification of the established truths, not the realization of the non-established truths.

Kuiji then consolidates with the trisvabhāva:

> 於非苦等中佛說為苦等，聲聞等不了，如言起著，今破彼執，故說為無。依他定非苦、集等相，真理何由有彼差別？由此並無。
> "In what is not-suffering and so on, the Buddha provisionally spoke of 'suffering' and so on. The śrāvakas and others did not understand, took the words at face value, and arose attachment. Now this attachment is broken, hence it says 'no.' The dependent nature is definitely not the characteristics of suffering, origination, and so on. How could ultimate truth have such distinctions? Therefore both are 'no.'"[^8]

The *parikalpita* (imagined) layer: the Buddha provisionally designated "suffering" and so on upon what, in ultimate reality, is not suffering. The śrāvakas, failing to understand this as pedagogical accommodation, grasped the words as denoting real entities. The sūtra's "no" breaks precisely this layer. The *paratantra* (dependent) layer: what arises dependently "is definitely not the characteristics of suffering, origination, and so on" — dependently arisen dharmas are not determinately suffering, not determinately origination; they are simply the flow of conditions. The *pariniṣpanna* (perfected) layer: in the reality of thusness, "how could there be such distinctions?" — thusness harbors no distinction between suffering and non-suffering, between saṃsāra and nirvāṇa.

Most incisively, Kuiji maps this to the bodhisattva path-stages:

> 故第五地雖作此觀，尚執有實染淨麁相，第六地中方除染淨障。染者有漏，即此所無苦、集二諦；淨者無漏，既此所無滅、道二諦。
> "Therefore, although the fifth-bhūmi [bodhisattva] already performs this contemplation, they still cling to coarse marks of real defilement and real purity. It is at the sixth bhūmi that the obstacle of defilement-and-purity is removed. 'Defilement' refers to the contaminated — the suffering and origination truths that are here declared 'no.' 'Purity' refers to the uncontaminated — the cessation and path truths that are here declared 'no.'"[^8]

The fifth-bhūmi bodhisattva is already contemplating the emptiness of the Four Truths, yet still holds that there are "truly" defiled states to eliminate and "truly" pure states to attain. Only at the sixth bhūmi is even this defilement-purity distinction released. Suffering and origination belong to the "defilement" side; cessation and path belong to the "purity" side. Even "practice is good, nirvāṇa is good" — this attachment, too, must be released.

### 2.5 Kuiji's Trisvabhāva Analysis: "No Wisdom and No Attainment"

Kuiji's commentary on "no wisdom and no attainment" represents the climax of the Heart Sūtra's contemplation of emptiness.

The supreme emptiness interpreter:

> 勝空者言，上無聲聞近觀，此無菩薩近觀。能證道名智，所證境名得，有能證智，可有所得；證智非有，所得亦空。
> "The supreme emptiness interpreter says: above, the proximate contemplation of the śrāvaka was negated; here, the proximate contemplation of the bodhisattva is negated. The path of realization is called 'wisdom'; the domain of realization is called 'attainment.' Where there is a wisdom that realizes, there can be something attained; when the realizing wisdom is not [taken as] real, what is attained is also empty."[^9]

The śrāvaka contemplates the Four Truths (already emptied); the bodhisattva contemplates the six pāramitās (now also to be emptied). "Wisdom" is the realizing path; "attainment" is the realized domain. Where there is a realizer, there can be something realized — now the realizing wisdom itself cannot be grasped as truly existent, so the realized attainment is likewise empty.

The as-appropriate interpreter deploys Yogācāra's precise tools:

> 無分別智證真如位，心境冥合，平等平等，能取、所取一切皆無；後得智中，離諸相縛，無虛妄執，亦離二取。
> "When non-discriminating wisdom realizes the position of thusness, mind and object merge in darkness, equal and equal; grasper and grasped are entirely absent. In subsequently obtained wisdom, freed from the bonds of all marks, without false grasping, also free from the two graspings."[^9]

"Mind and object merge in darkness, equal and equal, grasper and grasped entirely absent" — at the moment non-discriminating wisdom realizes thusness, mind and object are no longer two things; the contemplating and the contemplated no longer stand in opposition. This is not "the mind swallowing the object" or "the object disappearing," but the dissolution of the subject-object split — the very split that constitutes the root of avidyā.

Kuiji concludes the passage with the trisvabhāva:

> 此釋皆除遍計所執，依他幻事非定智、得，真如體寂都無二相，故依三性皆說為無，非真智生一切非有，說智及智處俱名般若，真無相取，不取相故。
> "This interpretation entirely removes the imagined; the dependent's illusory phenomena are not fixed wisdom or attainment; thusness in its essence is quiescent and entirely without dual marks. Therefore, according to all three natures it is declared 'no' — not that the arising of true wisdom renders everything non-existent. 'Wisdom and the domain of wisdom are both called prajñā' — truly no grasping at marks, for it does not take marks."[^9]

"According to all three natures it is declared 'no'" — from all three perspectives this is "no": the imagined wisdom and attainment simply do not exist; the dependent wisdom and attainment are not fixed; in the perfected, "the essence is quiescent and entirely without dual marks" — thusness is still, without the duality of obtainer and obtained.

In this passage Kuiji invokes the phrase "wisdom and the domain of wisdom are both called prajñā" (說智及智處俱名般若). This phrase circulates widely in the Chinese Buddhist commentarial tradition, uniformly attributed to the *Mahāprajñāpāramitopadeśa*. However, a full-text XML search of the Taishō edition of T1509 (all 100 fascicles) using the key terms 智及智處, 智處俱名, and 俱名般若 yields zero results. The phrase is in fact a paraphrase by later commentators — particularly Kuiji, who uses it in his *Jingang bore jingshu* (T1700), *Dasheng fayuan yilin zhang* (T1861), and the *Xinjing youzhan* itself; Chengguan's *Huayan jingshu* (T1735) also cites it.[^10] This finding does not diminish the phrase's doctrinal validity — it accurately encapsulates the core of Nāgārjuna's prajñā thought: prajñā is both wisdom itself and the reality that wisdom illuminates — but for scholarly citation, it should be marked as "commentarial paraphrase" rather than "original text of the treatise."

"Truly no grasping at marks, for it does not take marks" (真無相取，不取相故) — these eight characters are the final resting point of the Heart Sūtra's contemplation of emptiness. True prajñā is not "grasping marks" — not "I have seized emptiness" or "I have obtained wisdom" — but "not taking marks." You do not need to obtain prajñā; you need only cease grasping.

### 2.6 Kuiji's Interpretation of "With Nothing to Be Attained"

Kuiji positions "with nothing to be attained" (以無所得故) as the summation that retroactively explains all the preceding negations:

> 如應者言……所執六相都無所有，依他、圓成非定六相，故以無得通釋上無。
> "The as-appropriate interpreter says… the six characteristics that are grasped are entirely nonexistent; the dependent and perfected are not fixedly these six characteristics. Therefore, 'with nothing attained' provides a comprehensive explanation for all the preceding negations."[^11]

"'With nothing attained' provides a comprehensive explanation for all the preceding negations" (以無得通釋上無) — this is Kuiji's precise positioning of the five characters. Beginning from "in emptiness there is no form," the Heart Sūtra negated in succession the aggregates, sense-bases, dhātus, dependent origination, truths, wisdom, and attainment — the entire knowledge-architecture of Buddhist practice traversed by the character "no." Why? "With nothing to be attained" — because in the true mark of all dharmas, there has never been anything that could be obtained. This is not a concluding negation but an originating affirmation: precisely because there is nothing to be attained, the text that follows — "the bodhisattva, relying on prajñāpāramitā, has a mind without hindrance" — becomes possible.

### 2.7 The Vijñaptimātratāsiddhi: From "Something Attained" to "Nothing Attained"

Fascicle 9 of the *Vijñaptimātratāsiddhi* provides the most precise Yogācāra support for "no wisdom and no attainment" — the transition from the preparatory stage (*prayoga*) to the stage of penetration (*praveśa*):

> 〔加行位〕猶於現前安立少物，謂是唯識真勝義性，以彼空、有二相未除，帶相觀心有所得故，非實安住真唯識理。
> "[Preparatory stage:] There is still a slight something established before one, taken to be the true ultimate nature of consciousness-only. Because the dual marks of emptiness and existence have not yet been removed, and because one contemplates mind while carrying marks — there is 'something attained' — one does not truly abide in the genuine principle of consciousness-only."[^12]

The practitioner in the preparatory stage still "establishes a slight something" — still feels there is a "consciousness-only nature" that can be cognized. This is "something attained": contemplating while "carrying marks," with something still in mind.

> 若時於所緣，智都無所得，爾時住唯識，離二取相故。
> "When, regarding the object of contemplation, wisdom has nothing whatsoever attained — at that time one abides in consciousness-only, having left behind the marks of the two graspings."[^12]

The breakthrough at the stage of penetration lies here: "wisdom has nothing whatsoever attained." This is not mind becoming empty or the object becoming empty — it is the dissolution of the subject-object *structure*. "Leaving behind the marks of the two graspings" — departing from the opposition of grasper and grasped. This "departing" is not pushing something away but ceasing the activity of producing "two sides."

The verse in the same fascicle unfolds this process in four steps:

> 如是住內心，知所取非有，次能取亦無，後觸無所得。
> "Thus abiding within mind, one knows the grasped is not existent; next, the grasper too is absent; finally, one touches 'nothing to be attained.'"[^12]

First, seeing that the grasped (external object) is not truly existent; then, seeing that the grasper (cognizing subject) is also not truly existent; finally, mind "touches" nothing-to-be-attained — not touching an entity called "nothing to be attained" but the cessation of any object or subject for "touching."

The same fascicle connects this "nothing to be attained" with *āśrayaparāvṛtti* (transformation of the basis):

> 依謂所依，即依他起，與染、淨法為所依故。轉謂二分，轉捨、轉得。由數修習無分別智，斷本識中二障麁重，故能轉捨依他起上遍計所執，及能轉得依他起中圓成實性。
> "'Basis' means what is depended upon — that is, the dependent nature (*paratantra*), which serves as the basis for both defiled and pure dharmas. 'Transformation' has two aspects: transforming by relinquishing and transforming by obtaining. Through repeated cultivation of non-discriminating wisdom, the coarse heaviness of the two obstructions in the root-consciousness is severed; thus one can relinquish the imagined (*parikalpita*) superimposed upon the dependent, and obtain the perfected (*pariniṣpanna*) within the dependent."[^13]

"Relinquishing the imagined superimposed upon the dependent, obtaining the perfected within the dependent" — the structure of transformation is: upon the same dependent nature, the imagined layer is dropped and the perfected layer is revealed. It is not replacing one consciousness with another but a transformation upon the same consciousness — wiping away dust; the mirror remains the same mirror.

## III. Core Argument

### 3.1 The Four Noble Truths Are Addressed to a "Self"

The structure of the Four Noble Truths presupposes a grammatical subject — an "I."

Suffering — *I* am suffering. Origination — *my* suffering has a cause. Cessation — *I* want to end suffering. Path — *I* must walk this road. Every Truth has an "I" present. Without "I," whom does suffering oppress? Who invites origination? For whom does cessation appear? Who walks the path?

This is not wordplay. S2-P08 established a key insight: the essence of avidyā is a perceptual inversion — mistaking what flows for what is fixed, mistaking what arises dependently for what truly exists. The core of this inversion is the construction of "I." The seventh consciousness (*manas*) perpetually deliberates, seizing the perceiving aspect of ālayavijñāna as an "inner self" — this is the root of all inversion. When prajñā contemplation penetrates this illusion of "I," the entire framework of the Four Noble Truths loses its point of attachment.

It is not that the Truths are wrong — it is that their addressee has disappeared. Like a letter: however beautifully written, if the recipient does not exist, the letter has nowhere to be delivered. When the Buddha at Deer Park taught the Four Truths to the five bhikṣus, they still had an "I" — so the Truths were effective. After prajñā sees through the "self," the Truths are not overturned but rendered without a service-object.

Nāgārjuna's redefinition of the Four Truths' wisdoms proceeds from exactly this angle. *Mahāprajñāpāramitopadeśa* fascicle 23:

> 「苦智」名知苦相實不生。「集智」名知一切法離，無有和合。「滅智」名知諸法常寂滅如涅槃。「道智」名知一切法常清淨、無正無邪。
> "'Wisdom-of-suffering' means knowing that the mark of suffering has in reality never arisen. 'Wisdom-of-origination' means knowing that all dharmas are separate, without aggregation. 'Wisdom-of-cessation' means knowing that all dharmas are constantly quiescent like nirvāṇa. 'Wisdom-of-path' means knowing that all dharmas are constantly pure, without right or wrong."[^14]

"Wisdom-of-suffering" is not knowing "there is suffering" but knowing "the mark of suffering has in reality never arisen." "Wisdom-of-path" is not knowing "there is a path" but knowing "all dharmas are constantly pure, without right or wrong." The Four Truths in prajñā are redefined — not negated but inverted. The wisdom of the Four Truths is no longer "knowing what the Four Truths are" but "knowing what the Four Truths are not."

### 3.2 The Raft Simile: A Precise Definition of Dharma-Attachment

Nāgārjuna calls the Four Truths "medicine corresponding to illness." Kuiji, explaining their general meaning, uses a more accessible analogy:

> 如療病者知病、病因、病除、除法，觀生死苦、苦因、苦滅、滅法亦然。
> "Just as the healer knows the illness, its cause, its removal, and the method of removal — contemplating the suffering of saṃsāra, its cause, its cessation, and the method of cessation is likewise."[^8]

The Four Noble Truths constitute a complete medical protocol: diagnosis (suffering), etiology (origination), prognosis (cessation), treatment plan (path). This is the Buddha's pedagogical design as the Great Physician King — precise, practical, therapeutic.

But what happens after the cure?

A patient who has recovered but walks around every day carrying the doctor's prescription, clutching empty medicine bottles, telling everyone "look, this is what cured me" — is no longer a patient but has become "a former patient," a person defined by the experience of treatment. The illness is gone, but attachment to "having been treated" has itself become a new obstacle.

This is the precise meaning of *dharma-grāha* (dharma-attachment). *Ātma-grāha* (self-attachment) takes the "self" as real; dharma-attachment takes the "dharma" — the Buddha's teaching, the method of practice, the road to awakening — as possessing self-nature. S2-P06 established "unborn and unceasing" as the reality of thusness; in the face of that reality, even the method of arriving at reality cannot be grasped as truly existent — because method and reality are not two things.

Nāgārjuna in fascicle 94 consolidates this into an explicit practice instruction:

> 初得道，知二諦是虛誑。將入無餘涅槃，亦知道諦虛誑，以空空三昧等捨離道諦，如說栰喻。滅諦亦無定法。
> "Having initially attained the path, one knows the two truths are illusory. When about to enter nirvāṇa without remainder, one also knows the truth of the path is illusory, using emptiness-of-emptiness samādhi to release the truth of the path — as in the raft simile. The truth of cessation, too, has no fixed dharma."[^4]

"As in the raft simile" — Nāgārjuna explicitly invokes the raft analogy. At the initial attainment of the path, you know the two conventional truths are illusory. But when entering nirvāṇa, the truth of the path itself must also be known as illusory — released through the "emptiness of emptiness" (*śūnyatā-śūnyatā*). "As in the raft simile" directly echoes the *Vajracchedikā*'s "even the dharma must be abandoned, how much more non-dharma" — after crossing the river, release the raft. The doctrinal threads of the Heart Sūtra and the Diamond Sūtra converge at these five characters, "no suffering, no origination, no cessation, no path," and this is one of the core themes of the S5 Diamond Sūtra series.

### 3.3 Kuiji's Trisvabhāva Analysis of the Emptiness of the Four Truths

Deploying Kuiji's trisvabhāva analysis, the emptiness of the Four Noble Truths has three layers of meaning.

**First layer: the imagined is empty.** Ordinary beings and śrāvakas grasp the Four Truths as possessing real self-nature — truly there is a thing called "suffering" oppressing you, truly there is a path called "the way" waiting for you to walk it. Kuiji says the śrāvakas "did not understand, took the words at face value, and arose attachment" — they failed to see that the Buddha's teaching was a contextual accommodation, and they turned language into entities. The sūtra's "no" breaks first at this layer.

**Second layer: the dependent is not fixed.** The dependent Four Truths "are definitely not the characteristics of suffering, origination, and so on" — within dependent arising, dharmas are not determinately suffering, not determinately origination. The same phenomenon is suffering for the śrāvaka, a training ground for the bodhisattva, and a manifestation of dharmakāya for the Buddha. The "characteristics" of the Four Truths are not fixed — they depend on the observer's stage of realization.

**Third layer: the perfected is without distinction.** "How could ultimate truth have such distinctions?" — in the reality of thusness, suffering and happiness are not distinguished, delusion and awakening are not distinguished, saṃsāra and nirvāṇa are not distinguished. This does not mean suffering equals happiness — it means that at the level of thusness, these oppositions simply do not hold.

Kuiji's mapping to the path-stages — the fifth bhūmi still clings to coarse marks of defilement and purity; only the sixth bhūmi removes this obstacle — carries profound practice significance. Even a fifth-bhūmi bodhisattva, already contemplating the emptiness of the Four Truths, still feels "afflictions are truly bad (defilement), nirvāṇa is truly good (purity)." This is the attachment to "coarse marks of defilement and purity." At the sixth bhūmi, even this good-bad judgment is released — not meaning afflictions and nirvāṇa are equally good or equally bad, but no longer viewing them through the framework of "good and bad" at all.

### 3.4 "No Wisdom and No Attainment": The Final Step of Dispelling Marks to Realize the Nature

From S2-P07 through S2-P09, the Heart Sūtra executes a complete sequence of dispelling (*qian*):

S2-P07 dispelled the five aggregates, twelve āyatanas, and eighteen dhātus — emptying "the coarse dharma-marks that are observed." S2-P08 dispelled the twelve links — emptying "the causal chain of suffering." The first half of the present paper dispelled the Four Noble Truths — emptying "the Buddha's prescription." Now "no wisdom and no attainment" dispels the practitioner themselves — emptying "the wisdom that observes" and "the fruit of practice."

This is the final tier of the five-layered Yogācāra contemplation — "dispelling marks to realize the nature" (*qianxiang zhengxing*). Kuiji established the final link of this contemplative system in fascicle 1 of the *Youzhan*: "What is expressed by 'consciousness' has both principle and phenomena; phenomena as marks and function are dispelled and not grasped; principle as nature and essence should be sought and realized."[^15] Both "principle" and "phenomena" are within consciousness, but phenomenal marks are to be dispelled (released), while the essential nature is to be realized. What is dispelled? All dharma-marks — including "wisdom" and "attainment" as the last remaining dharma-marks. What is realized? Thusness.

The doctrinal depth of "no wisdom and no attainment" is this: it does not merely empty ordinary wisdom or ordinary attainment — it empties prajñā itself. The preceding negations — "no form," "no eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, mind," "no ignorance" — all emptied the *objects* of observation. Now "no wisdom" empties the *subject* of observation; "no attainment" empties the *result* of observation. Subject and object both vanish (*neng-suo shuang-wang*) — precisely what the *Vijñaptimātratāsiddhi* describes: "wisdom has nothing whatsoever attained — at that time one abides in consciousness-only, having left behind the marks of the two graspings."

But a lethal paradox lurks here: if prajñā itself is emptied, what do you use to empty prajñā?

Nāgārjuna's answer in fascicle 83:

> 不從有所得中無所得，不從無所得中無所得。有所得、無所得平等，是名無所得。
> "'Nothing to be attained' does not come from 'something attained,' nor does it come from 'nothing attained.' The equality of 'something attained' and 'nothing attained' — this is called 'nothing to be attained.'"[^7]

"Nothing to be attained" is not derived from "something attained" (that would presuppose a "something") nor from "nothing attained" (that would be circular). "Something attained" and "nothing attained" are equal — both are provisional designations. When you stop choosing sides between "getting" and "not getting," you are already within prajñā.

### 3.5 "With Nothing to Be Attained": The Heart Sūtra's Passcode

The five characters 以無所得故 ("with nothing to be attained") are the Heart Sūtra's most concentrated passcode.

Looking backward, they retroactively explain every preceding "no" — as Kuiji explicitly states: "with nothing attained provides a comprehensive explanation for all the preceding negations." Why is there no form in emptiness? With nothing to be attained. Why no eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, mind? With nothing to be attained. Why no suffering, origination, cessation, path? With nothing to be attained. Why no wisdom and no attainment? With nothing to be attained. The negations do not each have separate reasons — they share a single root: in the true mark of all dharmas, there has never been a self-nature that could be obtained.

Looking forward, these words are the foundation for everything that follows: "The bodhisattva, relying on prajñāpāramitā, has a mind without hindrance. Because of no hindrance, there is no fear; leaving far behind inverted dream-thinking, ultimate nirvāṇa." Why does the bodhisattva's mind have no hindrance? Because of "with nothing to be attained." Precisely because no dharma — including prajñā itself — is grasped as truly existent, nothing in the mind can become an obstacle. The precondition of hindrance is an "I" clinging to a "thing"; "nothing to be attained" dismantles this precondition.

Nāgārjuna's "two senses of nothing to be attained" complete their function here. The worldly sense is passive (seeking but failing to get, therefore giving up). The prajñā sense is active (knowing that no dharma possesses obtainable self-nature, therefore acting with a pure mind). The former is defeat; the latter is liberation. The Heart Sūtra's "with nothing to be attained" takes the latter — it is not a sentence of negation but a manifesto of practice-attitude: on the foundation of "knowing that no dharma can be possessed," one proceeds toward the bodhisattva path.

### 3.6 From Practice-Attitude to Fruit-Reality

"With nothing to be attained" is simultaneously the attitude at the causal stage and the reality at the stage of fruition.

At the causal stage (in practice), it is an attitude — cultivating the six pāramitās, severing afflictions, and liberating sentient beings with a mind of "nothing to be attained." This does not mean not practicing — Nāgārjuna explicitly states: "this does not mean there is no increase of merit, wisdom, and wholesome roots." It means practicing without attachment. Planting wholesome roots without clinging to "I am planting wholesome roots"; liberating beings without clinging to "I am liberating beings." This is the same principle as the *Vajracchedikā*'s "leading immeasurable sentient beings to nirvāṇa, yet in reality no sentient being attains nirvāṇa."

At the stage of fruition (in realization), it is reality itself — the "non-abiding nirvāṇa" (*apratiṣṭhita-nirvāṇa*) described in *Vijñaptimātratāsiddhi* fascicle 10 is precisely the fullest expression of this reality:

> 無住處涅槃，謂即真如出所知障，大悲般若常所輔翼。由斯不住生死涅槃，利樂有情，窮未來際，用而常寂，故名涅槃。
> "Non-abiding nirvāṇa: thusness itself, freed from cognitive obstructions, constantly flanked by great compassion and prajñā. By this, one abides neither in saṃsāra nor in nirvāṇa, benefiting and bringing joy to sentient beings, exhausting the future, active yet always quiescent — thus it is called nirvāṇa."[^16]

"Abiding neither in saṃsāra nor in nirvāṇa" — not dwelling in suffering (because there is no self for suffering to oppress) and not dwelling in nirvāṇa (because great compassion does not permit caring only for oneself). "Active yet always quiescent" — within the activity of benefiting sentient beings (active), the essence of thusness remains ever-still (quiescent). This is "with nothing to be attained" fully deployed at the stage of fruition: because there is nothing to be attained, one can give without limit — because no dharma is held as "mine," there is nothing one cannot release.

From S2-P08 to S2-P09, the practitioner's attitude completes a closed loop. P08 established the craving-grasping gap as the operational field for "going deep, illuminating" — that was practice with "something to do" (not-grasping in the gap). P09 subsumes that doing into the overarching attitude of "with nothing to be attained" — even "I am practicing" and "I have awareness" are not grasped. Not that one stops doing — but doing without attachment. This attitude is the pivot from practice (causal stage) to the bodhisattva's fruit (fruition stage, which P10 will develop).

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Dialogue

### 4.1 ⚠️ Heuristic Analogy: The Map Is Not the Territory

The Polish-American semanticist Alfred Korzybski proposed a famous principle of general semantics in 1931: "The map is not the territory." A map is an abstract representation of terrain — it simplifies topography, marks routes, establishes scale — but it is not the terrain itself. Confusing map and territory leads to systematic cognitive errors: you might believe two cities are close (two centimeters on the map) when in fact a mountain range lies between them.

The Four Noble Truths are a map the Buddha drew of the sea of suffering. Suffering is your current location, origination is the route that brought you here, cessation is your destination, and the path is the road from here to there. It is an excellent map — countless people have crossed the sea of suffering by following it over twenty-five hundred years. But the map is not the territory. If you take the map for the territory itself — believing there truly is a place called "suffering," truly a road called "the path," truly a destination called "nirvāṇa" waiting for you — you are mistaking the map for the territory.

"No suffering, no origination, no cessation, no path" is prajñā telling you: put down the map and look at the actual ground beneath your feet. The map is not wrong — it is very accurate. But you have arrived; if you keep staring at the map instead of looking at the road, the map transforms from a tool that helps you into a sheet of paper that obstructs your vision.

"No wisdom and no attainment" goes a step further: release not only the map but also the action of "looking at the road." You are not "a person walking the road" — you are the road.

The heuristic value lies here: modern knowledge systems equally suffer from "mistaking the map for the territory." Scientific models, economic theories, psychological classifications — all are valid maps, none are the territory. Prajñā's attitude is not "don't draw maps" (that would equal not studying the Dharma) but "use the map while knowing it is not the territory" — using the dharma without attachment to the dharma.

⚠️ This analogy has clear boundaries. Korzybski addresses the relationship between language and reality, operating at the level of semantics and epistemology. The Buddhist concept of *dharma-grāha* operates at the ontological level — not just what language describes but what you take to be truly existent. "Confusing map and territory" is a cognitive error that can be corrected through reflection; *dharma-grāha* is a structural grasping at the level of the seventh consciousness (*manas*), requiring prajñā contemplation and the force of meditative concentration to sever. Equating semantic reflection with prajñā contemplation would itself be "mistaking the map for the territory."

## V. Middle Way Corrections

### 5.1 ❌ "No Suffering, No Origination, No Cessation, No Path" Does Not Equal Denying Causation

This is the most common and most dangerous misunderstanding. "No suffering, no origination, no cessation, no path" refers to the Four Truths lacking self-nature, not to suffering being nonexistent or causation being invalid. Nāgārjuna specifically emphasizes in the *Mahāprajñāpāramitopadeśa*: the Four Truths as "medicine corresponding to illness" are fully effective at the conventional level. "No" negates the self-nature (*svabhāva*) of the Four Truths, not their function (*kriyā*). In Kuiji's trisvabhāva terms: the imagined Four Truths are empty; the dependent Four Truths are not. If "no suffering, no origination, no cessation, no path" is understood as "suffering does not exist" or "causation is false," that is not prajñā at all but a severe case of "nihilistic emptiness" (*dṛṣṭi-śūnyatā*) — against which Nāgārjuna sternly warns: "Better to cling to a view of self than to grasp emptiness wrongly."

### 5.2 ❌ "No Wisdom and No Attainment" Does Not Equal Anti-Intellectualism

"No wisdom" does not mean "wisdom is unnecessary" or "studying the Dharma is useless." What prajñā negates is the reification of wisdom — believing there is a thing called "wisdom" that you can possess, that can become your capital, your identity, your pride. In fact, Nāgārjuna explicitly states: "this does not mean there is no increase of merit, wisdom, and wholesome roots." You still need study, reflection, and meditation; you still need to cultivate the six pāramitās. The difference is in attitude: studying with a mind of "nothing to be attained," what you learn does not become an object of attachment. If "no wisdom" is read as "no need to study," that is using prajñā's language to justify laziness — taking medicine as poison.

### 5.3 ❌ "With Nothing to Be Attained" Does Not Equal Passive Inaction

Nāgārjuna's "two senses of nothing to be attained" clearly distinguish two attitudes. The worldly "nothing attained" is passive — wanting to obtain but failing, so one gives up. Prajñā's "nothing to be attained" is active — clearly seeing that in the true mark of all dharmas no self-nature can be obtained, and therefore practicing all wholesome dharmas with a pure mind. The *Vijñaptimātratāsiddhi*'s description of non-abiding nirvāṇa — "abiding neither in saṃsāra nor in nirvāṇa, benefiting and bringing joy to sentient beings, exhausting the future" — is the fullest expression of "with nothing to be attained": precisely because no dharma is held as "mine," one can benefit beings without limit, without fatigue. Passive inaction is a secularized misreading of "nothing to be attained." Prajñā's "nothing to be attained" is the most thorough form of active engagement — because no "I" calculates gain and loss, action is freed from the constraints of gain and loss.

### 5.4 ❌ The Tiantai Four Kinds of Four Noble Truths Are a Later Interpretive Framework

The Guide references the Tiantai school's four kinds of Four Noble Truths (arising-and-ceasing → non-arising → immeasurable → effortless) as an aid for understanding the Heart Sūtra's "no suffering, no origination, no cessation, no path." This classification system originates from Master Zhiyi's *Mohe zhiguan* and represents a systematic Tiantai interpretation of the Four Truths — it is not the Buddha's original teaching, nor the Heart Sūtra's direct import. While the Heart Sūtra's "no" doctrinally aligns with the "non-arising Four Truths" or beyond, it cannot be said in reverse that the Heart Sūtra is *teaching about* the Tiantai four kinds — this would be projecting a later interpretive framework onto an earlier text. Tiantai's classification serves as a valid scholarly reference but should not replace the first-hand doctrinal analysis of the sūtra text and Kuiji's commentary.

### 5.5 ❌ "Wisdom and the Domain of Wisdom Are Both Called Prajñā" Is Not an Original Text of the Mahāprajñāpāramitopadeśa

As detailed in §II, the phrase "wisdom and the domain of wisdom are both called prajñā" circulates widely in the Chinese Buddhist commentarial tradition. Major commentators (Kuiji in T1710, T1700, T1861; Chengguan in T1735; Shihui in T1713) unanimously attribute it to the *Mahāprajñāpāramitopadeśa*. However, a full-text XML search of the Taishō edition (T25n1509.xml, all one hundred fascicles) confirms the phrase does not appear in the original text. The phrase is doctrinally sound — it accurately encapsulates the core of Nāgārjuna's prajñā thought — but in scholarly citation, it should be marked as "commentarial paraphrase" rather than "original text of the treatise." This correction does not affect any doctrinal judgment but reflects this research program's scholarly commitment that every scriptural citation must be traceable to the primary source.

## VI. Conclusion

### 6.1 Core Insight

This paper argues that "no suffering, no origination, no cessation, no path; no wisdom and no attainment, with nothing to be attained" marks the Heart Sūtra's pivotal shift in prajñā contemplation from "emptying the other" to "emptying the self."

First step: the Four Noble Truths are addressed to an "I" — with "I" there is suffering, origination, cessation, path. When the self dissolves, the framework of the Truths loses its presupposed subject. Second step: the Four Noble Truths are a raft for crossing the river — the Buddha established this skillful means in response to beings' illness; when the illness is cured, the medicine too is released. Nāgārjuna's "as in the raft simile" and Kuiji's "medicine corresponding to illness" elucidate the same principle from two angles: dharma-attachment is the practitioner's final checkpoint. Third step: "no wisdom and no attainment" empties the last tools in the practitioner's hands — even prajñā wisdom and the fruit of practice cannot be grasped as truly existent. Kuiji concludes with "truly no grasping at marks, for it does not take marks," completing the Heart Sūtra's final circuit of dispelling marks to realize the nature.

"With nothing to be attained" is the Heart Sūtra's passcode. Looking backward, it explains all the negations — every dharma-mark is "no" because in the true mark of all dharmas, not a single dharma can be obtained. Looking forward, it opens the bodhisattva's fruit — precisely because there is nothing to be attained, the mind can have no hindrance; without hindrance there is no fear; leaving far behind inverted dream-thinking, one arrives at ultimate nirvāṇa.

### 6.2 The Loop

Looping back to S2-P08: the twelve links emptied the causal chain of suffering; this paper empties the Buddha's prescription. From "no ignorance, and no ending of ignorance" to "no suffering, no origination, no cessation, no path," prajñā's negation advances from causal laws to practice methods — even "the road to eliminating ignorance" cannot be grasped.

Looping back to S2-P06: "the true mark of all dharmas, unborn and unceasing" — in thusness there is no distinction of suffering and happiness, delusion and awakening, defilement and purity. This paper's "no suffering, no origination, no cessation, no path" is the dissolution of the Four Truths' marks of distinction under thusness's illumination.

Looping back to S2-P04: "illuminating the emptiness of the five aggregates, crossing beyond all suffering" — the deep logic of "crossing beyond suffering" is not "destroying suffering" but "after seeing no-self, suffering has nowhere to rest." This paper pushes that logic to its conclusion: not only does suffering have nowhere to rest, but the road for crossing beyond suffering also has nowhere to rest.

Looping back to S1-P07: the causal framework of karmic seeds — the "origination" (cause of suffering) and "path" (method of practice) among the Four Truths both operate within ālayavijñāna's seed mechanism. This paper's "no suffering, no origination, no cessation, no path" does not deny the function of the seed mechanism but breaks the reification of it — seeds are dependent nature, not imagined nature.

Looking forward to S2-P10: "with nothing to be attained" is the Heart Sūtra's pivot from "dispelling" to "realizing." What follows — "the bodhisattva, relying on prajñāpāramitā, has a mind without hindrance… ultimate nirvāṇa" — unfolds the fruit of the bodhisattva and non-abiding nirvāṇa. That is the fruit of "with nothing to be attained," which P10 will fully develop.

Looking forward to S5: "as in the raft simile" directly echoes the *Vajracchedikā*'s "even the dharma must be abandoned, how much more non-dharma." The S5 series will develop the breaking of dharma-attachment through the *jifei* tripartite formula — this paper's "no suffering, no origination, no cessation, no path" is that theme's first appearance in the Heart Sūtra context.

### 6.3 Practice Orientation

The "emptiness" of prajñā is not nihilism — it is the most thoroughgoing freedom.

You need the map of the Four Noble Truths to find the road, but once on the road, look at the road, not the map. You need the ladder of prajñā wisdom to climb high, but once you are high, look at the vista, not the ladder. You need the attitude of "nothing to be attained" for practice, but you do not need to turn "I am a practitioner of nothing-to-be-attained" into a new identity.

"With nothing to be attained" is the Heart Sūtra's final instruction to the practitioner, and its lightest — not asking you to do something, but to release the attachment to "doing something."

After releasing, the Heart Sūtra's next words are: "The bodhisattva, relying on prajñāpāramitā —"

Prajñā has not left. It does not need to be possessed by you; it only needs not to be obstructed by you.

Yet "letting go" sounds easy but doing it triggers the deepest fear on the path. The order of the sūtra text is not arbitrary — after "with nothing to be attained," what follows is not immediate liberation but "mind without hindrance; because of no hindrance, no fear." Fear does not appear here by accident — it is the necessary passage through which letting go must travel. When the *manas* — that perpetually deliberating "I" — suddenly discovers it has been told "you are not real," its response is not compliance but existential terror: without me holding on, who protects you? Like the moment a parent releases a child into independence — not from lack of love but from fear that the child might not fare better without them. Yet once the hands open, the child's own horizons and blessings naturally unfold. The Buddha himself is the most complete proof: from the splendor of a prince to the solitude of the ascetic forest, to awakening beneath the Bodhi tree, to forty-five years of daily alms-round in the most ordinary act — he did not teach "with nothing to be attained" in words; he lived it with his entire life. This is the doctrine that S2-P10 will develop.
