## 摘要 Abstract

本文處理《大佛頂首楞嚴經》卷九、卷十五十陰魔章的總綱與色陰十境。核心命題有二：其一，楞嚴所列的五十陰魔，特別是色陰十境，**不是楞嚴獨創的現象學目錄，而是釋迦牟尼佛在早期阿含中對禪定境界鑑別診斷教學的系統化延展**。阿含已經在長壽王本起經（11 種「患」）、行禪經（「知如真」四判準）、良馬喻經（真實禪與強良禪）、五蓋專題、瞿低迦經（時解脫的六次退轉）等處，建立了完整的禪定境界判準框架；楞嚴把這些分散的教學譜系化到五陰結構裡，並提供了一個適用於反聞入流深度禪觀的完整照妖鏡。其二，色陰十境的核心判準——「不作聖心，名善境界；若作聖解，即受群邪」——就是阿含「知如真」與「悉伏彼想」的楞嚴版本，不是新的義理，而是同一教法在更深定境中的精確展開。本文先引阿含七組經文建立阿含層的鑑別診斷框架，再引楞嚴卷九、卷十經文完整呈現五陰本因總綱與色陰十境逐條，然後以子璿《義疏》與交光《正脈疏》的注釋作結構支撐，最後以大智度論四魔與瑜伽師地論定障理論作旁證。核心論證部分建立阿含至楞嚴的譜系連續性，揭示色陰十境「身→聞→感→空→他」的五層內部結構，並說明第十境（善知識形變）為何從「名善境界」的公式中斷裂出來。

This paper addresses the opening framework of the *Fifty Skandha-Māras* chapter and the ten states of the form aggregate (*rūpa-skandha*) in fascicles 9 and 10 of the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* (T945). Two central theses are advanced.

**First**, the fifty skandha-māras — and especially the ten form-aggregate states — are **not a phenomenological catalogue original to the Śūraṅgama**, but a systematization of the Buddha's early Āgama teachings on the discernment of meditative states. The Āgamas already contain a complete diagnostic framework: the eleven *upakkilesa* ("afflictions," Ch. 患) that the Buddha enumerates in the *Dīrghāyu-rāja Jātaka* of the *Madhyama Āgama* as those he himself encountered before awakening; the four modes of "knowing as it truly is" (*yathābhūta-jñāna*) in the *Dhyāna-practice Sūtra*; the contrast between "true dhyāna" and "wild-horse dhyāna" in the *Noble Horse Sūtra* of the *Saṃyukta Āgama*; the five hindrances (*pañca-nīvaraṇa*) framed explicitly as "dharmas of regression" (*parihāna-dharma*); Godhika's six backslidings from temporary liberation (*sāmayika-vimokṣa*); the Ābhassara-deva's cosmogonic misrecognition recorded in the *Brahmajāla Sutta*; and the Buddha's severe treatment of those who claim sagely attainment without basis in the *Kaṭṭhūpama* and *Campā Suttas*. The Śūraṅgama genealogizes these scattered teachings into the five-aggregate structure and provides a complete diagnostic mirror for deep meditative states in the *śrotrāpatti*-via-hearing path introduced earlier in the sūtra.

**Second**, the Śūraṅgama's core criterion — *bùzuò shèngxīn, míng shànjìngjiè; ruò zuò shèngjiě, jí shòu qúnxié* (不作聖心，名善境界；若作聖解，即受群邪): "not taking it as sagely attainment, it is called a wholesome state; if construed as sagely, one is possessed by the host of demons" — is the Śūraṅgama rendition of the Āgama's "knowing as it truly is" and "subduing all perceptions" (悉伏彼想). It is not a new doctrinal position but the same teaching articulated for the far subtler states that arise in deep *dhyāna* practice.

The paper opens with seven groups of Āgama passages establishing the early-text diagnostic framework, then presents the Śūraṅgama's fascicle 9–10 passages in full, including the opening host-and-guest discourse, the fascicle-10 passage on the "five aggregates having the same root in false thought" (*wǔyīn běnyīn tóng shì wàngxiǎng* 五陰本因同是妄想), and all ten form-aggregate states verbatim. Commentarial support is drawn from Zǐxuán's *Yìshū* (T1799) and Jiāoguāng's *Zhèngmài shū* (X275); lateral support from the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* (T1509) and the *Yogācārabhūmi-śāstra* (T1579). The seven core arguments trace the Āgama-to-Śūraṅgama lineage, articulate the host-and-guest framework as the completed form of the earlier diagnostic framework, expose a five-layer internal structure within the ten form-aggregate states (body → hearing → sensation → space → other), and explain why the tenth state departs from the standard adjudication formula.

## 1. Introduction: Why Did Anuruddha's Light Disappear?

### 1.1 A Forgotten Āgama Scene

Anuruddha, Nandiya, and Kimbila were practicing meditation together in the Gosiṅga Forest grove. Their conduct of body, speech, and mind was marked by reciprocal kindness, each yielding his own preferences to the others, dwelling in concord. The Buddha came to visit them and asked Anuruddha about his meditative progress. Anuruddha candidly reported that in his meditation he "obtained light and saw forms" (*de guāngmíng ér jiàn sè* 得光明而見色), but that the light would immediately vanish. Why?

The Buddha's reply is one of the rarest passages in the entire Āgama corpus: **a first-person account by the Buddha himself of his meditative experience before awakening.** "Anuruddha," he says, "before I attained unsurpassed perfect awakening, I too obtained light and saw forms, and the light I saw would also immediately vanish." He then enumerates, one by one, the eleven "afflictions" (*huàn* 患, rendering *upakkilesa*) that he himself identified during that period as the causes of the vanishing.[^1] These are, in the order of the Chinese text: (1) *doubt* (疑患); (2) *inattention* (無念患); (3) *perception of bodily illness* (身病想患); (4) *drowsiness* (睡眠患); (5) *excess exertion* (過精勤患) — "like a man trying to catch a fly by gripping it too hard, killing it"; (6) *excess laxity* (太懈怠患) — "like a man trying to catch a fly by gripping it too loosely, letting it escape"; (7) *terror* (恐怖患) — "like a man walking on a road and seeing bandits approach from all four directions"; (8) *elation* (喜悅患) — "like a man who, seeking a single treasure, suddenly finds four"; (9) *self-exaltation* (自高心患); (10) *diverse perceptions* (若干想患); (11) *not attending to forms* (不觀色患).[^2] This teaching is preserved in the *Madhyama Āgama* fascicle 17, *Sūtra on the Origin of King Dīrghāyu* (T26 No. 72), and corresponds to the *Upakkilesa Sutta* of the Pāli *Majjhima Nikāya* (MN 128).

This is a critically important passage that has been largely neglected in the East Asian meditative tradition. Its importance lies in two facts.

**First, the Buddha himself encountered the phenomenon of "seeing light and forms" during his pre-awakening meditative practice, and he explicitly framed these phenomena as something requiring discernment and treatment — not as signs of attainment.** The eleven *upakkilesa*s are not presented as external obstacles or demonic interference but as internal afflictions of the meditator's own mind. The Buddha's diagnostic stance is crucial: each affliction must be individually recognized, individually named, individually addressed. Nothing is treated as generically "meditative experience" to be either embraced or rejected wholesale.

**Second, phenomena of light and visionary forms in meditation are not signs that something has gone wrong, but ordinary developmental features of deep concentration that even the Buddha himself experienced.** However, they are also not automatic evidence of awakening. If the meditator lacks the capacity to recognize and diagnose what is arising, the light will appear and disappear repeatedly, and the meditator will consume vast amounts of time and energy oscillating between gain and loss. The Buddha uses his own experience to tell Anuruddha: **you need a mirror to identify, each time the light vanishes, which of the eleven afflictions is at work.**

### 1.2 The Śūraṅgama's Fifty Skandha-Māras as the Systematization of This Mirror

The central thesis of this paper is that **the fifty skandha-māras of the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* fascicles 9 and 10 — and especially the ten form-aggregate states treated here — are not an original phenomenological catalogue invented by the Śūraṅgama, but the complete systematization of the diagnostic mirror already sketched in the Āgamas.** What the Śūraṅgama contributes is to take the Buddha's scattered early teachings — preserved in the *Sūtra on the Origin of King Dīrghāyu*, the *Dhyāna-practice Sūtra*, the *Noble Horse Sūtra*, the hindrance passages, the Godhika narrative, the Āryan Silence and Ābhassara-deva traditions, and the *Kaṭṭhūpama Sutta* — and organize them according to the five-aggregate structure, providing ten characteristic scenes of misrecognition for each aggregate, thereby furnishing meditators of the Latter Dharma era with a complete diagnostic mirror.

This thesis can be stated in a single sentence: **the fifty skandha-māras are a genealogical systematization of the Āgama diagnostic teaching on meditative states, not a new invention.** Three consequences follow directly.

First, placing the fifty skandha-māras back in their Āgama context dissolves much of the mystagogical misreading that has accreted around them in the Latter Dharma tradition. They are not "fifty uncanny incidents of demonic possession" but "fifty characteristic traps of misrecognition that meditators in deep concentration must expect to encounter," each with a clear phenomenological description, psychological mechanism, and corrective procedure.

Second, this reading provides contemporary East Asian meditators with a structural understanding: when you encounter phenomena similar to the ten form-aggregate states in meditation, you need neither panic nor jubilation. You need only recognize that these are structural side effects of meditative development, known from the Āgamas onward, encountered by the Buddha himself, and addressed by a single unwavering practice: **recognize it, do not cling to it.**

Third, it clarifies an academic misunderstanding: the fifty skandha-māras are not the "superstitious aberrant section" of the Śūraṅgama but arguably its most universally applicable and historically grounded chapter — the section most deeply continuous with early Buddhist meditative teaching.

### 1.3 Position within the S6 Series: From Three-Layered Perimeter to Demon-Revealing Mirror

This is the eighth paper in the S6 series on the Śūraṅgama. In the previous seven, we have incrementally built the architectural logic of the sūtra:

- **Breaking falsehood to reveal the true (gateway-selection).** S6-P01 *tathāgatagarbha* doctrine; S6-P02 the Seven Locations examination of mind; S6-P03 the Tenfold Revelation of Seeing; S6-P04 the Eightfold Discernment of Seeing; S6-P05 the Twenty-Five Gateways and Mañjuśrī's selection of the ear faculty. This is the **theoretical layer**: establishing right view and selecting the dharma-gate.
- **Establishing the three-layered perimeter (preparatory practice).** S6-P06 the Three Gradual Stages and the Ground of Dry Wisdom (innermost layer, purification of mind); S6-P07 the Four Clear and Decisive Instructions on Purity (outermost layer, ethical protection); and the *maṇḍala* and *Śūraṅgama-mantra* of fascicle 7 (middle layer, spatial protection). This is the **preparatory layer**: constructing the inner, outer, and middle perimeters before any serious deep meditative practice begins.

S6-P08 occupies the pivot: **the transition from "building the perimeter" to "consecrating the demon-revealing mirror."** The previous seven papers explain how to enter the gate, select the method, and build the perimeter. Beginning with this paper, we treat the fifty characteristic traps that the meditator will encounter within the perimeter, during deep meditative observation. These fifty traps are the fifty skandha-māras.

S6-P07 already laid the groundwork. The final instruction on purity — the injunction against great false speech — contains the phrase *chéng àijiàn mó, shī rúlái zhǒng* (成愛見魔，失如來種): "to become a demon of attachment-views and lose the seed of the Tathāgata." This directly anticipates the fifty skandha-māras of fascicles 9 and 10. Structurally, **the Four Clear and Decisive Instructions are the first perimeter before entering, while the fifty skandha-māras are the pathology report after entering.** If the practitioner has already failed at the first perimeter (ethical discipline), every subsequent entry in the fifty skandha-māras will claim them, and there will be no remedy. If the practitioner has passed the first perimeter and entered deep meditative practice, they still need the fifty skandha-māras as a diagnostic mirror, to discern at each moment whether a given meditative experience is a legitimate side effect of *dhyāna* or a "construed-as-sagely" trap.

This paper assumes the reader has read S6-P06 and S6-P07. For readers beginning here, the essential framework is simple: the Śūraṅgama presupposes that the meditator has already established right view, chosen a method, and kept the precepts. Under these conditions, deep meditative observation will inevitably encounter fifty characteristic traps, of which the ten form-aggregate states are the outermost, the most proximate to the body, the easiest to recognize — and, for that very reason, the most easily mistaken for "awakening."

## 2. Textual Basis

The textual basis is presented in two layers: **Section 2.1** cites the Āgama sources, establishing the original diagnostic framework; **Section 2.2** cites the Śūraṅgama, exhibiting the full systematization of the general framework and ten form-aggregate states. This ordering — Āgama first, Śūraṅgama second — is itself the instantiation of this paper's central thesis: what the Śūraṅgama does is to genealogize and complete what the Āgamas had already established, not to propound new doctrine.

### 2.1 Āgama Sources: The Buddha's Original Teaching on Meditative States

#### 2.1.1 The *Sūtra on the Origin of King Dīrghāyu*: The Buddha's Own Eleven *Upakkilesa*s

The *Madhyama Āgama* fascicle 17, *Sūtra on the Origin of King Dīrghāyu* (corresponding to MN 128 *Upakkilesa Sutta*), is the most direct Āgama source for the form-aggregate states. The Buddha visits Anuruddha, Nandiya, and Kimbila in the Gosiṅga Forest. Anuruddha reports that in deep concentration he "obtains light and sees forms" but that the light and forms immediately vanish. The Buddha replies that before his own awakening he experienced exactly this, and he enumerates the eleven *upakkilesa*s he himself identified and overcame in turn.[^3]

The eleven, in order, are: (1) *doubt* concerning the Dharma; (2) *inattention* (loss of sustained focus); (3) *perception of bodily illness* (clinging to somatic discomfort); (4) *drowsiness* (sinking, torpor); (5) *excess exertion* — *like gripping a fly too tightly and killing it*; (6) *excess laxity* — *like gripping a fly too loosely and letting it escape*; (7) *terror* — *like a traveler seeing bandits approach from all four sides*; (8) *elation* — *like one seeking a single treasure who suddenly finds four*; (9) *self-exaltation* in the states attained; (10) *diverse scattered perceptions*; (11) *failure to attend to forms*.[^4]

The structure of these eleven is critical for our argument. **First, they all arise in already deep meditation.** Only when the meditator has achieved the capacity to "obtain light and see forms" do these eleven become operative. A beginner who has not yet achieved basic concentration is not in a position to encounter them. This aligns precisely with the Śūraṅgama's precondition for the ten form-aggregate states: they arise only after the meditator "enters the site of awakening, dissolves thought down to its cessation" (*zuò dàochǎng, xiāoluò zhū niàn, qí niàn ruò jìn* 坐道場，銷落諸念，其念若盡) — that is, when quite deep concentration has already been attained.

**Second, several of the eleven map directly onto specific form-aggregate states in the Śūraṅgama.** The excess-exertion affliction corresponds to the Śūraṅgama's "refining the subtle clarity" (精研妙明) with excessive forcing; the elation affliction corresponds to the fourth state's sudden vision of Vairocana enthroned amid radiance; the self-exaltation affliction is the psychological core of "construing as sagely" across all ten states; the diverse-perceptions affliction corresponds to the third state, "hearing Dharma proclamations in space"; the failure-to-attend-to-forms affliction is precisely what enables the tenth state, the misrecognition of a "good spiritual friend" (*kalyāṇamitra*) whose form has been distorted.

**Third, the Buddha's handling of the eleven is diagnostic rather than summary.** He does not treat them as generic "demonic interference" but as individually identifiable and individually treatable afflictions. This is the earliest form of the very procedure the Śūraṅgama systematizes: name the state, do not cling to it, address it specifically.

The sūtra concludes with a summary verse: "Anuruddha! When the affliction of doubt arose in my mind, I attained clarity of mind. When inattention, the perception of bodily illness, drowsiness, excess exertion, excess laxity, terror, elation, self-exaltation, diverse perceptions, and failure to attend to forms arose, I attained clarity of mind."[^5] "Clarity of mind" (*xīn qīngjìng* 心清淨) does not mean the absence of phenomena; it means **truthful recognition without clinging** to each affliction as it arises.

#### 2.1.2 The *Dhyāna-practice Sūtra*: Four Meditators and "Knowing as It Truly Is"

The *Madhyama Āgama* fascicle 46, the *Dhyāna-practice Sūtra* (T26 No. 176), is the Āgama's most direct treatment of **misjudgment within meditation itself**. The Buddha enumerates four types of meditators:[^6]

> "O bhikkhus, there are truly four kinds of meditators in this world. Which four? There is the meditator who is progressing but thinks he is regressing; the meditator who is regressing but thinks he is progressing; the meditator who is regressing and knows that he is regressing as it truly is; and the meditator who is progressing and knows that he is progressing as it truly is."

The four types divide into two pairs: the first pair **does not know as it truly is** (*bù zhī rúzhēn* 不知如真); the second pair **knows as it truly is** (*zhī rúzhēn* 知如真). The Buddha elaborates each in relation to the nine successive attainments: a meditator whose mind is ascending toward a higher *dhyāna* (actually progressing) may misread this as losing the foundational *dhyāna* and panic, thereby losing concentration; a meditator whose mind is descending toward a lower *dhyāna* because of stray minor thoughts (actually regressing) may misread this as progress and fail to correct course.[^7]

The relevance for our argument is this: **the most serious danger in meditation is not the states themselves but the misjudgment of states.** Whether one is progressing or regressing, whether a state is wholesome or demonic — these judgments may themselves be mistaken, and once the judgment is wrong, the subsequent practice proceeds in a wholly wrong direction. The Śūraṅgama's central criterion for the ten form-aggregate states — "not construing as sagely, it is called a wholesome state; if construed as sagely, one receives the host of demons" — is precisely the elaboration of the Āgama's "knowing/not knowing as it truly is" at the far subtler level of fully developed *dhyāna* states. "Construing as sagely" is the paradigmatic instance of "not knowing as it truly is": the meditator mistakes a transient meditative side effect for definitive sagely attainment.

#### 2.1.3 The *Noble Horse Sūtra*: True Dhyāna and Wild-Horse Dhyāna

The *Saṃyukta Āgama* 926 (fascicle 33) presents the Buddha's discourse to Seniya Kātyāyana on the distinction between **true dhyāna** and **wild-horse dhyāna** (*qiángliáng chán* 強良禪):[^8]

> "Cultivate true dhyāna; do not practice wild-horse dhyāna. Like a wild horse tied to its post, such a horse does not think, 'What should I do, what should I not do?' but only thinks of grain and grass. Just so, a man who has greatly cultivated the fetter of sensual desire, thinking with a mind of sensual desire, does not truly know the path of escape; his mind constantly races, pursuing meditative attainment even while bound by sensual desire. And so too with anger, drowsiness, restlessness, and doubt — greatly cultivating these, he does not truly know the path of escape."

The image is strikingly concrete: a horse tied to its feeding post, outwardly receiving training but inwardly preoccupied only with food. Formally the practitioner is meditating; substantively his mind is driven by the five hindrances. The Buddha then describes true dhyāna: "If a true-bred horse is tied to its post, it does not think of water and grass but only thinks, 'matters of riding and driving.' Just so, a man who does not think of the fetter of sensual desire, dwelling in knowing the path of escape as it truly is, does not seek meditative attainment through the fetter of sensual desire, nor through anger, drowsiness, restlessness, and doubt; he greatly dwells in escape."

The deeper layer is the *ultimate definition* of true dhyāna as **"subduing all perceptions"** (*xī fú bǐ xiǎng* 悉伏彼想):

> "Seniya! Such a meditator does not depend on earth for meditation, nor on water, fire, wind, space, consciousness, nothingness, or the base of neither-perception-nor-non-perception; he does not depend on this world or the next, not on sun or moon, not on what is seen, heard, thought, or cognized, not on what is attained or sought, not on discursive thought or sustained examination."[^9]

When Vakkali asks how such a meditation is possible, the Buddha answers: "A bhikkhu, with respect to the perception of earth, can subdue the perception of earth; with respect to the perceptions of water, fire, wind, infinite space, infinite consciousness, nothingness, neither-perception-nor-non-perception; with respect to this world and the next, sun and moon, the seen, heard, thought, and cognized, what is attained and sought, what is examined and sustained — he subdues all those perceptions." The key phrase is "subdues all those perceptions": with respect to any perception that arises in any state, one neither clings nor rejects, neither depends on nor fastens to it — all are subdued beneath consciousness without becoming objects of grasping.

**This is the Āgama form of the Śūraṅgama's "not construing as sagely"**: not the absence of states, but the refusal to construe any state as sagely attainment.

#### 2.1.4 The Five Hindrances as "Dharmas of Regression"

The five hindrances (*pañca-nīvaraṇa*) are the Āgama's most basic and most systematic classification of meditative obstructions. *Saṃyukta Āgama* 705 (fascicle 26) directly identifies them as *dharmas of regression* (*tuìfǎ* 退法):[^10]

> "There are five dharmas of regression. What are the five? They are: the hindrances of sensual desire, of ill will, of drowsiness, of restlessness and remorse, and of doubt. These are the dharmas of regression. If one cultivates the seven factors of enlightenment, cultivating them much and making them increase and grow, this is the dharma of non-regression."

The structure is stark: five hindrances = regression; seven factors of enlightenment = non-regression. Progression and regression in meditation are not random or unpredictable; they have a determinate internal mechanism — whichever side has greater force, the direction of practice follows.

*Saṃyukta Āgama* 715 develops the "food" and "non-food" mechanism of the hindrances: what nourishes each hindrance and what starves it.[^11] The common denominator of nourishment is *wrong attention* (*bù zhèng sīwéi* 不正思惟); the five treatments for starving the hindrances are: *impurity-contemplation* for sensual desire, *loving-kindness contemplation* for ill will, *luminosity-contemplation* for drowsiness, *calm-contemplation* for restlessness, and *dependent-origination contemplation* for doubt.

The relevance for our argument is this: **the Śūraṅgama's fifty skandha-māras can be understood as the subtle transformations of the five hindrances in deep meditative states.** In the novice phase, the hindrances are coarse — sensual desire for food and sex, ill will toward persons and events. In deep meditation, the hindrances reappear in exquisitely subtle forms: craving for meditative experiences, aversion to meditative experiences, sinking into experiences, agitation over experiences, doubt as to whether an experience constitutes sagely attainment. Each of the ten form-aggregate states can be located in terms of a corresponding subtle hindrance structure.

#### 2.1.5 The Godhika Narrative: Six Backslidings from Temporary Liberation

*Saṃyukta Āgama* 1091 (fascicle 39) records the story of the bhikkhu Godhika:[^12]

> "The venerable Godhika, dwelling alone in seclusion, heedful and striving, practicing for his own welfare, attained temporary liberation of mind and bodily realization; he repeatedly backslid — one, two, three, four, five, six backslidings — regaining and again losing temporary liberation of mind and bodily realization."

"Temporary liberation" (*shí jiětuō* 時解脫, *sāmayika-vimokṣa*) is a state conditional upon the continued presence of certain causal conditions: when the conditions are present, the meditator can enter or maintain the state; when the conditions shift, the state lapses. Godhika had reached this attainment, but had repeatedly lost it — six times. Resolving that there would be no seventh backsliding, he took a knife and ended his own life. At this moment, Māra Pāpīyas hurried to the Buddha carrying a lyre of vaiḍūrya and requested in verse that the Buddha prevent the suicide. The Buddha replied in verse:

> "The steadfast and accomplished man
> dwells continually in subtle *dhyāna*;
> day and night he strives diligently,
> not regarding even life itself.
> Seeing the terror of the three realms,
> he has severed the craving for existence.
> Having crushed the army of Māra,
> Godhika has attained *parinirvāṇa*."

The Buddha led the bhikkhus to the Black Stone Cave, where Godhika's corpse was surrounded by a dark haze — **Māra Pāpīyas "searching for his consciousness"** (*zhōuzā qiú qí shíshén* 周匝求其識神). The Buddha explained: "The bhikkhu Godhika, with unsettled mind, took up the knife and ended his life." And he pronounced upon Godhika the highest declaration (*parinirvāṇa*).

Three points of decisive significance emerge from this narrative. **First, backsliding in meditation is not a beginner's problem.** Godhika had attained "temporary liberation of mind and bodily realization," yet repeatedly backslid six times. **Second, meditators in deep practice may attract the direct intervention of Māra himself.** Māra Pāpīyas is not a metaphor: he appears in person to search for Godhika's consciousness at the moment of dissolution. **Third, the Buddha's assessment of Godhika is that he has "crushed the army of Māra."** Godhika's six backslidings are not failures but the paradigm of attaining final *parinirvāṇa* precisely through unsettled-mindedness and non-attachment. **This is the strongest Āgama evidence that deep meditative practice structurally entails encountering māra-events.**

#### 2.1.6 Āryan Silence and the Ābhassara Devas: Structural Side Effects of Dhyāna

*Saṃyukta Āgama* 501 (fascicle 18) records Mahāmaudgalyāyana's reflection on the nature of **āryan silence** (*shèng mòrán* 聖默然):[^13]

> "I, dwelling alone in seclusion, reflected thus: 'What is āryan silence?' And I reflected further: 'If a bhikkhu, stilling initial and sustained thought, with inner purification and one-pointedness of mind, dwells fully in the second *dhyāna* with the joy and happiness born of concentration without initial or sustained thought — this is called āryan silence.'"

Mahāmaudgalyāyana entered and retreated from the second *dhyāna* three times, and the Buddha appeared via supernormal power to admonish him three times: "You should dwell in āryan silence; do not become negligent." Āryan silence = second *dhyāna* = the stilling of initial and sustained thought (*vitarka* and *vicāra*). This definition is explicit and stable throughout the Āgamas.

Paired with āryan silence is the **Ābhassara heaven** (*guāngyīn tiān* 光音天), the celestial destination of those who master the second *dhyāna*. The *Dīrgha Āgama* fascicle 21 records that when the fire-age is about to begin, "those who have attained the second *dhyāna* rise into the sky and proclaim, 'Friends! Know the joy of the second *dhyāna*, without initial or sustained thought!' Hearing this, beings of this world practice that path of *dhyāna* without initial or sustained thought, and at the dissolution of their body are reborn in the Ābhassara heaven."[^14] The defining feature of the Ābhassara heaven is precisely *light* (*ābhā*) and *sound* (*svara*): light becomes their body, thought becomes their nourishment, self-proclaiming "excellent" becomes their mode of expression. **Phenomena of light and sound in deep meditation, within the Āgama cosmological framework, have a precise cosmological correlate** — they are not demonic events, nor are they sagely attainments; they are the **structural side effects of meditative development**.

Still more illuminating is the story recounted in the *Dīrgha Āgama*'s *Brahmajāla Sutta* (fascicle 14) of how Ābhassara devas, after their lifespan expires in that heaven, are reborn into the empty Brahma heaven and come to misrecognize themselves as the creator:[^15]

> "When this kalpa begins anew, there are sentient beings whose merit, life, and karma have been exhausted, who, having died in the Ābhassara heaven, are reborn in the empty Brahma heaven. There they generate affection and attachment... This being, having generated his aspiration of attachment, is followed by other sentient beings who likewise, having died in the Ābhassara heaven, are reborn in the empty Brahma heaven. The first-born being then thinks, 'I am Brahmā, the Great Brahmā, I exist spontaneously, none has created me... I am the father of all beings, I alone existed first...'"

The first Ābhassara deva to arrive in the empty Brahma heaven, noticing that subsequent beings are born after him, infers that he has created them. **This is the cosmological form of "construing as sagely"**: temporal priority is mistaken for causal agency. The form-aggregate practitioner who sees Vairocana enthroned, sees mountains and rivers becoming Buddha-lands, sees myriad forms transforming into Tathāgatas — if they interpret these states as "I have attained," their psychological structure is **isomorphic** with that of the Great Brahmā of the Ābhassara tradition: both mistake a phenomenon conditioned by circumstance for proof of their own subjective agency.

One further detail from the *Ekottara Āgama* (fascicle 34) deepens the point. Ābhassara devas descending to the nascent earth tasted the "earth-savor" and became attached to it: "Those devas who ate much of the earth-savor lost their divine radiance, grew heavy with flesh and bone, and lost their power of flight."[^16] The devas originally possessed the "divine radiance and power of flight" that the second *dhyāna* bestows; once they became attached to phenomenal taste, they lost the radiance, grew heavy, and could no longer fly. **This is the compressed metaphor for the ten form-aggregate states**: the meditator in deep concentration possesses a "divine radiance" that *dhyāna* itself bestows; once they become attached to any phenomenal state, they lose the radiance, grow heavy, and cannot continue deeper.

#### 2.1.7 The *Kaṭṭhūpama Sutta* and *Campā Sutta*: Claiming What One Has Not Attained

The final group of Āgama passages addresses the ethical dimension of "construing as sagely." The *Madhyama Āgama* fascicle 1 *Sūtra of the Simile of the Burning Wood* presents seven grotesque images — burning wood, rope-bound thighs, amputated thighs, burning iron plates, burning iron balls, burning iron beds, burning iron cauldrons — each used repeatedly to drive home the fate of those who, "not being true *brahmacārins*, call themselves *brahmacārins*; not being true śramaṇas, call themselves śramaṇas": at the dissolution of their body they go to evil destinations and are reborn in hell.[^17] The *Campā Sutta* in *Madhyama Āgama* fascicle 9 records that on an Uposatha day the Buddha, at three successive night-watches, refused to recite the *Prātimokṣa*, declaring that "in this assembly there is one bhikkhu who has become impure." Mahāmaudgalyāyana identified the impure bhikkhu through supernormal power and expelled him.[^18] In the second *Campā Sutta* (fascicle 29), the Buddha gives the simile of the weed-rice: in a field of good rice there may grow a weed-rice whose root, stem, leaves, and flowers all resemble the true rice, but which must be pulled out, "lest it defile the true good rice."[^19]

The relevance for our argument is that **the Śūraṅgama's "construing as sagely" in the ten form-aggregate states is structurally identical with the Āgama's "non-*brahmacārin* calling himself a *brahmacārin*"**: both exhibit external forms indistinguishable from genuine practice, but both are structurally false. S6-P07 treated this issue at the level of explicit precept (the prohibition on great false speech); S6-P08 treats the same issue at the level of subtle meditative misrecognition. One need not vocalize "I have attained"; merely interpreting a meditative state as sagely is already "construing as sagely," already weed-rice, already "receiving the host of demons."

### 2.2 The Śūraṅgama: Mahāyāna Systematization

With the Āgama framework in place, we can now examine how fascicles 9 and 10 of the Śūraṅgama systematize and genealogize what the Āgamas had already scattered.

#### 2.2.1 The Host-and-Guest Discourse as General Warning

In fascicle 9, as the Buddha is about to rise from the lion's throne, he unexpectedly returns to offer an unsolicited teaching (*wú wèn zì shuō* 無問自說) — one of the rare passages in the sūtra where the Buddha speaks without having been asked. He begins:[^20]

> "You who are still in training, as *pratyekabuddha*s and *śrāvaka*s, have today turned your minds toward the supreme perfect awakening of bodhi. I have now taught you the true method of practice. But you still do not recognize the subtle māra-events in the cultivation of *śamatha* and *vipaśyanā*. When māra-states appear before you, you cannot recognize them; your mind-cleansing is not correct, and you fall into wrong views. Whether they are your own aggregate-māras, or celestial māras, or possessing demons, or encountered sprites, if your mind is not clear, you will mistake thieves for your own children."

The Buddha then explains why māra-events necessarily arise during serious *dhyāna* practice — the passage on "palaces shattered":

> "When even one of you awakens to the real and returns to the source, all the empty space throughout the ten directions is thereby dissolved; how could the lands within that space not quake and rend? ... The māra-kings and the celestial beings of the ordinary realms, seeing their palaces unaccountably crumbling and the great earth convulsing, with waters and flying creatures all in terror — they are all of them darkly bewildered. These beings possess the five supernormal powers, lacking only the exhaustion of āsravas, and remain attached to this defiled existence. How could they allow you to shatter their abodes? Therefore demons, celestial māras, yakṣas, and sprites come together at the time of *samādhi* to disturb you."[^21]

The logic is transparent: the meditator's serious movement toward awakening ("returning to the source") structurally destabilizes the three realms, and every being whose existence depends upon the deluded organization of the three realms feels their foundations tremble. **Māra does not appear randomly; māra's appearance is structurally necessary.** As long as one is genuinely cultivating *dhyāna*, māra will certainly arrive.

The Buddha then articulates the core **host-and-guest discourse** (*zhǔ kè lùn* 主客論):[^22]

> "But though those māras have great rage, they are within defilement while you are within wondrous awakening — like wind blowing against light, like a blade cutting water: they simply do not touch you. You are like boiling water; they are like solid ice. When warmth gradually approaches, in no time ice dissolves. Relying only on their supernormal powers, they are but guests. What completes their success at disruption is the *five-aggregate host* within your own mind. If the host is deluded, the guest gains his advantage. When in that *dhyāna*, if you are awake and without confusion, the māra-events can do nothing to you."

Three images ascend in intensity: wind blowing light (light is not moved), blade cutting water (water is not cut), boiling water melting ice (heat dissolves ice). The common structure of all three is that **māra possesses no independent destructive power; all of māra's force derives from the host's own delusion.** If the meditator remains "awake and without confusion in that *dhyāna*," the māra-events can do nothing. If the host is deluded, the guest gains his advantage. **This is the host-and-guest framework at its most concise**: māra is guest, the five aggregates are host, and the outcome depends entirely on the host's condition of awakening.

This passage establishes the adjudicative framework for the entire fifty-māra chapter. Every one of the fifty states that follows — from the form aggregate to the consciousness aggregate — is an application and specification of this host-and-guest discourse.

#### 2.2.2 "The Five Aggregates Have the Same Root in False Thought"

Curiously, the *general framework* passage for the fifty skandha-māras does not appear at the opening of fascicle 9 but at the end of fascicle 10, after all fifty states have already been enumerated. When Ānanda asks about the common root of the five aggregates, the Buddha gives a summative teaching that is the true theoretical frame for the entire chapter:[^23]

> Ānanda rose from his seat, having heard the Buddha's teaching, and paid homage. In the great assembly he again addressed the Buddha: "As the Buddha has said, 'Among the five-aggregate characteristics, five kinds of falsity are the root-thought,' but we have not been clearly instructed in the fine details. And these five aggregates: are they eradicated together, or in sequence? And what are the boundaries of these five layers? I beseech the Tathāgata, in great compassion, to clarify the minds and eyes of this assembly, to serve as future eyes for all beings of the Latter Dharma!"
>
> The Buddha told Ānanda: "The essential-real wondrously luminous primordial awareness, perfectly pure, does not retain death and birth or any defilement. Even empty space itself arises through false thought. Its origin is the wondrous primordial awareness and essential-real reality, falsely generating all the instrumental worlds. ... Therefore the Tathāgata now clarifies for you: **the root-cause of the five aggregates is all false thought.**"

The Buddha then specifies: the form aggregate is *solidified false thought* (堅固妄想); the receptive aggregate is *empty-bright false thought* (虛明妄想); the perceptive aggregate is *penetrating-merging false thought* (融通妄想); the formative aggregate is *hidden-subtle false thought* (幽隱妄想); the consciousness aggregate is *imagistic-empty upside-down false thought* (罔象虛無顛倒妄想). All five aggregates are false thoughts at progressively finer degrees, from coarse to subtle, from outer to inner. The Buddha concludes:[^24]

> "Ānanda! These five receptive aggregates are formed by five false thoughts. If you now wish to know the depths of these causal boundaries: form and space constitute the boundaries of the form aggregate; contact and separation constitute the boundaries of the receptive aggregate; memory and forgetting constitute the boundaries of the perceptive aggregate; extinction and birth constitute the boundaries of the formative aggregate; the entry of pure clarity into consolidated clarity constitutes the boundary of the consciousness aggregate. These five aggregates arise in overlapping succession: their birth proceeds from consciousness, their extinction proceeds from form. **In principle one may awaken to their nature all at once, and with that awakening they dissolve simultaneously; but in practice they cannot be eradicated all at once, and must be exhausted in sequence.**"

This passage is the most important structural declaration of the fifty skandha-māras. It tells us three things. First, *all* five aggregates are false thought; none has any reality independent of thought. Second, the aggregates are ordered from coarse to subtle: form is coarsest, consciousness is subtlest. Third, although in principle one may awaken to their unreality simultaneously, in practice one must eliminate them in sequence, starting with form. This is why the fifty skandha-māras begin with the form aggregate, and why the form-aggregate states are the first that any meditator will encounter.

#### 2.2.3 The Form-Aggregate Region, Its Exhaustion, and Solidified False Thought

The Buddha's definition of the form aggregate at the opening of the fifty-māra section reads:[^25]

> "Ānanda, you should know: when you sit at the site of awakening and dissolve thought down to its cessation, all the essential clarity beyond thought remains unmoving in motion and stillness, with remembering and forgetting as one. Dwelling in this state, you enter *samādhi*. It is as if a clear-sighted man stood in deep darkness: the essential nature is wondrously pure, but the mind has not yet emitted light — this is called *the region of the form aggregate*. If the eyes become bright and the ten directions open with no further darkness, this is called *the form aggregate exhausted*; such a one can transcend the turbidity of the *kalpa*. Observing its origin: **solidified false thought** is its root."

This passage establishes three pivotal concepts. **The region of the form aggregate** (*sèyīn qūyǔ* 色陰區宇) is the state of the form aggregate not yet exhausted: the meditator has dissolved thought to cessation and attained the deep concentration of "unmoving in motion and stillness, remembering and forgetting as one," but remains in the phase of "a clear-sighted man in deep darkness" — the eye (prajñā) is prepared, but the light has not yet emerged. **The form aggregate exhausted** (*sèyīn jìn* 色陰盡) is the state after its dissolution: "the ten directions open with no further darkness," the wisdom-light is fully released. **Solidified false thought** (*jiāngù wàngxiǎng* 堅固妄想) is the essential substance of the form aggregate: the form aggregate binds sentient beings in the illusion of "body" precisely because of a *solidified*, *stubbornly rooted*, *deeply entrenched* clinging to the four great elements as constituting something substantial, something that is the self, something that can be relied upon.

#### 2.2.4 The Ten Form-Aggregate States in Full

What follows is the complete text of the ten form-aggregate states in their canonical order. Each state follows the same structure: a description of the phenomenal content, followed by the adjudicative formula ("this is called X; attaining this only momentarily; it is not a sagely attainment; not construing as sagely, it is called a wholesome state; if construed as sagely, one receives the host of demons").

**First state: the body capable of passing through obstruction.**[^26]

> "Ānanda! When in this state you refine the subtle clarity, the four great elements no longer weave together, and in a moment the body is able to pass through obstruction. This is called *subtle clarity overflowing before sense-objects*; it is merely a momentary meritorious effect. It is not a sagely attainment. If not construed as sagely, it is called a wholesome state; if construed as sagely, one receives the host of demons."

**Second state: extracting parasites from within the body.**[^27]

> "Ānanda! If with this same mind you further refine the subtle clarity, the body becomes inwardly pervious, and the meditator suddenly extracts roundworms from within his body, with the body's form intact and undamaged. This is called *subtle clarity overflowing the bodily form*; it is merely a momentary subtle effect. It is not a sagely attainment. If not construed as sagely, it is called a wholesome state; if construed as sagely, one receives the host of demons."

**Third state: Dharma-voices heard in space.**[^28]

> "Again, with this mind refining inwardly and outwardly, at that time the vital spirits — soul and spirit, will and mind — apart from the sense-receptive body, all interpenetrate, alternating as host and guest; suddenly one hears Dharma-voices in the air, or hears the ten directions all expounding secret meanings at once. This is called *the vital spirits separating and recombining, completing wholesome seeds*; attaining this only momentarily, it is not a sagely attainment. If not construed as sagely, it is called a wholesome state; if construed as sagely, one receives the host of demons."

**Fourth state: all species transforming into Tathāgatas.**[^29]

> "Again, with this mind clear and pellucid, inner radiance manifest, the ten directions uniformly become the color of *jambūnada* gold, and all species of beings transform into Tathāgatas. At that moment the meditator suddenly sees Vairocana enthroned upon a celestial light-platform, surrounded by a thousand Buddhas, with hundreds of millions of lands and lotus flowers appearing simultaneously. This is called *the mind-spirit stained by numinous awakening, the mind-light in research illuminating worlds*; attaining this only momentarily, it is not a sagely attainment. If not construed as sagely, it is called a wholesome state; if construed as sagely, one receives the host of demons."

**Fifth state: empty space filled with jeweled colors.**[^30]

> "Again, with this mind refining the subtle clarity, observing without cease, pressing down, subduing, restraining, and transcending, suddenly the empty space of the ten directions becomes seven-jeweled colors, or a hundred-jeweled colors, all simultaneously filling space without mutual obstruction, with blue, yellow, red, and white each purely manifest. This is called *the force of pressing-down exceeding its measure*; attaining this only momentarily, it is not a sagely attainment. If not construed as sagely, it is called a wholesome state; if construed as sagely, one receives the host of demons."

**Sixth state: seeing things in a dark room as in daylight.**[^31]

> "Again, with this mind penetrating and pellucid, with the essential light undisturbed, suddenly at night in a closed dark room one sees various objects no different than in the daytime, yet the objects of the dark room are not removed either. This is called *the mind subtly refining its seeing, the vision penetrating obscurity*; attaining this only momentarily, it is not a sagely attainment. If not construed as sagely, it is called a wholesome state; if construed as sagely, one receives the host of demons."

**Seventh state: fire and blade unable to cause pain.**[^32]

> "Again, with this mind entering perfect empty-fusion, the four limbs suddenly become like grass and wood, and burning by fire or cutting by blade arouses no sensation. Further, the flame cannot burn and sever; the cutting of flesh is like shaving wood. This is called *the coalescence of defilements displacing the nature of the four elements, entering into purity*; attaining this only momentarily, it is not a sagely attainment. If not construed as sagely, it is called a wholesome state; if construed as sagely, one receives the host of demons."

**Eighth state: all lands becoming Buddha-lands.**[^33]

> "Again, with this mind perfecting purity, with the power of pure mind reaching its zenith, suddenly one sees the great earth and mountains and rivers of the ten directions all become Buddha-lands, complete with the seven jewels and brilliant light filling everywhere. One also sees Tathāgatas as numerous as the sands of the Ganges filling the realms of space, with magnificent pavilions and palaces. Below one sees the hells, above one observes the celestial palaces, all without obstruction. This is called *the concentration of aspiration and aversion growing deeper daily, thought long making images*; it is not a sagely attainment. If not construed as sagely, it is called a wholesome state; if construed as sagely, one receives the host of demons."

**Ninth state: seeing distant places and relatives.**[^34]

> "Again, with this mind researching into the deep and distant, suddenly in the middle of the night one sees distant marketplaces and streets, relatives and kin, and hears their conversations. This is called *the compelled mind being forced to its limit and flying out, seeing through partitions*; it is not a sagely attainment. If not construed as sagely, it is called a wholesome state; if construed as sagely, one receives the host of demons."

**Tenth state: the body of a spiritual friend transforming.**[^35]

> "Again, with this mind researching to the limit of refinement, one sees the body of a good spiritual friend transforming, suddenly, without cause, changing in various ways. This is called *the perverse mind receiving elemental sprites, or encountering celestial māras entering into the stomach of the mind, who then without cause preach Dharma and penetrate wondrous meanings*. This is not an everyday phenomenon; it is not a sagely attainment. **If not construed as sagely, the māra-events will dissipate; if construed as sagely, one receives the host of demons.**"

**The structural rupture at the tenth state must be specifically noted.** In the first nine states, the adjudicative formula reads "not construing as sagely, it is called a wholesome state" — the state itself is affirmed as wholesome (provided it is not construed as sagely). In the tenth state, the formula becomes "not construing as sagely, the māra-events will dissipate" — the state is no longer called wholesome, but explicitly called "māra-events," and the result of not construing it as sagely is merely that the māra-events dissipate.

This rupture is not rhetorical variation but structural signal. The first nine states are *the meditator's own temporary meritorious effects* (instances of meditative power momentarily exceeding delusion), and thus count as ordinary side effects of meditation. The tenth state is already *perverse mind receiving sprites* or *celestial māras entering the stomach of the mind* — it is **external māra** (not an internal state), and therefore cannot be called "wholesome" but only "if unrecognized as sagely, the māra-events will cease of themselves."

#### 2.2.5 The Chapter's Concluding Admonition

After the ten form-aggregate states, the Buddha provides a concluding exhortation for the section:[^36]

> "Ānanda! These ten kinds of *dhyāna* manifestations arise when the form aggregate's use of mind comes into mutual contention with meditative observation. Sentient beings in their stubborn delusion, not taking their own measure, encounter these conditions and, unable to recognize them, claim to have reached sagehood — the grave offense of false speech is completed, and they fall into the Avīci hell. You should, in the time after the Tathāgata's extinction, proclaim this teaching in the Latter Dharma age, ensuring that celestial māras find no opportunity, protecting and maintaining those who seek the unsurpassed path."

Three points emerge. First, the ten states arise through "the mutual contention of form aggregate and mind" — "contention" naming the wrestling between meditative observation and the solidified false thought of the form aggregate. Second, the consequence of construing these states as sagely is "the grave offense of false speech is completed, and they fall into the Avīci hell" — directly echoing the Śūraṅgama's fourth instruction on purity in fascicle 6. Third, the teaching is explicitly designated as a Latter Dharma necessity: the meditators most vulnerable to form-aggregate errors are precisely those whose good spiritual friends are scarce.

### 2.3 Commentaries: Zǐxuán's "Manifested States of Dhyāna" and Jiāoguāng's Three-Tier Classification

#### 2.3.1 Zǐxuán's *Yìshū*: Manifestation of Dhyāna States, Not Catalogue of Māras

The Northern Song commentator Zǐxuán, in his *Shǒuléngyán yìshū zhù jīng* (T1799), **does not use the term "fifty skandha-māras."** His preferred term is "the manifestation of states in *dhyāna*" (*chén chánnà xiànjìng* 陳禪那現境):[^37]

> "The seventh major division, *the manifestation of states in dhyāna*, concerns states that arise when the practitioner, through the gradual cultivation of precepts, concentration, and wisdom, generates internal power that stirs the afflictions and externally stirs the māra-kings. In the process of contemplation, the five aggregates gradually dissolve from coarse to subtle. As each aggregate is dissolved, ten states appear through the force of that process — whether afflictions, karmic seeds, deviant thoughts, or demons and spirits — manifesting in *dhyāna* through various unusual appearances. If the practitioner lacks broad hearing and wisdom to recognize them, he will cling and mistake what is not for what is. Having previously cultivated correctly, he turns into deviance. The Buddha, out of compassion for such beings, taught this chapter without being asked."

The selection of the term "manifestation of states in *dhyāna*" is of decisive importance. **It positions the entire chapter as "the phenomenal appearance of states in the course of meditative cultivation," rather than as "a catalogue of fifty māra-incidents."** The former is a positive description (a phenomenology of meditative development); the latter is a negative warning (a genealogy of demonic possession). Zǐxuán's framing is much closer to the *Upakkilesa Sutta*'s orientation: states are natural phenomena of meditative unfolding; the problem is not in the states themselves but in the practitioner's capacity to discern them.

Zǐxuán's most critical passage is his commentary on the adjudicative formula of the first state:[^38]

> "*Attaining this only momentarily, it is not a sagely attainment. Not construing as sagely, it is called a wholesome state; if construed as sagely, one receives the host of demons.* If one understands this to be the efficacy of meditative practice, then there is no fault, and so it is called a wholesome state. **If one dismisses all of it as māra, the beneficial effect is repressed. If one says it is already sagely, but the defilements are not yet severed — therefore one is counseled to recognize wholesomely and not cling.**"

This comment establishes a critical **middle-way principle**: *neither dismiss all as māra nor affirm as sagely*. If one categorizes the ten form-aggregate states wholesale as māra, one suppresses the wholesome meritorious effects that belong to *dhyāna*. If one claims them as sagely, one commits great false speech in a state where afflictions are not yet severed. Zǐxuán's conclusion is "recognize wholesomely and not cling" (*shàn shí ér wú qǔzhuó* 善識而無取著) — affirm the state as wholesome while refusing to cling to it. This middle-way is the most precise reading of the canonical formula "not construing as sagely, it is called a wholesome state."

Zǐxuán's comment on the tenth state explicitly confirms its structural difference from the first nine: "**Yet this section is not a wholesome state; it is purely māra-harassment, differing from the preceding text, where wholesome seeds prompting the thought of attainment are the beginning of māra-formation.**"[^39] This directly corroborates our analysis of the adjudicative rupture at the tenth state.

#### 2.3.2 Jiāoguāng's *Zhèngmài shū*: Aggregate-Māra, External Māra, Celestial Māra

The Ming commentator Jiāoguāng uses the term "five-aggregate māra-states" (五陰魔境) — again, **not "fifty skandha-māras."**[^40] His distinctive contribution is a three-tier classification of the entire chapter:[^41]

> "*Or your own aggregate-māras* — the ten kinds in the form aggregate are the beginner's own manifestation, without external māras, and are therefore called 'your own aggregate-māras.' The ten kinds in the receptive aggregate have already summoned external māras, but these māras still enter covertly and do not yet show themselves bodily. The ten kinds in the perceptive aggregate are where celestial māras, demons, and sprites appear; if one does not know this, one cannot avoid mistaking the thief for one's own child — either taking oneself to be sagely, or taking the māra to be sagely: both are called 'mistaking the thief for the child.'"

The three-tier structure is: (1) ten form-aggregate states = **aggregate-māras** (the practitioner's own form-aggregate delusion manifesting, without external māra intervention); (2) ten receptive-aggregate māras = **external māras** (external māras have been summoned but enter the body covertly, not yet manifesting); (3) ten perceptive-aggregate māras = **celestial māras, demons, and sprites** (external māras now manifest and operate directly on the practitioner). This three-tier classification is decisive, because it tells us the source of danger in the form-aggregate states lies entirely within the practitioner — there is no external māra at work; everything is a projection of the practitioner's own form-aggregate delusion. This is also why the form-aggregate adjudication formula is "not construing as sagely, it is called a wholesome state": because there is no real māra, only the practitioner's own misrecognition.

Jiāoguāng offers a memorably Chan-flavored gloss on "not construing as sagely" — the **Qín-gōng Ridge** metaphor:[^42]

> "*Not construing as sagely* means: the practitioner, encountering such a state, knows it to be an incidental meritorious effect and pays it no mind. As a recent enlightened one said: 'I ascended Qín-gōng Ridge, gazed upon the four continents — let the four continents be, I just keep watching the *huàtóu*.' *It is called a wholesome state* — this also suffices to verify that one's efforts are bearing fruit, that the mind's wonder is not empty; it can increase confidence, incentivize diligence, and is also a manifestation of past wholesome roots, a prelude to future stages of mind-opening. It is truly an auspicious state, free of fault."

"I ascended Qín-gōng Ridge, gazed upon the four continents — let the four continents be, I just keep watching the *huàtóu*." This is the Chan tradition's living commentary on "not construing as sagely." Even when one has ascended to a high place and seen the panorama of the four continents, one does not concern oneself with those continents but continues to keep watching the *huàtóu*. No matter how vast the states or how expansive the vision, they have nothing to do with the substance of practice; the task remains to keep watching the *huàtóu* — to continue turning hearing inward to the stream (*fǎnwén rùliú* 反聞入流).

### 2.4 Lateral Support: *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* and *Yogācārabhūmi*

#### 2.4.1 The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*: Four Māras as "Really One Māra"

The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* (T1509), fascicle 68, systematically defines the four māras — affliction-māra, five-aggregate māra, death-māra, and celestial-prince māra — and issues a critical qualification: "*they are really one māra; they are distinguished only by aspect.*"[^43]

> "Māra is of four kinds: affliction-māra, five-aggregate māra, death-māra, and celestial-prince māra. ... Question: If the one five-aggregate māra subsumes the other three, why are four distinguished? Answer: *They are really one māra; they are distinguished only by aspect.* ... Where there are five aggregates there are afflictions; where there are afflictions the celestial māra gains his advantage; the coordination of five aggregates and afflictions is what gives rise to the celestial māra."

This is critical for our argument: **the four māras are not four independent entities but four aspects of a single māra.** The five aggregates are the root; afflictions are the aggregates' activity; the celestial māra is the fruit of affliction; the death-māra is the terminus of the whole process. Hence the Śūraṅgama's unfolding of the māra-genealogy around the five-aggregate structure is perfectly consistent with the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*'s fundamental understanding: **the five aggregates are the axis; all other māras derive from them.** This also vindicates the host-and-guest discourse: why "host"? Because the five aggregates are the root of māra and the root of awakening; if the host is deluded, the guest (the other three māras) gains advantage.

Fascicle 5 offers the broadest possible definition of māra:[^44] "Apart from the real marks of all dharmas, everything else is called māra." This definition places the fifty skandha-māras within the overarching framework of *prajñā*. Each form-aggregate state is māra not because it represents demonic interference but because it is **not the real mark of all dharmas**; as long as one clings to it, one is outside reality.

#### 2.4.2 The *Yogācārabhūmi-śāstra*: Two Axes of Obstruction

The *Yogācārabhūmi-śāstra* (T1579) lists the four māras (aggregate, affliction, death, celestial) in fascicle 29, and enumerates "five kinds of concentration-obstruction" (anger, conceit, sensual craving, *satkāya-dṛṣṭi*, inability to endure) in fascicle 63.[^45] More importantly, the *Yogācārabhūmi-śāstra* establishes a **two-axis structure of obstruction**: concentration-obstruction and affliction-obstruction:

- *concentration-obstruction* not released, *affliction-obstruction* released = *wisdom-liberated arhat*
- *concentration-obstruction* released, *affliction-obstruction* not released = *one who has bodily realized* (the meditative adept who has not yet severed afflictions)
- both concentration-obstruction and affliction-obstruction released = *doubly-liberated arhat*

For our argument, this two-axis structure clarifies that **the ten form-aggregate states primarily concern concentration-obstruction**: the practitioner has already achieved considerable concentration power (sufficient to generate form-aggregate phenomena) but has not yet fully severed the afflictions, and therefore encounters the subtle reappearance of aggregate-delusion in deep states. This corresponds exactly to the Āgama level of Godhika's "temporary liberation": there is concentration, there are states, but concentration power and affliction-elimination are not identical, and the concentration can regress because the root of affliction remains.

## 3. Core Arguments

With the textual basis established, we can now develop the seven core arguments of this paper: (3.1) the fifty skandha-māras as the Mahāyāna systematization of Āgama diagnostic teaching; (3.2) the host-and-guest discourse as the completed form of the Āgama framework; (3.3) "not construing as sagely" as the unfolding of "knowing as it truly is" and "subduing all perceptions"; (3.4) the five-layer internal structure of the ten form-aggregate states; (3.5) the structural rupture at the tenth state; (3.6) the Ābhassara deva's cosmogonic misrecognition as a cosmological instance of "construing as sagely"; (3.7) the recursive echo with S6-P06 and S6-P07.

### 3.1 Genealogization, Not New Invention

The first and most fundamental argument of this paper is that **the fifty skandha-māras of the Śūraṅgama are not an original phenomenological catalogue invented by the text, but a Mahāyāna genealogization of the Āgama diagnostic teaching on meditative states.**

Three levels of evidence support this claim. **At the phenomenological level**, the ten form-aggregate states — "body passing through obstruction," "extracting parasites," "Dharma-voices in space," "all species transforming into Tathāgatas," "empty space filled with jeweled colors," "seeing in darkness," "fire and blade causing no pain," "all lands becoming Buddha-lands," "seeing distant places," "spiritual friend transforming" — each find direct correspondence in the eleven *upakkilesa*s of the *Sūtra on the Origin of King Dīrghāyu*. The Buddha's "obtaining light and seeing forms" directly corresponds to the fourth state's vision of Vairocana and the fifth state's jeweled colors; the elation affliction ("suddenly finding four treasures") directly corresponds to the euphoria at seeing such visions; the self-exaltation affliction is precisely the psychological mechanism of "construing as sagely"; the diverse-perceptions affliction directly corresponds to the third state's hearing of multiple Dharma-voices; the excess-exertion affliction ("gripping the fly too hard") directly corresponds to "refining the subtle clarity" with excessive forcing. These are not coincidental similarities; they are manifestations of the same meditative phenomenology at different levels of elaboration.

**At the adjudicative level**, the Śūraṅgama's core criterion "not construing as sagely, it is called a wholesome state; if construed as sagely, one receives the host of demons" has three precise Āgama precursors: (a) the Buddha's own "recognize but do not cling" methodology for the eleven *upakkilesa*s; (b) the *Dhyāna-practice Sūtra*'s "knowing as it truly is / not knowing as it truly is" fourfold classification; (c) the *Noble Horse Sūtra*'s "subdue all perceptions" as the defining property of true dhyāna. All three share the same structure: **the gravest danger in meditation is not the states themselves but the misjudgment of states.** The Śūraṅgama simply extends this single principle from the Āgama's compact formulations into a complete formula applicable to fifty specific states.

**At the structural level**, the Śūraṅgama's organization of meditative states into the five-aggregate framework is itself a deep application of the Āgama's five-aggregate theory. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* has already pointed out that "they are really one māra, distinguished only by aspect" — the four māras are centered on the five aggregates. The Śūraṅgama's innovation is not to propose a new classification of māras, but to organize the typical meditative states within each of the five aggregates into ten categories, thereby forming a complete 5 × 10 = 50 diagnostic matrix. Each cell of this matrix can be traced to an Āgama prototype.

The practical conclusion of this argument is: **a meditator encountering any of the ten form-aggregate states should neither panic nor rejoice, nor treat them as esoteric Śūraṅgama exotica, but should recognize them as a meditative matter the Buddha has been addressing since Āgama times, a structural phenomenon in meditative development, requiring only the Āgama-to-Śūraṅgama procedure of "recognize it, do not cling."** Returning the fifty skandha-māras to their Āgama context dissolves the mystagogical misreading that has accreted in the Latter Dharma tradition and restores this chapter to its proper status as a continuous part of the Buddhist pedagogy of meditative discernment.

### 3.2 The Host-and-Guest Discourse Completes the Āgama Diagnostic Framework

The Śūraṅgama's host-and-guest discourse — "if the host is deluded, the guest gains his advantage" — is the completed form of the Āgama diagnostic teaching.

In the Āgamas, "knowing as it truly is" and "not knowing as it truly is" are two parallel states, and the Buddha does not explicitly articulate the dynamic relation between them. The *Dhyāna-practice Sūtra* tells us there are four meditators, but does not explain why some are "progressing yet thinking they regress" and others "regressing yet knowing regression as it truly is" — where does this difference come from? The *Sūtra on the Origin of King Dīrghāyu* tells us of eleven *upakkilesa*s, but does not explain why some practitioners are vulnerable to them and others are not — who is the host of these afflictions? The *Noble Horse Sūtra* tells us of the opposition of true dhyāna and wild-horse dhyāna, but does not explain why, sitting on the same meditation seat, some are wild horses and others are true horses — what is the subject of this distinction?

The Śūraṅgama's host-and-guest discourse answers these questions: **the host is the five aggregates**. All such distinctions ultimately depend on whether this host is awake. The five aggregates are not a passive container, nor an enemy to be wholly annihilated, but a **subject-bearing observer** that can choose to recognize states without clinging or can choose to sink into states by construing them as sagely. The core insight of the discourse is: **in meditation, the five aggregates are not the guest; māra is the guest. The five aggregates are you; māra is an outsider. Whether the guest can succeed depends entirely on the host's state of awakening.**

This discourse provides an ontological grounding for the Āgama's "knowing as it truly is." Knowing as it truly is is not a technique, a method, or an externally acquired capacity; it is a **natural function that the five-aggregate host already possesses**. As long as the host is not deluded, knowing as it truly is occurs automatically. The Śūraṅgama calls this function "wondrous awakening" (*miàojué* 妙覺) — "within their defilement, within your wondrous awakening, like wind blowing light, like a blade cutting water." Wondrous awakening is innate to the five-aggregate host; it need not be cultivated, only uncovered by not being deluded.

This point is profoundly important for contemporary meditators. **You do not need to "learn" to recognize the form-aggregate states; you need only maintain non-delusion — and in the state of non-delusion, recognition occurs automatically.** The work of practice is not to acquire a diagnostic technique but to sustain the awakened condition of the five-aggregate host. The former is seeking outside; the latter is inner observation. The former grows heavier and more exhausting with study; the latter grows simpler and lighter with practice.

### 3.3 "Not Construing as Sagely" as the Unfolding of "Knowing as It Truly Is" and "Subduing All Perceptions"

The relation between the Śūraṅgama's "not construing as sagely, it is called a wholesome state" and the Āgama's "knowing as it truly is" and "subduing all perceptions" deserves further refinement. These are three expressions of the same teaching, but with subtly different emphases, forming a graduated three-layer diagnostic procedure.

"Knowing as it truly is" emphasizes **cognition**: recognizing exactly what the current state is without misjudgment. This is the basic requirement. "Subduing all perceptions" emphasizes **counteraction**: with respect to every perception that arises within a state, subdue them all, preventing them from elaborating further into grasping, distinction, and construal. This is a step beyond knowing as it truly is — not only to recognize, but actively to prevent extension. "Not construing as sagely, it is called a wholesome state" emphasizes **positioning**: affirming a state as wholesome (a legitimate side effect of *dhyāna*) while refusing to position it as "sagely attainment." This is subtler still — not to suppress the state entirely, but to give it a correct location (wholesome state) while refusing an incorrect one (sagely attainment).

The three together form a graduated three-layer diagnostic procedure: (1) *know as it truly is* — what is this that I am encountering?; (2) *subdue all perceptions* — do not let the mind elaborate this into further thought; (3) *do not construe as sagely* — label this as "a wholesome state of *dhyāna*," not as "attainment."

The practical guidance for contemporary meditators is: **when encountering any of the ten form-aggregate states, first do not react, but ask what this is (know as it truly is); second, do not allow the mind to elaborate further imagery or interpretation (subdue all perceptions); third, internally label it correctly as "a wholesome state of *dhyāna*, not awakening" (not construing as sagely).** The three steps complete, the state naturally dissipates, requiring no further intervention.

### 3.4 The Five-Layer Internal Structure of the Ten Form-Aggregate States

If the ten form-aggregate states were merely ten parallel items, they would constitute only a list. If we group them by internal structure, however, a clear five-layer gradation emerges: **body → hearing → sensation → space → other**. This fivefold structure is itself the sequential unfolding of the form aggregate.

| Layer | States | Content | Corresponding Faculty |
|-------|--------|---------|----------------------|
| **Layer 1: Body** | States 1–2 | Body passing through obstruction; extracting parasites | Sense of one's own body's permeability |
| **Layer 2: Hearing** | State 3 | Dharma-voices heard in space | Auditory faculty |
| **Layer 3: Sensation** | States 4–7 | Vairocana appearing; jeweled colors; seeing in darkness; no sensation of fire or blade | Visual and tactile reorganization |
| **Layer 4: Space** | States 8–9 | Mountains and rivers as Buddha-lands; seeing distant places | Expansion of spatial perception |
| **Layer 5: Other** | State 10 | Spiritual friend's body transforming | Cognition of others |

**Layer 1 is the body.** "Body passing through obstruction" and "extracting parasites" both treat the meditator's new perception of their own body — the body can be penetrated, the body can be inwardly visualized. These are the most basic, most physiologically proximate states of the form aggregate, because the form aggregate is itself the solidified false thought of the "body composed of the four great elements." When the solidified false thought is slightly loosened, the first thing to appear is the sense that "the body is not as solid as before."

**Layer 2 is hearing.** "Dharma-voices heard in space" — from the body outward to sound. This is the first step of the form aggregate's movement beyond the self. Why is hearing placed before vision? Because in the context of the ear-faculty perfect penetration taught in fascicle 6 of the Śūraṅgama, **hearing is the working surface of the practice of turning hearing inward to the stream.** The meditator's entire attentional focus is on the ear faculty, so when the form aggregate loosens, the earliest symptoms appear in the ear faculty. This is the structural reason for the Śūraṅgama's placement of hearing at the third state, consistent with the overall S6 trajectory of ear-faculty perfect penetration.

**Layer 3 is sensation.** "Vairocana appearing," "jeweled colors in empty space," "seeing in a dark room," "fire and blade causing no pain" — these four states all concern the reorganization and amplification of sensory function. Vision can see Buddhas, jeweled colors, and objects in darkness; the tactile sense can no longer register the pain of fire and blade. This layer represents the breakthrough of sensory function beyond the customary limits imposed by the four great elements after the form aggregate has loosened.

**Layer 4 is space.** "Mountains and rivers as Buddha-lands" and "seeing distant places" — from sensation outward to space itself. These are not "what I see" but "space itself has changed." Mountains and rivers becoming Buddha-lands is a change in the nature of space; seeing distant places is a change in the distance of space. This layer is subtler than the third, because it destabilizes not the senses themselves but the spatial framework on which the senses depend.

**Layer 5 is other.** "The spiritual friend's body transforming" — this state treats the cognition of an *other*: not the meditator's own body, not sound, not sensation, not space, but the body of *another practitioner*. This is the outermost, most interpersonal layer of the form aggregate. Strikingly, the subject of this state is a *good spiritual friend* — the very being onto whom the meditator projects the highest possible expectations. When the meditator's form-aggregate delusion is projected onto the spiritual friend, the friend's body "suddenly and without cause changes in various ways" — this is in fact a projection of the meditator's own consciousness, but is mistakenly perceived as a change in the spiritual friend.

These five layers share a common feature: **from inner to outer, from near to far, from body to other**. The unfolding of the form aggregate is itself the sequential extension of the habit "taking the body as self" from its inner core to its outer periphery. The ten form-aggregate states are exactly ten characteristic scenes in which this habit is progressively deconstructed from inner core to outer periphery by meditative force. Each state corresponds to a moment when one layer of the form-aggregate structure is destabilized, and each destabilizing moment can be mistaken for "awakening."

### 3.5 The Structural Rupture at the Tenth State

Section 2.2.4 already noted that the adjudicative formula of the tenth state differs from that of the first nine. The first nine read "not construing as sagely, it is called a wholesome state"; the tenth reads "not construing as sagely, the māra-events will dissipate." This rupture is not a stylistic variation but a **structural signal**: the tenth state no longer belongs to the form-aggregate internal category of "self-manifestation" but is the transitional point at which external māra begins to intervene as the form aggregate is about to dissolve.

Zǐxuán's commentary explicitly identifies this: "This section is not a wholesome state; it is purely māra-harassment, differing from the preceding text."[^46] Jiāoguāng concurs: "As the form aggregate is about to shatter, the māra-mind rises in fury at that very moment; this is why māra-harassment begins here."[^47] Both commentators agree that the tenth state is the bridge from the form-aggregate states to the receptive-aggregate māras — the transition from "aggregate-māras self-manifesting" to "external māras intervening."

Why does external māra begin to intervene as the form aggregate approaches dissolution? This is simply the specific enactment of the "palace-shattering" passage from the host-and-guest discourse: "When even one of you awakens to the real and returns to the source, all the empty space of the ten directions is dissolved... the māra-kings and celestial beings of the ordinary realms, seeing their palaces unaccountably crumbling..." When the practitioner is on the verge of shattering the form aggregate, their practice has reached a depth that begins structurally to destabilize the three realms, and only then do the māra-kings personally intervene. The māra of the first nine states is "the projection of the practitioner's own form-aggregate delusion"; only at the tenth does true external māra appear — "perverse mind receiving elemental sprites, or encountering celestial māras entering into the stomach of the mind."

The practical implication for meditators is: **the first nine states can be handled independently — not construing as sagely automatically returns them to the category of wholesome states; the tenth state requires vigilance — because this is no longer one's own form-aggregate delusion but the beginning of true external māra activity.** This is also why the adjudicative formula of the tenth state is "māra-events will dissipate" rather than "it is called a wholesome state": the phenomenon is acknowledged as māra-event, but as long as one does not construe it as sagely, the māra-event will dissipate of itself.

### 3.6 The Ābhassara Devas: Cosmogonic Version of "Construing as Sagely"

Section 2.1.6 cited the Brahmajāla Sutta narrative of Ābhassara devas being reborn into the empty Brahma heaven and misrecognizing themselves as the creator. This is not merely mythological; it is a profound case study in meditative psychology — **the cosmogonic version of "construing as sagely."**

Why does the first-born Great Brahmā of the empty Brahma heaven misrecognize himself as the creator? Because he knows only two facts: (1) I existed first; (2) others appeared afterwards. From these two facts he erroneously infers: *therefore others exist because of me*. The error lies in this: temporal priority does not entail causal agency. My existing before others does not mean that I am the cause of their existence; the real cause is the exhaustion of their own merit, life, and karma — a process with no relation to me whatsoever.

This psychological mechanism exactly corresponds to the "construing as sagely" structure of the ten form-aggregate states. In meditation, a practitioner sees light, sees Buddhas, sees jeweled colors — these states appear within his meditative experience; this is factual. But the practitioner then reasons erroneously: *these states appear in my meditative experience, therefore they are proof of my awakening*. The error lies exactly where the Great Brahmā's lay — **the priority of a state's appearing is not the causal agency of awakening**. That a state appears in your experience does not entail that you are its agent or causal subject; the real cause is that your meditative power has momentarily transcended the normal interaction with form-aggregate delusion, a process that has no relation to your personal attainment.

The Great Brahmā and the form-aggregate practitioner commit the same error: **mistaking "what happens to me" for "what is caused by me."** This error has a technical name: **overestimation** (*zēngshàng màn* 增上慢, *adhi-māna*).[^48] Overestimation is not ordinary arrogance; it is an exquisitely subtle cognitive error — mistaking a phenomenon produced by causes and conditions for an accomplishment of one's own subjectivity. The Āgamas repeatedly warn that overestimation is the source of "not obtaining the meaning, only great vexation," the subtlest trap a meditator must guard against. The Śūraṅgama's "construing as sagely" is precisely the subtle transformation of overestimation within deep meditative states.

This correspondence reveals that **the core problem of the fifty skandha-māras is not meditative phenomenology but the error in subjective cognition**. As long as the error in subjective cognition is not resolved, any state may become māra; conversely, once the error is resolved, even the most remarkable state is merely "a wholesome state." The Śūraṅgama host-and-guest discourse and the Āgama Ābhassara teaching are in perfect agreement on this point.

### 3.7 Recursive Echo with S6-P06 and S6-P07

S6-P08's position in the S6 series is not independent of the preceding papers but forms a recursive structure with S6-P06 and S6-P07.

**The first layer of echo (P07 → P08)** concerns the ethical-to-psychological extension. S6-P07's fourth instruction on purity (prohibition against great false speech) treats the ethical layer of "not having attained but claiming attainment" — once spoken aloud, it is a precept violation. S6-P08 treats the same issue at the psychological layer: **one need not speak it aloud; merely construing a state as sagely in one's mind already constitutes "construing as sagely."** The progression from ethical to psychological is the core link from P07 to P08. The concluding phrase of P07's fourth instruction — "to become a demon of attachment-views, to lose the seed of the Tathāgata" — is the explicit anticipation of this progression.

**The second layer of echo (P06 → P08)** concerns the preparatory-to-manifestation extension. S6-P06's Three Gradual Stages treat the final layer of purification before entering deep meditative observation: eliminating aiding causes, removing the inclining nature, violating present karma. These three gradual stages sever the roots of the form aggregate — attachment to aiding causes (the five pungent herbs), to inclining nature (the habitual momentum of sexual, murderous, thieving, and false-speech activity), and to present karma (the six senses meeting objects). **But no matter how thoroughly the Three Gradual Stages sever these roots, subtle reappearances of form-aggregate delusion in deep meditation still occur — and these are precisely the ten form-aggregate states.** P06 treats the severance of the roots; P08 treats the residual gleams of the form aggregate that still flicker in deep *dhyāna* after the roots have been severed. Without P06's root-severance, the form-aggregate states would not arise (one would still be at the coarser level of the five hindrances); only with P06's root-severance do the form-aggregate states become possible.

**The third layer of echo (P06 + P07 → P08)** concerns the perimeter-to-mirror extension. P06 is the innermost layer (purification of mind), P07 is the outermost layer (ethical protection), and together with the *maṇḍala* and *mantra* of fascicle 7 (middle layer, spatial protection), they constitute the three-layered perimeter. P08 is the **demon-revealing mirror** offered to the meditator within the three-layered perimeter, within deep meditative observation, at the critical stage when the form aggregate is about to dissolve. The three-layered perimeter is defense; the demon-revealing mirror is diagnosis — the former prevents external māra from entering, the latter discriminates the nature of arising states. Neither is dispensable: without the three-layered perimeter, the practitioner is not even qualified for the form-aggregate states to arise; without the demon-revealing mirror, the practitioner will inevitably falter in the form-aggregate states.

This recursive structure is the key to the overall S6 architecture. Papers 1–5 establish the theoretical ground (gateway-selection); papers 6–7 establish the perimeter (preparatory); papers 8–10 establish the demon-revealing mirror (diagnosis) — these three layers together form a complete support system for deep meditative practice. S6-P08 as the first paper of the diagnostic layer treats the general framework and the form-aggregate layer; P09 treats the receptive and perceptive aggregates; P10 treats the formative and consciousness aggregates; P11 provides the overall conclusion.

## 4. Interdisciplinary Resonance: Clinical Differential Diagnosis ⚠️

This section offers **clinical differential diagnosis** in contemporary medicine as a heuristic analogy for how the adjudicative structure of the ten form-aggregate states may be understood by a modern reader. Per this research program's consistent interdisciplinary methodology, the analogy is strictly framed as **heuristic** and is not intended to claim any reductive equivalence; the non-reducibility is explicitly marked at the end of the section.

### 4.1 Points of Similarity: Symptom Inventory, Exclusionary Logic, Adjudicative Matrix

Clinical differential diagnosis is the core diagnostic methodology of modern medicine. Faced with a patient presenting multiple symptoms, the physician cannot simply rely on first impressions but must first enumerate **all diseases that could account for those symptoms** (the differential list), and then progressively exclude possibilities until the most evidence-consistent diagnosis remains. The method's strength is in **excluding no possibility, presupposing no conclusion, letting the evidence speak**.

The adjudicative structure of the ten form-aggregate states exhibits three structural similarities with clinical differential diagnosis.

**First, the systematization of a symptom inventory.** Clinical differential diagnosis requires the physician to be familiar with all diseases that might correspond to a given class of symptoms (e.g., "chest pain" may indicate myocardial infarction, aortic dissection, pulmonary embolism, costochondritis, reflux esophagitis, psychogenic pain, etc.). Without a complete symptom inventory, no differential can be performed. The ten form-aggregate states likewise constitute a systematic symptom inventory: the full catalogue of ten characteristic manifestations corresponding to the symptom "supranormal phenomena arising in deep *dhyāna*." The meditator encountering such phenomena must be able to locate the current experience within one of the ten states in order to judge correctly.

**Second, the exclusionary logic of adjudicative hierarchy.** Clinical differential diagnosis operates by "ruling out the most severe first" — encountering chest pain, the physician first excludes myocardial infarction (fatal) before other possibilities. The adjudication of the ten form-aggregate states likewise has a hierarchy: **the first thing to exclude is the misrecognition "this is sagely attainment"** (because this leads directly to great false speech); **second, to exclude the misrecognition "this is māra"** (because this leads to panic and abandoning practice); only then does one locate the experience as "a wholesome state of *dhyāna*." This hierarchy is structurally isomorphic with clinical exclusionary logic.

**Third, the completeness of the adjudicative matrix.** Clinical differential diagnosis depends on a complete matrix of evidence — symptoms, signs, tests, imaging, history — that must mutually corroborate to arrive at diagnosis. The adjudication of the ten form-aggregate states likewise depends on a complete matrix: phenomenal content of the state, knowing-as-it-truly-is about the path of escape, the current condition of the five hindrances, the degree of precept observance, the reliance on a good spiritual friend. No single dimension of evidence is sufficient for complete diagnosis.

### 4.2 Fundamental Differences: Naming vs Non-Naming; Treatment vs Non-Clinging

However, clinical differential diagnosis and the adjudication of the ten form-aggregate states are **diametrically opposed in fundamental orientation**. This must be clearly stated, or the analogy will immediately slide into reductionism.

**The first fundamental difference is purpose.** Clinical differential diagnosis aims at **naming a disease**: mapping a set of symptoms onto a specific ICD code and selecting a treatment protocol accordingly. Naming is the terminus of the diagnostic process. The adjudication of the ten form-aggregate states operates in exactly the opposite direction: **do not name**. The essence of "not construing as sagely" is precisely not to map the state onto any definite "thing" (neither "sagely attainment" nor "māra"). Why? Because once named, the practitioner will begin to respond to this named "thing" — craving for sagely attainment, fear of māra; and these responses are themselves new delusional formations that strengthen form-aggregate delusion further. The meditative adjudication requires "recognize without naming, observe without clinging" — the direct opposite of clinical diagnosis's "naming as the precondition of treatment."

**The second fundamental difference is the subject-object structure.** Clinical differential diagnosis maintains a clear subject-object opposition between "the diagnosing physician" and "the diagnosed disease": the physician is the subject, the disease is the object, and diagnosis is the subject's cognitive operation on the object. The adjudication of the ten form-aggregate states, by contrast, is performed **in the process of dissolving the subject-object opposition**. In the work of turning hearing inward to the stream, the hearer (subject) and the heard (object) are progressively merging; the form-aggregate states that arise in this process are themselves side effects of the loosening of the subject-object boundary. Using the "diagnosing-diagnosed" structure to understand the form-aggregate states would immediately re-establish the subject-object opposition and thereby undermine the very work of turning hearing inward.

**The third fundamental difference is the ontology of healing.** Clinical differential diagnosis aims to heal a disease that is, by ontological assumption, *real* — myocardial infarction is a real pathological process; treatment is intervention in a real pathological process. The "treatment" of the ten form-aggregate states is built on an opposite ontology: the form aggregate itself is solidified false thought, and the ten states are all false thought. "Treatment" is not intervention in a real thing but recognition that all states "have the same root in false thought" — their very unreality. The former presupposes the real; the latter presupposes the unreal.

### 4.3 Non-Reducibility

Therefore, **the adjudication of the ten form-aggregate states absolutely cannot be reduced to a special case of clinical differential diagnosis.** They share a surface structure of "systematized symptom inventory + adjudicative matrix," but their internal purposes, subject-object structures, and ontological presuppositions are entirely different. Clinical differential diagnosis operates within a framework of subject-object opposition, realist ontology, and naming-as-treatment; the adjudication of the ten form-aggregate states operates within a framework of subject-object dissolution, non-realist ontology, and non-naming-as-liberation.

The proper use of the analogy is this: **borrow the contemporary reader's familiarity with differential diagnosis to understand that "the ten form-aggregate states are not a casual list but a diagnostic system with internal logic";** but once this is understood, the analogy itself must be set down and clinical thinking must not be used to process the form-aggregate states. This is the consistent "analogy-as-bridge" principle of this research program: analogy is the bridge of introduction, not the house in which one lives.

⚠️ **Heuristic analogy, not theoretical equivalence.** The adjudication of the ten form-aggregate states is a unique achievement of the Mahāyāna meditative tradition and cannot be reduced to the methodology of modern medical differential diagnosis. The two structures are similar at the surface level but entirely different in deep purpose, subject-object structure, and ontology.

## 5. Middle-Way Correction

This section, following the consistent middle-way methodology of this research program, addresses six common misreadings of the fifty skandha-māras and the ten form-aggregate states. Each misreading is marked ❌, followed by the correction.

### 5.1 ❌ Fifty Skandha-Māras = Avoid All Meditative States

**Misreading.** Since the fifty skandha-māras enumerate fifty states that all lead to problems, would it not be safest simply to have no meditative states at all? If one cultivates meditation into a condition entirely free of phenomenal experience, surely one is safe?

**Correction.** The states themselves are not the problem; they are natural phenomena of meditative development. In the *Sūtra on the Origin of King Dīrghāyu*, the Buddha himself, prior to awakening, "obtained light and saw forms" — this is not meditative failure but a normal phase of *dhyāna* development. The Āgama tradition concerning the Ābhassara devas makes the point still more explicitly: light and sound in deep meditation are the fruition of the second *dhyāna* heaven, structurally side effects of the concentration itself. The issue is not the state but the practitioner's interpretation of it. The point of "not construing as sagely, it is called a wholesome state" is not "no state is better"; it is "states are also acceptable, provided they are not construed as sagely." To set "no states at all" as one's meditative goal is in fact another form of construing as sagely — mapping "no state" onto "sagely attainment" exhibits precisely the same structural error as "construing as sagely."

### 5.2 ❌ Having States = Progress

**Misreading.** Since the ten form-aggregate states arise only in deep *dhyāna* characterized by "the four great elements no longer weaving," the very occurrence of such states should be confirmation of progress — evidence that meditative practice is bearing fruit.

**Correction.** The occurrence of a state is not equivalent to progress. The *Dhyāna-practice Sūtra* explicitly identifies two misjudgment modes — "progressing but thinking he is regressing," "regressing but thinking he is progressing" — and there is no one-to-one correspondence between the appearance or disappearance of states and actual meditative advance. More decisively, Godhika in *Saṃyukta Āgama* 1091 had attained the state of "temporary liberation of mind and bodily realization" and yet backslid six times consecutively. States are neutral: they may be side effects of progress, or harbingers of regression (as state ten is the entry point of external māra intervention). To treat any state as proof of progress is a violation of "knowing as it truly is."

### 5.3 ❌ The Fifty Skandha-Māras are Śūraṅgama's Original Phenomenology

**Misreading.** The detailed enumeration of fifty meditative states in the fifty skandha-māras is a unique contribution of the Śūraṅgama, a late Mahāyāna or tantric-style phenomenological catalogue with no direct relation to early Buddhist meditative teaching.

**Correction.** This is the central misreading this paper seeks to correct. The fifty skandha-māras are not original to the Śūraṅgama; they are the Mahāyāna systematization of the Āgama diagnostic teaching on meditative states. The eleven *upakkilesa*s of the *Sūtra on the Origin of King Dīrghāyu*, the four adjudications of the *Dhyāna-practice Sūtra*, the true-dhyāna definition of the *Noble Horse Sūtra*, the hindrance regression theory, the Godhika case, the light-phenomena tradition of the Āryan Silence discourses, the overestimation warnings of the *Kaṭṭhūpama Sutta* — these are all direct Āgama sources of the fifty skandha-māras. What the Śūraṅgama does is to genealogize and complete these scattered teachings, not to propound a new doctrine. Decontextualizing the fifty skandha-māras from their Āgama heritage, treating them as Śūraṅgama esoterica, constitutes a serious misreading of the entire Buddhist tradition of meditative discernment.

### 5.4 ❌ Ten Form-Aggregate States ≈ Clinical Hallucinations

**Misreading.** The phenomena described in the ten form-aggregate states — "body passing through obstruction," "hearing Dharma-voices in space," "seeing Buddhas before oneself" — closely resemble phenomena described by contemporary psychiatric science: dissociative hallucinations, hypnotic hallucinations, perceptual alterations induced by deep meditation. Therefore the ten form-aggregate states can be explained by contemporary neuroscience: they are simply the brain-functional changes induced by deep concentration, with no special religious significance.

**Correction.** This is the classic reductionist misreading that Section 4 already addressed. The ten form-aggregate states and clinical hallucinations may exhibit surface phenomenological similarities but differ entirely in internal structure: (a) clinical hallucinations arise within a framework of subject-object opposition (subject experiences object), while the form-aggregate states arise in the process of subject-object dissolution; (b) clinical hallucinations aim at symptom elimination and restoration of normal perception, while the form-aggregate states aim at non-clinging to *any* perceptual state and continued practice; (c) clinical hallucinations ontologically presuppose "normal perception is real, hallucination is deviation," while the form-aggregate states ontologically presuppose "all perception is false thought, with no 'normal' as baseline." Reducing the form-aggregate states to clinical hallucinations dismantles the entire Śūraṅgama meditative framework into unrelated neural phenomena, and can neither explain why these states have the five-layer structure (body → hearing → sensation → space → other) nor why the adjudication is "not construing as sagely, a wholesome state" rather than "symptom elimination = cure."

### 5.5 ❌ "Fifty Skandha-Māras" is the Sūtra's Own Term

**Misreading.** "Fifty skandha-māras" (*wǔshí yīnmó* 五十陰魔) is the standard section title used in the Śūraṅgama fascicles 9 and 10; it is the term the Buddha himself used for the content of this chapter.

**Correction.** The phrase "fifty skandha-māras" is **not the sūtra's original usage** but a later convention adopted in the tradition. The textual examination of commentators in Section 2.3 has made this clear: Zǐxuán in his *Shǒuléngyán yìshū zhù jīng* uses "the manifestation of states in *dhyāna*" (*chén chánnà xiànjìng*); Jiāoguāng in his *Zhèngmài shū* uses "five-aggregate māra-states" (*wǔyīn mójìng*); neither uses "fifty skandha-māras." The sūtra itself uses phrases like "the five-aggregate host," "māra-states," "manifestation of states in *dhyāna*," "wholesome states in *dhyāna*." The significance of this terminological observation is that the title "fifty skandha-māras" tempts the reader into reading the whole chapter as "a genealogy of fifty māras," reinforcing the mystagogical misreading; returning to Zǐxuán's more neutral "manifestation of states in *dhyāna*" restores the chapter to its proper status as "the phenomenological appearance of states in the course of *dhyāna*" — not a māra catalogue, but a systematization of meditative phenomenology.

### 5.6 ❌ Skandha-Māras are a Problem Only for Ordinary Beings, Not for Noble Ones

**Misreading.** The fifty skandha-māras describe problems encountered by ordinary (*pṛthagjana*) meditators; noble ones already at the fruitions — *srotāpanna*, *sakṛdāgāmin*, *anāgāmin*, *arhat* — do not encounter such problems.

**Correction.** The Godhika narrative is the most direct refutation of this misreading. Godhika had attained "temporary liberation of mind and bodily realization" — a state near arhatship — and yet he backslid six times before ending his life to prevent a seventh backsliding. The Āgama list of nine unlearning persons (*aśaikṣa*) explicitly includes "regression-prone arhat," "reflecting arhat," "protection-prone arhat," and other arhat types associated with regression; only "immovable arhats" are entirely free from regression. In other words, **with the sole exception of immovable arhats, all other arhats are subject to possible regression**, and the typical occasion for such regression is precisely the misjudgment of states resembling the ten form-aggregate states in deep concentration. Treating the fifty skandha-māras as "a problem only for ordinary beings" would allow fruit-level practitioners to grow complacent, making them more vulnerable to subtle-state errors, not less. The Śūraṅgama, in placing the general framework at the very end of fascicle 10 and addressing it to "all beings of the Latter Dharma," precisely affirms that this teaching covers the entire spectrum of deep meditative practice regardless of level of attainment.

## 6. Conclusion

### 6.1 Essential Restatement: The 2,500-Year Continuity from Eleven *Upakkilesa*s to Fifty Skandha-Māras

The central thesis of this paper is that the fifty skandha-māras of the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra*, fascicles 9 and 10 — and especially the ten form-aggregate states treated here — are not a mystagogical invention of the Śūraṅgama but the Mahāyāna systematization of the Buddha's diagnostic teaching on meditative states, a teaching that the Buddha had already been practicing since Āgama times. From the Buddha's first-person account of his own eleven *upakkilesa*s in the *Sūtra on the Origin of King Dīrghāyu*, to the four-type adjudication of meditators in the *Dhyāna-practice Sūtra*, to the true-dhyāna-versus-wild-horse-dhyāna contrast in the *Noble Horse Sūtra*, to the regression theory of the five hindrances, to Godhika's six backslidings from temporary liberation, to the light-phenomena tradition of the Āryan Silence corpus, to the overestimation warnings of the *Kaṭṭhūpama Sutta* — the Āgamas already established a complete diagnostic framework for meditative states. What the Śūraṅgama does is to genealogize these scattered teachings according to the five-aggregate structure, enumerating ten characteristic scenes of misrecognition for each aggregate, and threading the entire system with the simple and powerful adjudicative formula "not construing as sagely, it is called a wholesome state; if construed as sagely, one receives the host of demons." This is the same teaching continuously transmitted from the fifth century BCE in the Ganges basin to the eighth century CE in Chinese translation of the Śūraṅgama, a continuity of 2,500 years.

The ten form-aggregate states, as the first layer of this system, treat the outermost, coarsest, and most physiologically proximate ten states, unfolding in a five-layer internal structure of body → hearing → sensation → space → other. Each state is a typical moment in the mutual contention of meditative force and form-aggregate delusion, adjudicated by the formula "not construing as sagely, it is called a wholesome state" — the state is affirmed as a wholesome side effect of *dhyāna* without being positioned as sagely attainment. The tenth state is the bridge from the form aggregate to the receptive aggregate, and the adjudicative formula accordingly ruptures from "it is called a wholesome state" to "the māra-events will dissipate," signaling the beginning of external māra intervention and the imminent transition to the second layer of the fifty-māra system.

### 6.2 Practice Guidance: The Mirror is for Looking at Oneself

This 2,500-year continuity yields three concrete practical principles for contemporary meditators.

**First, do not panic and do not rejoice at the arising of states.** If, in meditation, you experience any of the phenomena resembling the ten form-aggregate states — disappearance of bodily sensation, hearing voices that are not there, seeing light, seeing Buddhas, seeing extraordinary visions, numbness to pain — your first response should be: *this is a normal phenomenon of meditative development; the Buddha himself experienced it; no panic is needed, and no jubilation is warranted.* It occurred in Āgama times, it occurred in the Tang dynasty, it will occur today. Do not mark something as special because it has occurred, and do not mark yourself as behind because it has not.

**Second, the demon-revealing mirror is for looking at oneself, not at others.** The adjudication of the fifty skandha-māras is to be applied to one's own meditation, not to evaluate the states of other meditators. This is of the greatest importance, for the commonest misuse in the Latter Dharma age is exactly this — treating the entries of the fifty skandha-māras as weapons with which to accuse other meditators: "you are in the fourth form-aggregate state," "you are in the fifth receptive māra." This use entirely violates the intent of the Śūraṅgama. Zǐxuán's commentary explicitly states "one must not dismiss all as māra" — if one may not dismiss even one's *own* states as māra, how much less another's? The mirror is for reflecting oneself; turned on another, what it reflects is not the other's māra but one's own conceit.

**Third, if the host is not deluded, māra-events dissipate of themselves.** The Śūraṅgama host-and-guest discourse teaches us that māra has no independent destructive force; all destructive force derives from the practitioner's own delusion. Conversely, as long as the host (the five-aggregate awakening) is not deluded, any māra-event will dissipate automatically. This does not mean the practitioner should undertake to "counteract" each state — indeed, attempted counteraction itself is a form of construing as sagely (treating the state as an enemy requiring intervention). The correct posture is **to sustain the original condition of non-delusion, allowing states to come and go of themselves.** This is the living commentary of the Chan tradition's Qín-gōng Ridge: "I ascended Qín-gōng Ridge, gazed upon the four continents — let the four continents be, I just keep watching the *huàtóu*." However vast the states, however expansive the vision, they have nothing to do with one's fundamental task; the task is to keep watching the *huàtóu* (continuing to turn hearing inward to the stream), and the states will process themselves.

### 6.3 Open Question: Why is the Receptive Aggregate More Dangerous Than the Form Aggregate?

The open question this paper leaves for the reader and for S6-P09 is this: **the form aggregate is the trap of the body, the receptive aggregate is the trap of sensation — why is the receptive aggregate more difficult than the form aggregate?** Each of the ten form-aggregate states has a definite phenomenal content (body, hearing, sensation, space, other); the meditator need only refrain from construing that content as sagely in order to categorize it as wholesome. The ten receptive-aggregate states, however, treat **sensation itself** — grief, joy, recollection, contentment, anxiety, happiness, arrogance, lightness, emptiness, desire — and these sensations have no definite "phenomenal content"; they *are* the state of the mind itself. One can refuse to construe "seeing Buddhas" as sagely (fourth form-aggregate state), but how does one refuse to construe "profound peace and bliss" as awakening? One can refuse to construe "seeing in darkness" as psychic power (sixth state), but how does one refuse to construe "all attachment has been released" as liberation?

The ten receptive-aggregate māras are more dangerous than the ten form-aggregate states precisely because sensation is what the meditator has been pursuing all along — one of the very aims of *dhyāna* is peace and joy. When true peace and joy arrive, how can one not cling? Jiāoguāng offers an explicit indication: the form-aggregate states are "the practitioner's own form-aggregate self-manifestation" without external māra intervention; the receptive-aggregate māras are already "external māras entering into the body covertly" — and the entry point of external māra is precisely the practitioner's clinging to sensation. This transition will be the subject of S6-P09. This paper ends here.

The companion sūtra 177 (*Shuō jīng*) adds a further fourfold analysis for each *dhyāna* stage: "equipped for regression," "dwelling," "equipped for ascent," "equipped for disenchantment toward *nirvāṇa*." *Taishō Tripiṭaka* vol. 1, no. 26.

[^8]: *Saṃyukta Āgama* 926 (fascicle 33), the Buddha's instruction to Seniya Kātyāyana: 「當修真實禪，莫習強良禪。」 *Taishō Tripiṭaka* vol. 2, no. 99.

[^9]: Ibid.: 「詵陀！比丘如是禪者，不依地修禪，不依水、火、風、空、識、無所有、非想非非想而修禪……悉伏彼想。」This passage is the ultimate definition of true *dhyāna* in the Āgamas.

[^10]: *Saṃyukta Āgama* 705 (fascicle 26): 「有五退法。何等為五？謂貪欲、瞋恚、睡眠、掉悔、疑蓋，是則退法。」 *Taishō Tripiṭaka* vol. 2, no. 99.

[^11]: *Saṃyukta Āgama* 715 (fascicle 27), the detailed "food and non-food" mechanism of the five hindrances. The five counteractive contemplations are: impurity contemplation (against sensual desire); loving-kindness contemplation (against ill will); luminosity contemplation (against drowsiness); calm contemplation (against restlessness); dependent-origination contemplation (against doubt). *Taishō Tripiṭaka* vol. 2, no. 99.

[^12]: *Saṃyukta Āgama* 1091 (fascicle 39), the Godhika narrative: 「尊者瞿低迦……時受意解脫身作證，數數退轉；一、二、三、四、五、六反退。」The parallel version is *Separately Translated Saṃyukta Āgama* fascicle 2, sūtra 30 (the Qiúdé Sūtra 求悳經). *Taishō Tripiṭaka* vol. 2, nos. 99 and 100.

[^13]: *Saṃyukta Āgama* 501 (fascicle 18), Mahāmaudgalyāyana's reflection on Āryan Silence: 「若有比丘息有覺有觀，內淨一心，無覺無觀三昧生喜樂，第二禪具足住，是名聖默然。」 *Taishō Tripiṭaka* vol. 2, no. 99.

[^14]: *Dīrgha Āgama* fascicle 21, "Chapter on the Three Disasters": 「火災始欲起時……有人得第二禪者，即踊身上昇於虛空中……身壞命終，生光音天。」 *Taishō Tripiṭaka* vol. 1, no. 1.

[^15]: *Dīrgha Āgama* fascicle 14, *Brahmajāla Sutta*: 「此劫始成，有餘眾生福盡、命盡、行盡，從光音天命終，生空梵天中。」This passage is the cosmological source of the "first-cause / creator-god" view. *Taishō Tripiṭaka* vol. 1, no. 1.

[^16]: *Ekottara Āgama* fascicle 34: 「光音天子……食地肥多者，轉無威神，又無光明，身體遂重而生骨肉，即失神足，不復能飛。」 *Taishō Tripiṭaka* vol. 2, no. 125.

[^17]: *Madhyama Āgama* fascicle 1, *Sūtra of the Simile of the Burning Wood* (*Kaṭṭhūpama Sutta*, sūtra 5), seven similes repeatedly emphasizing that the karmic fruit of "non-*brahmacārins* calling themselves *brahmacārins*, non-śramaṇas calling themselves śramaṇas" is birth in hell. *Taishō Tripiṭaka* vol. 1, no. 26.

[^18]: *Madhyama Āgama* fascicle 9, *Campā Sutta*: 「於此眾中有一比丘已為不淨」; Mahāmaudgalyāyana identifies the impure bhikkhu through supernormal power, "took him by the arm and led him out, opened the gate and set him outside." *Taishō Tripiṭaka* vol. 1, no. 26.

[^19]: *Madhyama Āgama* fascicle 29, the second *Campā Sutta*, with the similes of weed-rice, winnowing, and striking the tree: 「莫令污穢餘真好麥。」 *Taishō Tripiṭaka* vol. 1, no. 26.

[^20]: *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* fascicle 9, the Buddha's unsolicited teaching as he is about to rise from the lion's throne: 「汝猶未識修奢摩他、毘婆舍那微細魔事。」 *Taishō Tripiṭaka* vol. 19, no. 945.

[^21]: *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* fascicle 9: 「汝等一人發真歸元，此十方空皆悉銷殞……是故神鬼及諸天魔、魍魎、妖精，於三昧時僉來惱汝。」 *Taishō Tripiṭaka* vol. 19, no. 945.

[^22]: *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* fascicle 9, the host-and-guest discourse: 「如風吹光，如刀斷水……汝如沸湯，彼如堅氷，煖氣漸隣，不日銷殞……由汝心中五陰主人。主人若迷，客得其便。」 *Taishō Tripiṭaka* vol. 19, no. 945.

[^23]: *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* fascicle 10, Ānanda's question on the common root of the five aggregates and the Buddha's answer: 「五陰本因同是妄想。」 *Taishō Tripiṭaka* vol. 19, no. 945.

[^24]: *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* fascicle 10: 「此五陰元重疊生起，生因識有，滅從色除。理則頓悟，乘悟併銷；事非頓除，因次第盡。」 *Taishō Tripiṭaka* vol. 19, no. 945.

[^25]: *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* fascicle 9, the definition of the form-aggregate region: 「當住此處入三摩提，如明目人處大幽暗……名色陰盡，是人則能超越劫濁。觀其所由，堅固妄想以為其本。」 *Taishō Tripiṭaka* vol. 19, no. 945.

[^26]: *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* fascicle 9, form-aggregate state 1: 「精明流溢前境，斯但功用暫得如是。」 *Taishō Tripiṭaka* vol. 19, no. 945.

[^27]: *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* fascicle 9, form-aggregate state 2: 「精明流溢形體。」 *Taishō Tripiṭaka* vol. 19, no. 945.

[^28]: *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* fascicle 9, form-aggregate state 3: 「精魄遞相離合成就善種。」 *Taishō Tripiṭaka* vol. 19, no. 945.

[^29]: *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* fascicle 9, form-aggregate state 4: 「忽見毘盧遮那踞天光臺……心魂靈悟所染，心光研明照諸世界。」 *Taishō Tripiṭaka* vol. 19, no. 945.

[^30]: *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* fascicle 9, form-aggregate state 5: 「抑按功力踰分。」 *Taishō Tripiṭaka* vol. 19, no. 945.

[^31]: *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* fascicle 9, form-aggregate state 6: 「心細密澄其見，所視洞幽。」 *Taishō Tripiṭaka* vol. 19, no. 945.

[^32]: *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* fascicle 9, form-aggregate state 7: 「塵併排四大性一向入純。」 *Taishō Tripiṭaka* vol. 19, no. 945.

[^33]: *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* fascicle 9, form-aggregate state 8: 「欣厭凝想日深，想久化成。」 *Taishō Tripiṭaka* vol. 19, no. 945.

[^34]: *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* fascicle 9, form-aggregate state 9: 「迫心逼極飛出，故多隔見。」 *Taishō Tripiṭaka* vol. 19, no. 945.

[^35]: *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* fascicle 9, form-aggregate state 10: 「邪心含受魑魅，或遭天魔入其心腹……不作聖心，魔事銷歇。」The adjudicative formula differs from the first nine: "called a wholesome state" becomes "the māra-events will dissipate." *Taishō Tripiṭaka* vol. 19, no. 945.

[^36]: *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* fascicle 9, chapter conclusion for the form-aggregate states: 「如是十種禪那現境，皆是色陰用心交互故現斯事……謂言登聖，大妄語成，墮無間獄。」 *Taishō Tripiṭaka* vol. 19, no. 945.

[^37]: *Shǒuléngyán yìshū zhù jīng* fascicle 9a: 「大文第七、陳禪那現境者，此之境界是修行人由戒定慧漸修有力，內動煩惱、外動魔王，於觀行中五陰漸滅，從麁至細。」 *Taishō Tripiṭaka* vol. 39, no. 1799.

[^38]: *Shǒuléngyán yìshū zhù jīng* fascicle 9a, commentary on the first form-aggregate state's adjudicative formula: 「苟知此是禪者功力則無有失，故云善境。若總撥為魔，則抑善功用。若言即聖，又未斷惑，故令善識而無取著也。」 *Taishō Tripiṭaka* vol. 39, no. 1799.

[^39]: *Shǒuléngyán yìshū zhù jīng* fascicle 9a, commentary on the tenth form-aggregate state: 「然此一章非善境界，純是魔嬈，不同前文，皆稱善種起心作證方始成魔。」 *Taishō Tripiṭaka* vol. 39, no. 1799.

[^40]: *Shǒuléngyán jīng zhèngmài shū* fascicle 9: 「△一、無問自說五陰魔境。」Jiāoguāng uses the term "five-aggregate māra-states" (五陰魔境) rather than "fifty skandha-māras." *Xù Zàng Jīng* vol. 12, no. 275.

[^41]: *Shǒuléngyán jīng zhèngmài shū* fascicle 9, three-tier classification: 「色陰中十種，方是初心自現，尚無外魔，故言汝陰魔也。受中十種，已召外魔，然且潛入身中，而魔未現身。想中十種，方有天魔、鬼神、魑魅。」 *Xù Zàng Jīng* vol. 12, no. 275.

[^42]: *Shǒuléngyán jīng zhèngmài shū* fascicle 9, the Qín-gōng Ridge metaphor: 「如近世悟人云：上得秦公嶺，望見四部洲，從他四部洲，依然顧話頭是也。」 *Xù Zàng Jīng* vol. 12, no. 275.

[^43]: *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* fascicle 68: 「魔有四種：煩惱魔、五眾魔、死魔、天子魔……實是一魔，分別其義，故有四。」 *Taishō Tripiṭaka* vol. 25, no. 1509.

[^44]: *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* fascicle 5: 「除諸法實相，餘殘一切法，盡名為魔。」 *Taishō Tripiṭaka* vol. 25, no. 1509.
