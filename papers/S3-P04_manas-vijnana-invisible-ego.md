## Abstract

The manas-vijñāna (seventh consciousness) represents one of Yogācāra philosophy's most revolutionary theoretical innovations. It addresses a problem that has perplexed practitioners for millennia: why does "knowing there is no self" fail to free us from self-grasping? This paper takes Xuanzang's three verses on the seventh consciousness from the *Verses on the Characteristics of the Eight Consciousnesses* (八識規矩頌) as its structural framework, and adopts a sūtra-first citation architecture.

§2 establishes the scriptural and treatise basis in two parts: Part A (Sūtra Foundations) draws on the *Saṃyuktāgama* for early Buddhist observations of the sense-base of mind (意根) and self-view (身見), the *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra* for the canonical positioning of "mind" (意/manas) between ālaya and the six consciousnesses, the *Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra* for the "mind as stage-manager" verse, the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* for the analysis of the mind sense-base as illusory, and the *Ghanavyūha Sūtra* for the simile of manas grasping the storehouse consciousness like a magnet attracting iron. Part B (Treatise Foundations) draws on the *Cheng Weishi Lun*, *Yogācārabhūmi-śāstra*, *Mahāyānasaṃgraha*, and *Bajishi Guiju Buzhu* for systematic doctrinal analysis.

§3 unfolds the core argument in seven layers: (1) the unique positioning of manas through the "constant and deliberative" four-fold analysis; (2) the three-fold nature via "objective appearance with substance, obscured-neutral, bridging cognition and root"; (3) the epistemological implications of "non-valid cognition"; (4) the four afflictions and their internally generative logic; (5) the eighteen associated mental factors; (6) the role of manas as "basis for defilement and purity" and the three-tier architecture; (7) the five stages of transformation and the complete fruition of equality wisdom. §4 offers the Default Mode Network as a heuristic analogy, the Chama Sūtra as an Āgama bridge, and Chinul's "wind stops but waves still surge" as a Chan dialogue. §5 provides five middle-way corrections.

**Keywords:** manas-vijñāna, constant deliberation (恒審思量), four afflictions, objective appearance with substance (帶質境), obscured-neutral (有覆無記), sixth-eighth non-correspondence (六八不相應), basis for defilement and purity (染淨依), equality wisdom (samatā-jñāna), Verses on Eight Consciousnesses

## I. Introduction

### 1.1 Textual Position

S3-P03 unfolded the ālaya-vijñāna — the foundational basis of the eight-consciousness system, the repository of all seeds, the ground from which body and world are projected. This paper turns to the most hidden yet most critical link in the system: the manas-vijñāna.

Among the eight consciousnesses in the *Śatadharmaprakāśamukha-śāstra* (百法明門論), the manas is listed seventh.[^1] But "seventh" is not merely an ordinal number — it marks manas's unique structural position: upward, it takes the ālaya-vijñāna's perceiving aspect (見分) as its object; downward, it serves as the cognitive root (意根) for the sixth consciousness. Standing between the ālaya and the first six consciousnesses, manas is the mediating pivot of the entire system.

The three verses on the seventh consciousness in the *Verses on the Characteristics of the Eight Consciousnesses* encompass its complete doctrine:

> Objective appearance with substance, obscured, bridging cognition and root; / Following conditions, it grasps a self — its cognition is non-valid. / Eight major [subsidiary afflictions], five universals, object-specific wisdom — / Craving, delusion, self-view, and conceit accompany it always.[^2]

> Constant deliberation, with self-image ever in tow; / Sentient beings day and night are sunk in a deep stupor. / Four afflictions and eight major [subsidiaries] arise together; / The six active consciousnesses call it the basis of defilement and purity.[^3]

> At Supreme Joy, the initial mind attains equality nature; / At the stage of effortless action, the ego is forever crushed. / The Tathāgata manifests the enjoyment body for others — / Bodhisattvas of the ten grounds are the beneficiaries.[^4]

Three verses, three layers: the first describes its nature — how it exists; the second describes its function — what it does and what consequences follow; the third describes its transformation — how it becomes equality wisdom. This paper unfolds each layer in sequence.

### 1.2 The Problem

The deepest frustration for a practitioner is often not "not knowing" but "knowing yet being unable to act accordingly."

A person can master the doctrine of no-self, observe clearly in meditation that the five aggregates are empty, and yet feel a surge of anger the moment someone criticizes them after leaving the meditation hall. A person can sincerely believe in the equality of all beings, yet unconsciously defend their own position and protect their own face.

This gap has a precise explanation in Yogācāra philosophy: **the sixth consciousness can understand no-self, but the seventh manas does not listen to the sixth.**

The "constant deliberation" (恒審思量) of manas means it does not require your intentional thinking about "I" to operate — it grasps at "I" while you are awake, while you sleep, while you are unconscious, and while you are in the attainment of non-perception. It is not a belief you have chosen; it is a program that has been running automatically since beginningless time.

This paper addresses three core questions. First, what is the nature and operating mechanism of manas — how is it "invisible" yet omnipresent? Second, how do its four afflictions constitute the complete structure of self-grasping — what is the logical relationship among self-delusion, self-view, self-conceit, and self-love? Third, how does manas transform — what is the path from "constant deliberation with self-image ever in tow" to "equality wisdom"?

### 1.3 Hundred Dharmas Coverage and Structure

This paper covers eighteen dharmas from the hundred: the manas-vijñāna (seventh consciousness) from the mind-dharmas, and its eighteen associated mental factors — five universals (contact, attention, feeling, perception, volition), four root afflictions (self-delusion, self-view, self-conceit, self-love), eight major subsidiary afflictions (torpor, restlessness, non-faith, laziness, heedlessness, forgetfulness, distraction, incorrect knowing), and one object-specific factor (wisdom — which is identical with self-view).[^5]

The configuration of these eighteen mental factors itself reveals manas's character: it has the most basic mental activities (universals), unceasing afflictions (four root plus eight major subsidiaries), and one special form of "wisdom" — but this wisdom is not prajñā; it is the "clever mistake" that cognizes the non-self as self.

§2 adopts a sūtra-first citation architecture: Part A establishes sūtra foundations through the *Saṃyuktāgama*, *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra*, *Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra*, *Śūraṅgama Sūtra*, and *Ghanavyūha Sūtra*; Part B presents the treatise foundations through the *Cheng Weishi Lun*, *Yogācārabhūmi-śāstra*, *Mahāyānasaṃgraha*, and *Bajishi Guiju Buzhu*. §3 unfolds the core argument following the three-verse structure. §4 provides cross-disciplinary dialogue. §5 offers five middle-way corrections. §6 concludes.

## II. Scriptural and Treatise Foundations

The theory of manas as an independent consciousness reaches its most precise systematization in the Mahāyāna Yogācāra treatises. But the phenomenon it addresses — perpetual self-referencing, the intractability of self-grasping — was already clearly observed and documented in early Buddhist scriptures. This section first establishes sūtra authority (Part A), then unfolds the systematic doctrinal analysis through the treatise tradition (Part B).[^6]

### Part A: Sūtra Foundations

#### 2.1 The Āgamas — Early Teachings on the Mind Sense-Base and Self-View

The theory of manas as an independent consciousness is a Mahāyāna Yogācāra innovation. But the "mind sense-base" (manāyatana, 意入處) as one of the six internal bases had already been established in the Āgamas, providing early Buddhist observational foundations for the manas theory.

**The special status of the mind sense-base among the six.** The *Saṃyuktāgama*'s "Sūtra on the Six Sixes" (六六法經) organizes all dharmas under the twelve bases (āyatanas), with "the mind sense-base" as one of the six internal bases.[^7] Unlike the five sense-faculties (eye, ear, nose, tongue, body), the mind sense-base does not correspond to any specific physical organ, yet it is the basis for all cognition of dharma-objects — "conditioned by mind and dharmas, mind-consciousness arises" (Sūtra 68).[^8] This observation laid the canonical groundwork for the Yogācāra identification of "mind-root = manas-vijñāna."

The *Saṃyuktāgama* further records a distinctive characteristic of the mind sense-base: "The mind-root constantly seeks agreeable dharmas; toward disagreeable dharmas it generates aversion."[^9] This automatic approach-avoidance quality resonates with manas's nature of "turning spontaneously" (任運而轉) — the early Buddhist observation captures the automaticity that Yogācāra would later systematize.

**Self-view and self-identity — early observations of manas's four afflictions.** The Āgama observations of "self-identity view" (sakkāya-diṭṭhi, 身見) and "conceit" (māna, 我慢) are direct presentations, in the domain of practice experience, of what manas's four afflictions describe at the level of consciousness-structure. *Saṃyuktāgama* Sūtra 9 presents the five-aggregate no-self teaching as its core instruction:

> "Form is impermanent; what is impermanent is suffering; what is suffering is not-self; what is not-self also does not belong to self. Observing thus is called true observation. So too for feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness."[^10]

Sūtra 34 further develops the observation systematically, and identifies the three fetters severed by the stream-enterer: "self-identity view, attachment to rites and rituals, and doubt."[^11] Here, "self-identity view" is precisely the early formulation of what Yogācāra calls "self-view" (ātma-dṛṣṭi) — one of manas's four afflictions. The Āgama observation is at the phenomenological level (practitioners experience self-identity view); Yogācāra's contribution is at the mechanistic level (explaining the consciousness-structure underlying this view).

#### 2.2 The Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra — The Canonical Positioning of Mind (Manas)

The *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra*'s chapter on "The Characteristics of Mind, Thought, and Consciousness" (心意識相品) is the Mahāyāna sūtra that most directly addresses the relationship among "mind" (citta), "thought" (manas), and "consciousness" (vijñāna), providing canonical authority for manas as an independent consciousness.

> "Broad-Wisdom, you should know: among beings wandering through the six destinies... the first to mature is the all-seeds mind-consciousness... This consciousness is also called ādāna-vijñāna ['appropriating consciousness'], because this consciousness follows the body and holds it. It is also called ālaya-vijñāna ['storehouse consciousness'], because within the body it shelters, conceals, and shares its fate. It is also called 'mind' [citta], because it is the accumulation and growth of forms, sounds, smells, tastes, and tactile objects. **Broad-Wisdom, the ādāna-vijñāna serves as the basis and foundation, and thereby the six consciousness-bodies turn.**"[^12]

"The ādāna-vijñāna serves as the basis, and the six consciousness-bodies turn" — the sūtra then distinguishes the operation of the five sense consciousnesses (relying on the five sense-faculties) from that of the mind consciousness (relying on "mind" as its root). In Yogācāra exegesis, this "mind" (意) is identified as the manas-vijñāna — it turns dependent on the ādāna (ālaya), while simultaneously serving as the basis for mind-consciousness.[^13]

The canonical authority the *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra* provides for the manas theory lies in the Buddha's own establishment of the three-tier architecture of "mind—thought—consciousness" (citta—manas—vijñāna), with "thought" (manas) occupying the mediating position between "mind" (ālaya) and "consciousness" (the six consciousnesses) — this is the sūtra source for manas's role as "bridging cognition and root" (通情本).

#### 2.3 The Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra — "Mind as Stage-Manager"

The *Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra* captures the role-division of the eight consciousnesses in a single verse, with a particularly vivid positioning for manas:

> "Mind is like the lead performer (工伎兒); / Thought is like the stage-manager (和伎者); / The five consciousnesses are the supporting cast (伴侶); / Conceptual projection is the audience (觀伎眾)."[^14]

The verse uses the theater as its metaphor: "mind" (citta / ālaya) is the director — controlling everything behind the scenes but not directly appearing on stage. "Thought" (manas) is the stage-manager — coordinating between director and cast, relaying the director's vision while driving the actors' performance. The "five consciousnesses" are the supporting performers, acting according to the stage-manager's arrangements. "Conceptual projection" (the sixth consciousness's discriminating activity) is the audience — watching, judging, projecting from the seats.

The positioning of "thought as stage-manager" in this verse perfectly aligns with the Yogācāra doctrine of "bridging cognition and root" — the stage-manager faces upward toward the director ("root") and downward toward the performers and audience ("cognition"), serving as the indispensable mediating pivot. Manas is neither the hidden fundamental basis (that is the ālaya) nor the front-stage discriminator (that is the sixth consciousness); it is the go-between connecting both ends — and precisely the role most easily overlooked.

#### 2.4 The Śūraṅgama Sūtra — Analysis of the Mind Sense-Base

The *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* applies rigorous dialectical analysis to each of the six bases, six objects, and six consciousnesses. Its analysis of the "mind sense-base" (意入) provides another canonical perspective on manas's illusory nature.

Fascicle 3 on the mind sense-base:

> "Mind and its weariness are both the fatigued appearance (瞪發勞相) of bodhi. Caused by the two kinds of illusory dusts — arising and ceasing — knowledge gathers at the center, inhaling and grasping internal dusts... **Therefore you should know that the mind sense-base is illusory, neither caused by conditions nor arising naturally.**"[^15]

"The mind sense-base is illusory" — the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra*, from the prajñā perspective, identifies what the mind-root (manas) presents as "self" not as real existence but as a "fatigued appearance of bodhi" — a mirage produced when originally pure awareness "stares" (瞪) for too long. This diagnosis complements Yogācāra's judgment of "non-valid cognition" (perpetual miscognition).

Fascicle 3 further interrogates the causal relationship between mind-root and dharma-objects:

> "Mind and dharmas serve as conditions to produce mind-consciousness... If it arises from mind, there must be some thought within your mind to activate it; if there are no preceding dharmas, mind has nothing from which to arise; apart from conditions there is no form — of what use would consciousness be?... **Therefore you should know that mind, dharmas, and mind-consciousness as a triad are all without [independent existence].**"[^16]

"The triad is entirely without [independent existence]" — mind-root, dharma-objects, and mind-consciousness are all without independent self-nature. This provides support from the prajñā system for manas's "non-valid cognition" — the "self" it grasps has no self-nature to begin with.

Fascicle 4 records that the mind sense-base possesses twelve hundred meritorious capacities.[^17] This observation of the scope of capacities and transformative potential provides canonical foreshadowing for manas's conversion to equality wisdom — although in its afflicted state manas perpetually deliberates on self-image, its perfected capacities upon transformation can "manifest enjoyment bodies, lands, and image-differentiations according to sentient beings' inclinations."

#### 2.5 The Ghanavyūha Sūtra — Manas Grasping the Storehouse Like a Magnet Attracting Iron

The *Ghanavyūha Sūtra* (大乘密嚴經) is one of the rare scriptures that directly describes the relationship between manas and the ālaya-vijñāna as the Buddha's own teaching. Amoghavajra's translation captures manas's operating mechanism in a single verse:

> "Manas takes the storehouse consciousness as its object, / Like a magnet attracting iron; / Like a snake with two heads, / Each performing its own function. / The defiled mind (染意) is likewise — / It grasps the ālaya, / Able to perform the activities of 'self,' / Expanding 'mine' [我所]; / Furthermore, together with mind-consciousness, / It serves as cause and then passes away."[^18]

"Like a magnet attracting iron" — manas's apprehension of the ālaya is not an action requiring deliberate initiation but automatic, perpetual, effortless, like magnetic attraction. This canonical simile provides the most intuitive sūtra image for the "constancy" (恒) of "constant deliberation." "Like a snake with two heads" — manas faces upward toward the storehouse consciousness and downward toward mind-consciousness, precisely confirming "bridging cognition and root." "Able to perform the activities of self, expanding mine" — manas not only grasps "self" but continuously expands the scope of "mine" (belonging-to-self), causing self-grasping to perpetually intensify.[^19]

> It should be noted that the sūtra footprint of the manas-vijñāna is genuinely thinner than that of the ālaya-vijñāna. The *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra* discusses "mind" (意/manas) but does not elaborate it as extensively as it does the ālaya. The *Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra* devotes a single verse-line — "thought is the stage-manager" — to the topic. The *Ghanavyūha Sūtra* verse above is among the very few scriptural passages directly describing manas's operating mechanism. The precise systematization of the manas doctrine is primarily the contribution of the treatise masters — Dharmapāla and Kuiji in particular. The sūtra foundations provide phenomenological observation (the Āgamas' mind-root and self-identity view) and structural positioning (the *Saṃdhinirmocana*'s mind—thought—consciousness tripartition); the treatise tradition completes the full doctrinal architecture on this foundation.[^20]

### Part B: Treatise Foundations

#### 2.6 The Cheng Weishi Lun — The Second Transformation

The *Cheng Weishi Lun* organizes the eight consciousnesses under the framework of "three transformations" (tri-pariṇāma), with manas as the second transformation. The verse explicitly defines all of manas's core characteristics:

> "Next, the second transformation / Is the consciousness called manas. / It turns dependent on that [ālaya], takes that as its object, / And has deliberation as both its nature and characteristic. / Four afflictions constantly accompany it — / Self-delusion, self-view, / Together with self-conceit and self-love, / And the remaining [mental factors] such as contact. / It belongs to the obscured-neutral category, / Bound to whatever realm and ground it arises in. / Arhats, cessation attainment, / And supramundane paths are without it."[^21]

This verse contains every essential element of manas doctrine: its name (manas), its basis and object (dependent on that, taking that as object — dependent on the ālaya and taking the ālaya's perceiving aspect as object), its nature and characteristic (deliberation), its accompanying afflictions (four afflictions constantly present), its moral quality (obscured-neutral), and its three states of absence (arhat, cessation attainment, supramundane path).

#### 2.7 The Yogācārabhūmi — Four Afflictions of the Defiled Mind

The *Yogācārabhūmi-śāstra* designates manas as the "defiled mind" (染污意) and, in fascicle 51 of the Viniścaya-saṃgrahaṇī, provides a complete account of the four afflictions' perpetual character:

> "As stated before, manas constantly turns together with the ālaya-vijñāna. Until it is severed, one should know that it constantly and simultaneously accompanies four innate, spontaneous afflictions: satkāya-dṛṣṭi, self-conceit, self-love, and ignorance. These four afflictions, whether in meditative absorption or outside it, should be known to be perpetually active, not in contradiction with wholesome [states], and of obscured-neutral moral quality."[^22]

Two points are critical here. First, the four afflictions are "simultaneously accompanying" — all four operate at once, not sequentially. Second, "whether in meditative absorption or outside it" — even in deep concentration, manas's four afflictions persist. Meditative absorption cannot sever manas's self-grasping.

Fascicle 63 further states that manas's four afflictions "cannot even be suppressed or counteracted by worldly antidotes... because they still manifest even in those who have attained freedom from desire."[^23] This means that even attainment of the highest formless realm does not stop manas's self-grasping. No worldly path can reach it.

#### 2.8 The Mahāyānasaṃgraha — Mutual Causation

The *Mahāyānasaṃgraha* uses two similes — the lamp-wick and the bundle of reeds — to explain the interdependent relationship between the ālaya-vijñāna and defiled consciousness (including manas):

> "Just as the light of a lamp and the wick mutually serve as cause for the other's arising and burning at the same time; and just as a bundle of reeds can stand upright by leaning on each other at the same time."[^24]

This simile reveals that the relationship between manas and the ālaya is not one-directional "passive apprehension." Rather, they are mutually causal in a dynamic loop — manas grasps the ālaya as self, and this very grasping perfumes back into the ālaya, generating deeper seeds of self-grasping.

#### 2.9 The Bajishi Guiju Buzhu — Complete Commentary on the Three Verses

The *Bajishi Guiju Buzhu* by the Ming-dynasty master Putai provides sentence-by-sentence commentary on all twelve verses, and is the most systematic sub-commentary for understanding Xuanzang's verse text. This paper draws extensively on it as a bridge between verse and treatise.[^25]

## III. Core Argument

### 3.1 Constant Deliberation: The Unique Positioning of Manas

"Manas" (末那), from the Sanskrit, means "deliberation" or "cogitation." But deliberation is not manas's exclusive province — the sixth consciousness also deliberates. What makes manas unique are two qualities: **constancy** (恒) and **deliberativeness** (審).

*Cheng Weishi Lun*, fascicle 2, defines the three transformations:

> "The first is called vipāka — the eighth consciousness, because its nature is predominantly karmic maturation. The second is called deliberation — the seventh consciousness, because it constantly and deliberatively cogitates. The third is called object-apprehension — the first six consciousnesses, because their object-apprehension is comparatively coarse."[^26]

"Constant" means uninterrupted. "Deliberative" means examining and assessing, grasping at self. The *Bajishi Guiju Buzhu* arranges the eight consciousnesses in a four-fold analysis of constancy and deliberativeness:

> "First, constant but not deliberative: the eighth consciousness — it does not grasp at self, yet is never interrupted. Second, deliberative but not constant: the sixth consciousness — it grasps at self, yet is interrupted. Third, both constant and deliberative: the seventh consciousness — it grasps at self and is never interrupted. Fourth, neither constant nor deliberative: the first five consciousnesses — they do not grasp at self."[^27]

The logical structure of this four-fold analysis merits careful examination. The eighth consciousness is "constant but not deliberative" — the ālaya never ceases, but it does not examine, does not grasp at self; it merely holds seeds and projects. The sixth consciousness is "deliberative but not constant" — it can examine and reflect, but it is interrupted: in deep sleep, in fainting, in the attainment of non-perception. The first five are "neither constant nor deliberative" — they are interrupted and do not grasp at self.

Only manas is "both constant and deliberative" — never interrupted, and perpetually grasping at self. This means: it grasps at self while you sleep, while you are unconscious, while you are in meditation. Unlike the sixth consciousness, which requires you to "think about self" to operate, manas runs automatically — without your participation. The *Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra*'s simile of "thought as stage-manager" is the canonical image for this "constant and deliberative" quality — the stage-manager does not step on stage, yet from curtain-rise to curtain-fall is never absent.[^28]

This is precisely why a practitioner can understand no-self at the level of the sixth consciousness yet still defend "self" in daily life — because the sixth consciousness's no-self insight is "deliberative but not constant," while manas's self-grasping is "both constant and deliberative." An interrupted insight cannot directly override an uninterrupted fixation.

### 3.2 Objective Appearance with Substance, Obscured-Neutral, Bridging Cognition and Root: Three Aspects of Manas's Nature

The opening phrase of the first verse — "objective appearance with substance, obscured, bridging cognition and root" (帶質有覆通情本) — defines manas's nature from three angles: its cognitive object, its moral quality, and its structural position.

**Objective appearance with substance (帶質境).** Yogācāra epistemology classifies cognitive objects into three types: veridical appearance (性境), independent shadow (獨影境), and objective appearance with substance (帶質境). Manas apprehends the third type. The sub-commentary explains:

> "True 'appearance with substance': mind apprehending mind. The intermediate representational image arises from both ends, linked in its arising — this is called true objective appearance with substance."[^29]

The object manas apprehends is the ālaya-vijñāna's perceiving aspect — this is real (it has "substance"). But in the process of apprehension, manas misconstrues it as "self" — and this "self" is fabricated (it "carries" delusion). A daily-life simile: you stand before a mirror, and the reflection genuinely derives from you (it has a real basis), but if you mistake the reflection for an independently existing other person, you have the structure of "objective appearance with substance" — a real foundation, erroneously interpreted. The *Ghanavyūha Sūtra*'s "like a magnet attracting iron" captures precisely this objective-with-substance apprehension — manas is automatically "attracted" to the eighth consciousness's perceiving aspect, and in the process automatically adds the interpretation "self."[^30]

**Obscured-neutral (有覆無記).** *Cheng Weishi Lun*, fascicle 5:

> "To what moral category do the mental factors accompanying manas belong? They belong to the obscured-neutral category, and no other. The four afflictions and other [factors] accompanying this consciousness are defiled dharmas, obstructing the holy path and obscuring one's own mind — hence called 'obscured.' They are neither wholesome nor unwholesome — hence called 'neutral.'"[^31]

"Obscured" means it veils the mind and obstructs the holy path. "Neutral" means it does not directly create strong wholesome or unwholesome karma. Although manas's four afflictions are the root of all defilement, their operation is too subtle, too automatic, to directly generate the kind of vigorous karma that the sixth consciousness produces. The sub-commentary elaborates: "Because these accompanying defiled dharmas have a subtle basis and turn spontaneously, they too belong to the neutral category."[^32] Manas is a precision bias-generator — it does not shout, but it runs beneath every cognition as a silent default. The *Śūraṅgama Sūtra*'s judgment that "the mind sense-base is illusory, neither caused by conditions nor arising naturally" confirms, from the prajñā perspective, this "obscured" quality — the "self" that manas grasps is neither a real entity produced by causes nor a naturally existing substance.[^33]

**Bridging cognition and root (通情本).** This phrase identifies manas's core structural role as mediating pivot. "Cognition" (情) refers to the sixth consciousness (its discriminating function); "root" (本) refers to the eighth consciousness (the foundational basis). Manas faces upward toward the eighth consciousness (the "root") and faces downward as the cognitive root for the sixth consciousness (the "cognition"). Standing between them, it is both the grasper of the eighth and the driver of the sixth. This mediating position is the structural foundation for manas being called the "basis for defilement and purity." The *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra*'s three-tier architecture — "the ādāna-vijñāna serves as the basis, and the six consciousness-bodies turn" — with "thought" (manas) mediating between "mind" (ālaya) and "consciousness" (the six), is the canonical source for "bridging cognition and root."[^34]

### 3.3 Following Conditions, Grasping Self — Cognition Is Non-Valid

The verse phrase "following conditions, it grasps a self — its cognition is non-valid" (隨緣執我量為非) delivers a direct epistemological verdict: manas belongs to "non-valid cognition" (aprāmāṇa). The sub-commentary explains:

> "This seventh consciousness follows the eighth consciousness's perceiving aspect as its condition and grasps it as self. Because this consciousness possesses only innate self and dharma grasping — spontaneously arising, not the discriminatively acquired self and dharma grasping. Why? Because it does not arise through forceful conceptual construction. Among the three types of valid cognition, it is exclusively non-valid — not cognizing in accord with its object, constantly miscognizing. Therefore it is non-valid cognition."[^35]

In Yogācāra epistemology's three modes of cognition — direct perception (pratyakṣa), inference (anumāna), and non-valid cognition (aprāmāṇa) — manas belongs exclusively to non-valid cognition. It does not err occasionally; it errs **perpetually**. It cognizes the non-self (ālaya's perceiving aspect) as self, and this error is not produced by false teachings or wrong reasoning (that would be discriminatively acquired self-grasping, belonging to the sixth consciousness), but is "spontaneous" — running automatically since beginningless time.

The phrase "constantly miscognizing" (恒謬度) is worth savoring. We normally think of "error" as accidental, but manas's error is structural and perpetual — it is not an occasional malfunction of cognitive capacity, but rather the very mode of operation of this consciousness. This is epistemologically extraordinary: a cognitive function whose essential nature is "not knowing in accord with reality."

### 3.4 The Four Afflictions: The Complete Structure of Self-Grasping

Manas's four afflictions — self-delusion (我癡), self-view (我見), self-conceit (我慢), and self-love (我愛) — constitute the doctrinal core of this paper. *Cheng Weishi Lun*, fascicle 4, defines each:

> "Self-delusion means ignorance — being deluded about the characteristic of self, confused about the principle of no-self. Hence it is called self-delusion. Self-view means self-grasping — regarding what is not self as self through false conceptualization. Hence it is called self-view. Self-conceit means arrogance — relying on the grasped self, causing the mind to be inflated. Hence it is called self-conceit. Self-love means craving for self — being deeply attached to the grasped self. Hence it is called self-love. These four constantly arise, disturbing and defiling the inner mind, causing the outer active consciousnesses to be perpetually contaminated."[^36]

The four afflictions are not four independent psychological phenomena but a single internally generative structure:

**Self-delusion** (moha) is the root ignorance. It is not "not knowing that there is a self" but "not knowing that there is no self" — deluded about the self-characteristic, confused about the no-self principle. Self-delusion is the foundation: because one does not know there is no self, the subsequent three afflictions become possible.

**Self-view** (satkāya-dṛṣṭi / ātma-dṛṣṭi) is the view of the self-group. On the foundation of self-delusion, manas actively identifies the ālaya's perceiving aspect as "self" — "regarding what is not self as self." Self-view is not passive unknowing but active misidentification. The "self-identity view" (sakkāya-diṭṭhi, 身見) documented in the *Saṃyuktāgama* — one of the three fetters severed by the stream-enterer — is precisely the early observation of this affliction in the domain of practice.[^37] Yogācāra's contribution lies in revealing that self-identity view exists not only at the sixth consciousness's discriminative level (severable at the Path of Seeing) but more fundamentally at manas's innate level (requiring prolonged cultivation to sever).

**Self-conceit** (māna) is inflation. Having established a "self," comparison naturally follows — "self is higher" or "self is lower" — "relying on the grasped self, causing the mind to be inflated." Self-conceit does not manifest only as arrogance; low self-esteem is its other face. Wherever there is comparison between "self" and "other," there is self-conceit.

**Self-love** (ātma-sneha) is attachment to "self." Having identified a self and ranked it against others, deep attachment and protectiveness naturally arise — "being deeply attached to the grasped self." Self-love is the stickiest layer: it makes you unwilling to release this "self," even when you rationally know you should.

The logical chain: **not knowing there is no self (delusion) → identifying non-self as self (view) → comparing self against others (conceit) → deeply clinging to self (love)**.

The *Cheng Weishi Lun* further demonstrates why manas has **precisely these four** afflictions — no more and no fewer:

> "Because self-view is already present, other views cannot arise — there cannot be two instances of wisdom in a single mind... Because the view has determined [self's existence] through assessment, doubt has no room to arise. Because there is love for self, aversion cannot arise. Therefore the afflictions accompanying this consciousness are precisely four."[^38]

The logic is rigorous: since self-view (a function of wisdom) is already present, no other view can simultaneously arise — one mind cannot host two instances of wisdom. Because self-view has decisively determined the existence of self, "doubt" has no opening. Because it is attachment to one's own self, "aversion" cannot arise toward the very self one cherishes. Four afflictions are both sufficient and necessary.

### 3.5 Eighteen Associated Mental Factors: The Complete Psychological Profile of Manas

The mental factor configuration of manas, after the four-master debate in *Cheng Weishi Lun* fascicle 4, is established by Dharmapāla as eighteen:

> "The mental factors accompanying this consciousness are eighteen: the nine previously mentioned [five universals plus four root afflictions], eight subsidiary afflictions, and object-specific wisdom."[^39]

The structure of these eighteen mental factors can be understood as follows:

**Five universals** (contact, attention, feeling, perception, volition): the basic functions of any mental activity. Manas has contact (with the ālaya's perceiving aspect), attention (perpetually alert toward "self"), feeling (a subtle feeling regarding "self"), perception (taking the ālaya's perceiving aspect as "self-image"), and volition (the subtle willpower maintaining self-grasping). The Āgama observation that the mind-root "constantly seeks agreeable dharmas" — this driving automaticity corresponds in Yogācāra analysis to the perpetual operation of "attention" and "volition" among manas's universals.[^40]

**Four root afflictions** (self-delusion, self-view, self-conceit, self-love): as analyzed above.

**Eight major subsidiary afflictions** (torpor, restlessness, non-faith, laziness, heedlessness, forgetfulness, distraction, incorrect knowing): these eight accompany manas because they are present in "every defiled mental moment." Any defiled mental activity is necessarily accompanied by mental torpor and restlessness, non-faith in the true Dharma, laziness in practice, heedlessness, forgetfulness, distraction, and incorrect knowing. Since manas is perpetually accompanied by the four root afflictions, it is naturally also perpetually accompanied by these eight major subsidiaries.

**One object-specific factor — wisdom**: manas possesses only "wisdom" (prajñā/慧) among the five object-specific factors, and this "wisdom" is identical with self-view — "wisdom is self-view; hence it is not separately listed."[^41] As for why the other four object-specific factors (desire, resolve, mindfulness, concentration) are absent, the treatise's explanation is precise:

> "Desire is the wish for something not yet attained. This consciousness spontaneously apprehends what is already present — it has nothing to wish for. Resolve is the firm holding of something previously undetermined. This consciousness has apprehended the same determinate object since beginningless time — it has nothing to determine. Mindfulness is the recollection of past experience. This consciousness constantly apprehends a presently experienced object — it has nothing to recall. Concentration is the fixing of mind on a single object. This consciousness spontaneously apprehends moment-to-moment — it does not focus on one point."[^41]

Manas operates "spontaneously" — automatically, needing no desire (wish); it perpetually apprehends the same determined object (the ālaya's perceiving aspect), needing no resolve (determination); it apprehends only the present, not the past, needing no mindfulness (recollection); it shifts moment-to-moment, not dwelling on a single point, needing no concentration (fixation). The configuration of eighteen mental factors paints a precise portrait of manas's "character": an automated, unceasing, perpetually erroneous self-grasping mechanism.

### 3.6 Basis for Defilement and Purity, and Sixth-Eighth Non-Correspondence: The Pivotal Position of Manas

The verse phrase "the six active consciousnesses call it the basis of defilement and purity" (六轉呼為染淨依) directly names manas's most critical functional role in the eight-consciousness system. The sub-commentary states plainly:

> "Because in its afflicted state this consciousness constantly grasps at self internally, it causes the sixth consciousness to become defiled moment by moment. Because in its unafflicted state this consciousness constantly contemplates no-self, it causes the sixth consciousness to become purified moment by moment. Therefore whether the sixth becomes defiled or purified depends entirely on the seventh."[^42]

Whether the sixth consciousness operates in defilement or purity depends on manas's state. When manas grasps at self, the sixth operates under the contamination of self-grasping; when manas is converted to the unafflicted, the sixth operates in purity. Manas is not an ordinary wall but a lens — all light passing through it is either tinted or clarified.

This basis-for-defilement-and-purity role rests upon a more fundamental structural reality: **sixth-eighth non-correspondence** (六八不相應). The *Yogācārabhūmi-śāstra*, fascicle 51, first establishes this three-tier architecture:

> "Furthermore, because the ālaya-vijñāna exists, manas comes into being; because manas serves as basis, mind-consciousness can turn."[^43]

The *Cheng Weishi Lun*, fascicle 4, further strengthens this through a counterfactual argument: if the sixth consciousness could directly depend on the eighth, then the *Yogācārabhūmi* should have said "because the storehouse consciousness exists, mind-consciousness can turn" — but the scripture explicitly says "because the storehouse consciousness exists, manas comes into being; because manas serves as basis, mind-consciousness can turn."[^44] This proves that the sixth consciousness cannot skip manas to directly interdepend with the eighth — the three-tier architecture is indispensable. The *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra*'s "the ādāna-vijñāna serves as the basis, and the six consciousness-bodies turn" is precisely the canonical foundation for this three-tier architecture — in the chain of dependence, manas is the non-omissible intermediate link.[^45]

A daily-life simile: you (the sixth consciousness) want to change your deep habits (seeds in the eighth consciousness), but you cannot directly access that storehouse. You must go through a middle manager (manas), and this manager has a fixed bias — it believes everything in the storehouse is "mine." You can understand that "these things are not mine," but the manager does not listen to you. Only when the manager's bias is transformed does your access to the deep storehouse truly change.

*Cheng Weishi Lun*, fascicle 4, summarizes this three-tier co-arising basis in verse:

> "Because the ālaya serves as basis, manas turns; / Relying on mind and thought, the remaining active consciousnesses arise."[^46]

### 3.7 The Five Stages of Transformation and Equality Wisdom

The third verse — "At Supreme Joy, the initial mind attains equality nature; at the stage of effortless action, the ego is forever crushed; the Tathāgata manifests the enjoyment body for others — bodhisattvas of the ten grounds are the beneficiaries" — unfolds the complete path of manas's transformation from defilement to purity.

**The fundamental distinction between two types of self-grasping.** The prerequisite for transformation is distinguishing two types. *Cheng Weishi Lun*, fascicle 1:

> "Self-grasping is broadly of two types: first, innate; second, discriminatively acquired. Innate self-grasping has been perfumed by false habituation since beginningless time, arising through internal causal force, always accompanying the body; it does not depend on false teachings or false reasoning but turns spontaneously — hence called 'innate.'"[^47]

Innate self-grasping "perpetually continues in the seventh consciousness, taking the eighth consciousness [as object], generating its own mental image, and grasping it as a real self." Discriminatively acquired self-grasping "exists only in the sixth consciousness," arising through false learning or reasoning. The discriminatively acquired type "is coarse, hence easy to sever — eliminated at the initial Path of Seeing." The innate type "is subtle, hence difficult to sever — eliminated only through repeated cultivation of the superior emptiness-insight during the Path of Cultivation."[^47]

**Five stages of transformation.** Synthesizing *Cheng Weishi Lun* fascicles 1, 5, 9, and 10, manas's transformation can be organized into five stages:

First, the **Accumulation and Preparation stages**: innate self-grasping operates spontaneously. Discriminatively acquired self-grasping has been suppressed by worldly observation but its seeds remain intact. Manas is entirely in its defiled state — the four afflictions perpetually active, "sentient beings day and night sunk in deep stupor."

Second, the **Path of Seeing** (Supreme Joy ground): the sixth consciousness directly perceives the suchness of twofold emptiness. The verse "at Supreme Joy, the initial mind attains equality nature" refers to this stage — equality wisdom first manifests. But this stage severs only the seeds of **discriminatively acquired** self-grasping; manas's **innate** self-grasping can still arise. The sub-commentary explains the precise mechanism:

> "When manas's single grasping resides in seed-position, equality wisdom does not manifest. When, through the sixth consciousness entering the dual emptiness insight, manas's dual grasping is obstructed from arising — when the dual grasping returns to seed-position, equality wisdom then manifests. The seventh consciousness itself has no power to sever afflictions; the power to counter its grasping depends entirely on the sixth consciousness."[^48]

This passage is crucial: manas itself cannot sever its own afflictions — the transformative power comes from the sixth consciousness's emptiness insight. When the sixth consciousness contemplates emptiness, manas's two graspings are temporarily pressed back to seed-position, and only then can equality wisdom manifest.

Third, the **Path of Cultivation** (first through seventh grounds): "through the seventh ground, [innate self-grasping] can still intermittently arise"[^49] — innate self-grasping is gradually suppressed during this period but can still episodically manifest. The bodhisattva, through deliberate effort, ensures that manas's arising of self-grasping does not cause retrogression.

Fourth, **eighth ground and above** (Immovable ground): the verse's "at the stage of effortless action, the ego is forever crushed." At this stage, the sixth consciousness abides perpetually in emptiness insight (no longer interrupted), and manas's self-grasping "definitively ceases to operate"[^49] — its manifest activity is permanently suppressed. But seeds remain; total severance has not yet occurred. Dharma-grasping may still intermittently arise. The *Śūraṅgama Sūtra*'s twelve hundred meritorious capacities of the mind-root begin to manifest fully at this stage — from the narrowness of perpetual self-deliberation, manas opens into vast, unobstructed equal contemplation.[^50]

Fifth, **Buddhahood** (vajra-like concentration): manas's afflictive-obstruction seeds are "in a single moment, severed entirely across all three realms when the three vehicles are about to attain the fruition of no-more-learning."[^51] Equality wisdom is now complete, "continuing in a single flavor, exhausting the future."

**The complete fruition of equality wisdom.** *Cheng Weishi Lun*, fascicle 10:

> "The mind-and-mental-factor group associated with equality wisdom: this mind-group contemplates all dharmas, self and other sentient beings, as entirely equal. Great compassion and similar [qualities] perpetually accompany it. According to sentient beings' inclinations, it manifests enjoyment bodies, lands, and image-differentiations."[^52]

Equality wisdom is not a leveling "everything is the same." It "contemplates all dharmas, self and other sentient beings, as entirely equal" — what is equal is the reality at the level of dharma-nature, not phenomenal differences. Precisely because the equality of dharma-nature has been realized, the wisdom can "manifest enjoyment bodies, lands, and image-differentiations according to sentient beings' inclinations" — true equality responds to every being's individual needs.

The *Mahāyānasaṃgrahopanibandhana* (Vasubandhu) succinctly summarizes:

> "By transforming the defiled manas, one attains equality wisdom. It is first realized at the initial direct perception, further purified during the Path of Cultivation. Thereby one abides in non-abiding nirvāṇa, great compassion perpetually accompanies, and one can manifest Buddha-images according to [beings'] inclinations."[^53]

From "constant deliberation with self-image ever in tow" to "great compassion perpetually accompanying" — the "constancy" of manas does not disappear; it is redirected. The transformation is not the destruction of manas but the conversion of what it constantly deliberates upon — from "self" to "the equality of all sentient beings."

### 3.8 Cessation Attainment: A Precision Debate on Manas's Status

The status of manas in special meditative states is one of Yogācāra's most precise theoretical debates, and serves as an important thought-experiment for verifying manas's existence.

**The fundamental distinction between non-perception attainment and cessation attainment.** *Cheng Weishi Lun*, fascicle 7:

> "The attainment of non-perception... causes the non-constantly-active minds and mental factors to cease, with the cessation of perception as primary — hence named 'non-perception.' The cessation attainment... causes both the non-constantly-active and the constantly-active defiled minds and mental factors to cease — hence named 'cessation.'"[^54]

The attainment of non-perception ceases only the "non-constantly-active" — the first six consciousnesses. The cessation attainment additionally ceases the "constantly-active defiled" — manas's four afflictions. If both merely ceased the first six consciousnesses, what would distinguish them? The *Cheng Weishi Lun* uses precisely this as a key argument for manas's existence:

> "If the defiled mind did not exist in the attainments of non-perception and cessation, they should be indistinguishable. Both attainments cease the six consciousnesses and their mental factors, with no difference in their entities and number. Without the defiled mind being present in one and absent in the other, how would they differ?"[^55]

**The Dharmapāla-Sthiramati debate.** In cessation attainment, is manas entirely absent, or only its defiled aspect?

Sthiramati's school holds that manas is entirely absent in the three states (arhat, cessation, supramundane path) — "manas only has afflictive obstructions accompanying it; the sacred teachings all state it is absent in three states." Dharmapāla's orthodox position counters: the defiled manas (self-grasping) is suppressed, but dharma-grasping manas persists — "the supramundane manas is stated in the sūtras to exist." The reasoning: the storehouse consciousness constantly turns together with one consciousness (it cannot exist alone without any basis), and the attainment of equality wisdom by the active consciousnesses requires a purified consciousness as its basis.[^56]

*Cheng Weishi Lun*, fascicle 5, integrates this debate through the "three differentiations of manas":

> "This consciousness has broadly three differentiations: first, associated with the person-self view; second, associated with the dharma-self view; third, associated with equality wisdom."[^57]

Three differentiations, three phases: the ordinary person's person-grasping manas, the noble one's dharma-grasping manas, and the Buddha's equality-wisdom manas. Manas is not annihilated but purified layer by layer — from person-grasping to dharma-grasping to wisdom.

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Dialogue

### 4.1 The Default Mode Network: The Brain's Automatic Self-Referential System

⚠️ **This section is a heuristic analogy, not a reductive equivalence.**

Modern neuroscience has identified a system in the brain called the Default Mode Network (DMN). When a person is not engaged in any specific task — when "doing nothing" — the DMN automatically becomes active, engaging in: self-referential thinking (thoughts about "I" and "mine"), mental time travel (recalling the past, imagining the future), and social cognition (inferring others' views of "me").

Several features of the DMN structurally resonate with manas's "constant deliberation": first, it operates **automatically** — it does not need to be deliberately activated; rather, it requires deliberate attentional tasks to suppress it. Second, its core function is **self-reference** — weaving experience into a narrative centered on "I." Third, it is **significantly suppressed by meditation training** — long-term meditators show markedly reduced DMN activity.

The valid boundary of this analogy: the DMN is a brain activity pattern observed through functional magnetic resonance imaging, belonging to the domain of form-dharmas (material substrate); manas is a mind-dharma, a functional differentiation within consciousness. The two exist at different ontological levels and cannot be simply equated. But the discovery of the DMN at least provides modern readers with a bridge for understanding manas's "automatic self-referential mechanism" — the brain does indeed have a "default to thinking about self when idle" program, and Buddhism identified this phenomenon fifteen centuries ago.

### 4.2 The Chama Sūtra: "Residual Scent of Self-Conceit" in the Āgamas

The manas-vijñāna is a Mahāyāna Yogācāra construction, but the phenomenon it addresses — "knowing no-self yet unable to release self" — is already documented in the Āgamas. *Saṃyuktāgama*, fascicle 5, records the venerable Chama's famous self-testimony:

> "I observe the five appropriated aggregates as not-self and not-belonging-to-self, yet I am not an arhat. Why? Because the conceit 'I am,' the desire 'I am,' the tendency 'I am' have not been severed, not fully known, not abandoned, not expelled."[^58]

Chama used the simile of laundered cloth: the garment has been washed with various alkaline solutions to remove the dirt, but a residual scent remains, requiring further fumigation with various fragrances to eliminate. Likewise, "although the learned noble disciple observes the five appropriated aggregates as not-self and not-belonging-to-self, nevertheless the conceit 'I am' regarding the five appropriated aggregates has not been severed."[^58]

Chama's "residual scent of self-conceit" corresponds precisely to manas's innate self-grasping in Yogācāra analysis. The Path of Seeing (insight into no-self) severs discriminatively acquired self-grasping, but innate self-grasping — like "residual scent on laundered cloth" — requires prolonged cultivation to eliminate. This Āgama account provides the earliest experiential evidence for what the manas theory would later systematize — long before Yogācāra's formal framework, practitioners had already experienced the vast gap between "understanding no-self" and "severing self-grasping."

### 4.3 Chan "No-Thought" and Manas's "Constant Deliberation": A Dialogue

Chan Buddhism takes "no-thought as its tenet" (無念為宗), while manas's nature is "constant deliberation" — the two appear contradictory but are in fact complementary.

The Sixth Patriarch Huineng defines "no-thought" precisely in the *Platform Sūtra*:

> "If one sees all dharmas and the mind does not attach — this is no-thought. Functioning everywhere, yet not attaching anywhere. Simply purify the original mind, letting the six consciousnesses issue through the six gates without defilement or mixture amid the six sense-objects — coming and going freely, functioning without obstruction. This is prajñā-samādhi, self-liberation — it is called the practice of no-thought. If one does not think of anything at all, forcing thought to cease entirely, this is dharma-bondage, and is called a biased view."[^59]

"No-thought" is not "thinking of nothing" (that would be a biased view) but "mind not attaching." Translated into Yogācāra language: no-thought does not mean destroying manas's "constant deliberation" but letting the constant deliberation no longer center on "self" — transforming "constant deliberation **with self-image in tow**" into constant deliberation that is "without defilement or mixture."

The Korean master Chinul, in *Admonitions for the Cultivation of the Mind*, captures the tension between sudden awakening and the gradualism demanded by manas in a single quatrain:

> "Though sudden awakening equals the Buddha, / Habitual energies from many lives run deep; / The wind has stopped, yet waves still surge; / The principle is manifest, yet thought-streams still intrude."[^60]

"The wind has stopped" is like the Path of Seeing — one has understood no-self in principle. "Waves still surge" is like manas's innate self-grasping — the rational insight is clear, but the habitual waves continue. This is not Chan Buddhism denying the manas theory. Rather, Chan's "practice of no-thought" was always designed to address manas's "constant deliberation on self-image." Sudden awakening is the entry-point of the Path of Seeing; gradual cultivation is the prolonged transformation of manas's innate self-grasping during the Path of Cultivation — "relying on awakening, one cultivates; through gradual perfumation, the work is accomplished."[^60]

## V. Middle-Way Corrections

### 5.1 Manas Is Not an Enemy to Destroy

❌ **Common misunderstanding:** Manas is the practitioner's enemy, to be severed and annihilated.

✅ **Middle-way position:** Manas is not an enemy to be destroyed but a mechanism to be transformed. Equality wisdom is not built on the ruins of manas; it is the transformation-result of manas itself. "By transforming the defiled manas, one attains equality wisdom" — "transforming" is not "annihilating." Manas's "constant deliberation" persists even at Buddhahood; it merely shifts from "self-image in tow" to "great compassion perpetually accompanying." If one generates aversion toward manas — "I must destroy my self-grasping!" — that very aversion is itself manas at work.

### 5.2 Seeing Does Not Equal Severing

❌ **Common misunderstanding:** Once one has become aware that manas is operating, it has already been transformed.

✅ **Middle-way position:** Awareness by the sixth consciousness is a necessary but not sufficient condition for transformation. The venerable Chama "observed [the aggregates as] not-self and not-belonging-to-self" (awareness was present), yet "the conceit 'I am' had not been severed." Awareness is the starting point, not the destination. From awareness to transformation requires prolonged "repeated cultivation of the superior emptiness-insight" during the Path of Cultivation. Equating awareness with severance creates a false sense of accomplishment — the illusion that one has already realized no-self.

### 5.3 Equality Wisdom Does Not Erase Difference

❌ **Common misunderstanding:** Equality wisdom means "everything is the same" — no more distinctions of any kind.

✅ **Middle-way position:** Equality wisdom "contemplates all dharmas, self and other sentient beings, as entirely equal" — what is equal is the reality at the level of dharma-nature, not phenomenal differences. Precisely because the equality of dharma-nature has been realized, the wisdom can "manifest enjoyment bodies, lands, and image-differentiations according to sentient beings' inclinations." An "equality" that erases difference is another form of conceptual superimposition (parikalpita); an equality that responds to difference is the consummated reality (pariniṣpanna).

### 5.4 Chan "No-Thought" Does Not Deny Manas

❌ **Common misunderstanding:** Chan Buddhism advocates "no-thought," therefore it does not accept the manas theory.

✅ **Middle-way position:** The Sixth Patriarch explicitly stated: "If one does not think of anything at all, forcing thought to cease entirely, this is dharma-bondage, and is called a biased view." Chan's "no-thought" does not deny manas's existence but provides a direct practical path for facing manas's constant deliberation. The *Zongjing Lu* directly cites manas theory: "This seventh consciousness does not associate with other mental factors beyond the four afflictions, because: first, it is constant; second, it grasps internally; third, it apprehends a single type of object."[^61] The divergence between Chan and Yogācāra lies not in the diagnosis (both acknowledge perpetual self-fixation) but in the method (gradual insight-cultivation versus sudden direct realization). And Chinul's "wind stops but waves still surge" already indicates that even within the Chan tradition, gradual cultivation follows sudden awakening — precisely corresponding to manas's progression of suppression at the Path of Seeing and gradual severance during the Path of Cultivation.

### 5.5 Intellectual Understanding Does Not Equal Realized Transformation

❌ **Common misunderstanding:** Having read this paper is equivalent to understanding manas; understanding is equivalent to having transformed it.

✅ **Middle-way position:** This paper operates within the domain of "knowledge" — using words and concepts to analyze manas's structure, function, and transformation path. But the problem with manas is precisely that it operates **beneath** knowledge. The sixth consciousness's intellectual understanding (including understanding gained from reading a paper) cannot directly alter manas — this is the practical implication of the "sixth-eighth non-correspondence." From knowledge to realization, what is needed is not more reading but sustained contemplative practice. What an academic paper can do is provide a precise map for the practice; but the map is not the journey.

## VI. Conclusion

The manas-vijñāna is the most hidden yet most critical structural component of Yogācāra theory. It reveals the practitioner's deepest predicament: self-grasping does not require your participation to run automatically — it was operating before you knew, it continues after you become aware, it persists even after you understand no-self.

This paper has taken the three verses on the seventh consciousness as its framework and adopted a sūtra-first citation architecture to unfold the complete doctrine of manas layer by layer. The sūtra foundations — the Āgamas' mind-root and self-identity view, the *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra*'s mind—thought—consciousness tripartition, the *Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra*'s "thought as stage-manager," the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra*'s analysis of the mind sense-base as illusory, the *Ghanavyūha Sūtra*'s "like a magnet attracting iron" — establish canonical authority. The treatise foundations — the *Cheng Weishi Lun*'s systematic analysis, the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s four afflictions perpetually active, the *Mahāyānasaṃgraha*'s mutual causation, the *Bajishi Guiju Buzhu*'s verse commentary — complete the precise doctrinal architecture.

The first verse addresses nature: the cognitive mode of objective appearance with substance, the subtle quality of obscured-neutral, the mediating position of bridging cognition and root, the perpetual error of non-valid cognition. The second verse addresses function: the unique positioning of constant deliberation, the internally generative logic of the four afflictions, the precise configuration of eighteen mental factors, the pivotal role as basis for defilement and purity, the three-tier architecture of sixth-eighth non-correspondence. The third verse addresses transformation: from the full defilement of the Accumulation stage, to the initial turning at the Path of Seeing, to the gradual cultivation of the Path of Cultivation, to effortless action at the eighth ground, to the complete fruition of equality wisdom at Buddhahood.

The theoretical significance of manas lies in its precise explanation of the gap between "knowing" and "doing" — the sixth consciousness's insight is intermittent, while manas's self-grasping is uninterrupted. From the venerable Chama's "residual scent of self-conceit" to Chinul's "wind stops but waves still surge," different traditions in different languages point to the same experiential fact: the Path of Seeing does not equal the final severance of self-grasping.

The practical significance of manas lies in locating the pivot of transformation: it is not in the sixth consciousness (the observer) nor in the eighth (the storehouse), but in the seventh — manas's loosening is the key to liberation. And this loosening is not destruction but transformation — from "constant deliberation with self-image ever in tow" to "great compassion perpetually accompanying." The "constancy" of manas never disappears; it merely changes direction.

S3-P03 described the ālaya-vijñāna as "the hard drive of life." Manas is the operating system running on that hard drive — one that has shipped since the factory with "everything is mine" as its default setting. You can open a "no-self" application on the screen (the sixth consciousness), but as long as the operating system continues to run "everything is mine," that application will always be just one window. Transforming consciousness into wisdom means performing a fundamental system upgrade — from "everything is mine" to "all sentient beings are entirely equal."

This upgrade cannot be completed by reading a paper. But knowing the architecture of the operating system is the first step.

[^55]: Ibid.: 「無想滅定染意若無，彼應無別。謂彼二定俱滅六識及彼心所，體數無異。若無染意，於二定中一有一無，彼二何別？」("If the defiled mind did not exist... they should be indistinguishable.")

[^56]: *Cheng Weishi Lun*, fasc. 5: Sthiramati school: 「末那唯有煩惱障俱，聖教皆言三位無故。」("Manas only has afflictive obstructions accompanying it; the sacred teachings all state it is absent in three states.") Dharmapāla's orthodox position: 「彼說教理相違，出世末那經說有故。」("That position contradicts both teaching and reasoning — the supramundane manas is stated in the sūtras to exist.") Ibid.

[^57]: Ibid.: 「此意差別，略有三種：一補特伽羅我見相應、二法我見相應、三平等性智相應。」("This consciousness has broadly three differentiations: first, associated with the person-self view; second, associated with the dharma-self view; third, associated with equality wisdom.")

[^58]: *Saṃyuktāgama*, fasc. 5, Chama-sutta: 「我於五受陰觀察非我非我所，而非阿羅漢者……譬如乳母衣，付浣衣者……猶有餘氣，要以種種雜香薰令消滅。」("I observe the five appropriated aggregates as not-self and not-belonging-to-self, yet I am not an arhat... Just as a nurse's garment, given to a washer... a residual scent remains, requiring further fumigation with various fragrances to eliminate.") *Taishō* vol. 2, No. 0099.

[^59]: *Platform Sūtra*, fasc. 1: 「若見一切法，心不染著，是為無念……若百物不思，當令念絕，即是法縛，即名邊見。」("If one sees all dharmas and the mind does not attach — this is no-thought... If one does not think of anything at all, forcing thought to cease entirely, this is dharma-bondage, and is called a biased view.") *Taishō* vol. 48, No. 2008.

[^60]: *Susim Kyol* (修心訣): 「頓悟雖同佛，多生習氣深；風停波尚湧，理現念猶侵……故依悟而修，漸熏功成。」("Though sudden awakening equals the Buddha, habitual energies from many lives run deep; the wind has stopped, yet waves still surge; the principle is manifest, yet thought-streams still intrude... Therefore relying on awakening, one cultivates; through gradual perfumation, the work is accomplished.") *Taishō* vol. 48, No. 2020.

[^61]: *Zongjing Lu*, fasc. 52: 「此第七意，除四惑外，不與餘心所相應者，一恒故、二內執故、三一類境生故。」("This seventh consciousness does not associate with other mental factors beyond the four afflictions, because: first, it is constant; second, it grasps internally; third, it apprehends a single type of object.") *Taishō* vol. 48, No. 2016.
