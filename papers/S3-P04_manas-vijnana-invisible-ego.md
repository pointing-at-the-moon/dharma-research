## Abstract

The manas-vijñāna (seventh consciousness) represents one of Yogācāra philosophy's most revolutionary theoretical innovations. It addresses a problem that has perplexed practitioners for millennia: why does "knowing there is no self" fail to free us from self-grasping? This paper takes Xuanzang's three verses on the seventh consciousness from the *Verses on the Characteristics of the Eight Consciousnesses* (八識規矩頌) as its structural framework, drawing on the *Cheng Weishi Lun* (成唯識論), *Yogācārabhūmi-śāstra* (瑜伽師地論), and *Mahāyānasaṃgraha* (攝大乘論) as doctrinal foundations.

§2 establishes the scriptural basis through six major textual sources and the three-verse framework. §3 unfolds the core argument in seven layers: (1) the unique positioning of manas through the "constant and deliberative" (恒審) four-fold analysis; (2) the three-fold nature via "objective appearance with substance, obscured-neutral, bridging cognition and root" (帶質有覆通情本); (3) the epistemological implications of "non-valid cognition" (非量); (4) the four afflictions — self-delusion (我癡), self-view (我見), self-conceit (我慢), and self-love (我愛) — and their internally generative logic; (5) the eighteen associated mental factors and why the configuration is precisely thus; (6) the role of manas as "basis for defilement and purity" (染淨依) and the "sixth-eighth non-correspondence" (六八不相應) as proof of the three-tier architecture; (7) the five stages of transformation and the complete fruition of equality wisdom (平等性智). §4 offers the Default Mode Network as a heuristic analogy, the Chama Sūtra as an Āgama bridge, and Chinul's "wind stops but waves still surge" as a Chan dialogue. §5 provides five middle-way corrections: manas is not an enemy to destroy, seeing does not equal severing, equality wisdom does not erase difference, Chan "no-thought" does not deny manas, and intellectual understanding does not equal realized transformation.

**Keywords:** manas-vijñāna, constant deliberation (恒審思量), four afflictions, objective appearance with substance (帶質境), obscured-neutral (有覆無記), sixth-eighth non-correspondence (六八不相應), basis for defilement and purity (染淨依), equality wisdom (samatā-jñāna), Verses on Eight Consciousnesses

## I. Introduction

### 1.1 Textual Position

S3-P03 unfolded the ālaya-vijñāna — the foundational basis of the eight-consciousness system, the repository of all seeds, the ground from which body and world are projected. This paper turns to the most hidden yet most critical link in the system: the manas-vijñāna.

Among the eight consciousnesses in the *Śatadharmaprakāśamukha-śāstra* (百法明門論), the manas is listed seventh.[^1] But "seventh" is not merely an ordinal number — it marks manas's unique structural position: upward, it takes the ālaya-vijñāna's perceiving aspect (見分) as its object; downward, it serves as the cognitive root (意根) for the sixth consciousness. Standing between the ālaya and the first six consciousnesses, manas is the mediating pivot of the entire system.

The three verses on the seventh consciousness in the *Verses on the Characteristics of the Eight Consciousnesses* encompass its complete doctrine:

> Objective appearance with substance, obscured, bridging cognition and root; / Following conditions, it grasps a self — its cognition is non-valid. / Eight major [subsidiary afflictions], five universals, object-specific wisdom — / Craving, delusion, self-view, and conceit accompany it always.[^2]

> Constant deliberation, with self-image ever in tow; / Sentient beings day and night are sunk in a deep stupor. / Four afflictions and eight major [subsidiaries] arise together; / The six active consciousnesses call it the basis of defilement and purity.[^3]

> At Supreme Joy, the initial mind attains equality nature; / At the stage of effortless action, the ego is forever crushed. / The Tathāgata manifests the enjoyment body for others — / Bodhisattvas of the ten grounds are the beneficiaries.[^4]

Three verses, three layers: the first describes its nature — how it exists; the second describes its function — what it does and what consequences follow; the third describes its transformation — how it becomes equality wisdom. This paper unfolds each layer in sequence.

### 1.2 The Problem

The deepest frustration for a practitioner is often not "not knowing" but "knowing yet being unable to act accordingly."

A person can master the doctrine of no-self, observe clearly in meditation that the five aggregates are empty, and yet feel a surge of anger the moment someone criticizes them after leaving the meditation hall. A person can sincerely believe in the equality of all beings, yet unconsciously defend their own position and protect their own face.

This gap has a precise explanation in Yogācāra philosophy: **the sixth consciousness can understand no-self, but the seventh manas does not listen to the sixth.**

The "constant deliberation" (恒審思量) of manas means it does not require your intentional thinking about "I" to operate — it grasps at "I" while you are awake, while you sleep, while you are unconscious, and while you are in the attainment of non-perception. It is not a belief you have chosen; it is a program that has been running automatically since beginningless time.

This paper addresses three core questions. First, what is the nature and operating mechanism of manas — how is it "invisible" yet omnipresent? Second, how do its four afflictions constitute the complete structure of self-grasping — what is the logical relationship among self-delusion, self-view, self-conceit, and self-love? Third, how does manas transform — what is the path from "constant deliberation with self-image ever in tow" to "equality wisdom"?

### 1.3 Hundred Dharmas Coverage and Structure

This paper covers eighteen dharmas from the hundred: the manas-vijñāna (seventh consciousness) from the mind-dharmas, and its eighteen associated mental factors — five universals (contact, attention, feeling, perception, volition), four root afflictions (self-delusion, self-view, self-conceit, self-love), eight major subsidiary afflictions (torpor, restlessness, non-faith, laziness, heedlessness, forgetfulness, distraction, incorrect knowing), and one object-specific factor (wisdom — which is identical with self-view).[^5]

The configuration of these eighteen mental factors itself reveals manas's character: it has the most basic mental activities (universals), unceasing afflictions (four root plus eight major subsidiaries), and one special form of "wisdom" — but this wisdom is not prajñā; it is the "clever mistake" that cognizes the non-self as self.

## II. Scriptural Foundations

### 2.1 The Second Transformation in the Cheng Weishi Lun

The *Cheng Weishi Lun* organizes the eight consciousnesses under the framework of "three transformations" (tri-pariṇāma), with manas as the second transformation. The verse explicitly defines all of manas's core characteristics:

> "Next, the second transformation / Is the consciousness called manas. / It turns dependent on that [ālaya], takes that as its object, / And has deliberation as both its nature and characteristic. / Four afflictions constantly accompany it — / Self-delusion, self-view, / Together with self-conceit and self-love, / And the remaining [mental factors] such as contact. / It belongs to the obscured-neutral category, / Bound to whatever realm and ground it arises in. / Arhats, cessation attainment, / And supramundane paths are without it."[^6]

This verse contains every essential element of manas doctrine: its name (manas), its basis and object (dependent on that, taking that as object — dependent on the ālaya and taking the ālaya's perceiving aspect as object), its nature and characteristic (deliberation), its accompanying afflictions (four afflictions constantly present), its moral quality (obscured-neutral), and its three states of absence (arhat, cessation attainment, supramundane path).

### 2.2 The Yogācārabhūmi — Four Afflictions of the Defiled Mind

The *Yogācārabhūmi-śāstra* designates manas as the "defiled mind" (染污意) and, in fascicle 51 of the Viniścaya-saṃgrahaṇī, provides a complete account of the four afflictions' perpetual character:

> "As stated before, manas constantly turns together with the ālaya-vijñāna. Until it is severed, one should know that it constantly and simultaneously accompanies four innate, spontaneous afflictions: satkāya-dṛṣṭi (view of the self-group), self-conceit (māna), self-love (ātma-sneha), and ignorance (avidyā). These four afflictions, whether in meditative absorption or outside it, should be known to be perpetually active, not in contradiction with wholesome [states], and of obscured-neutral moral quality."[^7]

Two points are critical here. First, the four afflictions are "simultaneously accompanying" — all four operate at once, not sequentially. Second, "whether in meditative absorption or outside it" — even in deep concentration, manas's four afflictions persist. Meditative absorption cannot sever manas's self-grasping.

Fascicle 63 further states that manas's four afflictions "cannot even be suppressed or counteracted by worldly antidotes... because they still manifest even in those who have attained freedom from desire."[^8] This means that even attainment of the highest formless realm — the sphere of neither perception nor non-perception — does not stop manas's self-grasping. No worldly path can reach it.

### 2.3 The Mahāyānasaṃgraha — Mutual Causation

The *Mahāyānasaṃgraha* uses two similes — the lamp-wick and the bundle of reeds — to explain the interdependent relationship between the ālaya-vijñāna and defiled consciousness (including manas):

> "Just as the light of a lamp and the wick mutually serve as cause for the other's arising and burning at the same time; and just as a bundle of reeds can stand upright by leaning on each other at the same time."[^9]

This simile reveals that the relationship between manas and the ālaya is not one-directional "passive apprehension." Rather, they are mutually causal in a dynamic loop — manas grasps the ālaya as self, and this very grasping perfumes back into the ālaya, generating deeper seeds of self-grasping.

### 2.4 The Bajishi Guiju Buzhu — Complete Commentary on the Three Verses

The *Bajishi Guiju Buzhu* (八識規矩補註) by the Ming-dynasty master Putai provides sentence-by-sentence commentary on all twelve verses, and is the most systematic sub-commentary for understanding Xuanzang's verse text. This paper draws extensively on it as a bridge between verse and treatise.[^10]

## III. Core Argument

### 3.1 Constant Deliberation: The Unique Positioning of Manas

"Manas" (末那), from the Sanskrit, means "deliberation" or "cogitation." But deliberation is not manas's exclusive province — the sixth consciousness also deliberates. What makes manas unique are two qualities: **constancy** (恒) and **deliberativeness** (審).

*Cheng Weishi Lun*, fascicle 2, defines the three transformations:

> "The first is called vipāka — the eighth consciousness, because its nature is predominantly karmic maturation. The second is called deliberation — the seventh consciousness, because it constantly and deliberatively cogitates. The third is called object-apprehension — the first six consciousnesses, because their object-apprehension is comparatively coarse."[^11]

"Constant" means uninterrupted. "Deliberative" means examining and assessing, grasping at self. The *Bajishi Guiju Buzhu* arranges the eight consciousnesses in a four-fold analysis of constancy and deliberativeness:

> "First, constant but not deliberative: the eighth consciousness — it does not grasp at self, yet is never interrupted. Second, deliberative but not constant: the sixth consciousness — it grasps at self, yet is interrupted. Third, both constant and deliberative: the seventh consciousness — it grasps at self and is never interrupted. Fourth, neither constant nor deliberative: the first five consciousnesses — they do not grasp at self."[^12]

The logical structure of this four-fold analysis merits careful examination. The eighth consciousness is "constant but not deliberative" — the ālaya never ceases, but it does not examine, does not grasp at self; it merely holds seeds and projects. The sixth consciousness is "deliberative but not constant" — it can examine and reflect, but it is interrupted: in deep sleep, in fainting, in the attainment of non-perception. The first five are "neither constant nor deliberative" — they are interrupted and do not grasp at self.

Only manas is "both constant and deliberative" — never interrupted, and perpetually grasping at self. This means: it grasps at self while you sleep, while you are unconscious, while you are in meditation. Unlike the sixth consciousness, which requires you to "think about self" to operate, manas runs automatically — without your participation.

This is precisely why a practitioner can understand no-self at the level of the sixth consciousness yet still defend "self" in daily life — because the sixth consciousness's no-self insight is "deliberative but not constant," while manas's self-grasping is "both constant and deliberative." An interrupted insight cannot directly override an uninterrupted fixation.

### 3.2 Objective Appearance with Substance, Obscured-Neutral, Bridging Cognition and Root: Three Aspects of Manas's Nature

The opening phrase of the first verse — "objective appearance with substance, obscured, bridging cognition and root" (帶質有覆通情本) — defines manas's nature from three angles: its cognitive object, its moral quality, and its structural position.

**Objective appearance with substance (帶質境).** Yogācāra epistemology classifies cognitive objects into three types: veridical appearance (性境), independent shadow (獨影境), and objective appearance with substance (帶質境). Manas apprehends the third type. The sub-commentary explains:

> "True 'appearance with substance': mind apprehending mind. The intermediate representational image (相分) arises from both ends, linked in its arising — this is called true objective appearance with substance."[^13]

The object manas apprehends is the ālaya-vijñāna's perceiving aspect (見分) — this is real (it has "substance"). But in the process of apprehension, manas misconstrues it as "self" — and this "self" is fabricated (it "carries" delusion). A daily-life simile: you stand before a mirror, and the reflection genuinely derives from you (it has a real basis), but if you mistake the reflection for an independently existing other person, you have the structure of "objective appearance with substance" — a real foundation, erroneously interpreted.

**Obscured-neutral (有覆無記).** *Cheng Weishi Lun*, fascicle 5:

> "To what moral category do the mental factors accompanying manas belong? They belong to the obscured-neutral category, and no other. The four afflictions and other [factors] accompanying this consciousness are defiled dharmas, obstructing the holy path and obscuring one's own mind — hence called 'obscured.' They are neither wholesome nor unwholesome — hence called 'neutral.'"[^14]

"Obscured" means it veils the mind and obstructs the holy path. "Neutral" means it does not directly create strong wholesome or unwholesome karma. Although manas's four afflictions are the root of all defilement, their operation is too subtle, too automatic, to directly generate the kind of vigorous karma that the sixth consciousness produces. The sub-commentary elaborates: "Because these accompanying defiled dharmas have a subtle basis and turn spontaneously, they too belong to the neutral category."[^15] Manas is a precision bias-generator — it does not shout, but it runs beneath every cognition as a silent default.

**Bridging cognition and root (通情本).** This phrase identifies manas's core structural role as mediating pivot. "Cognition" (情) refers to the sixth consciousness (its discriminating function); "root" (本) refers to the eighth consciousness (the foundational basis). Manas faces upward toward the eighth consciousness (the "root") and faces downward as the cognitive root (意根) for the sixth consciousness (the "cognition"). Standing between them, it is both the grasper of the eighth and the driver of the sixth. This mediating position is the structural foundation for manas being called the "basis for defilement and purity."

### 3.3 Following Conditions, Grasping Self — Cognition Is Non-Valid

The verse phrase "following conditions, it grasps a self — its cognition is non-valid" (隨緣執我量為非) delivers a direct epistemological verdict: manas belongs to "non-valid cognition" (aprāmāṇa). The sub-commentary explains:

> "This seventh consciousness follows the eighth consciousness's perceiving aspect as its condition and grasps it as self. Because this consciousness possesses only innate self and dharma grasping — spontaneously arising, not the discriminatively acquired self and dharma grasping. Why? Because it does not arise through forceful conceptual construction. Among the three types of valid cognition, it is exclusively non-valid — not cognizing in accord with its object, constantly miscognizing. Therefore it is non-valid cognition."[^16]

In Yogācāra epistemology's three modes of cognition — direct perception (pratyakṣa), inference (anumāna), and non-valid cognition (aprāmāṇa) — manas belongs exclusively to non-valid cognition. It does not err occasionally; it errs **perpetually**. It cognizes the non-self (ālaya's perceiving aspect) as self, and this error is not produced by false teachings or wrong reasoning (that would be discriminatively acquired self-grasping, belonging to the sixth consciousness), but is "spontaneous" — running automatically since beginningless time.

The phrase "constantly miscognizing" (恒謬度) is worth savoring. We normally think of "error" as accidental, but manas's error is structural and perpetual — it is not an occasional malfunction of cognitive capacity, but rather the very mode of operation of this consciousness. This is epistemologically extraordinary: a cognitive function whose essential nature is "not knowing in accord with reality."

### 3.4 The Four Afflictions: The Complete Structure of Self-Grasping

Manas's four afflictions — self-delusion (我癡), self-view (我見), self-conceit (我慢), and self-love (我愛) — constitute the doctrinal core of this paper. *Cheng Weishi Lun*, fascicle 4, defines each:

> "Self-delusion means ignorance — being deluded about the characteristic of self, confused about the principle of no-self. Hence it is called self-delusion. Self-view means self-grasping — regarding what is not self as self through false conceptualization. Hence it is called self-view. Self-conceit means arrogance — relying on the grasped self, causing the mind to be inflated. Hence it is called self-conceit. Self-love means craving for self — being deeply attached to the grasped self. Hence it is called self-love. These four constantly arise, disturbing and defiling the inner mind, causing the outer active consciousnesses to be perpetually contaminated."[^17]

The four afflictions are not four independent psychological phenomena but a single internally generative structure:

**Self-delusion** (moha) is the root ignorance. It is not "not knowing that there is a self" but "not knowing that there is no self" — deluded about the self-characteristic, confused about the no-self principle. Self-delusion is the foundation: because one does not know there is no self, the subsequent three afflictions become possible.

**Self-view** (satkāya-dṛṣṭi / ātma-dṛṣṭi) is the view of the self-group. On the foundation of self-delusion, manas actively identifies the ālaya's perceiving aspect as "self" — "regarding what is not self as self." Self-view is not passive unknowing but active misidentification.

**Self-conceit** (māna) is inflation. Having established a "self," comparison naturally follows — "self is higher" or "self is lower" — "relying on the grasped self, causing the mind to be inflated." Self-conceit does not manifest only as arrogance; low self-esteem is its other face. Wherever there is comparison between "self" and "other," there is self-conceit.

**Self-love** (ātma-sneha) is attachment to "self." Having identified a self and ranked it against others, deep attachment and protectiveness naturally arise — "being deeply attached to the grasped self." Self-love is the stickiest layer: it makes you unwilling to release this "self," even when you rationally know you should.

The logical chain: **not knowing there is no self (delusion) → identifying non-self as self (view) → comparing self against others (conceit) → deeply clinging to self (love)**.

The *Cheng Weishi Lun* further demonstrates why manas has **precisely these four** afflictions — no more and no fewer:

> "Because self-view is already present, other views cannot arise — there cannot be two instances of wisdom [慧] in a single mind... Because the view has determined [self's existence] through assessment, doubt has no room to arise. Because there is love for self, aversion cannot arise. Therefore the afflictions accompanying this consciousness are precisely four."[^18]

The logic is rigorous: since self-view (a function of wisdom) is already present, no other view (which would also require wisdom) can simultaneously arise in the same moment of mind — one mind cannot host two instances of wisdom. Because self-view has decisively determined the existence of self, "doubt" has no opening — there is no wavering about whether self exists. Because it is attachment to one's own self, "aversion" (directed at what one rejects) cannot arise toward the very self one cherishes. Four afflictions are both sufficient and necessary.

### 3.5 Eighteen Associated Mental Factors: The Complete Psychological Profile of Manas

The mental factor configuration of manas, after the four-master debate in *Cheng Weishi Lun* fascicle 4, is established by Dharmapāla as eighteen:

> "The mental factors accompanying this consciousness are eighteen: the preceding nine (five universals plus four root afflictions), eight subsidiary afflictions, and the object-specific factor of wisdom."[^19]

The structure can be understood as follows:

**Five universals** (contact, attention, feeling, perception, volition): the basic functions present in any conscious activity. Manas has contact (with the ālaya's perceiving aspect), attention (perpetually directed toward "self"), feeling (subtle affect regarding "self"), perception (apprehending the ālaya's perceiving aspect as "self-image"), and volition (subtle volitional force sustaining self-grasping).

**Four root afflictions** (self-delusion, self-view, self-conceit, self-love): as analyzed in the previous section.

**Eight major subsidiary afflictions** (torpor, restlessness, non-faith, laziness, heedlessness, forgetfulness, distraction, incorrect knowing): these eight accompany "all defiled minds" — wherever a mind is afflicted, these eight are present. Since manas perpetually accompanies four afflictions, it perpetually accompanies the eight major subsidiaries as well.

**One object-specific factor** — wisdom: manas possesses only "wisdom" from among the five object-specific factors, and this wisdom is identical with self-view — "wisdom is identical to self-view; therefore it is not separately stated."[^20] The treatise gives precise reasons for the absence of the other four:

> "Desire is the wish for what has not yet been encountered. This consciousness spontaneously apprehends what is already encountered, with nothing to wish for — hence no desire. Resolve is the firm holding of what was previously uncertain. This consciousness has from beginningless time apprehended a determined object, with nothing to firmly hold — hence no resolve. Recollection is the remembering of what was previously experienced. This consciousness perpetually apprehends its present object, with nothing to remember — hence no recollection. Concentration is the focusing of mind on a single object. This consciousness spontaneously shifts from moment to moment, never fixed on one point — hence no concentration."[^20]

Manas operates "spontaneously" — no wishing needed (no desire); it has always apprehended its fixed object (no resolve); it perpetually apprehends the present, not the past (no recollection); it shifts from moment to moment rather than holding one point (no concentration). The eighteen-factor configuration paints a precise portrait: an automated, ceaseless, perpetually erring self-grasping mechanism.

### 3.6 Basis for Defilement and Purity, and the Sixth-Eighth Non-Correspondence: Manas as Structural Pivot

The verse phrase "the six active consciousnesses call it the basis of defilement and purity" (六轉呼為染淨依) directly names manas's most critical functional role. The sub-commentary states plainly:

> "Because this consciousness, when contaminated, constantly grasps self within, it causes the sixth consciousness moment by moment to become defiled. Because this consciousness, when uncontaminated, constantly contemplates no-self, it causes the sixth consciousness moment by moment to become purified. Therefore, whether the sixth becomes defiled or purified depends entirely on the seventh."[^21]

The sixth consciousness's defilement or purity depends on the state of manas. When manas grasps at self, the sixth operates under the shadow of that self-grasping; when manas transforms to the uncontaminated, the sixth operates in purity. Manas is not an ordinary partition wall but a lens — all light passing through it is either tinted or clarified.

This "basis for defilement and purity" role rests on a more fundamental structure: the **sixth-eighth non-correspondence** (六八不相應). The *Yogācārabhūmi-śāstra*, fascicle 51, first establishes this three-tier architecture:

> "Because the ālaya-vijñāna exists, therefore manas exists. Because manas serves as the basis, the mental consciousness [sixth] can turn."[^22]

*Cheng Weishi Lun*, fascicle 4, further buttresses this with a counterfactual argument: if the sixth consciousness could directly depend on the eighth, the *Yogācārabhūmi* should say "because the storehouse consciousness exists, the mental consciousness can turn" — but what the text actually says is "because the storehouse consciousness exists, therefore manas exists; because manas serves as the basis, the mental consciousness can turn."[^23] This proves that the sixth cannot bypass the seventh to interact directly with the eighth — the three-tier architecture is irreducible.

A daily-life simile: you (the sixth consciousness) want to change your deep-seated habits (the seeds in the eighth consciousness), but you cannot directly access that storehouse. You must go through a middle manager (manas), and this manager has a fixed bias — it considers everything in the storehouse to be "mine." You can understand that "these things are not mine," but the manager does not listen to you. Only when the manager's bias is transformed does your access to the deep storehouse genuinely change.

*Cheng Weishi Lun*, fascicle 4, summarizes this three-tier co-arising structure with a verse:

> "With the ālaya as basis, manas turns; / Dependent on mind and intention, the remaining active consciousnesses arise."[^24]

### 3.7 Five Stages of Transformation and Equality Wisdom

The third verse — "At Supreme Joy, the initial mind attains equality nature; / At the stage of effortless action, the ego is forever crushed; / The Tathāgata manifests the enjoyment body for others — / Bodhisattvas of the ten grounds are the beneficiaries" — unfolds the complete path of manas's transformation from defilement to purity.

**The foundational distinction: two types of self-grasping.** The prerequisite for transformation is distinguishing between innate and discriminatively acquired self-grasping. *Cheng Weishi Lun*, fascicle 1:

> "Self-grasping is broadly of two types: first, innate; second, discriminatively acquired. Innate self-grasping, through beginningless false perfumation and the force of internal causes, is perpetually present with the body — not dependent on false teachings or false reasoning, turning spontaneously. Hence it is called innate."[^25]

Innate self-grasping "constantly continues in the seventh consciousness, taking the eighth consciousness as its object, generating its own mind-image, and grasping it as a real self." Discriminatively acquired self-grasping "exists only in the sixth consciousness," produced through wrong learning or wrong reasoning. The discriminatively acquired type is "coarse and therefore easily severed — eliminated at the initial moment of the Path of Seeing." The innate type is "subtle and therefore difficult to sever — eliminated only through repeated cultivation of the superior emptiness-of-persons insight in the Path of Cultivation."[^25]

**Five stages of transformation.** Synthesizing *Cheng Weishi Lun* fascicles 1, 5, 9, and 10, manas's transformation can be organized into five positions:

First, the **Accumulation and Preparation stages** (資糧加行位): innate self-grasping operates spontaneously. Discriminatively acquired self-grasping has been suppressed by worldly investigation but its seeds are not yet severed. Manas at this stage is entirely defiled — the four afflictions perpetually active, "sentient beings day and night sunk in a deep stupor."

Second, the **Path of Seeing** (見道, Supreme Joy ground / 極喜地): upon entering the Path of Seeing, the sixth consciousness directly perceives the suchness of the emptiness of persons and dharmas. The verse phrase "at Supreme Joy, the initial mind attains equality nature" refers to this moment — equality wisdom first manifests. But at this stage, only the seeds of **discriminatively acquired** self-grasping are severed; manas's **innate** self-grasping can still manifest. The sub-commentary explains the precise mechanism:

> "When only one type of grasping in manas returns to seed-position, equality wisdom does not manifest. When both types of grasping in manas return to seed-position, equality wisdom manifests. The seventh consciousness has no power to sever afflictions on its own — suppressing the grasping depends entirely on the sixth consciousness."[^26]

This is critically important: manas cannot sever its own afflictions — the transformative power comes from the sixth consciousness's emptiness-insight. When the sixth consciousness contemplates emptiness, manas's dual grasping is temporarily suppressed to seed-position, and only then can equality wisdom manifest.

Third, the **Path of Cultivation** (修道位, grounds 1–7): "Through the seventh ground, [manas's innate self-grasping] can still occasionally manifest."[^27] Innate self-grasping is gradually suppressed at this stage but can still intermittently arise. Bodhisattvas, "through the power of deliberate intention," experience occasional manifestations of manas's self-grasping in the first seven grounds, but this does not cause regression.

Fourth, **the eighth ground and above** (不動地 / Immovable Ground): the verse phrase "at the stage of effortless action, the ego is forever crushed." At this stage, the sixth consciousness abides perpetually in the insight of emptiness-of-persons (no longer interrupted), and manas's self-grasping "forever ceases to manifest."[^27] But the seeds remain — they have not yet been severed in a single moment. Dharma-grasping can still intermittently arise at this stage.

Fifth, **Buddhahood** (金剛喻定 / Vajra-like concentration): "The afflictive-obstruction seeds accompanying the seventh consciousness are severed in a single moment across all three realms when the three vehicles are about to attain the fruition of no-more-learning."[^28] At this point, equality wisdom is complete, "of a single flavor, continuing uninterruptedly to the end of future time."

**The complete fruition of equality wisdom.** *Cheng Weishi Lun*, fascicle 10:

> "Equality wisdom associated mental-factor assembly: this assembly contemplates all dharmas, self and other sentient beings, as entirely equal. Great compassion and the like perpetually accompany it. According to sentient beings' inclinations, it manifests enjoyment bodies, lands, and image-differentiations."[^29]

Equality wisdom is not a "leveling equality" that erases all distinctions. It "contemplates all dharmas, self and other sentient beings, as entirely equal" — what is equal is the nature of dharmas, not their appearances; the reality, not the phenomena. Precisely because the equality of dharma-nature has been realized, the wisdom can "manifest enjoyment bodies, lands, and image-differentiations according to sentient beings' inclinations" — genuine equality responds to each being's individual needs.

The *Mahāyānasaṃgrahabāṣya* (Vasubandhu) succinctly summarizes the transformation:

> "By transforming the defiled manas, one attains equality wisdom. It is first realized at the initial direct perception [Path of Seeing], further purified during the Path of Cultivation. Thereby one abides in non-abiding nirvāṇa, great compassion perpetually accompanies, and one can manifest Buddha-images according to [beings'] inclinations."[^30]

From "constant deliberation with self-image ever in tow" to "great compassion perpetually accompanying" — the "constancy" (恒) of manas does not disappear; it is redirected. The transformation is not the destruction of manas but the conversion of what it constantly deliberates upon — from "self" to "the equality of all sentient beings."

### 3.8 Cessation Attainment: A Precision Debate on Manas's Status

The status of manas in special meditative states is one of Yogācāra's most precise theoretical debates, and serves as an important thought-experiment for verifying manas's existence.

**The fundamental distinction between non-perception attainment and cessation attainment.** *Cheng Weishi Lun*, fascicle 7:

> "The attainment of non-perception... causes the non-constantly-active minds and mental factors to cease, with the cessation of perception as primary — hence named 'non-perception.' The cessation attainment... causes both the non-constantly-active and the constantly-active defiled minds and mental factors to cease — hence named 'cessation.'"[^31]

The attainment of non-perception ceases only the "non-constantly-active" — the first six consciousnesses. The cessation attainment additionally ceases the "constantly-active defiled" — manas's four afflictions of self-grasping. If both attainments merely ceased the first six consciousnesses, what would distinguish them? The *Cheng Weishi Lun* uses precisely this as a key argument for manas's existence:

> "If the defiled mind [manas] did not exist in the attainments of non-perception and cessation, they should be indistinguishable. Both attainments cease the six consciousnesses and their mental factors, with no difference in their entities and number. Without the defiled mind being present in one and absent in the other, how would they differ?"[^32]

**The Dharmapāla-Sthiramati debate.** What exactly happens to manas in cessation attainment — is it entirely absent, or only its defiled aspect?

Sthiramati's school holds that manas is entirely absent in the three states (arhat, cessation, supramundane path) — "manas only has afflictive obstructions accompanying it; the sacred teachings all state it is absent in three states." Dharmapāla's orthodox position counters: "That position contradicts both teaching and reasoning — the supra-mundane manas is stated in the sūtras to exist." The reasoning: (1) defiled manas (self-grasping) is suppressed; (2) dharma-grasping manas persists, because the storehouse consciousness constantly turns together with one consciousness; (3) the attainment of equality wisdom by the active consciousnesses requires a purified consciousness as its basis.[^33]

*Cheng Weishi Lun*, fascicle 5, integrates this debate through the "three differentiations of manas":

> "This consciousness has broadly three differentiations: first, associated with the person-self view; second, associated with the dharma-self view; third, associated with equality wisdom."[^34]

Three differentiations, three phases: the ordinary person's person-grasping manas, the noble one's dharma-grasping manas, and the Buddha's equality-wisdom manas. Manas is not annihilated but purified layer by layer — from person-grasping to dharma-grasping to wisdom.

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Dialogue

### 4.1 The Default Mode Network: The Brain's Automatic Self-Referential System

⚠️ **This section is a heuristic analogy, not a reductive equivalence.**

Modern neuroscience has identified a system in the brain called the Default Mode Network (DMN). When a person is not engaged in any specific task — when "doing nothing" — the DMN automatically becomes active, engaging in: self-referential thinking (thoughts about "I" and "mine"), mental time travel (recalling the past, imagining the future), and social cognition (inferring others' views of "me").

Several features of the DMN structurally resonate with manas's "constant deliberation": first, it operates **automatically** — it does not need to be deliberately activated; rather, it requires deliberate attentional tasks to suppress it. Second, its core function is **self-reference** — weaving experience into a narrative centered on "I." Third, it is **significantly suppressed by meditation training** — long-term meditators show markedly reduced DMN activity.

The valid boundary of this analogy: the DMN is a brain activity pattern observed through functional magnetic resonance imaging, belonging to the domain of form-dharmas (material substrate); manas is a mind-dharma (citta), a functional differentiation within consciousness. The two exist at different ontological levels and cannot be simply equated. But the discovery of the DMN at least provides modern readers with a bridge for understanding manas's "automatic self-referential mechanism" — the brain does indeed have a "default to thinking about self when idle" program, and Buddhism identified this phenomenon fifteen centuries ago.

### 4.2 The Chama Sūtra: "Residual Scent of Self-Conceit" in the Āgamas

The manas-vijñāna is a Mahāyāna Yogācāra construction, but the phenomenon it addresses — "knowing no-self yet unable to release self" — is already documented in the Āgamas. *Saṃyuktāgama* (雜阿含經), fascicle 5, records the venerable Chama's famous self-testimony:

> "I observe the five appropriated aggregates as not-self and not-belonging-to-self, yet I am not an arhat. Why? Because the conceit 'I am,' the desire 'I am,' the tendency 'I am' have not been severed, not fully known, not abandoned, not expelled."[^35]

Chama used the simile of laundered cloth: the garment has been washed with various alkaline solutions to remove the dirt, but a residual scent remains, requiring further fumigation with various fragrances to eliminate. Likewise, "although the learned noble disciple observes the five appropriated aggregates as not-self and not-belonging-to-self, nevertheless the conceit 'I am,' the desire 'I am,' the tendency 'I am' regarding the five appropriated aggregates have not been severed."[^35]

Chama's "residual scent of self-conceit" corresponds precisely to manas's innate self-grasping in Yogācāra analysis. The Path of Seeing (insight into no-self) severs discriminatively acquired self-grasping, but innate self-grasping — like "residual scent on laundered cloth" — requires prolonged cultivation to eliminate. This Āgama account provides the earliest experiential evidence for what the manas theory would later systematize.

### 4.3 Chan "No-Thought" and Manas's "Constant Deliberation": A Dialogue

Chan Buddhism takes "no-thought as its tenet" (無念為宗), while manas's nature is "constant deliberation" (恒審思量) — the two appear contradictory but are in fact complementary.

The Sixth Patriarch Huineng defines "no-thought" precisely in the *Platform Sūtra*:

> "If one sees all dharmas and the mind does not attach — this is no-thought. Functioning everywhere, yet not attaching anywhere. Simply purify the original mind, letting the six consciousnesses issue through the six gates without defilement or mixture amid the six sense-objects — coming and going freely, functioning without obstruction. This is prajñā-samādhi, self-liberation — it is called the practice of no-thought. If one does not think of anything at all, forcing thought to cease entirely, this is dharma-bondage, and is called a biased view."[^36]

"No-thought" is not "thinking of nothing" (that would be a biased view) but "mind not attaching." Translated into Yogācāra language: no-thought does not mean destroying manas's "constant deliberation" but letting the constant deliberation no longer center on "self" — transforming "constant deliberation **with self-image in tow**" into constant deliberation that is "without defilement or mixture."

The Korean master Chinul, in *Admonitions for the Cultivation of the Mind* (修心訣), captures the tension between sudden awakening and the gradualism demanded by manas in a single quatrain:

> "Though sudden awakening equals the Buddha, / Habitual energies from many lives run deep; / The wind has stopped, yet waves still surge; / The principle is manifest, yet thought-streams still intrude."[^37]

"The wind has stopped" is like the Path of Seeing — one has understood no-self in principle. "Waves still surge" is like manas's innate self-grasping — the rational insight is clear, but the habitual waves continue. This is not Chan Buddhism denying the manas theory. Rather, Chan's "practice of no-thought" was always designed to address manas's "constant deliberation on self-image." Sudden awakening is the entry-point of the Path of Seeing; gradual cultivation is the prolonged transformation of manas's innate self-grasping during the Path of Cultivation — "relying on awakening, one cultivates; through gradual perfumation, the work is accomplished."[^37]

## V. Middle-Way Corrections

### 5.1 Manas Is Not an Enemy to Destroy

❌ **Common misunderstanding:** Manas is the practitioner's enemy, to be severed and annihilated.

✅ **Middle-way position:** Manas is not an enemy to be destroyed but a mechanism to be transformed. Equality wisdom is not built on the ruins of manas; it is the transformation-result of manas itself. "By transforming the defiled manas, one attains equality wisdom" — "transforming" is not "annihilating." Manas's "constant deliberation" persists even at Buddhahood; it merely shifts from "self-image in tow" to "great compassion perpetually accompanying." If one generates aversion toward manas — "I must destroy my self-grasping!" — that very aversion is itself manas at work.

### 5.2 Seeing Does Not Equal Severing

❌ **Common misunderstanding:** Once one has become aware that manas is operating, it has already been transformed.

✅ **Middle-way position:** Awareness by the sixth consciousness is a necessary but not sufficient condition for transformation. The venerable Chama "observed [the aggregates as] not-self and not-belonging-to-self" (awareness was present), yet "the conceit 'I am' had not been severed." Awareness is the starting point, not the destination. From awareness to transformation requires prolonged "repeated cultivation of the superior emptiness-insight" during the Path of Cultivation. Equating awareness with severance creates a false sense of accomplishment — the illusion that one has already realized no-self.

### 5.3 Equality Wisdom Does Not Erase Difference

❌ **Common misunderstanding:** Equality wisdom means "everything is the same" — no more distinctions of any kind.

✅ **Middle-way position:** Equality wisdom "contemplates all dharmas, self and other sentient beings, as entirely equal" — what is equal is the reality at the level of dharma-nature, not phenomenal differences. Precisely because the equality of dharma-nature has been realized, the wisdom can "manifest enjoyment bodies, lands, and image-differentiations according to sentient beings' inclinations" — beings of different capacities require different methods of teaching. An "equality" that erases difference is another form of conceptual superimposition (parikalpita); an equality that responds to difference is the consummated reality (pariniṣpanna).

### 5.4 Chan "No-Thought" Does Not Deny Manas

❌ **Common misunderstanding:** Chan Buddhism advocates "no-thought," therefore it does not accept the manas theory.

✅ **Middle-way position:** The Sixth Patriarch explicitly stated: "If one does not think of anything at all, forcing thought to cease entirely, this is dharma-bondage, and is called a biased view." Chan's "no-thought" does not deny manas's existence but provides a direct practical path for facing manas's constant deliberation. The *Zongjing Lu* (宗鏡錄) directly cites manas theory: "This seventh consciousness does not associate with other mental factors beyond the four afflictions, because: first, it is constant; second, it grasps internally; third, it apprehends a single type of object."[^38] The divergence between Chan and Yogācāra lies not in the diagnosis of the problem (both acknowledge perpetual self-fixation) but in the choice of method (gradual insight-cultivation versus sudden direct realization). And Chinul's "wind stops but waves still surge" already indicates that even within the Chan tradition, gradual cultivation follows sudden awakening — precisely corresponding to manas's progression of suppression during the Path of Seeing and gradual severance during the Path of Cultivation.

### 5.5 Intellectual Understanding Does Not Equal Realized Transformation

❌ **Common misunderstanding:** Having read this paper is equivalent to understanding manas; understanding is equivalent to having transformed it.

✅ **Middle-way position:** This paper operates within the domain of "knowledge" (知) — using words and concepts to analyze manas's structure, function, and transformation path. But the problem with manas is precisely that it operates **beneath** knowledge. The sixth consciousness's intellectual understanding (including understanding gained from reading a paper) cannot directly alter manas — this is the practical implication of the "sixth-eighth non-correspondence." From knowledge to realization, what is needed is not more reading but sustained contemplative practice. What an academic paper can do is provide a precise map for the practice; but the map is not the journey.

---

## VI. Conclusion

The manas-vijñāna is the most hidden yet most critical structural component of Yogācāra theory. It reveals the practitioner's deepest predicament: self-grasping does not require your participation to run automatically — it was operating before you knew, it continues after you become aware, it persists even after you understand no-self.

This paper has taken the three verses on the seventh consciousness as its framework and unfolded the complete doctrine of manas layer by layer. The first verse addresses nature: the cognitive mode of objective appearance with substance, the subtle quality of obscured-neutral, the mediating position of bridging cognition and root, the perpetual error of non-valid cognition. The second verse addresses function: the unique positioning of constant deliberation, the internally generative logic of the four afflictions, the precise configuration of eighteen mental factors, the pivotal role as basis for defilement and purity, the three-tier architecture of sixth-eighth non-correspondence. The third verse addresses transformation: from the full defilement of the Accumulation stage, to the initial turning at the Path of Seeing, to the gradual cultivation of the Path of Cultivation, to effortless action at the eighth ground, to the complete fruition of equality wisdom at Buddhahood.

The theoretical significance of manas lies in its precise explanation of the gap between "knowing" and "doing" — the sixth consciousness's insight is intermittent, while manas's self-grasping is uninterrupted. From the venerable Chama's "residual scent of self-conceit" to Chinul's "wind stops but waves still surge," different traditions in different languages point to the same experiential fact: the Path of Seeing does not equal the final severance of self-grasping.

The practical significance of manas lies in locating the pivot of transformation: it is not in the sixth consciousness (the observer) nor in the eighth (the storehouse), but in the seventh — manas's loosening is the key to liberation. And this loosening is not destruction but transformation — from "constant deliberation with self-image ever in tow" to "great compassion perpetually accompanying." The "constancy" of manas never disappears; it merely changes direction.

S3-P03 described the ālaya-vijñāna as "the hard drive of life." Manas is the operating system running on that hard drive — one that has shipped since the factory with "everything is mine" as its default setting. You can open a "no-self" application on the screen (the sixth consciousness), but as long as the operating system continues to run "everything is mine," that application will always be just one window. Transforming consciousness into wisdom means performing a fundamental system upgrade — from "everything is mine" to "all sentient beings are entirely equal."

This upgrade cannot be completed by reading a paper. But knowing the architecture of the operating system is the first step.
