## Abstract

The Heart Sūtra passage "The bodhisattva, relying on prajñāpāramitā, has a mind without obstruction; because there is no obstruction, there is no terror; far from inverted dream-thinking, [the bodhisattva attains] ultimate nirvāṇa" constitutes the culminating fruition of the entire awakening arc of the Heart Sūtra. This paper argues that "because there is nothing to attain" (以無所得故) does not bring immediate liberation — it inaugurates terror. When the seventh manas consciousness, which perpetually deliberates and clings to an "I," is illuminated by prajñā as non-substantial, its first response is not relief but existential dread: without "me" holding on, who will keep you safe? The Heart Sūtra's causal sequence precisely maps this path of traversal: mind without obstruction (both obstructions removed) → no terror (path of seeing, severing conceptual discrimination) → far from inverted dream-thinking (path of cultivation, severing the four inversions) → ultimate nirvāṇa (apratiṣṭhita-nirvāṇa, non-abiding nirvāṇa). The means of traversal is not to annihilate the "I" but to transform it — manas into samatā-jñāna (equality wisdom). The paper employs the Buddha's own life as the existential proof of "because there is nothing to attain": the four-gate excursion (longing for freedom) → renunciation and asceticism (turning inward) → subduing Māra under the Bodhi tree (traversing terror) → forty-five years of daily alms-round after enlightenment (living out apratiṣṭhita-nirvāṇa) → the final teaching before parinirvāṇa, "be your own lamp, take refuge in the Dharma" (releasing even the Buddha himself). Scriptural foundations draw on the *Dīrghāgama*'s four-gate excursion, the *Ekottarāgama*'s māra-subduing account, the *Vajracchedikā Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra*'s opening alms-round passage, and the *Mahāparinirvāṇa Sūtra*'s final teaching for the Buddha's biographical narrative; on Kuiji's *Bore boluomiduo xinjing youzhan*'s five-path × ten-bhūmi dual mapping as the doctrinal skeleton; on the *Mahāyānasaṃgrahabhāṣya*'s formulation "by prajñā one does not abide in saṃsāra; by karuṇā one does not abide in nirvāṇa" for the core expression of apratiṣṭhita-nirvāṇa; and on the *Vijñaptimātratāsiddhi*'s manas → samatā-jñāna transformation as the mechanism for traversing terror. Cross-disciplinary dialogue employs Heidegger's Angst as a heuristic analogy. Five Middle Way corrections: terror does not equal a fault in practice; subduing Māra is not "defeating" a demon king; existential Angst does not equal Buddhist terror; apratiṣṭhita-nirvāṇa does not equal worldly busyness; "self-reliance" does not equal "no need for a spiritual teacher." From S2-P02 (Avalokiteśvara = causal ground) to S2-P10 (mind without obstruction = fruition), the Heart Sūtra's awakening loop closes.

**摘要**

心經「菩提薩埵，依般若波羅蜜多故，心無罣礙，無罣礙故，無有恐怖，遠離顛倒夢想，究竟涅槃」一段，是整部心經覺醒弧線的收束果位。本文提出：「以無所得故」不是立刻的解脫，而是恐怖的開始。穿越恐怖的方式不是消滅「我」，而是轉化「我」——末那識轉為平等性智。以佛陀一生為「以無所得故」的存在證明。經典依據以佛傳敘事和窺基五道×十地雙重映射為骨架，以《攝大乘論釋》無住處涅槃和《成唯識論》轉識成智為義理核心。中道校正五條。S2-P02（觀自在 = 因地）到 S2-P10（心無罣礙 = 果位），覺醒迴路閉環。

## I. Introduction

### 1.1 Textual Position

S2-P09 reached the sharpest edge of prajñā's emptiness contemplation — even the path of practice was emptied, even prajñā-wisdom itself could not be attained. The four characters "because there is nothing to attain" (以無所得故) reclaimed the last tool from the practitioner's hands.

And then?

Common sense says: after letting go, you should feel light. The Heart Sūtra says: after letting go comes terror.

> 菩提薩埵，依般若波羅蜜多故，心無罣礙，無罣礙故，無有恐怖，遠離顛倒夢想，究竟涅槃。
> The bodhisattva, relying on prajñāpāramitā, has a mind without obstruction. Because there is no obstruction, there is no terror. Far from inverted dream-thinking, [the bodhisattva attains] ultimate nirvāṇa.[^1]

This passage is the culmination of the Heart Sūtra's entire awakening arc. From S2-P02 (Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva = the causal ground of longing for freedom) to this moment (mind without obstruction = the fruition of freedom), the intervening journey has traversed illuminating the emptiness of the five aggregates (S2-P04), deconstructing the eighteen dhātus (S2-P07), seeing the emptiness of the twelve links of dependent origination (S2-P08), releasing the Four Noble Truths and prajñā itself (S2-P09), and arriving at "because there is nothing to attain."

### 1.2 Research Questions

This paper addresses three core questions.

First, why does "because there is nothing to attain" trigger terror? If prajñā is wisdom and emptiness is liberation, why does the Heart Sūtra follow "mind without obstruction" not with "great joy" but with "no terror" — what exactly is this "terror," and where does it come from?

Second, what is the mechanism for traversing terror? The Heart Sūtra's causal sequence — mind without obstruction → no terror → far from inverted dream-thinking → ultimate nirvāṇa — is a precise psychological map. Kuiji maps these four phrases to the five paths and ten bhūmis in his commentary, unfolding the complete timeline of bodhisattva practice. How does this map operate?

Third, why is "ultimate nirvāṇa" not departure from the world? The nirvāṇa of the śrāvaka is described as "body and wisdom completely extinguished, like a lamp going out." The bodhisattva's nirvāṇa is "not abiding in saṃsāra, not abiding in nirvāṇa" — the same word, opposite directions. What is the doctrinal basis for this difference?

### 1.3 The Buddha: The Most Complete Existential Proof

The preceding nine papers analyzed doctrine. P10 does something different: it lets the Buddha himself walk in.

The Buddha did not teach "because there is nothing to attain" with words. He lived it with his entire life. The four-gate excursion: longing for freedom. Renunciation and asceticism: turning inward. Subduing Māra under the Bodhi tree: traversing terror. Forty-five years of daily alms-rounds after enlightenment: living with nothing-to-attain in the world for the benefit of beings. The final teaching before parinirvāṇa — "be your own lamp, take refuge in the Dharma" — releasing even the Buddha himself.

The Buddha's life and the Heart Sūtra's awakening arc run in precise parallel. This paper weaves the two threads together: doctrinal analysis as the warp, the Buddha's biography as the weft.

## II. Scriptural Foundations

This section proceeds along two axes. The first is the sūtra narrative of the Buddha's life — the four-gate excursion, subduing Māra, the daily alms-round, and the final teaching before parinirvāṇa — establishing the existential proof of "because there is nothing to attain." The second is the doctrinal foundations from the treatises and commentaries — Kuiji's five-path × ten-bhūmi mapping, the *Mahāyānasaṃgrahabhāṣya*'s apratiṣṭhita-nirvāṇa, the *Vijñaptimātratāsiddhi*'s transformation of consciousness into wisdom, and the Yogācāra positioning of the four inversions — establishing the doctrinal framework for the Heart Sūtra's causal chain.

### 2.1 The Buddha's Four-Gate Excursion — The Starting Point of Longing

The *Dīrghāgama*'s Mahāpadāna Sutta records the prince's four excursions through the city gates. At the first gate, he sees an old man:

> 見一老人，頭白齒落，面皺身僂，拄杖羸步。
> "[He] saw an old man — hair white, teeth fallen, face wrinkled, body bent, walking feebly with a cane."[^2]

The prince asks: "Shall I too become like this?" The answer: "Yes. Birth necessarily brings aging; there is no distinction of rank or status." At the second gate he sees a sick man, "body emaciated, belly swollen, face darkened, lying alone in filth." At the third gate he sees a dead man, "kinsfolk wailing and weeping."

The three-gate excursion is a process of perceptual inversion — the "perceptual inversion" analyzed in S2-P08 concretizes here. The aging, sickness, and death the prince saw were visible to everyone in the palace, but only the prince truly "saw into" them.

At the fourth gate he sees a renunciant:

> 沙門者，捨離恩愛，出家修道，攝御諸根，不染外欲，慈心一切。
> "A śramaṇa — one who has renounced affection, left home to cultivate the Way, disciplined all faculties, undefiled by external desires, with compassion for all."[^3]

The prince responds: "Excellent! This Way truly and forever severs the bonds of dust — subtle, pure, and clear." He immediately shaves his hair, dons the three dharma robes, and takes up the homeless life. A summary verse in the sūtra captures the entire turning point:

> 太子見老、病人，知世苦惱，又見死人，戀世情滅；及見沙門，廓然大悟。
> "The prince saw the aged and the sick and knew the world's suffering; seeing the dead, worldly attachment was extinguished; and seeing the śramaṇa, he awoke with vast clarity."[^4]

The four characters "awoke with vast clarity" (廓然大悟) deserve attention — this is not minor intellectual insight but the reversal of an entire life's direction. In the Heart Sūtra's arc, the four-gate excursion is the prototype of "Avalokiteśvara": longing for freedom from suffering.

### 2.2 Subduing Māra Under the Bodhi Tree — Traversing Terror

The *Ekottarāgama*'s Aśvaśiras chapter records the full narrative of subduing Māra. The demon king Pāpīyas arrives with an army of 1.8 billion, surrounding the Bodhi tree. The Buddha's response:

> 默然不對……我今執心無所畏懼。
> "He remained silent, giving no answer… 'I now hold my mind without any fear.'"[^5]

The Buddha responds in verse:

> 仁鎧三昧弓，手執智慧箭，福業為兵眾，今當壞汝軍。
> "Wearing the armor of benevolence, with the bow of samādhi, and wisdom-arrows in hand, meritorious deeds for my army — I shall now shatter your forces."[^6]

Māra's attacks fail to "move a single hair." Finally, the Buddha "extends his right hand and presses the earth with his fingers." A deity of the earth rises from the ground to bear witness, and Māra "grieves, suffers, and retreats into invisibility."[^7]

Nāgārjuna records the Buddha's vow before the encounter with Māra in the *Mahāprajñāpāramitopadeśa*:

> 坐金剛座而自誓言：要不破此結加趺坐，成一切智；不得一切智，終不起也。
> "Seated on the vajra-seat, he made this vow: 'I shall not uncross these legs until I attain omniscience; without attaining omniscience, I shall never rise.'"[^8]

The same treatise lists Māra's ten armies — the ten aspects of affliction. The sixth army is precisely "terror" (怖畏).[^9] This point is critical for the present paper: Māra is not an external supernatural enemy but the total projection of the practitioner's inner afflictions. Terror is a checkpoint that must be traversed on the path, not an accidental adversary.

### 2.3 Forty-Five Years of Alms-Round — The Daily Life of Nothing-to-Attain

After enlightenment, the Buddha did not enter nirvāṇa. He taught and collected alms for forty-five years. The *Vajracchedikā Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra* opens with a scene of seemingly ordinary dailiness:

> 爾時，世尊食時，著衣持鉢，入舍衛大城乞食。於其城中，次第乞已，還至本處。飯食訖，收衣鉢，洗足已，敷座而坐。
> "Then the World-Honored One, at mealtime, put on his robe, took up his bowl, and entered the great city of Śrāvastī to beg for food. Within the city, going from house to house in sequence, he finished his round and returned to his place. When the meal was done, he put away his robe and bowl, washed his feet, arranged his seat, and sat down."[^10]

Kuiji's commentary on the *Vajracchedikā* offers a precise reading of the three characters "in sequence" (次第乞):

> 從一巷至一巷，從一家至一家，為顯於一一法皆遍證至無相理。
> "From one lane to the next, from one household to the next — to demonstrate that in each and every dharma one realizes the principle of signlessness throughout."[^11]

"In sequence" means without selection — not favoring the wealthy, not skipping the poor. This is not a performance of humility; it is the bodily and verbal manifestation of equality wisdom (samatā-jñāna). Jizang's commentary further explains: alms nourish the physical body, while expounding prajñā benefits the dharma body; alms demonstrate the Tathāgata's contentment with little, manifest the Buddha's compassion, and shatter the mind of arrogance.[^12]

Forty-five years, every day the same. Donning the robe, taking up the bowl, begging for food, washing the feet, arranging the seat. This is the finest existential proof of "because there is nothing to attain" — not a theory but a way of living.

### 2.4 The Final Teaching Before Parinirvāṇa — Even the Buddha Lets Go

The Buddha is eighty years old, his back aching, parinirvāṇa approaching. Ānanda is anxious. The *Dīrghāgama*'s Youxing Jing records the Buddha's final teaching to his disciples:

> 當自熾燃，熾燃於法，勿他熾燃；當自歸依，歸依於法，勿他歸依。
> "Be your own lamp; make the Dharma your lamp. Do not seek another lamp. Take refuge in yourself; take refuge in the Dharma. Do not take refuge in anything else."[^13]

The method is the four foundations of mindfulness: "A bhikṣu observes the inner body with diligence and without negligence, mindful and without forgetfulness, removing worldly craving and worry. The outer body, the inner-and-outer body — observed with the same diligence." Feeling, mind, and dharmas likewise. The concluding statement is definitive: "After my passing, whoever can practice this method — that person is truly my disciple, foremost among learners."[^14]

The *Mahāparinirvāṇa Sūtra* records the absolute final teaching. First, "take the precepts as your teacher":

> 我昔為諸比丘，制戒波羅提木叉，及餘所說種種妙法，此即便是汝等大師。如我在世，無有異也。
> "I have established for the bhikṣus the precepts of the Prātimokṣa and all the various subtle dharmas I have spoken — these are your great teacher. As if I were still in the world, there is no difference."[^15]

Then the Buddha asks three times whether any disciple has doubts — "the bhikṣus remained silent, none seeking to resolve a question." Finally, the ultimate verse:

> 諸行無常，是生滅法，生滅滅已，寂滅為樂。
> "All formations are impermanent — this is the dharma of arising and ceasing. When arising and ceasing themselves cease, quiescent cessation is bliss."[^16]

> 此則是我最後教也。我般涅槃，其時已至。
> "This is my final teaching. My parinirvāṇa — the time has come."[^17]

Elsewhere in the same tradition, the Buddha's final word is even more concise:

> 無為放逸，我以不放逸故，自致正覺……此是如來末後所說。
> "Do not be heedless. It is through heedlessness that I myself attained perfect awakening… This is the Tathāgata's last utterance."[^18]

The Buddha's final act of letting go releases even "the Buddha" as an object of reliance. "Take the precepts as your teacher" — not the Buddha as your teacher, but the Dharma as your teacher. You no longer need me; the Dharma is in your hands. This is the greatest single act of "because there is nothing to attain" in an entire lifetime.

### 2.5 Kuiji's Commentary: The Five-Path × Ten-Bhūmi Dual Mapping

Kuiji's commentary on this Heart Sūtra passage unfolds a precise dual mapping system.

First, the doctrinal definitions. Kuiji uses a Yogācāra reading to define "obstruction" (罣) as the afflictive obstruction (kleśāvaraṇa) — because it blocks nirvāṇa — and "hindrance" (礙) as the cognitive obstruction (jñeyāvaraṇa) — because it blocks bodhi.[^19] The five terrors arise respectively from: fear for one's livelihood, fear of ill repute, fear of death, fear of lower rebirths, and fear of assemblies — each rooted in specific forms of self-grasping. The seven inversions include inversion of perception, inversion of view, inversion of mind, and the four specific inversions of taking the impermanent as permanent, suffering as pleasure, the impure as pure, and the selfless as self. Kuiji specifically notes that "dream-thinking" (夢想) is a synonym for vikalpa (discriminative conceptualization): "not having awakened to true wisdom, one perpetually dwells in a dream; therefore the Buddha speaks of the long night of saṃsāra."[^20]

Next, the five-path mapping: "mind without obstruction" corresponds to the stages of resolute understanding and preparation (progressively suppressing the manifest activity of both obstructions); "no terror" corresponds to the path of seeing (severing conceptual discrimination); "far from inverted dream-thinking" corresponds to the path of cultivation (understanding and practice broadly increase, severing all inversions); "ultimate nirvāṇa" corresponds to the path of no-more-learning.[^21]

Then, the ten-bhūmi mapping: "mind without obstruction" corresponds to the first bhūmi, the Stage of Joy (permanently severing the "skin-level" coarseness); "no terror" corresponds to the eighth bhūmi, the Stage of Effortless Non-Conceptuality (permanently severing the "flesh-level" coarseness); "far from inverted dream-thinking" corresponds to the tenth bhūmi, the Supreme Fulfilled Stage (permanently severing the "bone-level" coarseness, entering the Tathāgata's abode); "ultimate nirvāṇa" corresponds to the Buddha stage (all obstructions of three levels severed).[^22]

The metaphor of "three levels of coarseness" (三麁重) deserves attention: skin (coarse), flesh (middle), bone (subtle) — three depths of obstruction. Practice does not cut through in a single stroke; it peels layer by layer, from surface to marrow.

Kuiji also enumerates four types of nirvāṇa: inherently pure nirvāṇa (tathatā as the true mark of all dharmas), apratiṣṭhita-nirvāṇa (tathatā freed from the cognitive obstruction, constantly sustained by great compassion and wisdom), nirvāṇa with remainder (tathatā revealed by the exhaustion of the origin of suffering), and nirvāṇa without remainder (tathatā revealed by the exhaustion of suffering).[^23]

### 2.6 The Mahāyānasaṃgrahabhāṣya: Apratiṣṭhita-Nirvāṇa

"Apratiṣṭhita-nirvāṇa" (non-abiding nirvāṇa) is one of the core doctrinal concepts of this paper. The clearest formulation appears in the *Mahāyānasaṃgrahabhāṣya*:

> 二乘與菩薩同以惑滅為滅諦，二乘惑滅一向背生死趣涅槃，菩薩惑滅不背生死不背涅槃，故異二乘。
> "Both the Two Vehicles and the bodhisattva take the cessation of afflictions as the truth of cessation. But for the Two Vehicles, cessation of afflictions means turning entirely away from saṃsāra toward nirvāṇa; for the bodhisattva, cessation of afflictions means turning away from neither saṃsāra nor nirvāṇa — hence the difference."[^24]

> 由般若不住生死、由慈悲不住涅槃，若分別生死則住生死，若分別涅槃則住涅槃，菩薩得無分別智，無所分別，故無所住。
> "By prajñā one does not abide in saṃsāra; by karuṇā one does not abide in nirvāṇa. If one discriminates saṃsāra, one abides in saṃsāra; if one discriminates nirvāṇa, one abides in nirvāṇa. The bodhisattva attains non-discriminating wisdom — discriminating nothing, and therefore abiding nowhere."[^25]

The *Vijñaptimātratāsiddhi*'s definition is even more refined:

> 無住處涅槃，謂即真如出所知障，大悲般若常所輔翼。由斯不住生死涅槃，利樂有情，窮未來際，用而常寂，故名涅槃。
> "Apratiṣṭhita-nirvāṇa: tathatā freed from the cognitive obstruction, constantly sustained by great compassion and prajñā. Thereby not abiding in saṃsāra or nirvāṇa, benefiting and delighting sentient beings to the end of future time — active yet always quiescent (用而常寂, *yòng ér cháng jì*), therefore called nirvāṇa."[^26]

The four characters "active yet always quiescent" (用而常寂) are extraordinarily precise — in the "activity" of benefiting beings, the essential nature of tathatā remains "quiescent." It is not that one is too busy to remember stillness; stillness is inherent in the activity itself.

The same treatise notes who can fully possess all four types of nirvāṇa: "All sentient beings possess the first. Śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas at the stage of no-more-learning may possess the first three. Only our World-Honored One can be said to possess all four."[^27]

### 2.7 The Vijñaptimātratāsiddhi: Manas → Samatā-Jñāna

The mechanism for traversing terror is, in Yogācāra terms, the transformation of the manas consciousness. The *Vijñaptimātratāsiddhi* describes samatā-jñāna (equality wisdom):

> 平等性智相應心品：謂此心品，觀一切法，自他有情，悉皆平等。大慈悲等，恒共相應，隨諸有情所樂，示現受用身土影像差別。妙觀察智不共所依，無住涅槃之所建立，一味相續，窮未來際。
> "The mental assembly associated with samatā-jñāna: this mental assembly observes all dharmas, self and other sentient beings, as entirely equal. Great compassion and the like are perpetually associated with it. In accordance with what sentient beings desire, it manifests various images of enjoyment bodies and lands. It serves as the exclusive basis for pratyavekṣaṇā-jñāna (profound observation wisdom) and establishes apratiṣṭhita-nirvāṇa — of one flavor, continuing to the end of future time."[^28]

Manas operates in three modes: in ordinary beings, associated with pudgala-ātma-dṛṣṭi (personal self-view — the defiled mode); when the fruit of dharma-emptiness wisdom has not yet manifested, associated with dharma-ātma-dṛṣṭi; in the Tathāgata and in bodhisattvas during the path of seeing and cultivation when the fruit of dharma-emptiness wisdom is manifest, associated with samatā-jñāna.[^29]

The key lies in the transformation mechanism. The *Bashi guiju buzhu* (Supplement to the Eight Consciousnesses Verse) notes: "Manas itself has no power to sever afflictions; it relies entirely on the sixth consciousness." Only when the sixth consciousness enters the twofold emptiness contemplation does manas's dual grasping return to its seed state, and samatā-jñāna can manifest.[^30] The timeline: first manifest at the initial moment of the first bhūmi (path of seeing); still intermittent below the seventh bhūmi; from the eighth bhūmi onward, personal self-grasping is permanently subdued; continuous only after the tenth bhūmi.

Asvabhāva directly connects this line to apratiṣṭhita-nirvāṇa in his *Mahāyānasaṃgrahabhāṣya*:

> 轉染污末那故得平等性智，初現觀時先已證得，於修道位轉復清淨，由此安住無住涅槃，大慈大悲恒與相應，能隨所樂現佛影像。
> "By transforming the defiled manas, one attains samatā-jñāna. At the initial direct perception [of the path of seeing], it has already been realized; during the path of cultivation, it becomes further purified. Thereby one abides in apratiṣṭhita-nirvāṇa, perpetually associated with great compassion, able to manifest images of the Buddha in accordance with beings' desires."[^31]

Manas → samatā-jñāna → apratiṣṭhita-nirvāṇa. This is the complete passage from terror to freedom.

### 2.8 The Four Inversions in Yogācāra

The "inversions" in "far from inverted dream-thinking" are defined in the *Yogācārabhūmi* in three layers: inversion of perception (saṃjñā-viparyāsa — discriminative conceptualization), inversion of view (dṛṣṭi-viparyāsa — grasping with approval and attachment), and inversion of mind (citta-viparyāsa — afflictions such as greed).[^32] The root is avidyā.

The *Mahāyānasaṃgrahabhāṣya* further reveals the relationship between the four inversions and parikalpita-svabhāva (the imagined nature):

> 無量行相意識遍計顛倒生相，故名遍計所執。顛倒生相者，謂是能生虛妄顛倒所緣境相。
> "Because the mano-vijñāna, through limitless modes of activity, projects the characteristics that generate inversions, it is called 'the imagined.' The 'characteristics that generate inversions' means those objective appearances that produce false inversions."[^33]

"Characteristics that generate inversions" IS the definition of parikalpita itself — the four inversions are not one aspect of parikalpita; they are its core operating mechanism.

Each inversion has a specific cognitive root. The *Madhyāntavibhāga* commentary indicates: the permanence-inversion arises from "similar continuous flow" (the similar stream of momentary events mistaken for permanence); the pleasure-inversion from "imagined antidote" (using imagined pleasure to counter suffering); the purity-inversion from "habituation" (long familiarity producing a false sense of purity); the self-inversion from "aggregate-grasping" (taking the totality of the five aggregates as "I").[^34]

The antidote for the four inversions is articulated in the *Mahāprajñāpāramitopadeśa* through the structure of the four foundations of mindfulness: "Contemplation of the body is taught to counter the purity-inversion; contemplation of feeling to counter the pleasure-inversion; contemplation of mind to counter the permanence-inversion; contemplation of dharmas to counter the self-inversion."[^35] This resonates with the fact that the Buddha's final teaching to his disciples was precisely the four foundations of mindfulness.[^14] The tool for countering the inversions was the very thing the Buddha used from beginning to end, and the last thing he placed in his disciples' hands.

### 2.9 Bodhisattva Nirvāṇa Versus Śrāvaka Nirvāṇa

Why does the bodhisattva not enter nirupādhiśeṣa-nirvāṇa (nirvāṇa without remainder)? The *Ratnagotravibhāga* cites the *Śrīmālādevī Sūtra* in explanation: the śrāvaka's nirvāṇa is "expedient means" —

> 聲聞辟支佛得涅槃者，是佛方便……見諸眾生於長道曠野遠行疲惓，恐有退轉，為止息故造作化城。
> "That śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas attain nirvāṇa — this is the Buddha's skillful means… Seeing sentient beings exhausted from long travel through a vast wilderness, fearing they might retreat, he conjured a phantom city for them to rest."[^36]

The phantom city is a rest stop, not the destination. The śrāvaka believes they have arrived; in reality they are only midway.

Asvabhāva's description of the śrāvaka's nirvāṇa in the *Mahāyānasaṃgrahabhāṣya* is even more direct:

> 聲聞等住無餘依涅槃界中，身智永盡如燈焰滅；是諸菩薩得成佛時，所證法身窮生死際無有斷盡。
> "Śrāvakas and the like, abiding in the realm of nirvāṇa without remainder, have body and wisdom completely extinguished — like a lamp going out. When bodhisattvas attain Buddhahood, the dharma body they realize extends to the furthest reaches of saṃsāra without ever being exhausted."[^37]

The śrāvaka's nirvāṇa is like a lamp going out — once gone, they do not return. The bodhisattva's dharma body extends through saṃsāra without end — not departing, yet not trapped.

The *Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra* states the bodhisattva's deeper reason for not entering nirvāṇa:

> 若彼菩薩住三昧分者，休息度脫一切眾生，斷如來種滅如來家……菩薩摩訶薩，以見寂靜三昧樂門，憶念本願大慈悲心度諸眾生……是故不即入於涅槃。
> "If that bodhisattva were to remain in samādhi only, ceasing to liberate all sentient beings, [it would] sever the Tathāgata's seed and extinguish the Tathāgata's family… The bodhisattva mahāsattva, even seeing the blissful gate of quiescent samādhi, remembers the original vow of great compassion to deliver all beings… Therefore does not immediately enter nirvāṇa."[^38]

If the bodhisattva entered nirvāṇa for their own sake alone, it would "sever the Tathāgata's seed and extinguish the Tathāgata's family." The bodhisattva remains in the world not out of attachment but out of "the original vow of great compassion."

## III. Core Argument

### 3.1 The Genesis of Terror: Why Letting Go Is Where Terror Begins

After "because there is nothing to attain," why is it not joy but terror?

The answer lies in the seventh manas consciousness.

The function of manas is perpetual deliberation (恆審思量) — it operates ceaselessly, perpetually taking the subjective aspect (darśana-bhāga) of the eighth ālaya-vijñāna as its object and grasping it as "self." S2-P03 analyzed how "going deep" means turning inward into the formations aggregate (saṃskāra-skandha), and manas is the deepest layer of formations — it is already "doing self" on your behalf before you know it.

As prajñā's illumination progressively advances — the five aggregates are empty (S2-P04), the eighteen dhātus are empty (S2-P07), the twelve links are empty (S2-P08), the Four Noble Truths are empty (S2-P09) — manas retreats step by step, but there is always something left to grasp: at least there is "emptiness" to understand, "practice" to do, "wisdom" to attain. When "because there is nothing to attain" reclaims the last straw, there is nothing left to hold.

What manas emits at this point is not cognitive doubt (that belongs to the sixth consciousness) but existential dread: if "I" am not real, then who is experiencing all this? If there is no "I" in charge, what remains safe?

What does this resemble? A parent who has been a full-time caregiver for twenty years — the child is leaving for college. Rationally, you know it is time to let go — you know the child has grown up, you know this is a good thing. But the moment your hands loosen, what rises in your body is not joy but deep-seated fear: without me there to protect them, will they be hurt? Without me worrying, do they know to put on a jacket when it gets cold?

This fear is not a deficit of love — it arises precisely because the commitment was too total. Manas grasps "I" not because it is malicious, but because it has been doing with utmost seriousness something that never needed doing. "Protecting you" never required a fixed "I" to execute, but manas does not know this — it believes that without it, everything will collapse.

✅ Doctrinally sound: terror is not a failure of practice but a sign that practice has reached the right depth. The Heart Sūtra places "no terror" after "mind without obstruction" and not before, precisely because terror is part of the traversal process, not an error to be avoided.

### 3.2 The Causal Chain of "Mind Without Obstruction": A Precise Psychological Map

Kuiji's five-path mapping expands the Heart Sūtra's four phrases into a timeline of practice:

First station: mind without obstruction — the stages of resolute understanding through the path of preparation. At this stage, the practitioner, through the power of hearing, thinking, and cultivating, "progressively suppresses the manifest activity of the two obstructions." Kuiji defines "obstruction" (罣) as kleśāvaraṇa and "hindrance" (礙) as jñeyāvaraṇa; "mind without obstruction" means both are suppressed.[^19] But note: this is "suppression" (伏), not "severance" (斷) — like pressing a stone onto water; when the pressure is present, it sinks; when the pressure slackens, it floats back up.

Second station: no terror — the path of seeing. The conceptually discriminated forms of both obstructions are severed here. Why does "no terror" come after "mind without obstruction"? Because both obstructions must first be suppressed (mind without obstruction) before prajñā-wisdom can manifest at the path of seeing; only after the path of seeing severs conceptual grasping does terror truly vanish.

Third station: far from inverted dream-thinking — the path of cultivation. After the path of seeing there is still the path of cultivation, because while conceptual grasping has been severed, innate grasping remains. The path of cultivation "broadly increases understanding and practice," progressively purifying the four inversions. In the ten-bhūmi mapping, the eighth bhūmi severs the flesh-level coarseness, the tenth bhūmi the bone-level — from coarse to subtle, layer by layer.

Fourth station: ultimate nirvāṇa — the path of no-more-learning, the stage of the Buddha. Both obstructions across all three levels of coarseness are fully severed; all four types of nirvāṇa are complete.

From the five-path perspective, this is a straight line. But from the ten-bhūmi perspective, one sees a striking temporal span: from the first bhūmi (mind without obstruction) to the eighth bhūmi (no terror) lies an interval of seven grounds. The traversal of terror is not a single dramatic moment but a long journey across the path of cultivation.

### 3.3 The Buddha's Life: Existential Proof of the Awakening Arc

The Buddha's life runs in precise parallel with the Heart Sūtra's awakening arc.

**Longing for freedom → Avalokiteśvara (S2-P02).** The four-gate excursion: the prince sees aging, sickness, death — "worldly attachment was extinguished" — sees the śramaṇa — "awoke with vast clarity."[^4] This is the arising of bodhicitta — not aversion to the world, but seeing suffering. The Buddha later recounts: "At the age of twenty-nine, I left home to seek the noble Way."[^39] The heart that longs for liberation is the causal ground of Avalokiteśvara.

**Turning inward → Going deep (S2-P03).** Releasing the throne — the greatest object of worldly "I"-attachment — renunciation and six years of asceticism. The asceticism was a trial-and-error process. The Buddha tried extreme austerities and discovered that extremes, too, are grasping — not grasping at pleasure, but grasping at "practice" itself. The discovery of the Middle Way was the first rehearsal of "because there is nothing to attain": even the tool of asceticism had to be released.

**Traversing terror → Mind without obstruction.** Under the Bodhi tree, the Buddha "seated on the vajra-seat, vowed: 'I shall not uncross these legs until I attain omniscience; without attaining omniscience, I shall never rise.'"[^8] Then Māra arrives. 1.8 billion troops, every form of terror.

Subduing Māra, within this paper's doctrinal framework, is the process of traversing manas's final counter-assault. Māra's ten armies — desire, worry, hunger, craving, sloth, terror, doubt, malice, profit-seeking, arrogance — are not ten external enemies but ten afflictions projected by manas through the stage of the sixth consciousness.[^9] The sixth army is precisely "terror." The Buddha's response: "remaining silent… I hold my mind without any fear."[^5] Not confrontation but stillness. Māra's army was not "defeated"; it dissipated of its own accord in the Buddha's immovability.

The earth-touching gesture (bhūmisparśa-mudrā) seals the traversal. The Buddha "extends his right hand and presses the earth"[^7] — not calling upon heaven for help, but asking the earth to bear witness. The earth deity rises and testifies. What the earth witnesses is not the Buddha's "power" but the fact that in the face of terror, he did not waver.

**Living out apratiṣṭhita-nirvāṇa → Ultimate nirvāṇa.** After enlightenment, the Buddha did not leave. He remained in the world for forty-five years, every day donning his robe, taking up his bowl, entering the city for alms, going from house to house in sequence, returning to his place.[^10] This daily narrative contains what Kuiji saw in "going in sequence": "from one lane to the next, from one household to the next, to demonstrate that in each and every dharma one realizes the principle of signlessness."[^11] Every step, every door — equality wisdom made manifest. No selection, no skipping; not begging an extra bowl from the wealthy, not passing over the poor.

Forty-five years of alms-rounds: the daily demonstration of "active yet always quiescent."[^26]

**The final release.** Before parinirvāṇa, the Buddha let go of the last thing — his disciples' dependence on him. "Be your own lamp; make the Dharma your lamp."[^13] Take the precepts as your teacher, not the Buddha as your teacher.[^15] Then the triple silence, "all formations are impermanent — this is the dharma of arising and ceasing; when arising and ceasing cease, quiescent cessation is bliss."[^16] "This is my final teaching."[^17]

The complete arc: longing → releasing → turning inward → traversing terror → enlightenment → forty-five years of embodied living → finally releasing even himself. One passage of sūtra; one lifetime of demonstration.

### 3.4 Ultimate Nirvāṇa: Not Departure but Freedom-Within

"Ultimate nirvāṇa" in the context of this paper is not the śrāvaka's nirvāṇa with or without remainder but the bodhisattva's apratiṣṭhita-nirvāṇa.

The difference between śrāvaka nirvāṇa and bodhisattva nirvāṇa originates in the direction of "abiding." The śrāvaka's direction is departure — "turning entirely away from saṃsāra toward nirvāṇa."[^24] The bodhisattva's direction is non-abiding — "turning away from neither saṃsāra nor nirvāṇa."[^24] Practitioners of the Two Vehicles believe nirvāṇa is "over there," reachable only by leaving "here." The bodhisattva, through non-discriminating wisdom, sees that the very distinction between "here" and "there" is the operation of the cognitive obstruction.

The principle in the *Mahāyānasaṃgrahabhāṣya* is supremely clear: "If one discriminates saṃsāra, one abides in saṃsāra; if one discriminates nirvāṇa, one abides in nirvāṇa."[^25] To discriminate is to abide. Not to discriminate is not to abide. The bodhisattva's non-discriminating wisdom does not mean failing to recognize saṃsāra and nirvāṇa; it means not treating the two as endpoints requiring a choice of sides.

This is the dual-axis structure of "by prajñā one does not abide in saṃsāra; by karuṇā one does not abide in nirvāṇa."[^25] Prajñā is the axis of wisdom — seeing that saṃsāra has no self-nature, one is not trapped in saṃsāra. Karuṇā is the axis of compassionate vow — sentient beings are still suffering, so one does not enter nirvāṇa alone. Where the two axes intersect: apratiṣṭhita-nirvāṇa.

Asvabhāva uses a vivid metaphor in his *Mahāyānasaṃgrahabhāṣya*:

> 害彼勢力如彼呪蛇，雖不棄捨而無染故。
> "[The bodhisattva] neutralizes [affliction's] power like a charmed snake — although not discarding it, [the bodhisattva] remains undefiled."[^40]

A charmed snake — a snake upon which mantras have been recited. The snake is still there; its venom has been neutralized. The bodhisattva dwells in saṃsāra; the "snake" of afflictions is still present, but it is harmless. It is not that the snake is killed (that is the śrāvaka's path); it is that the snake remains yet cannot poison (this is the bodhisattva's path).

The *Vijñaptimātratāsiddhi* connects the establishment of apratiṣṭhita-nirvāṇa back to samatā-jñāna:

> 由施等不住涅槃，及由後三（精進禪定般若）不住生死，為無住處涅槃資糧。
> "Through giving and the other [first three pāramitās], one does not abide in nirvāṇa; through the last three (vīrya, dhyāna, prajñā), one does not abide in saṃsāra — these constitute the provisions for apratiṣṭhita-nirvāṇa."[^41]

Dāna, śīla, kṣānti — the practices of benefiting others prevent abiding in nirvāṇa (not seeking one's own escape alone). Vīrya, dhyāna, prajñā — the practices of self-cultivation prevent abiding in saṃsāra (not being bound by afflictions). The complete structure of the six pāramitās is the dual-axis provision for apratiṣṭhita-nirvāṇa.

Returning to the Buddha's life: forty-five years of teaching and alms-rounds are precisely the daily demonstration of these two axes. Entering the city each day for alms is the compassion axis (not abiding in nirvāṇa); returning to wash the feet, arrange the seat, and enter meditation is the prajñā axis (not abiding in saṃsāra).

### 3.5 Inverted Dream-Thinking: Traversing the Four Inversions

The "dream-thinking" (夢想) in "far from inverted dream-thinking" is not a compound of "dreams" and "thoughts" but a synonym for vikalpa (discriminative conceptualization). Kuiji makes this explicit: "Not having awakened to true wisdom, one perpetually dwells in a dream; therefore the Buddha speaks of the long night of saṃsāra."[^20] Ordinary beings are dreaming — not the dreams of sleep, but the dreams of waking life. Believing in an unchanging "I" (permanence-inversion), believing that what one grasps can bring happiness (pleasure-inversion), believing that body and mind are pure (purity-inversion), believing that the aggregate of the five skandhas constitutes a self (self-inversion).

The position of the four inversions in Yogācāra is as the core operating mechanism of parikalpita-svabhāva. The *Mahāyānasaṃgrahabhāṣya* directly defines inversion as the essence of parikalpita: "the characteristics that generate inversions — those objective appearances that produce false inversions."[^33] This is not saying the four inversions are "contained within" parikalpita — the four inversions ARE the way parikalpita operates. How do you take something of paratantra-svabhāva (dependent nature) and render it parikalpita (imagined)? Through inversion.

The two characters "far from" (遠離) also carry doctrinal weight. They do not mean "never having had" but freedom after having traversed. You are not someone who never dreamed of permanence, pleasure, purity, and self — you dreamed, you woke, you are free. If one had never dreamed at all, that would be absence of dream-experience. To have dreamed and then to awaken — that is "far from dream-thinking."

S2-P08 analyzed how the four foundations of mindfulness are the practice intervention for the twelve links of dependent origination. The antidote structure in the *Mahāprajñāpāramitopadeśa* echoes this: body-contemplation counters the purity-inversion, feeling-contemplation counters the pleasure-inversion, mind-contemplation counters the permanence-inversion, dharma-contemplation counters the self-inversion.[^35] The last method the Buddha taught before parinirvāṇa was also the four foundations of mindfulness.[^14] The tool for countering the inversions is the one the Buddha used throughout his life and the last thing he entrusted to his disciples.

### 3.6 The Awakening Loop Closes

From S2-P02 to S2-P10, the Heart Sūtra completes a full awakening narrative.

S2-P02: Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva — the causal ground, the moment of longing for freedom.
S2-P03: Going deep into prajñāpāramitā — the method, turning inward into the formations.
S2-P04 through S2-P09: The unfolding of prajñā's illumination — seeing the five aggregates as empty, deconstructing the eighteen dhātus, seeing the twelve links as empty, releasing the Four Noble Truths, releasing prajñā itself.
S2-P10: Mind without obstruction — the fruition, freedom after traversing terror.

The causal ground is "Avalokiteśvara"; the fruition is "mind without obstruction." Avalokiteśvara's "freedom" (自在) is the direction of longing; "without obstruction" (無罣礙) is the state of arrival. From longing to arrival, the entire prajñā contemplation of the Heart Sūtra lies between.

The Buddha's life parallels this arc with precision: the four-gate excursion (longing) → renunciation and asceticism (turning inward) → under the Bodhi tree (illumination) → subduing Māra (traversing terror) → enlightenment (mind without obstruction) → forty-five years of teaching and alms (living out apratiṣṭhita-nirvāṇa) → the final teaching before parinirvāṇa (releasing even the Buddha himself).

At a deeper level, S1-P01 (The Butterfly Effect of Initial Aspiration) through S2-P10 (Mind Without Obstruction) also forms a grand cross-series closure: the initial aspiration is the very first thought of the causal ground; mind without obstruction is the ultimate fruition of that thought. One thought of bodhicitta, one lifetime's path of awakening. A butterfly beats its wings, and in a distant place, a storm arises — but that storm is not destruction; it is freedom.

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Dialogue

### 4.1 Heidegger's Angst — Structurally Similar, Different in Depth

Martin Heidegger proposed the concept of Angst (existential anxiety) in *Being and Time*. Angst is not fear of specific objects — you are not afraid of snakes, exams, or unemployment. Angst is the unease that arises when facing "existence itself": Why am I here? What is the meaning of all this? If everything will eventually pass, is what I am doing still worthwhile?[^42]

The hallmark of Angst is that it has no definite object. You cannot articulate what you fear, yet you are unmistakably afraid. Heidegger argues that most people flee this anxiety through "everyday busyness" — immersing themselves in work, socializing, entertainment, leaving no time to face the "why."

⚠️ Heuristic analogy: Structurally, Heidegger's Angst resembles what this paper describes as the terror of the manas consciousness — both are deep-seated unease when the foundation of selfhood is shaken, neither is directed at a specific object, and both can be temporarily evaded through busyness.

But the two differ in depth. Angst operates primarily at the level of the sixth consciousness (mano-vijñāna, conceptual thinking) — it is the response after "thinking about" the meaninglessness of existence. Manas's terror operates at the level of the seventh consciousness — it is the response not to "thinking about" selflessness but to "being seen through" as selfless. The former is your emotional reaction to a proposition; the latter is the deconstruction of your very identity.

This difference in depth means: Heideggerian Angst can be addressed through philosophical reflection, artistic creation, or the existentialist response of "courageously facing" finitude. Manas's terror cannot — because the very subject doing the "courageous facing" is itself what is being deconstructed. You cannot use "I" to solve the problem of "I do not exist."

The Yogācāra path is transformation, not confrontation: not annihilating manas but transforming manas from "grasping at self" into "samatā-jñāna" — from clinging to everything to treating everything equally. Terror is not "overcome"; it dissolves of its own accord in the process of transformation.

S2-P09 employed Korzybski's "the map is not the territory" as a cross-disciplinary analogy. P10 turns to Heidegger's existential Angst, forming a continuity within the Western philosophical tradition — from philosophy of language to existential philosophy. Both address "the human mind's response to encountering fundamental truths," but Buddhist doctrine reaches greater depth in both analysis and resolution.

## V. Middle Way Corrections

### 5.1 Terror Does Not Mean Practice Has Gone Wrong

❌ Common misunderstanding: Practice should make one increasingly stable and happy. If terror arises during practice, something must be wrong.

Middle Way view: The Heart Sūtra's own causal sequence negates this assumption. "No terror" follows "mind without obstruction," indicating that terror is part of the traversal process, not a signal of deviation from the correct path. If you have never felt unsettled during practice, it may not be because you have already transcended terror, but because prajñā's illumination has not yet penetrated to the level of the manas consciousness. Of course, this does not mean all forms of terror during practice are signs of "reaching the right depth" — terror may also stem from physical or mental imbalance, environmental stress, or inappropriate methods. The criterion is: does this terror accompany deeper awareness? If there is only terror without awareness, guidance from a spiritual teacher may be needed.

### 5.2 Subduing Māra Is Not "Defeating" a Demon King

❌ Common misunderstanding: Subduing Māra is the Buddha using supernatural power to defeat an external enemy — a narrative akin to a hero slaying a dragon.

Middle Way view: The sūtra describes the Buddha's response to Māra's army as "remaining silent" and "not moving."[^5] Māra was not "defeated" but dissipated of its own accord in the Buddha's stillness. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitopadeśa* explicitly identifies Māra's ten armies as ten inner afflictions — desire, worry, hunger, craving, sloth, terror, doubt, malice, profit-seeking, arrogance.[^9] This is a psychological event, not a military one. The earth-touching gesture is not a summoning of the earth's power — it is asking the earth to bear witness to the fact that the Buddha did not waver in the face of terror. The sūtras do include descriptions of supernatural powers (such as "transforming Māra's weapons into garlands"), but these belong to the narrative tradition of Buddha-biography literature and should not be used to establish a doctrinal claim that "the Buddha possessed supernatural combat abilities."

### 5.3 Existential Angst Does Not Equal Buddhist Terror

❌ Common misunderstanding: Heidegger's existential Angst and Buddhist terror are describing the same thing — the two traditions are pointing at the same phenomenon.

Middle Way view: The two share structural similarities (both are unease when the foundation of existence is shaken), but differ in depth. As analyzed in §IV, Angst operates primarily at the level of the sixth consciousness's conceptual thinking ("thinking about" the meaninglessness of existence), while manas's terror operates at the seventh consciousness's level of self-identity ("being seen through" as selfless). The more fundamental difference lies in the way out: Heidegger offers "authenticity" — facing anxiety, accepting finitude, making resolute decisions. Buddhism offers āśraya-parāvṛtti (transformation of the basis) — manas is not accepted or overcome but transformed into samatā-jñāna. The former still responds within the framework of "I"; the latter transcends the framework of "I" itself. Equating the two would underestimate the depth of Buddhist practice and distort the intent of Heidegger's thought.

### 5.4 Apratiṣṭhita-Nirvāṇa Does Not Equal Worldly Busyness

❌ Common misunderstanding: The bodhisattva's apratiṣṭhita-nirvāṇa simply means "not leaving the world, continuing to do things" — equivalent to an engaged, worldly attitude toward life.

Middle Way view: The four characters "active yet always quiescent" (用而常寂) are key.[^26] "Active" (用) is benefiting sentient beings in the world, but "quiescent" (寂) is the substance. It is not "too busy to remember stillness" but "stillness inherent in the activity itself." A merely busy person and a bodhisattva abiding in apratiṣṭhita-nirvāṇa may look identical on the surface — both are active in the world. The difference: the former is led by the objects of activity; the latter, within activity, has tathatā as essential nature, quiescent and unmoving. Apratiṣṭhita-nirvāṇa is not a lifestyle choice but the natural state after both obstructions are removed and āśraya-parāvṛtti is complete. Reducing it to "an engaged spirit" loses its doctrinal depth.

### 5.5 "Self-Reliance" Does Not Mean "No Need for a Spiritual Teacher"

❌ Common misunderstanding: The Buddha said "be your own lamp, take refuge in the Dharma," so practice needs no teacher — reading the sūtras on one's own is sufficient.

Middle Way view: The timing of the Buddha's statement is critical — it was spoken before parinirvāṇa, to disciples who had already studied with him for up to forty-five years and already possessed a complete foundation of hearing, thinking, and cultivating. The Buddha's "letting go" was because the Dharma had been fully transmitted, not because the Dharma does not need transmitting. In the same teaching, the Buddha also said "take the precepts as your teacher"[^15] — precepts are the concrete form of the Dharma, and the transmission of the Dharma itself presupposes a teacher-student relationship. "Self-reliance" means: after having established a complete foundation in the Dharma through a teacher, one ultimately relies on one's own awareness and practice. It does not negate the necessity of spiritual teachers (kalyāṇa-mitra) in the early and middle stages of practice.

## VI. Conclusion

### 6.1 Core Argument in Review

This paper's core thesis may be distilled to a single sentence: the Heart Sūtra's awakening arc is not a straight line from suffering to happiness but a curve from longing through terror to freedom.

"Because there is nothing to attain" triggers terror because the manas consciousness's perpetually deliberating "I" faces an existential crisis of deconstruction. The mechanism for traversing terror is not annihilating the "I" but transforming it — manas → samatā-jñāna. "Mind without obstruction" is the state where both obstructions are removed; "ultimate nirvāṇa" is apratiṣṭhita-nirvāṇa — not abiding in saṃsāra, not abiding in nirvāṇa, "by prajñā not abiding in saṃsāra, by karuṇā not abiding in nirvāṇa," "active yet always quiescent."

Kuiji's five-path × ten-bhūmi dual mapping reveals that the Heart Sūtra's four phrases (mind without obstruction → no terror → far from inverted dream-thinking → ultimate nirvāṇa) constitute the complete timeline of the bodhisattva's practice from the path of preparation to the stage of the Buddha. The four inversions are the core operating mechanism of parikalpita-svabhāva; "far from" does not mean "never having had" but freedom after traversal.

### 6.2 The Buddha's Existential Proof

The Buddha's life is the most complete existential proof of "because there is nothing to attain." He did not teach letting go with words; he demonstrated every step of letting go with his body: releasing the throne (the greatest object of worldly "I"-attachment), releasing asceticism (releasing even the tool of practice), traversing the terror under the Bodhi tree (subduing Māra), then living for forty-five years with the bearing of samatā-jñāna in the world (the daily alms-round = the everyday demonstration of nothing-to-attain), and finally releasing even his disciples' dependence on him ("be your own lamp; make the Dharma your lamp").

Sūtras can articulate doctrine; commentaries can analyze structure; but only a person's entire life can prove that this path can actually be walked. The Buddha is that proof.

### 6.3 The Awakening Loop Closes, and What Lies Ahead

S2-P02 (Avalokiteśvara = causal ground) → S2-P10 (mind without obstruction = fruition): the Heart Sūtra's awakening loop closes here. From cause to fruit, from longing to freedom, the complete arc is drawn.

But the Heart Sūtra is not yet finished. "All Buddhas of the three times, relying on prajñāpāramitā, attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi" — not just one bodhisattva's awakening, but the shared path of all Buddhas across three times. Then "therefore know that prajñāpāramitā is the great transcendent mantra, the great luminous mantra, the unsurpassed mantra, the unequaled mantra" — the shift from exoteric doctrinal contemplation to esoteric dhāraṇī. Finally, "gate gate pāragate pārasaṃgate bodhi svāhā" — gone, gone, gone beyond, gone altogether beyond — O what an awakening!

The Heart Sūtra's final passage no longer asks "how does one person awaken" but "how does awakening become the path for all." That is the task of S2-P11 — three kinds of practitioners, one Heart Sūtra, the unity of exoteric and esoteric.
