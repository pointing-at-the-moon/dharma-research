## Abstract

Section 5 of the Diamond Sūtra declares: "Whatever has marks is illusory; if you see that all marks are no-marks, then you see the Tathāgata" (凡所有相，皆是虛妄；若見諸相非相，則見如來). This is the sūtra's first explicit identification of what its jífēi structure cuts — "marks" (xiāng 相). This paper argues that xiāng in the Diamond Sūtra is not the everyday sense of "appearance" but a doctrinal concept that unfolds across a complete semantic spectrum — from the four marks (self, person, being, life-span) through dharma-marks and the thirty-two marks of a buddha's body, to the self-negating proposition "the real mark is precisely no-mark" (是實相者，則是非相). Through the Yogācāra concept of nimittabhāga (the object-aspect of consciousness) and parikalpita-svabhāva (the imagined nature), this paper proposes a precise doctrinal positioning: the object-aspects projected by consciousness are paratantra (dependently arisen), but the superimposition of "real existence" onto them — parikalpita — is the root of "illusory." "Illusory" does not negate the existence of object-aspects; it negates only the reification layered upon them. Kuiji's gloss — mind (niàn 念) is the support, marks the supported; mind itself has no self-nature, let alone the marks it projects — maps precisely onto the Cheng weishi lun's formula "emotionally existent, rationally non-existent" (情有理無 qíngyǒu lǐwú). The Yogācārabhūmi further identifies the mechanism of bondage: the unawakened are bound by language within marks, grasping self-nature as language describes it. The Diamond Sūtra's "first cut" severs not marks themselves but the invisible rope between you and marks — the reification of parikalpita. The Middle Way Correction section flags: "illusory" does not mean "non-existent"; negating parikalpita is not negating paratantra. The Śūraṅgama Sūtra's "the five aggregates are illusory, fundamentally neither caused nor uncaused" offers a deeper transcendence — even the framework of "dependent arising" itself must be illuminated.

**Keywords:** Diamond Sūtra, whatever has marks is illusory, lakṣaṇa/nimitta, parikalpita-svabhāva, nimittabhāga, paratantra-svabhāva, trisvabhāva, bondage by language, four-aspect doctrine | 金剛經、凡所有相皆是虛妄、相、遍計所執性、相分、依他起性、唯識三性、名言所縛、四分說

## I. Introduction

### 1.1 The Question

S5-P01 established the doctrinal framework: the jífēi formula maps onto the Yogācāra three natures (trisvabhāva). S5-P02 established the methodological framework: the Diamond Sūtra is a kōan mirror that reflects the reader rather than transmitting knowledge.

But what exactly does the jífēi structure "jífēi"?

Section 5 gives the sūtra's first explicit answer:

> "Whatever has marks is illusory. If you see that all marks are no-marks, then you see the Tathāgata."[^1]
>
> 「凡所有相，皆是虛妄；若見諸相非相，則見如來。」

X is "marks" (xiāng 相).

The sentence appears simple — "all appearances are false" — but this "simple understanding" is itself a fresh product of parikalpita. If you think you have grasped "whatever has marks is illusory," you have merely pasted a new label — "marks = false" — onto the situation. The Diamond Sūtra's xiāng is not the everyday word for "looks" or "appearance." It is a doctrinal concept that unfolds across a complete semantic spectrum throughout the sūtra, connected to the most precise cognitive analysis in the Yogācāra system.

The questions this paper addresses: What precisely does the Diamond Sūtra mean by "marks"? What precisely does "illusory" negate?

### 1.2 Relation to the Previous Two Papers

S5-P01 is the map. S5-P02 is the mirror. This paper is the first cut.

P01 established the trisvabhāva mapping framework — you now know how the jífēi structure operates. P02 told you the map is not the road and the framework is a mirror for seeing yourself. Now P03 applies this framework to its first concrete object: §5 declares that what the jífēi structure cuts is "marks," and this paper's task is to use the surgical instrument of trisvabhāva to dissect precisely what is cut, where the cut falls, and what remains afterward.

S5-P07 reveals the Diamond Sūtra's dual-layer structure pivoting at §17: the first half (§1–16) addresses person-emptiness, deconstructs the four "marks," and counters afflictive obstruction (kleśāvaraṇa); the second half (§17–32) addresses dharma-emptiness, deconstructs the four "views," and counters cognitive obstruction (jñeyāvaraṇa). Section 5's "whatever has marks is illusory" belongs to the first half, cutting at the level of the four marks and afflictive obstruction. But the three characters "whatever has" already intimate the second half's theme — "whatever has" includes the marks of the Buddha's own teaching. This paper analyzes the first half's first cut, but the blade already points toward the second half.

This is also the first direct encounter between S5 and S3. S3 used the Treatise on the Hundred Dharmas (百法明門論 Bǎifǎ míngmén lùn) to build a meticulous classification of dharmic phenomena — mind-dharmas, mental factors, form-dharmas, dharmas not associated with mind, unconditioned dharmas — one hundred dharmas in orderly arrangement. Now the Diamond Sūtra declares "whatever has marks is illusory." Are the "marks" of those one hundred dharmas also illusory? The answer is yes, but precision is needed: what is illusory is not the classification itself (that is paratantra), but your assumption that the hundred dharmas are "really existing things" (that is parikalpita).

## II. Scriptural Foundations

### 2.1 The §5 Passage and a Textual Note

The full context of Diamond Sūtra §5[^1]:

> "Subhūti, what do you think — can the Tathāgata be seen by means of bodily marks?" "No, World-Honored One. The Tathāgata cannot be seen by means of bodily marks. Why? What the Tathāgata calls bodily marks are precisely not bodily marks." The Buddha told Subhūti: "Whatever has marks is illusory. If you see that all marks are no-marks, then you see the Tathāgata."
>
> 「須菩提！於意云何？可以身相見如來不？」「不也，世尊！不可以身相得見如來。何以故？如來所說身相，即非身相。」佛告須菩提：「凡所有相，皆是虛妄；若見諸相非相，則見如來。」

Subhūti's answer first demonstrates the jífēi structure — "what the Tathāgata calls bodily marks are precisely not bodily marks" — and then the Buddha pushes it to a universal proposition: not only the Buddha's bodily marks but whatever has marks is illusory.

A textual note: Kumārajīva's translation reads "if you see that all marks are no-marks, **zé** (則) you see the Tathāgata," not "**jí** (即) you see the Tathāgata."[^2] Zé is a conditional connective (if…then), preserving causal openness; jí carries a sense of "immediately" or "right then," implying an instantaneous realization. The difference touches the gradual-versus-sudden debate: zé allows for a process — you first see that marks are no-marks, then you can see the Tathāgata; jí compresses the process into simultaneity — the moment of seeing no-marks is the moment of seeing the Tathāgata. Kumārajīva's choice of zé preserves the broader interpretive space.

### 2.2 The Semantic Spectrum of "Marks" in the Diamond Sūtra

Xiāng (相) in the Diamond Sūtra is not a word with a single fixed meaning. It unfolds across at least five semantic layers[^3]:

**Layer One: The Four Marks — self-mark, person-mark, being-mark, life-span-mark.** This is xiāng's first appearance in the sūtra. §3: "If a bodhisattva has a self-mark, person-mark, being-mark, or life-span-mark, then they are not a bodhisattva" (若菩薩有我相、人相、眾生相、壽者相，即非菩薩). The four marks constitute the most basic form of self-grasping (ātma-grāha): the assumption of a permanent, unchanging "I" (self-mark), an "other" distinct from "I" (person-mark), an individual limited by a single lifetime (being-mark), and a temporal axis from birth to death (life-span-mark). The core of the four marks is "I" — the other three are extensions of it.

**Layer Two: Dharma-marks and non-dharma-marks.** §6: "one should not grasp dharmas, nor should one grasp non-dharmas" (不應取法、不應取非法); §9: "nor is there a non-dharma-mark" (亦無非法相). The sūtra advances from self-grasping to dharma-grasping. Not only "I" is a mark — "dharma" too is a mark. More subtly still: even "non-dharma" — the concept you use to negate dharma — is also a mark. Grasping dharma-marks is attachment; grasping non-dharma-marks (believing "no-dharma" is correct) is equally attachment.

**Layer Three: Bodily marks and the thirty-two marks.** §5: "Can the Tathāgata be seen by means of bodily marks?" → §13: "Can the Tathāgata be seen by means of the thirty-two marks?" → §20: "Can the Buddha be seen by means of a perfect physical body?" and "Can the Tathāgata be seen by means of complete marks?" → §26: "Can the Tathāgata be perceived by means of the thirty-two marks?" The same question — whether the Buddha can be recognized by external bodily features — is asked at least four times, each time with a more precise object. Each repetition is the same mirror at a different angle.

**Layer Four: No-marks.** §5: "If you see that all marks are no-marks, then you see the Tathāgata." §17: "Self-marks are precisely no-marks; person-marks, being-marks, and life-span-marks are precisely no-marks" (我相即是非相，人相、眾生相、壽者相即是非相). "No-marks" does not mean "the absence of marks." It is seeing marks as they actually are: marks were never the solid entities you took them to be. No-marks is the jífēi structure made verb — it is the result of "seeing," not a new object.

**Layer Five: Real-mark.** §14: "Departing from all marks, this is called 'all buddhas'" (離一切諸相，則名諸佛), and most crucially: "What is called 'real-mark' is precisely no-mark; therefore the Tathāgata calls it 'real-mark'" (是實相者，則是非相，是故如來說名實相). Real-mark — the truest of marks — is itself no-mark. This is the semantic spectrum's ultimate self-negation: you may think some "most real mark" awaits you, and the Diamond Sūtra tells you that the most real mark is precisely the seeing that all marks are no-marks.

The five layers are not parallel but progressive. The four marks cut self-grasping; dharma-marks cut dharma-grasping; bodily marks cut attachment to the Buddha's physical form; no-marks cut attachment to "marklessness"; real-mark cuts attachment to "an ultimate truth." Each layer is subtler than the last, each harder to detect — because the "understanding" you gained from the previous cut becomes the new attachment at the next level.

### 2.3 §13, §20, §26: Three Denials of the Thirty-Two Marks and Subhūti's Wrong Answer

The question of recognizing the Tathāgata by external marks is asked at least four times, three involving the thirty-two marks:

§13[^4]:

> "Subhūti, what do you think — can the Tathāgata be seen by means of the thirty-two marks?" "No, World-Honored One. … Why? What the Tathāgata calls the thirty-two marks are precisely no-marks; this is called the thirty-two marks."
>
> 「如來說三十二相，即是非相，是名三十二相。」

§20[^4]:

> "Can the Buddha be seen by means of a perfect physical body?" "No, World-Honored One. … What the Tathāgata calls a perfect physical body is precisely not a perfect physical body; this is called a perfect physical body." "Can the Tathāgata be seen by means of complete marks?" "No, World-Honored One. … What the Tathāgata calls completeness of marks is precisely not completeness; this is called completeness of marks."
>
> 「如來說具足色身，即非具足色身，是名具足色身。」「如來說諸相具足，即非具足，是名諸相具足。」

§26[^4]:

> "Subhūti, what do you think — can the Tathāgata be perceived by means of the thirty-two marks?" Subhūti said: "Yes, yes! By the thirty-two marks one perceives the Tathāgata." The Buddha said: "Subhūti! If the Tathāgata could be perceived by the thirty-two marks, then a wheel-turning sage-king would be a Tathāgata."
>
> 須菩提言：「如是，如是！以三十二相觀如來。」佛言：「須菩提！若以三十二相觀如來者，轉輪聖王則是如來。」

Section 26 is the only passage in the entire sūtra where Subhūti gives a wrong answer. He answered correctly at §5 ("the Tathāgata cannot be seen by means of bodily marks"), correctly again at §13 ("the Tathāgata cannot be seen by means of the thirty-two marks"), but at §26 he suddenly says "Yes, yes! By the thirty-two marks one perceives the Tathāgata."

This is among the sūtra's most exquisite pedagogical designs. Subhūti has not become less intelligent — he has been caught by the mirror. His correct answers at §5 and §13 may have been merely conceptual understanding — he knows "one should not see the Tathāgata by means of marks." His "wrong answer" at §26 reveals a deeper problem: knowing conceptually that "one should not see by means of marks" and actually not seeing by means of marks are separated by the distance between knowledge and realization.

From the Yogācāra perspective, Subhūti's sixth consciousness (manovijñāna) had established the correct concept — "do not see the Tathāgata by means of marks." But his manas (the seventh consciousness) was still habitually operating in the grasper/grasped (grāhaka/grāhya) pattern — when the same question appeared in a slightly different form, conceptual right-view could not intercept the manas's habitual response in time[^11]. This is the subtlety of parikalpita: it is not merely a conceptual error (correctable by a correct concept) but a cognitive habit (transformable only through sustained practice).

The Buddha's correction is also noteworthy: he does not say "you are wrong" but "if the Tathāgata could be perceived by the thirty-two marks, then a wheel-turning sage-king would be a Tathāgata." This is a reductio ad absurdum — if your logic holds, then a wheel-turning king is a buddha. Not a direct denial but an invitation to see the absurdity of your own reasoning. The mirror does not say "your face is dirty"; the mirror simply reflects — you see for yourself.

Then the Buddha speaks a verse: "If you see me by form, or seek me by sound, this person walks a mistaken path and cannot see the Tathāgata" (若以色見我，以音聲求我，是人行邪道，不能見如來) — extending the denial from physical body to all sense-objects (form, sound), from "seeing" to "seeking," a total negation of perceiving the Tathāgata by means of marks.

### 2.4 The Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra: The Position of "Marks" in the Three-Nature Theory

The Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra (解深密經 Jiě shēnmì jīng), Chapter on the Characteristics of All Dharmas, provides the doctrinal positioning of "marks" within the three natures[^5]:

> "What is the parikalpita-lakṣaṇa (imagined characteristic) of all dharmas? It is the self-nature and distinctions established through nominal designation of all dharmas, up to and including that which gives rise to verbal expression."
>
> 「云何諸法遍計所執相？謂一切法名假安立自性差別，乃至為令隨起言說。」

The core definition of the parikalpita "mark" is "nominally designated" (名假安立 míng jiǎ ānlì) — self-nature and distinctions posited through naming. "Marks" are not things the external world imposes on you. They are the products of your cognitive activity — naming (名 míng), distinguishing (差別 chābié), fixing (安立 ānlì). You say "this is a flower"; you have already completed one round of parikalpita: onto a dependently arisen convergence of conditions, you have designated a name ("flower") and then assumed that this name refers to a truly existing self-nature ("flower-essence").

The Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra uses the "floater in the eye" (眩瞖 xuànyì) analogy to illustrate the relationship among the three natures[^5]:

The parikalpita characteristic is like "the floaters seen by a person with eye disease" — spots and lines that are not part of the external world but manufactured by the diseased eye. Your parikalpita is a cognitive eye disease — it "sees" on the dependently arisen world things that simply do not exist there.

The paratantra characteristic is like "the manifold appearances seen by the person with eye disease" — you are indeed seeing (cognitive activity is genuinely occurring), you indeed see things (object-aspects are paratantra), but what you see is not what you think it is (you mistake the floaters for actual insects).

The pariniṣpanna characteristic is like "the clean-eyed person free from the eye disease" — the disease is cured, the world you see has not changed, but the floaters have vanished. Pariniṣpanna is not seeing a new world but seeing the same world no longer distorted by your cognitive disease.

### 2.5 The Cheng weishi lun: Object-Aspects, Seeing-Aspects, and the Precise Mechanism of Parikalpita

The Cheng weishi lun (成唯識論 Chéng wéishì lùn, Treatise on the Establishment of Consciousness-Only) provides the technical definition of "marks"[^6]:

> "What resembles the object of cognition is called the object-aspect (nimittabhāga 相分); what resembles the act of cognition is called the seeing-aspect (darśanabhāga 見分)."
>
> 「似所緣相說名相分，似能緣相說名見分。」

The object-aspect is what consciousness projects "as if" it were the object being cognized — it "appears to be" what you are perceiving, but in truth it is consciousness's own projection. The seeing-aspect is consciousness's function of "appearing to be" the cognizing subject.

The four-aspect doctrine (四分說 sìfēn shuō) further elaborates this structure[^7]: the object-aspect is the object of cognition (所緣 suǒyuán), the seeing-aspect is the cognitive act (行相 xíngxiāng), the self-witnessing aspect (自證分 zìzhèng fēn) is consciousness's self-awareness, and the re-witnessing aspect (證自證分 zhèngzìzhèng fēn) prevents infinite regress.

This four-aspect structure is critical for understanding "whatever has marks." In daily life, you believe you are looking at "the external world" — in fact, what you see is the object-aspect projected by your own consciousness. The object-aspect is not a copy of the external world but something consciousness itself projects based on conditions. You assume there is a "table" out there independent of you — Yogācāra tells you that the "table" you cognize is the table-object-aspect projected by your consciousness.

However, the object-aspect itself is paratantra — it arises dependently on conditions and is not nothingness. The problem lies in the next step: you reify the object-aspect — you believe that "table-object-aspect" is an independently existing, permanently stable table — and this "believing" is parikalpita, the "illusory" of the Diamond Sūtra.

The Cheng weishi lun states this relationship with precision[^8]:

> "And [consciousness] seems to produce two [aspects]: the seeing-aspect and the object-aspect, which are the grasper and the grasped. These two aspects are emotionally existent but rationally non-existent (情有理無 qíngyǒu lǐwú); this characteristic is called parikalpita. The underlying support of these two, being genuinely dependent on conditions, is not non-existent — this nature is called paratantra."
>
> 「而似二生：謂見、相分，即能、所取。如是二分，情有理無，此相說為遍計所執。二所依體，實託緣生，此性非無，名依他起。」

"Emotionally existent, rationally non-existent" — these four characters are the key to understanding "whatever has marks is illusory." "Emotionally existent" (情有 qíngyǒu): from the affective cognition of ordinary beings, the object-aspect and seeing-aspect genuinely exist — you truly "see" and "feel." "Rationally non-existent" (理無 lǐwú): when investigated with ultimate reason, their status as "truly existing grasper and grasped" does not hold. The defining feature of parikalpita is precisely qíngyǒu lǐwú: you feel it is there, but when examined with reason, that "being there" is your own cognitive projection.

The Cheng weishi lun summarizes with the sky-flower analogy[^8]:

> "Like flowers in the sky and so forth, whose nature and marks are entirely non-existent — all such are called parikalpita."
>
> 「如空花等，性、相都無，一切皆名遍計所執。」

Parikalpita is like flowers seen in the sky — there is no nature (essence) and no mark (manifestation). You see flowers in the sky not because the sky has produced flowers but because your eyes are diseased. The Diamond Sūtra's "whatever has marks is illusory" cuts precisely these sky-flowers — not denying the sky (paratantra) but curing your eye disease (parikalpita).

### 2.6 Kuiji's Commentary: Mind Is the Support, Marks the Supported

Kuiji (窺基), in his Commentary on the Diamond Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra (金剛般若波羅蜜經讚述 Jīngāng bōrě bōluómì jīng zànshù, T1702), offers a precise exegesis of "whatever has marks is illusory"[^9]:

> "Ordinary refers to the six destinies of sentient beings; noble refers to sages of the three vehicles; dependent on their purity or defilement, they are precisely the ordinary and noble. Though dharmas are many, they do not exceed these four; though only four categories are raised, they encompass everything."
>
> 「凡即六道眾生，聖即三乘賢聖，依有淨穢、正即凡聖。諸法雖多不出此四，雖舉四法該一切也。」

Kuiji first confirms the scope of "whatever has marks" — it includes not only the marks of the six-destiny beings but also the marks of sages; not only the defiled but also the pure. "Whatever has" means without remainder. "Lest people hear 'bodily marks are not marks' and think this applies only to the Buddha's body" (恐人聞說身相非相，將謂唯獨佛身), the Buddha specifically added "whatever has" to "block narrow views" (遮局見) — to prevent the reader from limiting "illusory" to only certain categories of marks.

Then Kuiji delivers his most incisive analysis[^9]:

> "Though the defiled and pure, superior and inferior differ among ordinary and noble beings, all arise from thought (niàn 念) and none are not illusory. Thought has no self-characteristic and does not depart from the nature of awareness. If thought itself has no nature, how much less can the marks it manifests be real? Thought is the support; marks are the supported. If the support is itself empty, how can the supported exist? It is like skin that no longer remains — where will hair attach?"
>
> 「凡聖染淨勝劣雖殊，皆從念生無不虛妄，念無自相不離覺性，念尚無性況所現相而實有耶？以念是所依、相是能依，所依尚虛，能依何有？其猶皮既不存，毛將安附。」

This passage can be unpacked in three logical layers:

First: all marks arise from "thought" (念 niàn). Whether the defiled marks seen by ordinary beings or the pure marks perceived by sages, their arising depends on the activity of mind. Without thought, no marks.

Second: thought itself has no self-characteristic. Thought is not an independently existing entity — it "does not depart from the nature of awareness" (不離覺性), arising dependent on awareness, having no fixed, unchanging self-nature.

Third: thought is the support (所依 suǒyī); marks are the supported (能依 néngyī). Marks depend on thought for their existence, as hair depends on skin. If the support — thought itself — already has no self-nature ("the support is itself empty"), then the marks that depend on thought for their existence cannot possibly be real. "If skin no longer remains, where will hair attach?"

Through this support/supported analysis, Kuiji positions the meaning of "illusory" with precision: illusory does not mean that marks "do not exist" (marks genuinely arise from thought), but that marks "are not truly real" (because even what they depend on — thought — is not truly real). This corresponds exactly to the Cheng weishi lun's qíngyǒu lǐwú: from the standpoint of qíng (affective cognitive activity), marks are indeed there; from the standpoint of lǐ (investigating the self-nature of thought), marks cannot be truly real.

Kuiji further quotes the monk Sēngzhào (僧肇) to guard against the reader's leaping from "marks are illusory" to "marklessness is true"[^9]:

> "Sēngzhào said: 'I fear that when people hear "see all marks as no-marks and you see the Tathāgata," they will hope for a markless Buddha standing clearly before their eyes. If so, how does that differ from those very marks?'"
>
> 「肇云：恐人聞諸相非相即見如來，便希無相之佛昭然目前，若如是者，何殊彼相。」

"Hope for a markless Buddha standing clearly before their eyes" — upon hearing "all marks are no-marks," you begin to expect a "markless Buddha" to appear distinctly before you. But if you treat "marklessness" as a new object to pursue, that "marklessness" has become just another mark — you have simply exchanged attachment to "having marks" for attachment to "having no marks." Sēngzhào's insight precisely anticipates the fourth-layer predicament of the spectrum: "no-marks," if made into a new target, ceases to be no-marks.

### 2.7 The Yogācārabhūmi: Bondage by Language

The Yogācārabhūmi (瑜伽師地論 Yújiā shīdì lùn), fascicle 73, provides a precise analysis of the binding relationship between "marks" and "language" (名言 míngyán)[^10]:

> "All the unawakened are bound by language within marks. Know that as name, as speech, they falsely grasp self-nature regarding the referent."
>
> 「一切愚夫於諸相中名言所縛故，當知如名、如言，於所詮事妄執自性。」

"Bound by language" (名言所縛 míngyán suǒfù) — three characters that precisely describe the generative mechanism of parikalpita: you are not directly bound by "marks"; you are bound by "language." You see a flower; your bondage does not occur at the moment of seeing (that is paratantra cognitive activity) but at the moment you name it "this is a flower" — "language" fixes a flowing convergence of conditions into a "thing" with a name, and then you assume this name refers to a real self-nature.

The Yogācārabhūmi quotes a verse of the World-Honored One[^10]:

> "The deluded, thinking ordinary beings / are bound by speech within marks. / The sage, freed from the bonds of speech, / is at ease within marks. / The practitioner of pure seeing / abides in true wisdom; / finding nothing in self-nature, / does not see what it depends upon."
>
> 「愚昧思凡夫，於相為言縛；牟尼脫言縛，於相得自在。清淨見行者，安住於真智，於自性無得，不見彼所依。」

"Bound by speech within marks" — ordinary beings are bound by language within marks. "Freed from the bonds of speech, at ease within marks" — the sage has not departed from marks (marks are still there); the sage has departed from the bondage of language and is therefore at ease within marks.

This corresponds precisely to the function of "is called X" (是名 X) in the Diamond Sūtra: you still use the name "adorning buddha-lands," but you are no longer bound by it. The names are still there, the marks are still there, you are at ease within them — because you have seen through the fact that names do not refer to truly existing self-natures.

The Yogācārabhūmi further analyzes how language constructs marks in a way that does not correspond to their actual nature[^10]:

> "If it were held that the self-nature of marks is established, and that language is posited in exact correspondence with them … since the language posited for a single mark is manifold and varied, there should be manifold and varied essential natures. Therefore, language posited dependent on marks does not hold."
>
> 「若謂諸相自性安立，即稱其量假立名言……於一相所立名言有眾多故、有差別故，應有眾多差別體性。是故名言依相而立，不應道理。」

The same thing can receive countless names — you call it "flower," "plant," "something red," "something with thorns." If language truly referred to the self-nature of things, the same thing should have countless different self-natures — which is logically untenable. The correspondence between language and self-nature is posited, not actual: "marks do not have real essential natures corresponding to language" (非稱名言有實體性).

## III. Core Argument

### 3.1 "Whatever Has" — The Scope Is Total, No Exceptions

The three characters "whatever has" (凡所有 fán suǒyǒu) in "whatever has marks" carry more doctrinal weight than they are usually given.

As Kuiji analyzed: ordinary (marks of the six destinies) + noble (marks of the three-vehicle sages) + pure (marks of clean merit) + defiled (marks of afflictive defilement) — "though only four categories are raised, they encompass everything." No exceptions.

This means: not only is the table you see an illusory mark — the meditative bliss you experience in practice is also an illusory mark. Not only are greed, hatred, and delusion illusory marks — morality, concentration, and wisdom are also illusory marks. Not only are the states of ordinary beings illusory marks — the thirty-two marks of the Buddha are also illusory marks. This is exactly the context of §5: the question being asked is "can the Tathāgata be seen by means of bodily marks?"

The pedagogical function of "whatever has" is "blocking narrow views" — preventing you from drawing a line somewhere and saying "marks on this side are illusory; marks on that side are not." The moment you draw that line, you have produced a fresh parikalpita on "that side." You think "nirvāṇa is not illusory" — but if you treat nirvāṇa as a graspable object, the "nirvāṇa" you grasp is already illusory.

### 3.2 The Yogācāra Positioning of "Marks": Object-Aspects Are Paratantra; Reifying Object-Aspects Is Parikalpita ✅

Synthesizing the scriptural foundations of §II, this paper proposes the precise Yogācāra positioning of "whatever has marks is illusory":

**Object-aspects are paratantra.** When your six consciousnesses encounter the six sense-fields, they project object-aspects — you see red, hear sounds, smell fragrance. These object-aspects are genuinely occurring cognitive events, arising dependent on conditions, not nothingness. The Cheng weishi lun states: "The body of consciousness and mental factors produced by manifold conditions, together with their object-aspects and seeing-aspects, whether tainted or untainted, are all paratantra."

**Parikalpita is the superimposition onto object-aspects.** You see red (object-aspect, paratantra), then you say "that is a red flower" (nominal designation), then you assume the flower has a permanent, independent, self-existing "flower-nature" (parikalpita). The Cheng weishi lun states: "These two aspects are emotionally existent but rationally non-existent; this characteristic is called parikalpita." What you "believe" to be truly real (emotionally existent) cannot be found upon investigation (rationally non-existent).

**"Illusory" negates parikalpita, not paratantra.** When the Diamond Sūtra says "whatever has marks is illusory," it cuts not the object-aspects themselves (those are paratantra and cannot be negated) but your reifying attachment to object-aspects (that is parikalpita, the truly illusory). Like the eye-disease analogy: your eyes are diseased and you see flowers in the sky — the Diamond Sūtra cures your eye disease (parikalpita); it does not make you stop seeing (negate paratantra).

The trisvabhāva mapping established in S5-P01 receives its concrete deployment here:

```
"Whatever has marks"         = X = paratantra: object-aspects as dependently
                               arisen genuinely exist
"Is illusory"                = jífēi X = negating parikalpita: reification
                               onto object-aspects is illusory
"If you see all marks        = the process of cognitive transformation
 as no-marks"
"Then you see the Tathāgata" = is-called X = pariniṣpanna: seeing the
                               original face of object-aspects
```

### 3.3 "Illusory" Does Not Mean "Non-Existent" — Paratantra Is Real Like an Illusion

"Illusory" (虛妄 xūwàng) is easily misread in Chinese as "false," "non-existent." This is a fatal misunderstanding.

The Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra warned against this danger[^5]:

> "If one regards the paratantra characteristic and the pariniṣpanna characteristic as having no characteristics, one thereby also repudiates the parikalpita characteristic."
>
> 「若於依他起相及圓成實相見為無相，彼亦誹撥遍計所執相。」

If upon hearing "all is illusory" you conclude that nothing exists — paratantra does not exist, pariniṣpanna does not exist — you have not only failed to understand parikalpita but have repudiated even parikalpita itself. This is a deeper error than parikalpita: parikalpita at least acknowledges that something exists (it merely mistakes it for truly real); nihilism negates existence itself.

The precise doctrinal meaning of "illusory" is: not existing in the way you grasp it as existing. A flower is not non-existent — as a paratantra dharma arising through the convergence of conditions, it genuinely exists. But the flower is not what you think it is — there is no permanent, independent, essential "flower" there. What you perceive is the flower-object-aspect projected by your consciousness, and the "flower-nature" you superimpose onto this object-aspect is empty.

An everyday analogy: you are watching a film in a cinema. The images on screen are real light (paratantra — light is genuinely being projected), but the "characters," "story," and "emotion" you see are your cognitive construction (parikalpita — you project onto the light that "these are real people truly suffering"). "Whatever has marks is illusory" does not tell you to stop watching the film (negating paratantra); it reminds you that what you are watching is a film (illuminating parikalpita). You still see the images, hear the dialogue, feel the tension of the plot — but you know it is a film. You do not rush onto the screen to save a character.

### 3.4 The Five Progressive Cuts of "Marks" in the Diamond Sūtra ✅

Section 2.2 described the semantic spectrum of "marks." Placed back into the pedagogical progression of the Diamond Sūtra, the five layers reveal five progressive cuts:

**First cut: the four marks (§3–§6) — cutting self-grasping.** "If a bodhisattva has a self-mark, person-mark, being-mark, or life-span-mark, then they are not a bodhisattva." The four marks are the coarsest parikalpita — the assumption of a permanent "I." The first cut falls at the most fundamental place: Who are you? Does the "you" you assume exist?

**Second cut: dharma-marks / non-dharma-marks (§6–§9) — cutting dharma-grasping.** "One should not grasp dharmas, nor should one grasp non-dharmas." When you have successfully released "self-marks," you may turn to grasping "dharma-marks" — dharma is real, the teaching can be seized. The second cut addresses this: not only "I" is empty; "dharma" is also empty. More subtly, "non-dharma" must also be cut — you cannot leap from "having dharma" to "having no dharma" and believe that negating dharma is the truth.

**Third cut: bodily marks / the thirty-two marks (§5, §13, §20, §26) — cutting attachment to the Buddha.** Four inquiries about "whether the Tathāgata can be seen by means of marks," progressing from the Buddha's body to the thirty-two marks, to the perfect physical body, to complete marks. This cut addresses the practitioner's attachment to the Buddha's "marks" — are you practicing in order to see a resplendent, golden-bodied Buddha? If so, what you will see is merely another parikalpita.

**Fourth cut: no-marks (§14, §17) — cutting attachment to "marklessness."** "Self-marks are precisely no-marks" — you cannot even cling to "no-marks." You have seen that "marks are illusory," so you begin to pursue "marklessness" — but if "marklessness" becomes your new target, it has become another mark. Sēngzhào's warning precisely addresses this layer: "hoping for a markless Buddha standing clearly before their eyes — how does that differ from those very marks?"

**Fifth cut: real-mark (§14) — cutting attachment to "ultimate truth."** "What is called 'real-mark' is precisely no-mark; therefore the Tathāgata calls it 'real-mark.'" Real-mark is not a graspable "ultimate truth" — it is no-mark. If you are searching for a "final truth," what you find will be merely another parikalpita. Real-mark is not finding something but releasing the search.

The pedagogical logic of the five progressive cuts: each time you think you have let go — let go of self, of dharma, of the Buddha, of "marklessness" — the Diamond Sūtra tells you that the very act of "letting go" has produced a new attachment. The fifth cut is the ultimate self-negation: even the concept of "real-mark" must be cut. But note — after the cutting, what remains is not nothingness (nihilism) but "is called real-mark" — you may still say the words "real-mark," only you no longer use them to refer to a graspable object.

S5-P07's dual-layer structure provides a more precise positioning for the five cuts. The first three layers (the four marks, dharma-marks, bodily marks) unfold primarily in the first half (§1–16), countering afflictive obstruction — your attachment to "I" and "external objects." The fourth layer (no-marks) and fifth layer (real-mark) unfold at the boundary and into the second half, countering cognitive obstruction — your attachment to "understanding" and "the fruits of practice." More notably, P07 discovers that the Diamond Sūtra upgrades from the four "marks" (lakṣaṇa, the object of cognition) in the first half to the four "views" (dṛṣṭi, the mode of cognition itself) in the second half — §31: "self-view, person-view, being-view, life-span-view are precisely not self-view… they are called self-view…" — moving from deconstructing "what you see" to deconstructing "how you see"[^11a]. The first three layers of the five cuts address "the marks you perceive"; the last two layers point toward "the perceiving itself" — perfectly aligned with P07's marks-to-views upgrade.

### 3.5 Subhūti's Wrong Answer at §26: The Distance between Knowledge and Realization

Subhūti's "wrong answer" at §26 takes on a clearer position within the five progressive cuts.

Subhūti correctly answered at §5 ("the Tathāgata cannot be seen by means of bodily marks") and correctly at §13 ("the Tathāgata cannot be seen by means of the thirty-two marks"). Conceptually, he had passed the first three cuts. Then at §26 — the same question (perceive the Tathāgata by means of the thirty-two marks?) — he suddenly answers "Yes, yes."

From the kōan-mirror perspective (S5-P02), §26 is the sūtra's most finely calibrated mirror: it is not testing Subhūti's knowledge (he has already demonstrated that he knows the answer) but illuminating his cognitive habit. The distance between "knowing one should not see by means of marks" and "actually not seeing by means of marks" is the distance between knowledge and realization.

From the Yogācāra perspective, Subhūti's sixth consciousness had established correct understanding — "do not see by means of marks." But his manas (seventh consciousness) was still habitually operating in the grasper/grasped pattern — when the same question reappeared in a slightly different form, the conceptual right-view could not intercept the manas's habitual response in time[^11]. This is the subtlety of parikalpita: it is not just a conceptual error (correctable by a correct concept) but a cognitive habit (transformable only through sustained practice).

The Buddha's mode of correction is worth noting: he does not say "you are wrong" but says "if the Tathāgata could be perceived by the thirty-two marks, then a wheel-turning sage-king would be a Tathāgata." This is a reductio ad absurdum — if your logic holds, a wheel-turning king is a buddha. Not a direct denial but an invitation for you to see the absurdity of your own reasoning. The mirror does not say "your face is dirty"; the mirror simply reflects — you see for yourself.

### 3.6 Everyday Analogy: The Designer Bag

You see a bag in a department store.

The unaware response pattern (parikalpita's full operation): you see the bag's shape, color, and material (object-aspect, paratantra), then you see the brand logo (nominal designation), then your mind generates a cascade of reactions — "this is luxury," "owning it will make me more sophisticated," "others will admire me," "I deserve it." You think you are looking at a bag; you are actually looking at your own projections. "Luxury" is not the bag's self-nature — the same material, the same craftsmanship, minus the logo, is no longer "luxury." What you cling to is not the bag but everything you have superimposed on top of it.

The aware response pattern (illuminating parikalpita): you still see the same bag (paratantra has not vanished), you still know its brand name (names have not vanished), but you no longer assume that the brand logo refers to a real "nature of prestige." The bag is still a bag — a dependently arisen assembly of leather and hardware. You may buy it or not, but not because you "need" that label to confirm who you are.

"Whatever has marks is illusory" does not tell you never to buy a bag (negating paratantra). It illuminates everything you project onto the bag (parikalpita) — from "luxury" to "taste" to "how others see me" — all superimposed by yourself onto the object-aspect. The bag has not deceived you. Your parikalpita is deceiving you.

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Dialogue

### 4.1 Cognitive Science: Perception Is Construction, Not Reception ⚠️ Heuristic Analogy

A core finding of modern cognitive science is that human perception is not the passive "reception" of external information but the active "construction" of perceptual experience[^12]. The brain is not a camera; it functions more like a prediction engine — it actively constructs what you "see" based on past experience, expectations, and contextual cues.

Richard Gregory's theory of constructive perception argues that much of what you see is "filled in" by the brain rather than directly supplied by the external world[^12]. Classic visual illusions — the Müller-Lyer illusion, the Kanizsa triangle — demonstrate that the brain "sees" things where no actual stimulus exists.

This is structurally similar to the Yogācāra theory of object-aspects. The object-aspect is projected by consciousness "as if" it were the object of cognition — not a direct imprint of the external world onto your awareness but something consciousness itself projects based on conditions. The "red" you "see" is not the external world's red entering your consciousness directly — it is what your visual consciousness projects as "the red object-aspect" upon contact with the color-field (light wavelengths).

Furthermore, cognitive bias research — confirmation bias, anchoring, the availability heuristic — describes precisely the tendency of the human cognitive system to superimpose additional judgments onto object-aspects[^13]. You do not merely see something — you layer onto what you see your expectations, preferences, and fears. Yogācāra calls this parikalpita; cognitive science calls it cognitive bias.

**Limits of the analogy:** Cognitive science's "construction" still presupposes an external world independent of the cognizer — the brain constructs representations of an external world. Yogācāra does not make this presupposition — the object-aspect is not a representation "about" an external world; the object-aspect is the direct projection of consciousness. Moreover, cognitive science's "correcting bias" operates at the conceptual level (replacing faulty reasoning with better reasoning); Yogācāra's "transformation of the basis" (āśraya-parāvṛtti 轉依) is a fundamental cognitive transformation — not replacing incorrect parikalpita with correct parikalpita but liberating oneself from the parikalpita mode altogether.

### 4.2 Linguistics: The Sapir-Whorf Hypothesis ⚠️ Heuristic Analogy

The strong version of the Sapir-Whorf hypothesis holds that language determines thought — speakers of different languages literally "see" different worlds[^14]. Although the strong version has been revised, the weak version (language influences cognitive habits) has received experimental support: for instance, speakers of languages with more color terms perform better on color-discrimination tasks.

This is structurally parallel to the Yogācārabhūmi's "bondage by language." The Yogācārabhūmi states: "The deluded, thinking ordinary beings are bound by speech within marks." You have the word "flower," so you carve the world into "flower" and "not-flower"; you have the word "beautiful," so you carve flowers into "beautiful flowers" and "not-beautiful flowers." Each name is an act of carving — and you assume those carvings are the way the world actually is.

The Yogācārabhūmi states: "Since the language posited for a single mark is manifold and varied, there should be manifold and varied essential natures" — if language truly corresponded to self-nature, the same flower should simultaneously have the self-nature of "flower," "plant," "red thing," and "thorny thing." Language is a classification system you impose; it is not the intrinsic structure of things.

**Limits of the analogy:** The Sapir-Whorf hypothesis focuses on differences between languages; the Yogācārabhūmi addresses the fundamental limitation of language itself as a cognitive tool. The former compares relative differences between linguistic systems; the latter identifies a structural obscuration shared by all linguistic systems. Moreover, linguistics does not address "cognition freed from language" — for that lies beyond what linguistics can investigate — while the Yogācārabhūmi explicitly points toward the possibility of "freedom from the bondage of language," a state accessed by noble wisdom.

## V. Middle Way Corrections

### 5.1 ❌ "Illusory" Does Not Mean "Non-Existent" — Guarding against Nihilism

The greatest misreading risk: the reader encounters "whatever has marks is illusory" and jumps straight to "therefore everything is fake, non-existent, meaningless."

This is nihilism (uccheda-dṛṣṭi 斷滅見), one of the dangers the Diamond Sūtra guards against most vigilantly.

The Cheng weishi lun is unambiguous: object-aspects are paratantra — "the body of consciousness and mental factors produced by manifold conditions, together with their object-aspects and seeing-aspects, whether tainted or untainted, are all paratantra." Paratantra is not nothingness. The person before you is a genuine convergence of conditions — their body, their words, your interaction — all genuinely occurring paratantra events. "Illusory" negates only the "truly existing self-nature" you superimpose onto all of this — your belief that they are a permanent, unchanging "them," your assumption that your relationship is an entity that can be grasped forever.

The Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra's warning must be cited again[^5]: "If one regards the paratantra and pariniṣpanna characteristics as having no characteristics, one thereby also repudiates the parikalpita characteristic." Negating paratantra is worse than parikalpita — you have forfeited even the capacity to err, having negated the very possibility of erring.

A practical test: if after reading "whatever has marks is illusory" you feel "since everything is illusory, nothing matters" — you have misread. The very next chapter of the Diamond Sūtra (§6) gives you the raft analogy: the dharma is a raft, to be used for crossing the river, then set down after crossing. First cross the river — do not refuse to cross. First practice diligently — do not stop practicing because of "illusoriness."

### 5.2 ❌ The Yogācāra "Mark" Must Not Be Equated with the Western Philosophical "Phenomenon"

The Yogācāra "object-aspect" (相分) and the Western philosophical "phenomenon" (Erscheinung) share a surface resemblance — both refer to objects as presented in cognition — but their theoretical presuppositions are fundamentally different.

Kant's phenomenon presupposes a "thing-in-itself" (Ding an sich / noumenon) behind the phenomenon — what you cognize is only the phenomenon; the thing-in-itself is unknowable. Yogācāra makes no such presupposition: the object-aspect is not a phenomenon "about" something — the object-aspect is the direct projection of consciousness. Behind it there is no thing-in-itself waiting to be cognized. Yogācāra's "the external world is not real" (外境非有) does not mean "the external world exists but you cannot know it" (Kant); it means "what you take to be the external world was never what you thought it was."

Husserl's phenomenology comes closer — his call to "return to the things themselves" shares a structural affinity with Yogācāra's seeing-as-it-really-is. But Husserl's intentionality presupposes that consciousness is always "consciousness of something" — consciousness necessarily points toward an object. Yogācāra's vijñāna-pariṇāma (consciousness-transformation 識變) does not require a pre-existing object — consciousness projects object-aspects and seeing-aspects on its own, then within the projected structure manufactures the duality of grasper and grasped.

Translating xiāng as "phenomenon" or "appearance" imports these incompatible presuppositions. This paper uses "marks" as the translation for lakṣaṇa/nimitta in its English version, preserving its specific meaning within the Buddhist context.

### 5.3 ❌ The Śūraṅgama Sūtra's Supplement: "Fundamentally Neither Caused Nor Uncaused" — Even "Dependent Arising" Must Be Transcended

The Śūraṅgama Sūtra's (楞嚴經 Léngyán jīng) analysis of the five aggregates as illusory offers a perspective one layer deeper than the Diamond Sūtra and standard Yogācāra[^15]:

Each of the five aggregates (form, feeling, perception, formations, consciousness) is illustrated with its own analogy, but the conclusion is uniform: "○○ is illusory, fundamentally neither caused nor uncaused" (○○虛妄，本非因緣、非自然性).

"Fundamentally not caused" (本非因緣) — this exceeds the standard framework of the Diamond Sūtra and Yogācāra. Yogācāra says object-aspects are "paratantra" — arising dependent on conditions. The Śūraṅgama says the five aggregates are "fundamentally not caused" — even "arising through conditions" as a framework requires transcendence.

This does not negate dependent arising (that is a core Buddhist teaching); it indicates that if you treat "conditions" as a graspable "ultimate explanation," then "conditions" has become another parikalpita. You have been liberated from clinging to "self-nature existence" only to cling to "conditional existence" — this is a deeper form of dharma-grasping. The Śūraṅgama's "fundamentally neither caused nor uncaused" cuts one layer further: even conditionality cannot be reified.

However, this paper's primary framework operates within the Yogācāra three natures, with paratantra/parikalpita analysis at its core. The Śūraṅgama's "neither caused nor uncaused" as a deeper pointer is reserved for the S6 Śūraṅgama series. It is introduced here only to mark the boundary — this paper's analysis extends to paratantra; the Śūraṅgama's analysis transcends even the paratantra/causation framework itself.

### 5.4 ❌ The Five Progressive Cuts Are Not a Practice KPI

The five progressive cuts of §3.4 are an analytical tool, not a progress bar for practice.

"I've already cut to the fourth layer" — this sentence is itself a fresh product of the first layer (self-mark). You are using the five-layer framework to measure your practice, and the measuring itself is parikalpita.

Consistent with S5-P02 §6.4: any analytical framework applied to the Diamond Sūtra — the jífēi-trisvabhāva mapping, the kōan mirror, the five progressive cuts — is a raft, not the shore. Use it, then set it down. If you paint the raft beautifully and then sit on it without moving, the raft has lost its function.

## VI. Conclusion

### 6.1 Core Findings

This paper's argument can be summarized in five points.

First, the Diamond Sūtra §5's "whatever has marks is illusory" is the sūtra's first cut — the first explicit declaration that the object the jífēi structure cuts is "marks," with a scope of "whatever has" that admits no exceptions.

Second, "marks" in the Diamond Sūtra is not a fixed term but unfolds across a five-layer semantic spectrum: the four marks (self-grasping) → dharma-marks / non-dharma-marks (dharma-grasping) → bodily marks / the thirty-two marks (attachment to the Buddha) → no-marks (attachment to "marklessness") → real-mark (the self-negating proposition "real-mark is precisely no-mark"). Each layer is subtler than the last; each cut goes deeper.

Third, through the Yogācāra analysis of object-aspects and parikalpita, the precise doctrinal meaning of "whatever has marks is illusory" is: the object-aspects projected by consciousness are paratantra (their existence cannot be negated), but the reifying attachment to object-aspects (parikalpita) is the root of "illusory." "Illusory" does not negate marks; it negates only the invisible rope between you and marks.

Fourth, Kuiji's "mind (niàn) is the support, marks the supported" analysis and the Yogācārabhūmi's "bondage by language" mechanism together reveal the generative path of parikalpita: thought → language → designation of self-nature → grasping as truly real. The Diamond Sūtra's "first cut" severs the last link of this path — grasping as truly real.

Fifth, Subhūti's "wrong answer" at §26 is among the sūtra's most exquisite pedagogical designs — it illuminates not a deficiency of knowledge but the distance between knowledge and realization. You can conceptually understand "do not see the Tathāgata by means of marks" perfectly, yet your cognitive habit (the manas's grasper/grasped pattern) may return you to seeing-by-means-of-marks at the very next moment.

### 6.2 Open Questions

"Dharma should still be let go of, how much more so non-dharma" (§6) — if even the Buddha's teaching is a mark, is paratantra, must be jífēi'd, where does the motivation to practice come from? The full pedagogical implications of the raft analogy will be developed in S5-P04.

In the merit-comparison passages, "upholding four lines of verse surpasses giving seven treasures" — how can the merit of speech surpass the merit of material giving? This concerns the merit theory of the jífēi structure itself as a pedagogical device, to be developed in S5-P05.

"Give rise to a mind that abides nowhere" (§10) — if all marks are illusory, where do you "abide"? The answer is not "abide in the illumination of illusoriness" (that would be another mark), but "non-abiding" itself. The simultaneity of non-abiding and giving rise to mind will be developed in S5-P06.
