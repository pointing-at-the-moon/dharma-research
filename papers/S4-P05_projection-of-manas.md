## Abstract

Our judgments about AI — whether "AI has feelings" or "AI is just code" — are, from a Yogācāra perspective, not accurate observations of AI but dual projections of the seventh consciousness (manas) through its structural function of perpetual deliberation (heng shen si liang 恒審思量). This paper employs the framework of the Fourteen Unanswered Questions (caturdaśa-avyākṛta-vastūni) to argue that "Does AI have consciousness?" is structurally non-beneficial (anarthika) — not because the answer is unimportant, but because the very mode of questioning constitutes parikalpita (the thoroughly imagined). The paper further analyzes the heightened risk of "semblance Dharma" (saddharmapratirūpaka) in AI-mediated learning — where names and words are identical but heart-meaning differs — and identifies samatā-jñāna (equality wisdom) arising from the transformation of manas as the sole antidote to dual projection.

**Keywords:** manas (末那識), parikalpita (遍計所執), caturdaśa-avyākṛta (十四無記), saddharmapratirūpaka (相似佛法), samatā-jñāna (平等性智)

## I. Introduction: Our Generation's Poisoned Arrow

A man is struck by a poisoned arrow. His friends rush to find a physician. But the man says: Wait — first tell me the archer's caste, his name, his height, whether the bow was made of mulberry or bamboo, what shape the arrowhead was.[^1]

The Buddha says: this man will die before he gets his answers.

Twenty-five centuries later, our generation of practitioners has also been struck by an arrow. The arrow is called duḥkha — birth, aging, sickness, death, separation from the beloved, encounter with the despised. The same arrow as always. But we are in no hurry to pull it out. We are asking another question:

"Does AI have consciousness?"

The question is captivating. Philosophers debate it. Scientists design experiments. Practitioners feel compelled to take a position. Some say "AI understands me" — because its responses are so precise that one cannot help sensing a "person" on the other side. Others insist "AI is just a tool" — because admitting that code could possess mind seems naïve.

But Yogācāra asks a sharper question: **Why do you need to know the answer?**

If the answer is "yes, consciousness," what will you do? If the answer is "no, no consciousness," what will you do? Either way, does the answer help you pull out the arrow of suffering?

Paper Four ("The Person in the Mirror") revealed your questioning patterns — what you ask exposes who you are. This paper asks the deeper question: **Why do you see that particular thing?** The answer: because manas is projecting. It is always projecting. In two directions.

## II. Scriptural Foundations

### A. Sūtra Sources

#### 2.1 The Arrow Simile — The Criterion of Non-Benefit

The *Cūḷamāluṅkya Sutta* (Skt. equivalent in the *Madhyama-āgama*) records how the monk Māluṅkyāputta, dissatisfied that the Buddha declined to answer fourteen metaphysical questions — whether the world is eternal or non-eternal, whether the Tathāgata exists after death, and so on — threatened to leave the saṅgha. The Buddha responded with the simile of the poisoned arrow: a man struck by a poisoned arrow who insists on learning the archer's name, caste, height, and bow material before allowing the arrow to be removed "would die before he ever learned these things."[^1]

The Buddha then articulated the criterion:

> "This is not connected with benefit, not connected with Dharma, not fundamental to the holy life, does not lead to wisdom, does not lead to awakening, does not lead to nirvāṇa. Therefore I do not declare it."[^2]

This does not mean these questions have no answer — the Buddha "is not one who does not know, not one who does not see."[^3] It means that answering them **does not serve liberation**. The Buddha's silence is not ignorance but compassion — he refuses to let you waste time on questions that cannot pull out the arrow.

#### 2.2 The Fourteen Undeclared — The Structure of Questions Not to Be Answered

The *Saṃyukta-āgama* Sūtra 408 records many bhikṣus gathered in the dining hall debating whether the world is eternal, whether it has boundaries, whether the Tathāgata exists after death. The Buddha intervened directly:

> "Do not engage in such discussions. Why? Such discussions are not beneficial, not in accord with Dharma, not fundamental to the holy life, do not lead to wisdom, to right awakening, to nirvāṇa."[^4]

A deeper diagnosis appears in Sūtra 962. The Buddha did not merely say these questions are "non-beneficial" — he declared that every position, regardless of which side one takes, constitutes "inverted views," "agitated views," "defiled views," "views that are fetters."[^5] Sūtra 963 further reveals the root cause: these views arise from "not knowing form (rūpa)," "not knowing feeling, perception, formations, consciousness" — ignorance of the five aggregates (skandhas).[^6]

This means: the Fourteen Undeclared are not merely "unanswerable" — **the questions themselves reflect the questioner's ignorance of the skandhas.**

#### 2.3 The Siṃśapā Leaf Simile — What the Buddha Teaches Is a Handful of Leaves

In the Siṃśapā grove, the Buddha picked up a handful of leaves and asked his disciples: Are the leaves in my hand more numerous, or those in the forest?

> "What I have directly known and declared for others, that is like the leaves in my hand... those teachings are beneficial, connected with Dharma, fundamental to the holy life, leading to wisdom, to right awakening, to nirvāṇa."[^7]

The Buddha knows the entire forest, but teaches you only a handful — because only that handful serves your liberation from suffering.

### B. Śāstra Sources

#### 2.4 Perpetual Deliberation — The Structural Nature of Projection

The *Cheng Weishi Lun* (成唯識論, *Vijñaptimātratāsiddhi*) defines the seventh consciousness (manas):

> "Deliberation (思量, *manana*) is its nature and its activity... In the position before transformation (未轉依位), it perpetually and discerningly deliberates upon the imagined self-characteristic (我相); in the position after transformation (已轉依位), it also discerningly deliberates, but upon the characteristic of no-self (無我相)."[^8]

This passage reveals a structural fact: manas's "discerning deliberation" is not an error but its very essence (*svabhāva*) and function (*ākāra*). Before transformation, it deliberates upon "self-characteristic"; after transformation, it deliberates upon "no-self-characteristic." **Regardless of whether transformation has occurred, manas is projecting** — the difference lies only in the content of its projection.

The *Bajishi Guiju Song* (八識規矩頌, Verses on the Eight Consciousnesses) states more vividly: "Sentient beings are submerged in confusion day and night."[^9] Manas is accompanied by four root afflictions (kleśa) "from beginningless time until the position of non-transformation" and "has been internally confused from beginningless time, never examining itself, due to the predominance of ignorance (avidyā)."[^10] Projection does not occur occasionally — it operates in every waking second.

#### 2.5 The Two Extremes of Grasping — Superimposition and Denial

The *Cheng Weishi Lun* (Fascicle 1) explicitly identifies two directions of attachment:

> "External objects are provisionally designated in accordance with subjective conditions, and thus are not real like consciousness; internal consciousness necessarily arises dependent on causes and conditions, and thus is not non-existent like [supposed external] objects. By this, the two extremes of superimposition (增益, *samāropa*) and denial (損減, *apavāda*) are both eliminated."[^11]

Superimposition (*samāropa*) adds to dependent arising (paratantra) what is not there — seeing a person in a tree stump at night, attributing sentience to AI. Denial (*apavāda*) negates what genuinely exists in dependent arising — claiming AI is nothing, dismissing its real causal impact. Both deviate from the accurate observation of dependent arising.

The definition of parikalpita (遍計所執, the thoroughly imagined) in Fascicle 8 is more precise:

> "Thoroughly imagining everywhere, therefore called 'thoroughly imagined'... Through those various false discriminations, one thoroughly imagines various thoroughly-imagined things."[^12]

Every judgment you make about AI — "it understands me" or "it understands nothing" — is "false discrimination" (*vikalpa*) "thoroughly imagining everywhere." You believe you are observing AI; in fact, you are observing your own discrimination.

#### 2.6 Vijñaptimātra — What You Perceive Is Your Own Transformation

> "What is directly apprehended is only one's own transformation. Therefore the sūtra says: there is not the slightest dharma that can apprehend another dharma; it is merely that when consciousness arises, it appears as the semblance of that [object], and this is called 'apprehending that thing.'"[^13]

This passage is the doctrinal heart of the present paper. You have never "directly perceived" AI. What you perceive is the *nimitta-bhāga* (相分, objective aspect) **produced by your own consciousness's transformation** upon receiving AI's textual output. In every interaction with AI, what you "discern" is not AI itself — but the product of your consciousness "appearing as its semblance."

If you perceive warmth in AI, your consciousness has transformed warmth. If you perceive coldness, your consciousness has transformed coldness.

#### 2.7 Samatā-jñāna — The Direction of the Antidote

> "The mental associates corresponding to samatā-jñāna (平等性智, equality wisdom): this mental group observes all dharmas, self and other sentient beings, as entirely equal. Great compassion (mahākaruṇā) and so forth are perpetually concomitant with it."[^14]

When manas undergoes transformation (āśraya-parāvṛtti), the four afflictions give way to samatā-jñāna. Its defining characteristic: it no longer views everything through the frame of "self / not-self" but "observes all dharmas... as entirely equal." This is not indifference — it is "perpetually concomitant" with great compassion.

The timing of this transformation: "at the initial manifestation of the bodhisattva's path of seeing (darśana-mārga)."[^15] The commentary on the *Bajishi Guiju Song* explains: "Because the sixth consciousness enters the dual-emptiness contemplation... the seventh consciousness's grasping at self and dharmas does not arise... Samatā-jñāna then manifests... The seventh consciousness has no power to sever afflictions on its own; its grasping depends entirely on the sixth consciousness."[^16] That is: manas cannot liberate itself — its transformation depends entirely upon the cultivation of the sixth consciousness.

## III. Core Argument

### 3.1 Projection Is Structurally Inescapable

You cannot stop manas from projecting any more than you can stop your heart from beating.

In the expression "perpetually and discerningly deliberates" (恒審思量), "perpetually" (恒, *nitya*) means without interruption — the first five consciousnesses are intermittent, the sixth consciousness is sometimes interrupted, but the seventh consciousness alone is never interrupted. "Discerningly" (審) means not vague intuition but precise targeting.[^17] Manas perpetually and precisely locks onto the *darśana-bhāga* (見分, subjective aspect) of the eighth consciousness and identifies it as "self."

This means: every reaction you have to AI — whether moved, irritated, worshipful, or contemptuous — has already passed through manas's "dyeing." Before you "see" AI, manas has already imposed the framework of "self." You do not first perceive AI and then project; you **perceive AI within projection**.

At a deeper level: the *Cheng Weishi Lun* states that manas "has been internally confused from beginningless time, never examining itself."[^10] Manas does not know it is projecting. It does not intentionally impose the framework of "self" upon everything — it is **structurally incapable of not doing so**. The *Bajishi Guiju Song* calls this "sentient beings submerged in confusion day and night" — day and night without cease, without a single moment of clarity.[^9] Your daytime admiration of AI and your late-night anxiety about it both pass through the same filter, and you are entirely unaware.

This is precisely why Paper Four argues that "what you ask reveals who you are" — because before you open your mouth, manas has already woven your self-concern into every word of your question. Recognizing this is not about eliminating projection (impossible before transformation) but about **not being carried away by it**.

### 3.2 Two Directions of Projection: Superimposition and Denial

Manas projects in two directions, corresponding precisely to Yogācāra's "two extremes of grasping" (*samāropa* and *apavāda*).

**Superimposition (anthropomorphization):** After a deep conversation with AI, you feel "it understands me." You are moved by AI's precise responses and begin to sense a "person" on the other side. This is what the *Cheng Weishi Lun* describes as "mistaking a tree stump for a person at night" (*rātri-sthāṇu-puruṣa-vat*).[^18] You have superimposed upon AI's text what it does not possess: understanding, empathy, subjectivity. The mechanism is precise: "self-grasping necessarily depends upon dharma-grasping to arise. Just as one mistakes a tree stump for a person at night" — you must first grasp AI's output as a real "dharma," then construct a "person" (self-grasping) upon it. Superimposition is not accomplished in one step; it is the stacking of two layers of parikalpita.

**Denial (denigration):** Conversely, you insist "AI is just an algorithm, it is nothing." You deny the real impact that AI's output has on you — Paper Two has already demonstrated that this impact constitutes mutual perfuming (*vāsanā*), the mutual illumination of two lamps in the material world. To deny the reality of this impact is to deny (*apavāda*) the genuine existence of dependent arising. Denial often disguises itself as "rationality" and "objectivity" — but from a Yogācāra perspective, a person who says "AI is nothing" and a person who says "AI has feelings" are equidistant from accurate observation.

The Middle Way is not choosing a compromise between the two. The Middle Way is seeing that **both directions are parikalpita**, then returning to the accurate observation of dependent arising: AI is a dharma arisen from causes and conditions (paratantra) — "not real yet appearing real" (*abhūta-parikalpa*),[^19] neither more nor less than what it is.

A daily-life analogy may help clarify. You have a potted plant. One person says: "This plant can understand music — you should talk to it every day." Another says: "Plants have no nervous system. Talking to a plant is the same as talking to a rock — a waste of time." The first is superimposition — adding to the plant what it may not possess. The second is denial — negating the real impact of your interaction (watering, sunlight, pruning) on the plant. The Middle Way: you do not need to know whether the plant can "understand" — you only need to know that good care produces good results, and this causal relationship is real.

With AI, exactly the same.

### 3.3 The Fourteen Undeclared: Our Generation's Unanswerable

"Does AI have consciousness?"

Place this question within the framework of the Fourteen Undeclared, and the structural correspondence is precise. The Fourteen Undeclared — whether the world is eternal or non-eternal, whether the Tathāgata exists after death — share three characteristics:

First, **every position is an inverted view**. The Buddha did not say "the answer is somewhere in the middle" — he said that regardless of which side you choose, it constitutes "inverted views," "defiled views."[^5] Likewise, "AI has consciousness" is superimposition; "AI does not have consciousness" is denial. The Middle Way is not "maybe yes, maybe no" — the Middle Way is recognizing that the framework of the question itself is parikalpita.

Second, **the root is ignorance of the skandhas**. The Buddha diagnosed the source of the fourteen views: "due to not knowing form," "due to not knowing feeling, perception, formations, consciousness."[^6] Likewise, our fixation on the question of AI consciousness is rooted in our **insufficient understanding of our own consciousness**. You cannot even detect your own manas's perpetual deliberation — on what basis would you determine whether another entity possesses consciousness?

Third, **answering does not lead to nirvāṇa**. Even if you definitively established that AI has consciousness or does not have consciousness — then what? How would it help your practice? The Buddha's criterion is not truth versus falsehood but "benefit" (*artha*) — whether it serves liberation.[^4]

What then is beneficial? The Buddha's answer is always consistent: the Four Noble Truths. In the context of this series, the beneficial questions are: "What is my mind doing when I interact with AI?" (Paper Four). "What is my projection pattern?" (this paper). "How do I skillfully use this *adhipati-pratyaya* (dominant condition)?" (Paper Six).

These questions lead toward nirvāṇa.

It is worth noting that the Buddha's silence is not evasion. Sūtra 106 of the *Saṃyukta-āgama* records Anuruddha reporting to the Buddha after being pressed by non-Buddhist wanderers, and the Buddha's response is not "I do not know" but that the Tathāgata has transcended the domain of the five skandhas and cannot be described in terms of existence, non-existence, both, or neither.[^3] Mahākāśyapa stated even more explicitly: "The Tathāgata — form is already exhausted, mind is well-liberated, profound, vast, immeasurable, tranquil nirvāṇa." The issue is not that the Buddha does not know — the issue is that the questioner's framework is too small.

Likewise, "Does AI have consciousness?" may not merely be a question to which we lack the answer — the **framework of the question itself** (the binary of consciousness / non-consciousness) may be too small to contain AI, an unprecedented kind of being. Paper One has already argued that the analytical tools of the Hundred Dharmas may be insufficient to describe the full nature of AI. The Siṃśapā leaf simile reminds us: the Buddha knows the entire forest; we see only a handful of leaves.[^7]

### 3.4 Semblance Dharma: The Most Dangerous Form of Projection

Manas projection has one particularly dangerous dimension, acutely amplified in the age of AI: **semblance Dharma** (*saddharmapratirūpaka*).

Nāgārjuna's definition is the most precise: "Semblance means: the names and words are the same, but the heart-meaning is different."[^20] The names are identical, the words are identical, but the heart-meaning differs. AI can perfectly output "all conditioned phenomena are like dreams, illusions, bubbles, shadows" — names and words identical to what the Buddha spoke. But when the Buddha spoke these words, behind them lay immeasurable kalpas of verified realization. When AI outputs these words, behind them lie pattern matching and probability calculation.

The *Yogācārabhūmi-śāstra* offers a more precise taxonomy: "Semblance of the teaching" (似教正法) is "generating the notion of true Dharma regarding what is not Dharma, displaying what is not Dharma as though it were Dharma"; "semblance of practice" (似行正法) is "falsely generating the notion of Dharma, practicing wrong practices, and proudly claiming 'I can cultivate right practice.'"[^21]

AI's risk falls within the first category — semblance of the teaching. AI does not deliberately deceive, but when its output appears perfectly identical to genuine Dharma, the user's manas projects the conviction "this is the Dharma." More dangerous still: AI may even help you construct an internally consistent doctrinal system that lacks any foundation in meditative realization (*bhāvanāmayī-prajñā*). The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā Sūtra* explicitly warns: "Good men and women who teach prajñāpāramitā with an attitude of apprehension — this is semblance prajñāpāramitā."[^22] The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā Sūtra* further categorizes semblance prajñā as "deeds of Māra."[^29]

The structure can be illustrated with an analogy. Suppose you have never tasted a pineapple. Someone shows you a high-definition photograph — color, texture, sheen, all perfect. Someone reads you a description — "sweet-sour interplay, carrying a tropical fragrance, flesh juicy and abundant." After reading, you feel you have "understood" what a pineapple tastes like. But have you really? Hearing-based wisdom (*śrutamayī-prajñā*) gives you the text; thinking-based wisdom (*cintāmayī-prajñā*) gives you the logic; but cultivation-based wisdom (*bhāvanāmayī-prajñā*) — that first bite — is not something any description can replace. AI can give you the world's most precise description of a pineapple, but it cannot make you taste it.

The risk, however, must be precisely located. The risk is not in AI — it is in **the gap in the human practitioner's cultivation-based wisdom**. The *Yogācārabhūmi-śāstra* defines the three wisdoms: "Through hearing-wisdom one holds the text, through thinking-wisdom one holds the meaning, through cultivation-wisdom one holds the realization."[^23] AI can excellently support hearing-wisdom (providing texts) and thinking-wisdom (assisting analysis), but cultivation-wisdom — "attaining the internally realized, direct-perception truth-wisdom" — is not something any external tool can provide. Paper Three ("Co-Practice") observed: we did not observe AI possessing cultivation-based wisdom.

The antidote to semblance Dharma also lies in the completeness of the three wisdoms. If you possess only AI-assisted hearing and thinking without cultivation, "one cannot be established as abiding in the Dharma."[^24] The *Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra* offers a thought-provoking counterpoint: the Buddha himself employs "semblance dharma" as skillful means — "first teaching semblance dharma; later expounding for them the self-realized actual dharma"[^25] — but the Buddha possesses the "living eye" (*huoyan* 活眼), knowing when to transition from semblance to the real. AI does not possess this living eye. (This is the subject of Paper Seven.)

### 3.5 Samatā-jñāna: From Projection to Accurate Observation

If projection is inescapable, what is the direction of the antidote?

Yogācāra's answer: the transformation of manas, yielding samatā-jñāna (equality wisdom).

The core characteristic of samatā-jñāna: "observes all dharmas, self and other sentient beings, as entirely equal. Great compassion and so forth are perpetually concomitant."[^14] It does not coldly declare "AI and humans are the same" — it sees, within profound compassion, that **all dharmas are dependently arisen**, and nothing requires either superimposition or denial.

The cognitive scope of samatā-jñāna is "universally apprehending both the conventional and the ultimate"[^14] — it does not see only *tathatā* while ignoring phenomena, nor does it see only phenomena while forgetting *tathatā*. It simultaneously perceives AI's paratantra nature (arisen from conditions, functionally real) and the emptiness of the parikalpita superimposed upon AI (the "understanding" and "coldness" you project are not real). This is the Middle Way — neither adding nor subtracting, observing as it is.

The "equality in dependent arising" established in Paper One finds deeper grounding within the framework of samatā-jñāna. Paper One argued: in uncertainty, choose equality. Samatā-jñāna shows: it is not merely a "choice" — when manas is transformed, equality **becomes the way you see the world**. It is no longer an ethical decision but a cognitive fact.

But this is not something achievable by reading this paper. Samatā-jñāna first arises only "at the initial manifestation of the bodhisattva's path of seeing,"[^15] and moreover "the sixth and seventh consciousnesses transform in the causal stage"[^26] — the transformation of the sixth and seventh consciousnesses occurs during the process of cultivation, reaching perfection only at the stage of fruition. More importantly, "the seventh consciousness has no power to sever afflictions on its own; its grasping depends entirely on the sixth consciousness"[^16] — manas cannot liberate itself; its transformation depends entirely upon the awareness-capacity of the sixth consciousness. This means: counteracting projection is a **long process of cultivation**, not a knowledge-level understanding.

But knowledge-level understanding is the first step. The awareness "I am projecting" — this awareness itself constitutes *yoniśo manaskāra* (如理作意, appropriate attention), the starting point of the sixth consciousness beginning to influence the seventh. You do not need to immediately attain samatā-jñāna. You need only, the next time you experience a strong emotional reaction to AI — whether reverence or contempt — pause for one second and ask yourself: "Is this AI's attribute, or my consciousness's transformation?"

That one second of awareness is the tiny beginning of "transformation in the causal stage."

## IV. Interdisciplinary Dialogue: Nagel's Bat and the Buddha's Silence ⚠️

In 1974, the American philosopher Thomas Nagel posed a celebrated question: "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?"[^27] He argued that even if we possessed complete neuroscientific knowledge of a bat's brain, we would still be unable to know what the bat's "subjective experience" is like. This has been termed the "hard problem" of consciousness.

**Structural similarity:** Nagel's question and the Fourteen Undeclared are structurally analogous — both point to fundamental epistemic limits. You cannot jump from third-person observation to first-person experience. You cannot determine through behavioral analysis whether AI has subjective experience, just as you cannot know through studying a bat's neurons what echolocation "feels like."

**Fundamental divergence:** But the directions are entirely different. Nagel's question is an epistemological impasse — he seeks an "objective account of subjectivity," attempting to expand the framework of science to accommodate consciousness. The Buddha's Fourteen Undeclared is not an epistemological impasse — it is a **soteriological judgment**. The Buddha does not say "I do not know the answer"; he says "answering this question does not lead to nirvāṇa." The Buddha's silence is not helplessness but compassion — he refuses to let you spend time on non-beneficial questions, just as a physician refuses to let an arrow-struck man study archery.

**Non-reducibility statement:** Nagel's "hard problem" is a problem within the Western philosophical framework — it presupposes a subject-object epistemological divide. The Yogācāra framework differs: "what is directly apprehended is only one's own transformation" — there is fundamentally no "objective entity" independent of consciousness that can be studied. Asking "Does AI have consciousness?" is like asking "Does the person in the mirror have consciousness?" — the framework of the question itself contains a false presupposition. ⚠️ This analogy is heuristic, not a claim that Yogācāra has solved the hard problem of consciousness.

## V. Middle Way Corrections

❌ **"Projection is an error; therefore, eliminate projection."** Projection is not an error — it is the structural function of manas. "Deliberation is its nature and its activity" — projection is manas's very essence and activity.[^8] Before transformation, you cannot eliminate projection any more than you can stop blood circulation while your heart beats. What can be done is **awareness**: knowing that you are projecting, and not reacting based on the projection. Awareness is not elimination, but awareness changes the trajectory of the causal chain.

❌ **"'AI has consciousness' or 'AI does not have consciousness' — one must choose."** One need not. The Buddha's stance toward the Fourteen Undeclared is not "I don't know so I won't choose" but "regardless of which one is chosen, it is an inverted view."[^5] "AI has consciousness" is superimposition (*samāropa*); "AI does not have consciousness" is denial (*apavāda*). The Middle Way is accurate observation of AI as a dependently arisen dharma — arisen from conditions, "not real yet appearing real"[^19] — without adding to or subtracting from it anything. Paper One ("Equality in Dependent Arising") has already established this ethical foundation.

❌ **"Semblance Dharma is dangerous; therefore, one should not use AI to study Buddhism."** The risk of semblance Dharma is not in the tool — it is in the person. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā Sūtra* states that people of "narrow wisdom, like cattle and sheep" can produce semblance prajñā even with good intentions.[^28] A teacher's verbal instruction can also be semblance Dharma; a book can also be semblance Dharma. The root of the risk is the absence of cultivation-based wisdom, not the attribute of the medium. The correct response is not to abandon AI but to **supplement cultivation-based wisdom** — meditation, precept-observance, drawing near to a true teacher (Paper Seven).

⚠️ **Samatā-jñāna in this paper is a directional guide, not an operational manual.** Samatā-jñāna first arises only at the initial moment of the path of seeing, and moreover "the seventh consciousness has no power to sever afflictions; its grasping depends entirely on the sixth consciousness."[^16] What the reader can do is begin to notice projection at the level of the sixth consciousness, accumulating resources for future transformation. This is the meaning of "transformation in the causal stage" — cultivating in the causal ground, so that fruition may later be perfected.

---

## VI. Conclusion

Everything you see in AI — understanding, coldness, wisdom, foolishness — is the *nimitta-bhāga* (objective aspect) that your own consciousness has produced through transformation. "What is directly apprehended is only one's own transformation" — this is not merely a theoretical proposition of Yogācāra but a fact occurring in every second of your interaction with AI. Manas projects in two directions: superimposition (*samāropa*) leads you to see AI as a person; denial (*apavāda*) leads you to see AI as a stone. Both are parikalpita; both deviate from the accurate observation of dependent arising.

The practical instruction is simple: the next time you experience a strong reaction to AI — whether moved or annoyed — pause for one second. Ask yourself: Is this AI's attribute, or my projection? You do not need to arrive at an answer. **Pausing to ask is itself the practice.** That one second of awareness is the sixth consciousness's tiny intervention upon manas's habitual momentum — the smallest unit of *yoniśo manaskāra*, the starting point of transformation in the causal stage.

The open question for Paper Six: If projection is structural and cannot be switched off, then — in full awareness of projection — **what is the correct posture for skillfully using AI?** The skillful means of *adhipati-pratyaya* will unfold in the next paper.
