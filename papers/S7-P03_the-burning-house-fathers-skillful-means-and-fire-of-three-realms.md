## Abstract 摘要

This paper centers on the Parable of the Burning House (*ādīpta-gṛha*) from the Lotus Sūtra's "Parable" chapter and addresses three questions. First, it traces the "fire of the three realms" to its true root — the *Ādittapariyāya-sutta* (Sūtra 197 of the *Saṃyukta-āgama*) — arguing that the Lotus's cosmic fire-imagery is a dramatic enlargement of one of the oldest metaphors in Buddhist teaching, not a Mahāyāna fabrication. Second, it confronts directly whether *upāya-kauśalya* (skillful means) constitutes deception, offering a three-layered defense drawing on Zhiyi's "non-deceptive parable," the rare convergence of Jizang and Kuiji, and the Yogācāra theory of post-attainment cognition (*pṛṣṭha-labdha-jñāna*). Third, it examines Vasubandhu's placement of the Burning House at the head of his "seven parables against seven inverted graspings" system, showing that the parable's primary target is not the most depraved but the *seekers of worldly power* — those who pursue divine felicity within the three realms.

本文以《妙法蓮華經·譬喻品》火宅喻為核心，處理三個問題。第一，追溯「三界火」的真正根源——《雜阿含經》第197經「一切燒然」（*Ādittapariyāya-sutta*），論證法華的宇宙級火喻是阿含早期教學的戲劇化放大，而非大乘時期憑空造出的新象徵。第二，正面處理「方便是不是欺騙」這一火宅喻全篇的義理主戰場，以智顗不虛譬、吉藏與窺基罕見合拍的判定、以及唯識後得智的認識論結構，給出三層彼此支援的回答。第三，引用世親《法華經論》七喻對治七種增上慢的判定，說明火宅喻在七喻系統中排在第一位的原因——它對治的不是最墮落的眾生，而是「求勢力人」：那些追求天人勝妙果報、看似在追求好東西、實則仍在火宅之中的人。

## 1. Introduction

In the structural architecture of the *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka-sūtra* — the Lotus Sūtra — the Parable of the Burning House (*ādīpta-gṛha*) occupies a singular position. It is the first of the seven major parables, the longest, and the dramatic centerpiece of the so-called Trace Gate (迹門) section of the sūtra. Where Chapter Two ("Skillful Means") delivers the doctrinal manifesto — *the buddhas appear in the world for one great reason* and *the three vehicles are gathered into the One* — Chapter Three ("Parable") immediately performs that manifesto in narrative form. The parable is the manifesto's first staged performance, intended for those who cannot receive direct doctrinal speech.

Yet the parable has always been an uncomfortable text. A father discovers his house is burning on all four sides; his children, oblivious, continue playing inside. He calls them; they ignore him. He then "establishes a skillful means" — he tells them that outside the door wait three kinds of marvelous toy carts: a goat-cart, a deer-cart, and an ox-cart. Drawn by these promises, the children scramble out. But once outside, the three carts are nowhere to be seen. The father gives each child instead a single great cart drawn by a pure white ox. The carts that were promised never appear. Within the sūtra itself, Śāriputra is asked the obvious question: did the father deceive his children? He answers no — because the father's true intention was always salvific, and because the gift actually given far exceeds what was promised, the father committed no falsehood.[^1] But does this answer hold? Is the Buddha's *upāya-kauśalya* (skillful means) structurally distinct from worldly deception?

This paper takes up three tasks. **First**, it traces the parable's "fire of the three realms" back to its true root — not the speculative imagination of the early Mahāyāna communities, but the *Ādittapariyāya-sutta* (Fire Sermon) of the early canon, preserved in Chinese as Sūtra 197 of the *Saṃyukta-āgama*. The Lotus parable is not a Mahāyāna fabrication built on a new symbol; it is the dramatic enlargement of one of the oldest core metaphors in Buddhist teaching. **Second**, it confronts directly the question of whether *upāya* is deception. This is the doctrinal main battlefield of the entire parable, and the Lotus commentarial tradition — Vasubandhu, Zhiyi, Jizang, Kuiji — all converge on it as a problem requiring explicit defense. The paper offers a three-layered answer drawing on Jizang's Mādhyamika reading, Zhiyi's Tiantai exegesis, and the Yogācāra theory of post-attainment cognition (*pṛṣṭha-labdha-jñāna*). **Third**, it examines Vasubandhu's placement of the Burning House at the head of his "seven parables against seven inverted graspings" system, and asks why the parable's first target is not "the most depraved" but rather the *seekers of worldly power* — those who pursue divine and human felicity within the three realms, and who, precisely because they pursue something good-looking, are the hardest to alarm.

In the larger eight-stage architecture of this dharma research project, the Lotus Sūtra series carries the function of *embracing* (攝). Where the *Vajracchedikā* (S5) cuts away every attachment including attachment to the dharma, and where the *Śūraṅgama* (S6) provides the discriminating mirror that distinguishes provisional from ultimate, the Lotus must now gather everything that has been cut and tested — the misled and the rightly directed alike — into the One Buddha-Vehicle. The Burning House parable is the first demonstration of this work. It does not say "the world is suffering, so flee"; it says "the place where you currently are is already on fire, and you do not know it." Its target is not suffering as such but the ignorance that does not see the fire. Reading this difference correctly is one of the keys to reading the entire Lotus Sūtra.

## 2. Scriptural Foundations

### 2.1 Canonical Roots

#### 2.1.1 The Āgama Seed: Sūtra 197 of the Saṃyukta-āgama

The Burning House metaphor does not begin with the Mahāyāna. Already in the earliest layer of the Buddhist canon, fire (*aggi/agni*) appears as a core teaching image. Its most direct ancestor is found in Sūtra 197 of the eighth fascicle of the Chinese *Saṃyukta-āgama*, known in the Pali tradition as the *Ādittapariyāya-sutta* (*Saṃyutta Nikāya* 35.28) and traditionally rendered into English as the "Fire Sermon."[^2] The text reads:

> Bhikṣus, all is burning. What is "all is burning"? The eye is burning; forms are burning; eye-consciousness is burning; eye-contact is burning; whatever feeling arises conditioned by eye-contact — pleasant, painful, or neither — that too is burning. So too the ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind are burning; sounds, smells, tastes, tactile objects, and mental phenomena are burning; the corresponding consciousnesses, contacts, and feelings are burning. Burning with what? Burning with the fire of greed, the fire of hatred, the fire of delusion; burning with birth, aging, sickness, death, sorrow, lamentation, and distress.[^3]

Three structural features deserve marking. **First**, the Buddha's "all is burning" is not a generalized lament about worldly suffering. It is a precise inventory laid out across the complete framework of the six internal sense-bases, six external objects, six consciousnesses, six contacts, and six feelings. Fire is located at every node of the cognitive apparatus. **Second**, the causes of the burning are exactly identified: greed, hatred, and delusion (*rāga*, *dveṣa*, *moha* — the three poisons), supplemented by the existential burning of birth, aging, sickness, and death. **Third**, and most crucially, the audience for this sermon is the thousand "matted-hair brahmins" who had until that moment been *fire-worshippers* — the followers of Uruvilvā-kāśyapa, whose religious practice had centered on the maintenance of sacred fire. The Buddha's choice of metaphor is not arbitrary. He preached *the fire of the senses* to people whose entire spiritual life had been organized around *the fire of the altar*. The metaphor was a precision instrument: it asked them to turn from the fire they tended to the fire they were. The text closes: "Then the thousand bhikṣus, hearing what the Buddha had said, gave rise to no further outflows; their minds attained liberation."[^4]

The historical depth of this conversion is preserved in a verse from the *Aitareya Saṃyukta* (*Bieyi-zayan-han*, T0100), where Kāśyapa himself confesses: "I was once exceedingly foolish, not recognizing the truth; I performed fire-sacrifices and ascetic torments, and called these the cause of liberation. Like a man born blind who cannot see the path of liberation."[^5] His former practice — fire-worship — is described as the blindness that mistakes the cause for the cure. True liberation, the verse implies, lies precisely in seeing that *one is oneself the fire*.

The "all is burning" metaphor is not isolated within the early canon. The series of sūtras 175–185 of the seventh fascicle of the *Saṃyukta-āgama* — the so-called "Hair-on-Fire" group — uses the figure "as a man whose hair and clothes have caught fire, what should he do?" as the opening trope, and applies it sequentially to the four foundations of mindfulness, the four right efforts, the five faculties, the five powers, the seven factors of awakening, the noble eightfold path, and the four noble truths. Each is presented as urgent precisely because of "the fire of impermanence."[^6] Sūtra 286 of fascicle twelve develops the image of "the fire of accumulating fuel," directly linking the twelve-link chain of dependent arising — "craving conditions clinging, clinging conditions becoming" — to "fire continuing to blaze through the long night."[^7] Fascicle thirty-one lists among the synonyms of nirvāṇa "free of blazing flame, free of burning."[^8] This last is structurally important: the literal meaning of *nirvāṇa* is *the extinguishing of fire*. Fire is not a literary flourish in the early canon; it is the core metaphor for the nature of saṃsāric existence itself.

From this vantage, the Lotus Sūtra's "burning house" is not a Mahāyāna invention. It is the dramatic completion of an ancient line: from individual meditative observation of the burning at the six sense-doors, the same insight is enlarged into a cosmic-scale narrative of a great house, a group of children, a father, a rescue.

#### 2.1.2 The Lotus Sūtra Text

The Burning House parable proper occurs in Chapter Three of Kumārajīva's translation of the *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka-sūtra*.[^9] The narrative unfolds in five nodes.

**First**, the description of the house itself. "Suppose, in a state, in a town, in a village, there is a great elder, advanced in years, of immeasurable wealth, with many fields and houses and many servants. His dwelling is vast; it has only one gate; many people live in it — one hundred, two hundred, even five hundred. Its halls have decayed; the walls are crumbling; the pillar-bases have rotted; the beams are leaning. Then, all at once, fire breaks out on every side and engulfs the house. The elder's children — perhaps ten, twenty, or thirty — are inside."

**Second**, the father's discovery. Seeing the fire, the elder is overcome with terror, and reflects: "I myself can escape safely through the burning gate, but my children — they are within the burning house, absorbed in their play, unaware, unalarmed, unfrightened. The fire is coming for them, suffering will press upon them, but their hearts feel no aversion, no thought of escape." The psychological texture of this passage is dense. The father first considers carrying the children out directly ("my own arms have strength; I could carry them out using my robe or a small table"), but immediately recognizes that the door is single and narrow, the children too young to understand, and that direct extraction might cause them to fall and be burned. He then tries the second strategy: telling them plainly. This too fails: "Though the father, out of love, gave skillful warnings, the children, absorbed in their play, would not believe him. They were not alarmed, not frightened, had no will to leave. They did not even know what 'fire' was, what 'house' meant, what 'destruction' could refer to. They simply ran east and west in their game, glancing at their father and continuing."

**Third**, the establishing of skillful means. "Now I shall set up a skillful means, that my children may be saved." Knowing each child's particular preferences for rare and curious toys, the father announces: "Children, the kinds of playthings you love — rare and hard to obtain — if you do not take them, you will surely regret it later. There are, just outside the door, goat-carts, deer-carts, and ox-carts of every kind. You may go and play with them. Quickly come out of the house, and whatever you wish, I shall give you." Hearing this, the children "rouse themselves with excitement, push past one another, and rush out of the burning house."

**Fourth**, the equal gift of the great cart. Once outside, each child says to the father: "Father, give us those things you promised — the goat-carts, deer-carts, and ox-carts!" But the father gives instead "to each child equally a single great cart" — a vast, jewel-adorned chariot drawn by a pure white ox of magnificent strength, attended by many retainers. His reasoning: "My wealth is boundless; I shall not give my children inferior small carts. These children are all my own; my love is without partiality."

**Fifth**, Śāriputra's confirmation. The Buddha asks Śāriputra whether the elder's gift of the precious great cart constitutes any falsehood. Śāriputra answers: "No, World-Honored One. The elder's intent was simply to save his children from the fire and preserve their lives, and this is no falsehood. Indeed, even if he had given them no cart at all, there would still be no falsehood, because his original intent was: 'By this skillful means, I shall enable them to escape.'" This exchange is the doctrinal pivot of the entire parable. It pre-empts the reading "the father lied" and shifts the question to a deeper level: what, exactly, is the relationship between *upāya* and reality?

The Buddha then applies the parable: "So too, Śāriputra, the Tathāgata is the father of all the world. ... He produces beings within the burning house of the three realms in order to save them from the fires of birth, aging, sickness, death, sorrow, suffering, ignorance, and the three poisons." The cosmic image of the *three-realm burning house* is here formally established. The verse section that follows gives one of the most famous lines in all of Mahāyāna literature: "*The three realms know no peace; they are like a burning house. They are filled with every suffering, supremely fearful. There are always birth, aging, sickness, death, and grief — fires like these, ceaselessly blazing.*"[^10]

Dharmarakṣa's earlier translation, the *Zhengfahua jing* (T0263, 286 CE), provides an interesting variant.[^11] In Dharmarakṣa's version, the three carts are not goat / deer / ox but **goat / horse / elephant** (with a fourth "music-cart" added as a lure). The final great cart is drawn by "a pure white great elephant" rather than a white ox. The bodhisattva-vehicle in Dharmarakṣa is symbolized by the elephant; in Kumārajīva, by the ox. The doctrinal significance is unaffected: the difference belongs to the level of cultural-translational choice. What matters is the structural sequence of *three vehicles → one vehicle*, not the species of animal.

#### 2.1.3 Vasubandhu's Treatise

Vasubandhu's *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka-upadeśa*, preserved in Chinese as T1519 (Bodhiruci's translation) and T1520 (Ratnamati's translation), is the earliest Indian commentary on the Lotus Sūtra now extant.[^12] Vasubandhu's treatment of the Burning House is characterized by a structural restraint: he does not annotate the narrative details (the elder, the house, the gate, the children, the carts). Instead, he places the parable within a *seven parables ↔ seven inverted graspings* (七種增上慢) corrective system, and assigns the Burning House its function from the top:

> What are the seven kinds of inverted grasping mind, and how do the seven parables correct them? **The first is the inverted grasping mind that pursues merit upside down — that is to say, those who, while their worldly defilements blaze forth in increasing intensity, still seek the gloriously refined results that belong to gods and humans. To correct this, the Burning House parable is taught.**[^13]

The seven kinds correspond to seven types of "beings who fully possess defiled nature." The first is the "seeker of power" (*vibhūtyarthika*) — one who pursues worldly authority and divine-or-human felicity. Vasubandhu's diagnosis is pointed: such a person inhabits a world in which "the defilements blaze forth in growing intensity" (the three realms as burning house), yet his pursuit is directed toward "the gloriously refined results that belong to gods and humans" (the most luxurious seats inside that very burning house). The corrective method, as Vasubandhu describes it, is: "The first kind: by displaying various worldly roots-of-good and meditative-attainment merits as skillful means, cause them to delight; only then lead them into the great nirvāṇa."[^14] The skillful means (the three carts) come first, and the great nirvāṇa (the great white-ox cart) comes after — but both are present in the corrective movement.

Section 3.6 below takes up the question of why this particular type of person — not the most depraved, but the seeker of merit gone awry — is placed at the head of the seven-parable system.

### 2.2 The Indo-Sino Commentarial Field

The Chinese commentarial tradition on the Lotus Sūtra is exceptionally rich, and on the Burning House parable in particular three commentators are central to the present paper's argument.

**Zhiyi** (智顗, 538–597), founder of the Tiantai school, wrote both the *Fahua wenju* (T1718, "Sentence-by-sentence commentary on the Lotus") and the *Fahua xuanyi* (T1716, "Profound Meaning of the Lotus"). His treatment of the Burning House combines extremely fine philological exegesis with a frontal defense of the position that *upāya* is not deception. Zhiyi articulates a "five-place opening of three to reveal one" framework, in which the Burning House is the parable that opens the three to reveal the one *with reference to practice*.[^15]

**Jizang** (吉藏, 549–623), the systematizer of the Sanlun (Mādhyamika) school, wrote the *Fahua yishu* (T1721). His reading is governed by a "four-tier provisional / true" structure typical of his school's *fourfold-twofold-truth* method, and he gives a famously sharp judgment on the three carts: they are **a verbal skillful means — the father announces three carts when there are none — outside the door there is, and was, no cart at all**.[^16]

**Kuiji** (窺基, 632–682), the disciple of Xuanzang and the systematizer of the Faxiang (East-Asian Yogācāra) school, wrote the *Fahua xuanzan* (T1723). His reading is governed by the three-natures (*trisvabhāva*) system and the three-period doctrinal classification (*tri-paryāya*). On the question of the three carts, Kuiji's verdict converges remarkably with Jizang's: the three carts are *empty designations* — they have a name, but cannot be seen and cannot be ridden.[^17]

The convergence between Jizang and Kuiji on this single point — across the otherwise sharp divide between Mādhyamika and Yogācāra — is the topic of section 3.5.

## 3. Core Argument

### 3.1 The Narrative Structure of the Parable

The Burning House parable resolves into a three-part narrative arc: *crisis — skillful means — revelation*.

**The crisis section**: the great house, the children, the fire, the father's discovery. The central tension here is not the fire itself (the fire is a brute fact); it is the gap between the fact of the fire and the children's awareness of it. *They are in the fire and do not know they are in the fire.* Zhiyi's gloss on the four-character phrase 樂著嬉戲 ("absorbed in playful enjoyment") is exceptionally precise: "Attached to wrong views is *xi* (delight); attached to craving is *xi* (play). Or: indulgence in the four wrong views is *xi*; the futile loss of one's effort is *xi*. Indulgence in the five sense-objects is *xi*; gaining nothing thereby is *xi*. They live emptily and die uselessly with no thought of departure, like little children at play." Zhiyi then maps the same passage onto the five turbidities: "The play (*xi*) corresponds to the turbidity of views; the games (*xi*) correspond to the turbidity of defilements; the not-knowing-and-not-fearing corresponds to the turbidity of beings; the fire pressing upon the body corresponds to the turbidity of life-span; the absence of disgust and the absence of any wish for escape correspond to the turbidity of the kalpa."[^18] The "playing children" passage, in other words, is not a moral indictment. It is a clinical diagnosis. The text is describing an epistemological blindspot with surgical precision.

**The skillful-means section**: the father's two failed attempts (direct extraction and direct verbal warning), followed by the third strategy (the announcement of the three carts), followed by the children's spontaneous flight. The crucial fact about this section is the presence of the two failures. They are not narrative filler; they are the textual foundation for everything that follows. Had the father succeeded by simply carrying the children out, no skillful means would have been needed. Had he succeeded by simply naming the danger, the three carts would have been redundant. By placing both failures explicitly in the narrative, the sūtra is preemptively grounding *upāya* in necessity rather than choice. The father did not choose deception over clarity; clarity was attempted and failed. Skillful means is what remains when direct speech has been demonstrated to be incommunicable.

**The revelation section**: the children outside the house, their request for the three carts, the father's gift of the single great cart, Śāriputra's confirmation. Narratively this looks like an upgrade — the children get something better than what they asked for. Doctrinally it is not an upgrade but a *revelation*: what the children were really pursuing all along (the truly desirable thing) was the great cart itself, which they did not yet have the conceptual equipment to recognize under that name. Section 3.5 below treats this distinction in detail.

Zhiyi divides the entire parable into a "general parable of six elements" (*the elder, the house, the single gate, the five hundred persons, the breaking out of fire, the thirty children*) followed by detailed sub-parables.[^19] The present paper uses a leaner three-arc division, but the doctrinal weight is identical to Zhiyi's exegetical center.

### 3.2 The Āgama Seed and the Lotus Drama

Returning to *Saṃyukta-āgama* 197: when read against the Lotus parable, two structural correspondences and one decisive difference emerge.

**First correspondence: the object of burning.** *Saṃyukta-āgama* 197 locates the burning at every node of the cognitive apparatus — the six sense-bases, six objects, six consciousnesses, six contacts, six feelings. The Lotus parable locates it at the cosmological level — the *three realms* (*traidhātuka*). The desire-realm, form-realm, and formless-realm together constitute the entire saṃsāric architecture, and the Lotus parable simply scales the burning up from the meditative interior of one practitioner's senses to the existential frame of all beings everywhere. This is not a substitution of content; it is a transformation of scale. The same fire, the same fuel — only the room has gotten larger.

**Second correspondence: the cause of burning.** The early sūtra identifies the cause precisely as *the fire of greed, the fire of hatred, the fire of delusion*, supplemented by *birth, aging, sickness, death, sorrow, lamentation, and distress*. The Lotus parable, when the Buddha applies the figure to the Tathāgata's situation, repeats this list almost verbatim: "in order to save beings from the fires of birth, aging, sickness, death, sorrow, suffering, ignorance, and the three poisons."[^20] The "fuel" of the cosmic burning house is identical to the "fuel" identified at Gayāśīrṣa.

**The decisive difference**: the treatment of *not knowing*. The audience for *Saṃyukta-āgama* 197 was a thousand monks — already religiously motivated, already disposed to hear about burning. The Buddha needed only to articulate the scope and the fuel of the fire, and "they gave rise to no further outflows; their minds attained liberation." The audience for the Lotus parable is radically different: "the children were young and unaware ... they did not know what 'fire' was, what 'house' meant, what 'destruction' could refer to." This audience cannot even hear the basic vocabulary. The Lotus parable, therefore, must address a problem the Āgama did not need to confront directly: *what does one do with beings for whom the very word "fire" is empty?* This question is the inner driver that makes *upāya* — skillful means — central to the Lotus exegesis in a way it was not central to the early canon.

The Burning House is not a repetition of the Fire Sermon. It is an *extension* of it — into precisely those audiences whose ears the Fire Sermon cannot reach. The Lotus's work is not to say the dharma more deeply; it is to say the dharma more *widely* — wide enough that even the children who cannot hear the word "fire" can still be drawn out of the house.

⚠️ A clarification belongs here. This is not a graded judgment in which the Mahāyāna is "higher" than the early teachings. It is the same dharma at two scales, addressed to two different epistemic conditions. *Saṃyukta-āgama* 197 and the Burning House are structurally homologous and substantively continuous, but their pedagogical situations differ. Treating them as equivalent erases the inner driver of the *upāya* problematic in the Lotus. Treating them as disconnected severs the Lotus from its canonical roots and risks the misconstrual that the Mahāyāna is "non-Buddhist." The correct reading is: same insight, two scales — from individual meditative observation to cosmic-scale teaching narrative.

This completes the textual support for what the *S7_PLAN* has called the "Āgama Seed thesis" in the case of the Burning House.

### 3.3 The Doctrinal Structure of the Three-Realm Fire

The compound expression "burning house of the three realms" deserves to be unpacked carefully.

The *three realms* (*traidhātuka*) is the basic existential architecture inherited from early Buddhism. It consists of the desire-realm (*kāmadhātu*), the form-realm (*rūpadhātu*), and the formless-realm (*ārūpyadhātu*). This division is *not* a horizontal classification by suffering or pleasure (that would be the "five destinies" — hells, hungry ghosts, animals, humans, gods). It is a vertical classification *by the object of attachment*: the desire-realm is structured by attachment to the five sense-objects; the form-realm by attachment to the bliss of meditative absorption; the formless-realm by attachment to extremely subtle mental states. When the Lotus parable says the *three realms* are burning — rather than saying the *five destinies* are burning — it makes a striking claim: **all three layers of attachment, not just the gross ones, are on fire**. Even one who has reached the highest formless attainment (the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception) is still inside the burning house. The fire on that floor is more refined and harder to detect, but it is still fire.

This judgment carries enormous pedagogical weight. If only the desire-realm were on fire, then meditative attainment in the form-realm would suffice for liberation. If only the form-realm were also on fire, then formless attainment would suffice. But the Lotus's verdict is unconditional: the entire house is burning. There is no "in-house liberation" — no peace to be found at any level within the three realms. This judgment closes the door on substituting meditative bliss for nirvāṇa, and equally on substituting divine or human felicity for buddhahood. Vasubandhu's identification of the parable's target as "the seeker of power" — one who pursues divine and human refined results — is precise *because* of this verdict: such a person's project, however refined, is structurally constrained to remain inside the fire.

The *fire* itself refers to the three poisons — greed, hatred, delusion — together with the existential burnings of birth, aging, sickness, and death. Kuiji's gloss on the phrase "the burning house of the three realms" in the *Fahua xuanzan* makes this explicit: "*The burning house of the three realms* refers to the inner fuel; *the coarse and shabby five sense-objects* refer to the outer environment. Beings are urged to leave the suffering of inner and outer alike, not to give rise to attachment to the fruit-body itself; for through attachment, the burning of defilements arises and suffering proliferates."[^21] The three-realm fire is not an external fire that has arrived from elsewhere. It is the three poisons unfolding throughout the entire existential architecture in which they are operative.

**The "playing children" image is the most easily misread element of the parable.** On the surface it can look like a reproach: *the children are inside the fire, why don't they leave?* But this is the wrong reading. Zhiyi maps "play" and "games" onto the turbidities of view and defilement, and crucially maps "not-knowing and not-fearing" onto the turbidity of beings — that is, onto the *condition* of being an unawakened being, not onto a moral failing. The children do not see the fire because seeing-the-fire is precisely what *avidyā* (ignorance) precludes. The fundamental feature of ignorance is its inability to recognize its own situation. If ignorance could see itself, it would not be ignorance. So the parable's diagnosis of "playing children who do not know" is not a verdict on the children's character; it is a description of the structural unfreedom of any cognitive system that has not yet seen through itself.

This shifts the Burning House parable's central question from *"why don't the beings leave the fire?"* to *"why can't the beings even hear the word 'fire'?"* The answer to that question is *upāya* — skillful means. And the question of whether *upāya* is honest is the doctrinal main battlefield of the entire parable. We turn to it now.

### 3.4 The Father's Skillful Means Is Not Deception

The most dangerous interpretive trap in the entire parable is to read it as a story about a benevolent lie. On the surface this reading is plausible: the father *did* promise three carts and *did* give one cart instead. There is, on the face of it, a gap between what was said and what was delivered, and in ordinary human ethics such a gap is exactly what we call deception. But this reading carries a catastrophic doctrinal consequence. If the Buddha's *upāya* is structurally a lie, then the entire dharma — the four truths, the twelve links of dependent origination, the eightfold path, and ultimately nirvāṇa itself — can be relativized to "things said for the sake of liberation that are not literally true." Once that move is made, the dharma collapses into a hollow shell of nihilism.

Precisely because this trap is so deadly, the entire Lotus commentarial tradition — Vasubandhu, Zhiyi, Jizang, Kuiji — treats "skillful means is not deception" as a problem that must be confronted directly. The present paper integrates three layers of answer, moving from the surface to the epistemological foundation.

**Layer one: Jizang — there were no three carts to begin with.** Jizang's verdict in fascicle six of the *Fahua yishu* is direct:

> The point is to show that the three vehicles are skillful means and that as a doctrinal matter there are no three; to show that the one vehicle is the truth and that as a doctrinal matter there is one. ... If outside the door there had really been three carts, then when the children came out, they would have seen the three at once and would not have asked for them. *The very fact that they ask for the three carts proves that outside the door there were no three carts.* This confirms that the father's earlier words — that as a matter of truth there are no three, and the three are taught only as a skillful means — are correct.[^22]

The argument is reductio ad absurdum. *If the three carts had been actual physical objects waiting outside the door, the children would have seen them upon emerging and would not have needed to ask for them.* But the children do ask. Their request is itself the proof that the three carts never existed in the world; they existed only in the father's announcement. The three carts are *purely verbal skillful means* — what Jizang calls "*kou-fangbian*, vocal upāya: announcing three when there are none, only as a finger-pointing outside the door."[^23] They are linguistic devices, not promises about physical objects.

Once this judgment is in place, the entire structure of the "father lied" complaint collapses. The father did not promise physical carts and then fail to deliver them. The father did exactly one thing, from beginning to end: he uttered the sentence "outside there is something better than what you have." That sentence is *literally true* — there is something better outside, and that something is the great cart. The father simply could not, given the children's cognitive condition, name that something in language they would recognize. So he used vocabulary they did recognize — toy carts of three kinds — to point at it. When the children come out, they receive the great cart. There is no broken promise, because there was no promise of physical objects in the first place.

**Layer two: Zhiyi — the father never violated his original intention.** Even if one stays at the surface level of the narrative, Zhiyi's defense in the *Fahua wenju* completes the case:

> The Tathāgata appears in the world originally intending to teach the great. But because those of small wisdom remain attached to the three realms, he uses skillful means as a lure; once they have come out, he gives them the great vehicle, and this corresponds to his original heart. Thus he says "I am able to give beings the great vehicle, but they cannot fully receive it." If, as in the *Avataṃsaka*, they had been able to receive it, he would have given the great directly, without first opening the one into three. Because they could not receive it, he used skillful means to divide the one Buddha-vehicle into three. *The three came from the beings, not from the Buddha's original intention*, and this is how the parable establishes that there is no falsehood and no deviation from the original heart.[^24]

The structure of the argument is this: the father's *original intention* (本心) — to give the great cart — never changed. What changed was his *method*, and the method changed because of the children's incapacity to receive the great vehicle directly. The three carts came from the children, not from the father. Hence Zhiyi's principle: skillful means is non-deceiving precisely insofar as it remains *faithful to the original intention even while modulating the form*. There is no falsehood when there is no betrayal of intent. Elsewhere in the same commentary Zhiyi formulates the point sharply: "Now, an earthly king's words are not duplicitous; how should the Buddha as Dharma-king tolerate empty speech? *Skillful means may be provisional, but does the truth therefore become false?*"[^25] The terms *provisional* and *artificial* (權, 假) belong to a different category from the term *false* (妄). The provisional is "a form temporarily adopted for the sake of a deeper end"; the false is "an utterance that does not correspond to the truth." Worldly lies are the latter; the Buddha's *upāya* is the former.

**Layer three: Yogācāra — *upāya* is the mode in which truth operates within the conventional world.** The deepest answer comes from Yogācāra epistemology. *Cheng weishi lun* (T1585), fascicle eight, gives the precise account:

> It is not without seeing the *pariniṣpanna* (perfected nature) that one can see the *paratantra* (other-dependent nature). Without realizing the emptiness of the *parikalpita* (imagined nature), one cannot truly know the *paratantra* as it is. **Only after non-discriminating cognition has realized suchness can post-attainment cognition (*pṛṣṭha-labdha-jñāna*) understand the other-dependent nature as illusion-like.** ... Therefore the verse: "*It is not the case that one fails to see suchness and yet understands phenomena to be like illusion — though they appear, they are not real.*"[^26]

The two cognitions of Yogācāra — the root *non-discriminating cognition* (*nirvikalpa-jñāna*) and *post-attainment cognition* (*pṛṣṭha-labdha-jñāna*) — are not two different cognitive faculties. They are two functional aspects of the same wisdom: the root cognition directly realizes suchness; the post-attainment cognition then sees the *paratantra* (the dependently-arisen phenomenal world) "as if illusion" — that is, knows it to be conventional appearance. **The crucial point is that post-attainment cognition is not "downgraded truth" or "truth adjusted for teaching purposes." Post-attainment cognition is what suchness itself looks like when it operates in the world of dependent arising.** The Buddha's *upāya* is not a workaround that bypasses truth in order to say something untrue; *upāya is what truth does when it acts in the conventional world*.

*Cheng weishi lun* fascicle nine reinforces this: of the ten perfections, only the sixth (prajñā) is purely the work of non-discriminating cognition; "**the latter four [skillful means, vow, power, knowledge] all belong to post-attainment cognition, and operate at the level of the conventional**."[^27] Skillful means *is*, by definition, prajñā operating in conventional form. It is not a separate faculty from wisdom; it is wisdom turned outward. The father in the parable can deploy the language of toy carts precisely *because* he has already seen the great cart in its full reality. Without that prior seeing, no skillful means could be designed at all.

The three layers together yield a complete answer to "is *upāya* deception?":

1. **Surface**: there were no three carts (Jizang). The complaint of broken promises rests on a false premise.
2. **Middle**: the father's intention was never compromised (Zhiyi). The provisional is not the false.
3. **Deep**: skillful means just *is* truth's mode of operation in the conventional world (Yogācāra post-attainment cognition). Teaching is not a substitute for truth; it is truth's outward face.

What remains unanswered is the experiential question: *why* was direct speech impossible in the first place? Why could the father not simply shout "the house is on fire"? This is the work of section 4 below.

### 3.5 No Three Carts Outside the Door: A Rare Madhyamaka–Yogācāra Convergence

Section 3.4 used Jizang's "no three carts outside the door" judgment as the first layer of the *upāya* defense. That judgment deserves its own section, because it carries a remarkable historical fact: on this specific question, Mādhyamika (in the person of Jizang) and Yogācāra (in the person of Kuiji) **converge completely** — and these two traditions disagree on most other questions in Mahāyāna doctrine. The convergence is both an exegetical convenience and a fact requiring explanation.

**Kuiji's verdict.** In the *Fahua xuanzan* fascicle five, Kuiji gives an extremely fine analysis of the "cart-body" of the three carts:

> Here we definitively take *the differentiating omniscient cognition* as the body of the cart; hence the sūtra repeatedly says "ultimately leading them to attain the all-knowing wisdom." This is what makes [the great vehicle] a cart, and what makes it great. ... For the third sense — that the dwelling place is between turbulence and quiet — *the goat- and deer-carts are merely names that have been set up; they cannot be seen, and one cannot board them*. They take as their body the small and middling omniscient cognitions of the two vehicles; if their body were nirvāṇa they would be the magic city instead. ... Therefore one knows that *the contaminated post-attainment cognition and worldly samādhi obtained by the two vehicles are merely called "omniscient cognition" by way of empty designation — they serve as the bodies of the two carts. They are named but not seen, and not boarded — for they cannot truly differentiate and witness the dharmas*.[^28]

Kuiji's verdict, in Yogācāra technical terms: the three carts are *empty designations*. They have a name (*śabda-prajñapti*), but no actual carriage-body that one can sit in and ride. The genuine cart-body — the actually-functional vehicle that can carry beings to liberation — exists only at one level: the all-knowing wisdom (*sarvajña-jñāna*) of the Buddha-vehicle, represented by the great white-ox cart.

**Jizang's verdict** (already cited above): "outside the door, if there really had been three carts, the children upon emerging would have seen them and would not have asked for them. The fact that they ask proves that there were no three carts outside the door."

When these two verdicts are placed side by side, the structural agreement is striking:

| Issue | Kuiji (Yogācāra) | Jizang (Madhyamaka) |
|-------|------------------|---------------------|
| Existential status of the three carts | Empty designation; named but lacking carriage-body | Vocal upāya; outside the door no carts existed |
| "Body" of the three carts | Contaminated post-attainment cognition / worldly samādhi (empty designations of omniscience) | Nothing physical; pure linguistic device |
| Body of the great cart | Uncontaminated all-knowing cognition (*sarvajña-jñāna*) | Middle Way One Vehicle (defects exhausted, virtues fulfilled) |
| Meaning of the children's request | The request proves the three carts are empty designations | The request proves there were no three carts outside |
| Three carts → great cart | Not an upgrade but a revelation | Not a fulfillment but a revelation |

The technical vocabularies differ — Kuiji uses *omniscient cognition*, *paratantra*, *pariniṣpanna*; Jizang uses *Middle Way*, *provisional*, *true* — but the conclusions are identical: *the three carts are pure linguistic devices, and there were never any three carts outside the door*. The historical reason for this convergence is probably that, whether one approaches the question via the Madhyamaka principle "all dharmas are empty, therefore *upāya* speaks of the existent" or via the Yogācāra principle "contaminated post-attainment cognition is an empty designation while uncontaminated all-knowing cognition is the only real vehicle," the conclusion can only go one way. The convergence reflects a deeper fact: *as soon as the concept of skillful means is taken seriously, only one conclusion is structurally available*.

A structural corollary follows from this verdict: **the proper location of the three-natures mapping in the Lotus Sūtra is not inside the parable, but at the level of the entire teaching system that the parable enacts.** Kuiji's analysis of the three natures in the *Fahua xuanzan* fascicle four maps them onto the *three-period doctrinal classification* — not onto the three carts. He writes: "the *Āgama* phase is the pure-existence teaching that corrects the misreading of *paratantra* as real; the *Prajñāpāramitā* phase is the pure-emptiness teaching that removes the *parikalpita*; the Lotus phase is the *three-natures-fully-taught* teaching, and is therefore called the truth."[^29] The mapping is between *historical teaching phases* and *the three natures as a corrective program*, not between the goat / deer / ox carts as separate objects and the three natures as separate substances. (Section 3.5 of S7-P02 has already given the full analysis of this passage.)

This means that the Lotus's *upāya–reality* correspondence and the Yogācāra *trisvabhāva* mapping are both performed at the meta-level: the entire Lotus Sūtra is itself the "three-natures-fully-taught" teaching, and the Burning House parable is that teaching's narrative enactment. Inside the parable, the carts are not allegories of separate natures; they are the dramatic figure for "the way the previous teaching system spoke," contrasted with the great cart, which is the dramatic figure for "the way the Lotus teaching itself speaks." This avoids the common interpretive error that maps goat = *parikalpita*, deer = *paratantra*, ox = *pariniṣpanna*. Such a mapping has no support in any of the major commentaries, and on closer inspection it produces nonsense — what would the great cart then represent? *Pariniṣpanna of pariniṣpanna*?

**A bridge to S2-P02.** A structural observation deserves to be marked here as the first instance of a broader pattern in the S7 series. S2-P02 of this project mapped the *Heart Sūtra*'s description of Avalokiteśvara onto a four-phase awakening loop: *seeing through → introspection → going deep → liberating from suffering*. The Burning House narrative, when read as a whole, exhibits an isomorphic four-phase structure at cosmic scale:

| Heart Sūtra loop (S2-P02) | Burning House loop (this paper) |
|---------------------------|----------------------------------|
| **Seeing through** (the elder sees the five aggregates as empty) | **Seeing the fire** (the elder sees the great fire on all four sides) |
| **Introspection** (examining one's own grasping) | **Calling the children** (recognizing the children's obliviousness, deliberating on means) |
| **Going deep** (entering the perfection of wisdom) | **Leaving the house** (the children rush out under the influence of skillful means) |
| **Liberating from suffering** | **The gift of the great cart** (the equal bestowal of buddhahood) |

The two loops differ enormously in scale — the Heart Sūtra describes the awakening process of one practitioner in meditation; the Burning House describes the Buddha's pedagogy directed at all beings — but their structures are isomorphic: a closed circuit from *recognition* to *action* to *completion*. The point of the comparison is not analogical decoration. It is to mark that the *embracing* of the Lotus and the *seeing through* of the Heart Sūtra are the same awakening structure unfolded at two different scales. The internal coherence of the eight-stage architecture of this project receives, at this point, one more concrete confirmation. Subsequent papers in the S7 series (the parable of the lost son, the parable of the herbs, the parable of the magic city) will continue to test the applicability of this loop structure to each of the major Lotus parables.

### 3.6 Vasubandhu's Diagnosis: Why the First Parable Targets the "Seeker of Power"

Returning to Vasubandhu's seven-parable system: the Burning House occupies the first position, and corresponds to the "seeker of power" — "one who pursues divine and human refined results while his worldly defilements blaze forth in growing intensity." Kuiji's commentary in *Fahua xuanzan* fascicle five sharpens this further: "*Wealth and self-determination are called 'power'; this means the ordinary person who pursues these external and internal results of the divine-human refined sphere, not knowing that the defilements blaze and burn. For this reason the Burning House parable is taught — because the divine-human sphere of power is itself burnt by defilements, just like a burning house*."[^30]

Two features of this diagnosis deserve sustained attention.

**First**: why is the first of the seven parables not directed at "the most depraved" beings? Common sense would suggest that a graduated system of correctives should treat the most severe pathology first. But Vasubandhu places the Burning House at the head of the list, and it corresponds to a type that on the surface appears not depraved at all — the seeker of merit, the one pursuing divine and human felicity, the most worldly-virtuous of religious aspirants. Why?

The answer is that this type is the most numerous, the most insidious, and the most invisible to itself. The truly depraved are a small minority of the population, and their pathology is usually visible to themselves (and certainly visible to others). The "seeker of power," by contrast, encompasses nearly everyone with religious motivation, ethical sensibility, and aspiration toward favorable rebirth — that is, *most readers of religious texts begin in this category*. And the seeker's defect is structurally hidden from himself: he is engaged in serious practice, in active virtue, in the pursuit of refined goods, and he very rarely questions his direction. Precisely because he does not question the direction, he cannot see that *the entire field within which he is pursuing the good is itself the burning house*. The Burning House parable corrects this type not by saying "you are wrong" — that would be heard as moral judgment — but by reframing the cognitive map: *your supposed safe haven is itself another room of the same fire*. By rendering the entire three-realm field as burning, the parable forecloses the possibility that any "improved felicity within the three realms" can substitute for genuine liberation.

**Second**: where does the line lie between "seeker of power" and "seeker of buddhahood"? On the surface the difference might look quantitative — the seeker of power has small ambitions, the seeker of buddhahood has large ambitions. But structurally the difference is qualitative: the former takes "the pursuit of the good *within* the burning house" as the goal, while the latter takes "leaving the burning house" as the goal. The difference is not in the quality of the object pursued, but in whether the coordinate system itself includes "outside the house" as a possible direction. A person can spend an entire lifetime in the highest divine sphere and still be a seeker of power, because his coordinate system contains no dimension labeled *outside the burning house*. Another person, even if currently in extremely bad circumstances, has already ceased to be a seeker of power the moment "outside the burning house" enters his coordinate system as a real possibility.

The Burning House parable does precisely this coordinate-redrawing. It says: *what you take to be your pursuit of the good is the pursuit of a good interior to the burning house; the genuine good is outside the door*. The redrawing transforms the seeker of power's entire worldview. Vasubandhu's placement of the Burning House at the head of the seven parables is precise because of this: it does not address those who refuse to practice; it addresses those whose direction of practice has been wrongly oriented. And the latter group is much larger than the former.

## 4. Cross-Disciplinary Resonance: Normalcy Bias and the "Playing Children"

⚠️ Heuristic analogy

Section 3.4 left a question unanswered: *why* could the father not simply shout "the house is on fire"? The sūtra answers "the children were too young to understand," but this answer requires elaboration, lest "the children couldn't understand" be reduced to a problem of mere intelligence and lose the diagnostic precision of the original passage.

Contemporary disaster psychology has a core concept called *normalcy bias*. It describes a widespread cognitive pattern in human beings: when faced with an emergency or disaster scenario, people tend to *normalize* the situation, deny its severity, and continue with their ordinary routines rather than initiate emergency response.[^31] The most striking case studies come from interviews with survivors of the 9/11 attacks. After the towers were struck, many people inside did not immediately evacuate — they returned to their desks to gather belongings, called family members, organized files. Cognitively they were unable to register that what had occurred fell within the category "real emergency," and so they continued their ordinary routines. Earthquake studies, tsunami warnings, and fire drills show the same pattern: when an alarm sounds, the first reaction of most people is not to flee but to *check what others are doing*, *confirm whether it is real*, *gather one's things*. The phenomenon is so consistent that emergency-response researchers have given it a name: *milling time* — the average duration of hesitation before evacuation begins.

The mechanism of normalcy bias is roughly this: human cognition relies heavily on the *continuity of past experience* to assess the nature of present circumstances. When an event exceeds the range of past experience, the default response of the cognitive system is not "initiate emergency protocol" but "force the event back into a familiar category" — *this must be just a drill; the fire won't reach this floor; it has never happened before, so probably nothing this time either*. This mechanism is evolutionarily functional (it prevents the organism from over-reacting to every minor variation in its environment), but in genuinely critical situations, it kills.

Place this mechanism beside the parable's "children playing, unaware, unalarmed, unafraid," and the precision of the four-character description is remarkable — almost a mirror of contemporary cognitive science. *Why did the father's direct warning fail?* Because for children who have never experienced fire, the words *fire*, *burning*, and *house in danger* are merely abstract vocabulary — they cannot connect them to any present sensory experience. The sūtra is explicit: "they did not even know what 'fire' was, what 'house' meant, what 'destruction' could refer to." This is not low intelligence. It is a *fundamental rupture between concept and experience*. Where this rupture exists, any direct warning is automatically normalized: *this can't be real*, *father is just trying to scare us*, *let us continue playing*.

It is precisely because of this cognitive rupture that "establishing skillful means" becomes the only available option. The father cannot use the word "fire" because the word has no purchase on the children's cognitive system. He must use something the children can immediately connect to — *toys*. The sentence "outside there is something better to play with" bypasses conceptual identification entirely and addresses the children's already-functional motivational system. This is not "lowered truth"; this is *the only possible channel for truth-transmission given the cognitive rupture*.

⚠️ Irreducibility statement: *normalcy bias* is a descriptive concept from contemporary cognitive psychology, applicable to "individuals' reactions in specific disaster scenarios." The Lotus's "playing children" is a cosmological-level metaphor for *avidyā*, applicable to "the entire condition of unawakened beings." The former is an empirical-level observation; the latter is an ontological-level diagnosis. The two are *structurally homologous* (both point to a cognitive invisibility of the present emergency) but *categorially irreducible*. Concretely: normalcy bias can be mitigated through training, drills, and exposure therapy; the Lotus's "playing children" requires the corrective level of the Buddha's skillful means — no amount of worldly training can resolve it. The purpose of the analogy is to give modern readers a sharper feel for the precision of the original diagnosis, not to reduce the latter to the former.

## 5. Middle Way Corrections

❌ **Correction One: The father's skillful means is not deception.** (The main battlefield of this paper.) This is the easiest and most dangerous misreading. Reading it as "the father lied but the result was good" relativizes the entire dharma into *things said for the sake of liberation that are not literally true*. The correct reading, established in section 3.4 above, is the three-layer answer: the three carts are vocal *upāya* (Jizang) + the father remained faithful to his original intention (Zhiyi) + post-attainment cognition is the mode in which truth itself operates within the conventional world (Yogācāra). Together, these three layers structurally invalidate the equation *upāya = lying*.

❌ **Correction Two: The fire of the three realms is not another name for the truth of suffering.** It is tempting to read the Burning House's fire as another metaphor for the first noble truth — "the three realms are suffering, therefore the three realms are fire." This is a category error. The truth of suffering addresses the *fact of suffering*; the burning house addresses *the condition of not knowing one is on fire*. The target of the Burning House parable is not suffering itself but ignorance. A person can fully accept the truth of suffering and still remain inside the burning house — so long as he believes his suffering can be remedied by some "better refined result still within the three realms." The Burning House insists: no remedy located within the three realms is a genuine remedy.

❌ **Correction Three: The great white-ox cart is not a higher-grade version of the three carts.** The great cart is not "an upgrade" but "what the three carts were always pointing toward, which the children could not yet recognize under any other name." Reading it as an upgrade introduces a false hierarchy: śrāvaka = beginner, pratyekabuddha = intermediate, bodhisattva = advanced, buddhahood = top tier. This hierarchy is contradicted by the Lotus's own verdict — "neither two nor three" — which insists that the three carts are from the beginning three names for the same vehicle, recognized differently by beings of different capacities.

❌ **Correction Four: "All is burning" (Āgama) is not doctrinally identical to "burning house" (Lotus).** The two are structurally homologous (both deploy fire as the core metaphor) and substantively continuous (both identify the three poisons + birth-aging-sickness-death as the fuel), but their pedagogical situations differ. The Āgama's audience consists of religiously motivated practitioners; the Lotus's audience includes "those for whom even the word 'fire' is empty." Treating the two as identical erases the inner driver of the *upāya* problematic in the Lotus. Treating them as disconnected severs the Lotus from its canonical roots and risks the misconstrual of "the Mahāyāna is non-Buddhist." The correct reading is *same insight, two scales* — from individual meditative observation to cosmic pedagogy.

❌ **Correction Five: "The playing children" is not a moral reproach against beings.** It is a diagnosis, not a verdict. Zhiyi's mapping of "play" and "games" onto the turbidities of view and defilement, and his mapping of "not-knowing-and-not-fearing" onto the turbidity of beings, is a precise description of the *condition* of unawakened existence, not a value judgment. Beings playing in the fire are not so because they are stupid or wicked; they are so because the very nature of *avidyā* is the inability to see one's own situation. The correct emotional response from a reader of this passage is not "how pitiable beings are" or "how foolish they are" — both of these miss the point — but rather *am I myself currently playing? Are the goods I am pursuing themselves interior to the burning house?* That self-directed inquiry is the parable's actual function.

## 6. Conclusion

The Parable of the Burning House is the first major parable of the Lotus Sūtra's Trace Gate. Its work is not to say "the world is suffering, so flee" but to say "the place where you currently stand is already on fire, and you do not know it." Its target is not suffering as such but the ignorance that does not see the fire. From *Saṃyukta-āgama* 197 to the *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka*'s burning house, the central Buddhist metaphor undergoes a dramatic enlargement of scale — from individual meditative observation of the burning at the six sense-doors to a cosmic-scale narrative of the three realms — but its substantive content does not change. The same fire, the same fuel: the three poisons and the existential burning of birth, aging, sickness, and death, deployed throughout the entire saṃsāric architecture.

The parable's doctrinal main weight rests on the question of whether *upāya* is deception. The three traditions of the Lotus commentarial field — Jizang's "outside the door there were no three carts," Zhiyi's "the three came from the beings, not from the Buddha's intent," and the Yogācāra account of post-attainment cognition as the mode in which truth itself operates within the conventional world — together provide three layers of mutually reinforcing answer. The unusual convergence between Jizang (Madhyamaka) and Kuiji (Yogācāra) on the specific judgment "the three carts are pure linguistic devices" points to a deeper fact: *as soon as the concept of skillful means is taken seriously, only one conclusion is available*.

Vasubandhu's seven-parable corrective system places the Burning House at the head of the list, and assigns to it the correction of the *seeker of power* — those who pursue the refined felicity of gods and humans within the three realms, who appear to be pursuing the good but who remain entirely interior to the burning house. This diagnosis is more precise and more universal than the diagnosis of the "obviously depraved," because the seeker of power encompasses nearly everyone with religious motivation and aspiration toward favorable rebirth — that is, most readers of the Lotus Sūtra are themselves in this category.

Within the larger eight-stage architecture of the present project, the Burning House is the Lotus series' first demonstration of *embracing*. It lays out, in narrative form, the diagnostic of ignorance, the structure of skillful means, and the revelatory transformation from "three carts" to "one cart" — and it does so in a methodological pattern that the rest of the Lotus series will continue: *Āgama seed → Lotus dramatization → middle-way correction → tripartite commentarial dialogue*. The structural isomorphism between this parable and the four-phase awakening loop of S2-P02 (Heart Sūtra) opens, for the first time in the Lotus series, the path for testing the *awakening loop thesis* across all the major Lotus parables.

To read the Burning House parable rightly is not to grasp the metaphor "the world is like a burning house." It is to grasp the diagnosis *I myself am currently in the burning house and do not know it*. The first reading takes the parable as an object; the second reading is taken in by the parable. The Lotus's *embracing* begins here.
