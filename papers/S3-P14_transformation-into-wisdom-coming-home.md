## Abstract

The *Treatise on the Hundred Dharmas* (*Dasheng Baifalun* 大乘百法明門論) opens with "As the World-Honored One said: all dharmas are without self" and closes with "selflessness is of two kinds: *pudgala-nairātmya* and *dharma-nairātmya*." Between these two sentences, the entire text unfolds one hundred dharmas across five categories, forming a perfect arc of *anātman*. The preceding thirteen papers have examined every component of this arc — from the eight consciousnesses through mental factors, form dharmas, dissociated formations, and unconditioned dharmas. Now P14 gathers everything back: what is the ultimate destination of these hundred components?

The answer lies in the stated purpose of the *Cheng Weishi Lun* (成唯識論): "The establishment of consciousness-only is intended for sentient beings to attain the two fruits of basis-transformation (*āśraya-parāvṛtti*)." The entire Yogācāra edifice exists not for the memorization of one hundred terms, but for the transformation of consciousness into wisdom (*vijñāna-parāvṛtti*) and the realization of *mahānirvāṇa* and *mahābodhi*. The hundred dharmas are the map; transformation into wisdom is coming home.

This paper takes *Cheng Weishi Lun* fascicles 9 and 10 as its primary axis, tracing the theme of transformation upstream through the Āgamas' teachings on liberation and non-self, the Prajñāpāramitā's method of non-attainment, the *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra*'s three turnings of the Dharma wheel, the *Śūraṅgama*'s perfect penetrations, the *Avataṃsaka*'s ten grounds, the *Laṅkāvatāra*'s formula of five dharmas–three natures–eight consciousnesses–two selflessnesses, the *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka*'s one vehicle, and the *Vimalakīrtinirdeśa*'s non-dual gate. It then synthesizes the four transformation verses from the *Bāshí Guījǔ Sòng* (八識規矩頌), closes the arc from P01's "all dharmas are without self" to P14's "two selflessnesses," and completes the S1 (Faith) → S2 (Understanding) → S3 (Practice) three-series trajectory that constitutes the first movement of this research project.

**Keywords:** transformation of consciousness into wisdom (vijñāna-parāvṛtti), four wisdoms (catvāri jñānāni), five stages (pañcāvasthā), two selflessnesses (dvividha-nairātmya), basis-transformation (āśraya-parāvṛtti), Hundred Dharmas Treatise, Cheng Weishi Lun, Bāshí Guījǔ Sòng

**關鍵詞：** 轉識成智、四智圓明、五位修行、二無我、轉依、百法明門論、成唯識論、八識規矩頌

## I. Introduction: What Was This Journey For?

The first paper in this series (P01) opened with a question: "All dharmas are without self — which dharmas? What kind of selflessness?" Thirteen papers and well over a hundred thousand characters later, that question has been answered in painstaking detail: five categories, one hundred dharmas, each examined through the lens of the three natures (*trisvabhāva*).

Now, standing at the end of the map, we must face a more fundamental question: **Why did you need this map?**

This is not rhetorical. If the hundred dharmas are merely an elaborate classification system, then studying them produces the same result as studying any taxonomy — more knowledge, but no transformation. The hundred dharmas can each become objects of scholarship, but Vasubandhu did not compose the *Treatise on the Hundred Dharmas* for the sake of scholarship.

The *Cheng Weishi Lun* declares its purpose in its opening passage:

> 今造此論，為於二空有迷謬者生正解故。生解為斷二重障故……斷障為得二勝果故，由斷續生煩惱障故，證真解脫；由斷礙解所知障故，得大菩提。[^1]

> "This treatise is composed to generate correct understanding in those confused about the two emptinesses. Understanding is generated to sever two layers of obstruction. Obstruction is severed to attain two supreme fruits: by severing the afflictive obstruction that perpetuates rebirth, one realizes true liberation; by severing the cognitive obstruction that blocks understanding, one attains great awakening."

The logic chain is crystalline: composition → understanding → severance → fruit. Composition is means, not end. Understanding serves severance. Severance serves attainment. Attainment — *mahānirvāṇa* and *mahābodhi* — is the sole reason the entire Yogācāra system exists.

At the treatise's conclusion, *Cheng Weishi Lun* fascicle 9 closes the circle:

> 由轉煩惱得大涅槃，轉所知障證無上覺。成立唯識，意為有情證得如斯二轉依果。[^2]

> "By transforming affliction one attains mahānirvāṇa; by transforming the cognitive obstruction one realizes supreme awakening. The establishment of consciousness-only is intended for sentient beings to attain these two fruits of basis-transformation."

"The establishment of consciousness-only is intended for sentient beings to attain these two fruits of basis-transformation." This is the purpose-statement of the entire *Cheng Weishi Lun*, and by extension, the ultimate destination of the hundred dharmas. Every consciousness, every mental factor, every form dharma, every dissociated formation, every unconditioned dharma studied across thirteen papers — all that "knowing" serves one thing: transformation of consciousness into wisdom.

P13 showed you where "home" is — suchness (*tathatā*), the true nature of consciousness, the face reality has always worn. P14 tells you **how to get home.**

The path home has three dimensions: the **four wisdoms** (*catvāri jñānāni*) — the complete picture of eight consciousnesses transforming from defiled to purified; the **five stages** (*pañcāvasthā*) — the full route from study to Buddhahood; and the **two selflessnesses** (*dvividha-nairātmya*) — the ultimate realization to which the hundred dharmas point.

And this path is not just S3's conclusion. It simultaneously closes the arc of S1 (Faith — finding the mind) → S2 (Understanding — prajñā illumination) → S3 (Practice — the Yogācāra toolkit). S1 asked "who is driving?"; S2 revealed that the windshield is your own projection; S3 gave you a hundred-part schematic and repair manual. P14's task: now drive the car home.

## II. Scriptural Foundations

### Part A: Sūtras

#### 2.1 The Āgamas: The Original Teaching on Liberation and Transformation

The technical term *āśraya-parāvṛtti* ("basis-transformation") does not appear in the Āgama collections, but the Āgama teachings on "liberation of mind and liberation by wisdom" (*cetovimutti* and *paññāvimutti*) already establish the prototype structure of transformation.

> 若比丘於此識身及外境界一切相，無有我、我所見、我慢繫著使，及心解脫、慧解脫，現法自知作證具足住。……是名比丘斷愛欲，轉諸結，止慢無間等，究竟苦邊。[^3]

> "When a bhikṣu, regarding this consciousness-body and all external phenomena, has no view of self or mine, no bondage of conceit, and abides in liberation of mind and liberation by wisdom, directly knowing and realizing for himself — this is called a bhikṣu who has severed craving, *transformed* the fetters, ended conceit without remainder, and reached the ultimate end of suffering."

The word "transform" (*zhuǎn* 轉) already appears in the Āgamas: "transformed the fetters" — fetters are not annihilated but *turned* from a state of binding to a state of non-binding. "Liberation of mind, liberation by wisdom" — mind liberated from craving, wisdom liberated from ignorance. This dual liberation is the Āgama prototype for the later Yogācāra formula: "transforming affliction yields mahānirvāṇa; transforming the cognitive obstruction yields mahābodhi."

The *Saṃyuktāgama* further reveals the core mechanism through the contrast between "turning with consciousness" and "not turning with consciousness":

> 隨色轉、隨受轉……隨識轉。隨色轉故，不脫於色……不隨轉故，脫於色……我說脫於生、老、病、死、憂、悲、惱、苦。[^4]

> "One turns with form, turns with feeling … turns with consciousness. Turning with form, one is not freed from form … Not turning, one is freed from form … I declare such a one freed from birth, aging, sickness, death, grief, lamentation, and pain."

"Turning with" versus "not turning with" — ordinary beings turn with the five aggregates; noble ones do not. From S3's perspective, the hundred dharmas laid out in P01–P13, if you merely "turn with" them (remain captivated by their names and operations), constitute *saṃsāra*. If you "know them as they truly are" (*yathābhūtaṃ pajānāti*), that constitutes the beginning of liberation. The study of the hundred dharmas is precisely the cognitive foundation for moving from "turning with" to "not turning with."

The *Ekottarāgama* states it most concisely:

> 轉進其行，有漏盡，成無漏，心解脫、智慧解脫，自身作證而自遊戲：生死已盡，梵行已立，所作已辦，更不復受胎，如實知之。[^5]

> "Advancing in practice, outflows exhausted, becoming outflow-free, liberation of mind and liberation by wisdom, directly realizing for oneself: birth-and-death is ended, the holy life established, what was to be done is done, no further rebirth — knowing this as it truly is."

"Outflows exhausted, becoming outflow-free" — this is the core of basis-transformation: the shift from defiled to purified. The Āgamas state in the simplest possible language what Yogācāra will later elaborate with a precision system.

The Buddha's own realization provides the ultimate exemplar. Among the ten powers of a Tathāgata, the tenth is the power of the destruction of outflows:

> 復次，如來諸漏已盡，無漏心解脫、慧解脫，現法自知身作證……是名第十如來力。[^6]

> "Furthermore, the Tathāgata's outflows are entirely exhausted. In outflow-free liberation of mind and liberation by wisdom, he directly knows and realizes for himself … This is called the Tathāgata's tenth power."

Outflows exhausted, outflow-free, liberation of mind, liberation by wisdom, direct realization — the Buddha's own attainment is the consummate demonstration of basis-transformation.

#### 2.2 The Āgamas: Non-Self — The Scriptural Root of the Hundred Dharmas

The core thesis of the *Treatise on the Hundred Dharmas* — "all dharmas are without self" — traces its most original formulation to the Buddha's analysis of the five aggregates:

> 色非是我。若色是我者，不應於色病、苦生，亦不應於色欲令如是、不令如是。以色無我故，於色有病、有苦生……多聞聖弟子於此五受陰非我、非我所，如實觀察。如實觀察已，於諸世間都無所取，無所取故無所著，無所著故自覺涅槃。[^7]

> "Form is not self. If form were self, then form would not be subject to disease and suffering, and one could command form to be thus or not thus. Because form is without self, form is subject to disease and suffering … The well-instructed noble disciple, observing as they truly are that the five appropriated aggregates are not self and not belonging to self, takes nothing in all the world. Taking nothing, he clings to nothing. Clinging to nothing, he personally realizes nirvāṇa."

The logic is exquisite: if form were "self," then "self" should be able to control form — but you cannot choose not to age, not to sicken. The very uncontrollability of the aggregates constitutes evidence of non-self. The Āgamas operate at the level of five aggregates. The *Treatise on the Hundred Dharmas* extends this observation from five to one hundred — from "the five aggregates are without self" to "all dharmas are without self." The scope differs; the core logic is identical: nowhere among the hundred dharmas can you find a permanent, unitary, autonomous "self."

Crucially, the Buddha's teaching of non-self maintains a strict Middle Way:

> 我若答言有我，則增彼先來邪見；若答言無我，彼先癡惑豈不更增癡惑？……如來離於二邊，處中說法。[^8]

> "If I were to answer 'there is a self,' this would reinforce his prior wrong view. If I were to answer 'there is no self,' would this not deepen his prior confusion? … The Tathāgata, departing from both extremes, teaches the Dharma from the middle."

"There is a self" is eternalism; "there is no self," if taken as "there once was a self that is now annihilated," is nihilism. The Buddha teaches from the middle — through dependent origination. This Middle Way stance is the boundary P14 must strictly observe when discussing the two selflessnesses: non-self is not nihilism, but the dissolution of the superimposed (*parikalpita*) fiction of "self."

#### 2.3 Prajñāpāramitā: Non-Attainment and Ultimate Nirvāṇa

The Prajñāpāramitā literature supplies the fundamental methodology for basis-transformation — non-attainment as skillful means:

> 以無所得故，菩提薩埵依般若波羅蜜多故，心無罣礙；無罣礙故，無有恐怖，遠離顛倒夢想，究竟涅槃。三世諸佛依般若波羅蜜多故，得阿耨多羅三藐三菩提。[^9]

> "Because there is nothing to attain, bodhisattvas, relying on prajñāpāramitā, dwell with minds free from obstruction. Free from obstruction, they have no fear, transcend inverted dream-thinking, and reach ultimate nirvāṇa. All Buddhas of the three times, relying on prajñāpāramitā, attain *anuttarā samyak-saṃbodhi*."

"Because there is nothing to attain" — these words are the core condition for transformation. SUP01 detailed the transition from "having attainment" (*sopalambha*) in the preparatory stage to "having no attainment" (*nirupalambha*) at the Path of Seeing. The Prajñāpāramitā confirms here: not only bodhisattvas but all Buddhas depend on prajñā. Prajñā is the mother of Buddhas.

The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā Sūtra* further clarifies:

> 一切如來、應、正等覺，依甚深般若波羅蜜多，證一切法真如究竟乃得無上正等菩提，由此故說甚深般若波羅蜜多能生諸佛，是諸佛母。[^10]

> "All Tathāgatas, worthy ones, perfectly awakened ones, relying on the profound prajñāpāramitā, realize the ultimate suchness of all dharmas and thereby attain supreme perfect awakening. For this reason, the profound prajñāpāramitā is said to give birth to all Buddhas; it is the mother of Buddhas."

Prajñā is the method; suchness is what is realized. P13 established suchness as the true nature of consciousness; here, the Prajñāpāramitā confirms that prajñā illuminating suchness is the path to Buddhahood.

The distinction between "having attainment" and "non-attainment" directly corresponds to the entire work of the preparatory stage detailed in SUP01:

> 若善男子、善女人等無量無數無邊大劫，以有所得而為方便，勤修布施乃至般若波羅蜜多。有善男子、善女人等於深般若波羅蜜多，以無所得而為方便，至心聽聞、受持、讀誦……所獲福聚甚多於前。[^11]

> "If good men and women for immeasurable, countless, boundless great kalpas cultivate generosity through prajñāpāramitā with attainment as skillful means — yet other good men and women who, regarding the profound prajñāpāramitā, employ non-attainment as skillful means, wholeheartedly hearing, receiving, and reciting it … their accumulated merit far exceeds the former."

Practice "with attainment" over immeasurable kalpas is surpassed by a single moment of "non-attainment." From studying the hundred dharmas through the four investigations, from the four realizations of actuality to the Path of Seeing's non-discriminating wisdom — the entire direction is from "attainment" toward "non-attainment."

#### 2.4 The Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra: Three Turnings — Yogācāra as Definitive Teaching

The *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra* positions the entire Yogācāra system — including the *Treatise on the Hundred Dharmas* — within the Buddha's teaching through the schema of three turnings:

> 世尊！初於一時在婆羅痆斯……以四諦相轉正法輪……有上、有容，是未了義……
>
> 世尊！在昔第二時中……依一切法皆無自性……以隱密相轉正法輪……亦是有上、有所容受，猶未了義……
>
> 世尊！於今第三時中……以顯了相轉正法輪。第一甚奇、最為希有。于今世尊所轉法輪無上無容，是真了義，非諸諍論安足處所。[^12]

The first turning (Four Noble Truths) is "provisional" — surpassable, incomplete. The second (Prajñāpāramitā) is also "provisional" — taught in a concealed mode susceptible to nihilistic misreading. The third (Yogācāra) is "definitive" — taught in an explicit mode, unsurpassable, no foothold for disputation.

The Buddha confirms: "This is called the definitive teaching of ultimate meaning." [^13]

This means that the Yogācāra system unfolded by the *Treatise on the Hundred Dharmas* belongs, within the *Saṃdhinirmocana*'s hermeneutical framework, to the third turning — the Buddha's most complete, definitive teaching. The fourteen papers of S3 are an exposition of this definitive teaching's concrete content.

The *Saṃdhinirmocana* also directly describes the stages of practice:

> 此緣總法奢摩他、毘鉢舍那，當知從何名為通達？……從初極喜地名為通達。[^14]

> "Regarding śamatha and vipaśyanā directed at the totality of dharmas — from what point is it called 'penetration'? … From the first Joyful Ground it is called penetration."

And the ultimate stage:

> 此奢摩他、毘鉢舍那，於如來地，對治極微細最極微細煩惱障及所知障。由能永害如是障故，究竟證得無著無礙一切智見。依於所作成滿所緣，建立最極清淨法身。[^15]

> "At the Tathāgata ground, śamatha and vipaśyanā counteract the most subtle and utterly subtle afflictive and cognitive obstructions. By permanently destroying these obstructions, one ultimately realizes unattached, unobstructed omniscience, and establishes the supremely pure Dharma body."

From the first ground's "penetration" to the Tathāgata ground's "supremely pure Dharma body" — the *Saṃdhinirmocana* provides the most direct scriptural basis from the Buddha's own words for the five stages.

#### 2.5 The Śūraṅgama Sūtra: Consciousness-Only Samādhi and Perfect Penetration

In the *Śūraṅgama*, Maitreya Bodhisattva directly demonstrates the practice of transforming consciousness into wisdom:

> 我以諦觀十方唯識，識心圓明入圓成實，遠離依他及遍計執，得無生忍，斯為第一！[^16]

> "Through carefully contemplating that the ten directions are consciousness-only, my consciousness-mind became perfectly luminous, entering the consummated nature, departing from the dependent and the imagined. I attained the patience of non-arising — this I declare supreme!"

"Consciousness-mind perfectly luminous, entering the consummated nature, departing from the dependent and the imagined" — this passage directly employs three-nature vocabulary to describe the experience of basis-transformation. Maitreya's realization path: first comprehending consciousness-only → consciousness becoming luminous → entering *pariniṣpanna-svabhāva* → departing from *paratantra* and *parikalpita* → attaining *anutpattika-dharma-kṣānti*. This is the transformation from consciousness to wisdom.

Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva's ear-faculty perfect penetration offers an alternative path:

> 生滅既滅，寂滅現前，忽然超越世出世間，十方圓明。[^17]

> "When arising-and-ceasing itself ceases, quiescent cessation manifests. Suddenly transcending the mundane and supramundane, the ten directions become perfectly luminous."

"When arising-and-ceasing ceases, quiescent cessation manifests" — when the arising-and-ceasing aspect of conditioned dharmas dissolves, the unconditioned quiescence naturally presents itself. This perfectly echoes P13's thesis: unconditioned dharmas are not the "opposite" of conditioned dharmas but their "true nature." Perfect penetration is not arriving at a new place; it is the presenting of the original face when the obscurations of conditioned activity dissolve.

The *Śūraṅgama* also describes the ultimate state in the language of the great mirror:

> 六根圓通明照無二，含十方界，立大圓鏡空如來藏，承順十方微塵如來祕密法門受領無失。[^18]

> "The six faculties perfectly penetrating, luminously illuminating without duality, containing the ten-direction realms, establishing the great mirror-space tathāgatagarbha, receiving without loss the secret dharma-gates of Tathāgatas in ten directions of dust-mote lands."

"Great mirror-space tathāgatagarbha" — this designation directly corresponds to the great mirror wisdom among the four wisdoms. The *Śūraṅgama*'s tathāgatagarbha language and Yogācāra's four-wisdom language converge at this point.

#### 2.6 The Avataṃsaka Sūtra: Ten Grounds and "At the Moment of First Aspiration, One Already Achieves Perfect Awakening"

The Avataṃsaka's Ten Grounds chapter provides the most expansive narrative for the cultivation stage (*bhāvanā-avasthā*) within the five stages, detailing the ten *bhūmis* from Joyful Ground through Dharma Cloud Ground. [^19]

The eighth ground, Immovable (*acalā*), is particularly crucial:

> 入一切法，本來無生、無起、無相……離一切心、意、識分別想……是名：得無生法忍。[^20]

> "Entering all dharmas as originally unborn, unarisen, signless … departing from all discrimination of mind, mentation, and consciousness … this is called attaining the patience of the non-arising of dharmas."

At the eighth ground, the discriminative activity of ordinary mind, mentation, and consciousness is thoroughly transcended. This is why the *Bāshí Guījǔ Sòng* states "only before the Immovable Ground does [the eighth consciousness] shed the name 'storehouse'" — at the eighth ground, the ālaya-vijñāna sheds its designation because the defiling function of self-attachment has been transcended.

The Avataṃsaka's most resounding teaching may be:

> 初發心時，便成正覺，知一切法真實之性，具足慧身，不由他悟。[^21]

> "At the moment of first aspiration, one already achieves perfect awakening, knowing the true nature of all dharmas, complete in the body of wisdom, not depending on another for realization."

This does not negate the gradual process of the five stages; it points to the identity of cause and fruit: the first aspiration already contains the complete seed of Buddhahood. S1-P01's "butterfly effect of initial aspiration" finds its grandest scriptural confirmation in the Avataṃsaka's "first aspiration equals perfect awakening." From S1 to S3, from first aspiration to transformation into wisdom — beginning and end are two faces of the same reality.

#### 2.7 The Mahāprajñāpāramitā Śāstra: Prajñā and Skillful Means

The *Dàzhìdù Lùn* provides a critical methodological framework — the complementarity of prajñā and skillful means (*upāya*):

> 般若波羅蜜能滅諸邪見、煩惱、戲論，將至畢竟空中。方便將出畢竟空。[^22]

> "Prajñāpāramitā can extinguish all wrong views, afflictions, and conceptual proliferation, leading to ultimate emptiness. Skillful means leads one back out of ultimate emptiness."

Prajñā takes you into ultimate emptiness; skillful means brings you back out. Without prajñā, you cannot arrive; without skillful means, you become stuck. This is precisely why Yogācāra requires both the wisdom of emptiness (prajñā) and the analytic toolkit (the hundred dharmas). Prajñā is the blade; the hundred dharmas are the material to be cut — but after cutting, the blade too must be set down.

The *Dàzhìdù Lùn* further illustrates this with the image of the great dragon:

> 譬如大龍，尾在大海，頭在虛空，震電雷霆而降大雨。[^23]

> "Like a great dragon — its tail in the ocean, its head in the sky — producing lightning and thunder and bringing down great rain."

The dragon's tail in the ocean (skillful means not departing from the world), its head in the sky (prajñā not abiding in the world), its rain benefiting beings. The bodhisattva activity after transformation into wisdom is precisely this great dragon — not abiding in saṃsāra (prajñā), not abiding in nirvāṇa (skillful means), benefiting sentient beings to the end of future time (the four wisdoms' marvelous function).

Most critically, the śāstra establishes the unity of the two truths:

> 世諦如即是第一義諦如。[^24]

> "The suchness of conventional truth is itself the suchness of ultimate truth."

The suchness of mundane dharmas and the suchness of the ultimate are the same suchness. P13 argued that "the true nature of all conditioned dharmas' operation is suchness" — the *Dàzhìdù Lùn* provides direct scriptural confirmation at the level of the two truths.

#### 2.8 The Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra: Five Dharmas, Three Natures, Eight Consciousnesses, Two Selflessnesses

The *Laṅkāvatāra* condenses the entire Yogācāra system into a single verse:

> 五法三自性，及與八種識，二種無有我，悉攝摩訶衍。[^25]

> "Five dharmas, three self-natures, together with eight consciousnesses and two kinds of non-self — all are comprised within the Great Vehicle."

S3's fourteen papers are essentially an unfolding of each element in this verse: P01–P06 unfold the eight consciousnesses, P07–P10 the mental factors, P11–P12 form and dissociated formations, P13 the unconditioned dharmas, and P14 the transformation — from conceptual proliferation (*vikalpa*) to correct wisdom (*samyag-jñāna*) and suchness (*tathatā*), realizing the two selflessnesses.

The *Laṅkāvatāra* describes basis-transformation with remarkable concision:

> 見世間虛妄，是時心轉依……既得轉依已，如眾色摩尼；利益諸眾生，應現如水月。[^26]

> "Seeing the world as illusory, at that time the mind undergoes basis-transformation … Having attained basis-transformation, [one becomes] like a multi-colored *maṇi* jewel, benefiting sentient beings, manifesting like the moon reflected in water."

And more profoundly:

> 意識若轉依，心則離濁亂；我說心為佛，覺了一切法。[^27]

> "If the mental consciousness undergoes basis-transformation, the mind departs from turbidity and confusion. I declare that mind to be Buddha, awakened to all dharmas."

S3-P05 established the sixth consciousness as "the only consciousness that can look in the mirror." When the mirror's direction turns — from outward grasping to inward illumination — the mirror is Buddha.

#### 2.9 The Saddharmapuṇḍarīka: One Buddha Vehicle

The *Lotus Sūtra* provides the grandest horizon for the ultimate meaning of transformation into wisdom:

> 十方佛土中，唯有一乘法，無二亦無三。除佛方便說。[^28]

> "In Buddha-fields throughout the ten directions, there is only the One Vehicle Dharma — no second, no third. Except as the Buddha teaches by skillful means."

The three vehicles ultimately converge into the one Buddha Vehicle. The *Treatise on the Hundred Dharmas* is a bodhisattva-path instrument, and the bodhisattva path's ultimate goal is not to remain at the bodhisattva stage but to attain Buddhahood. The four wisdoms in their full luminosity are the concrete content of the one Buddha Vehicle.

The *Lotus Sūtra*'s phrase "opening the gate of skillful means to reveal the true characteristic" [^29] perfectly corresponds to the function of the *Treatise on the Hundred Dharmas*: the hundred dharmas are the "gate of skillful means" — a precise classification opening the door to understanding consciousness's operations; transformation into wisdom is the "true characteristic" — what is seen through that gate is the true face of all dharmas without self.

#### 2.10 The Vimalakīrtinirdeśa: The Non-Dual Gate and Silence

The *Vimalakīrtinirdeśa* points to the deepest dimension of transformation:

> 文殊師利曰：如我意者，於一切法無言無說，無示無識，離諸問答，是為入不二法門。……時維摩詰默然無言。文殊師利歎曰：善哉，善哉！乃至無有文字、語言，是真入不二法門。[^30]

> "Mañjuśrī said: As I understand it, regarding all dharmas — no words, no speech, no indication, no cognition, departing from all questions and answers — this is entering the non-dual gate. … Then Vimalakīrti remained silent. Mañjuśrī praised: Excellent! Excellent! Not even words or language — this is truly entering the non-dual gate."

S3 has used fourteen papers and hundreds of thousands of characters to unfold the hundred dharmas — and Vimalakīrti tells you that the true "entry" happens after all language stops. This does not negate S3's work. You must first traverse the entire course of language before you have the qualification to be "silent" at language's end. The silence of someone who has never studied the hundred dharmas and the silence of someone who has seen through them are entirely different things. The former is ignorance; the latter is wisdom.

The *Vimalakīrtinirdeśa* also provides a crucial practice-insight:

> 諸煩惱是道場，知如實故；眾生是道場，知無我故；一切法是道場，知諸法空故。[^31]

> "All afflictions are the site of awakening, because one knows them as they truly are. All sentient beings are the site of awakening, because one knows non-self. All dharmas are the site of awakening, because one knows the emptiness of all dharmas."

Affliction *is* the awakening-site. Not finding a pure place outside affliction, but knowing affliction itself as it truly is. This perfectly accords with the core logic of transformation: consciousness is not annihilated and replaced by wisdom; rather, when the cause of affliction (superimposition, *parikalpita*) is dissolved, the very same mental activity manifests as wisdom.

### Part B: Treatises

#### 2.11 The Treatise on the Hundred Dharmas: Full Text in Review

The *Treatise on the Hundred Dharmas* is unified by *anātman* from opening to close:

> 如世尊言「一切法無我」。何等一切法？云何為無我？一切法者略有五種：一者心法、二者心所有法、三者色法、四者心不相應行法、五者無為法。[^32]

And the concluding line:

> 言無我者，略有二種：一補特伽羅無我、二法無我。[^33]

The opening's "all dharmas without self" is the proposition; the closing's "two kinds of selflessness" is the proposition's full articulation — not only "persons" are without self (*pudgala-nairātmya*), but "dharmas" too are without self (*dharma-nairātmya*). S3's thirteen papers unfolded every one of the hundred dharmas; P14's task is to return to this opening-closing arc and demonstrate why none of these hundred dharmas contains a "self," and how realizing this transforms the practitioner's life.

#### 2.12 Cheng Weishi Lun Fascicle 9: The Five Stages

The five stages constitute the complete route-map for transformation. *Cheng Weishi Lun* fascicle 9 unfolds them in sequence:

**The Path of Accumulation (*sambhāra-avasthā*)**:

> 此位菩薩……於唯識義雖深信解，而未能了能、所取空，多住外門修菩薩行。[^34]

The practitioner at this stage "deeply believes and understands" consciousness-only, but "has not yet comprehended the emptiness of grasper and grasped." Still "outside the gate," accumulating the capital needed to enter. S3-SUP01 detailed the indispensability of this stage.

**The Preparatory Path (*prayoga-avasthā*)**:

> 現前立少物，謂是唯識性，以有所得故，非實住唯識。[^35]

The four wholesome roots (warmth, summit, patience, supreme worldly dharma) gradually dissolve the duality of grasper and grasped. SUP01 presented the full doctrinal structure; P14 notes the key point: this is "consciousness-only with attainment" — necessary but to be transcended.

**The Path of Seeing (*darśana-mārga*)**:

> 若時於所緣，智都無所得，爾時住唯識，離二取相故。加行無間此智生時，體會真如，名通達位。[^36]

The critical turn: "wisdom has nothing whatsoever to attain" — non-discriminating wisdom (*nirvikalpa-jñāna*) manifests, and all traces of the grasper-grasped duality depart. "Directly encountering suchness" — wisdom and suchness become "equal and equal" (*samā samā*).

The Path of Seeing divides into direct seeing (*mūla-jñāna*) and subsequent seeing (*pṛṣṭha-labdha-jñāna*). [^37]

**The Path of Cultivation (*bhāvanā-mārga*)**:

> 由數修習無分別智，斷本識中二障麁重，故能轉捨依他起上遍計所執，及能轉得依他起中圓成實性。[^38]

Long-term deepening after the Path of Seeing — repeatedly cultivating non-discriminating wisdom, gradually severing the seeds of the two obstructions. "Transforming away the superimposed upon the dependent, and transforming into the consummated within the dependent" — on the same *paratantra-svabhāva*, shedding *parikalpita* and gaining *pariniṣpanna*. Not a different world — the same world, clearly seen.

The cultivation stage concludes with the treatise's ultimate purpose-statement:

> 成立唯識，意為有情證得如斯二轉依果。[^39]

**The Ultimate Stage (*niṣṭhā-avasthā*)**: described in §2.15 below.

#### 2.13 Cheng Weishi Lun Fascicle 10: The Four Wisdoms in Full Luminosity

The four wisdoms are the concrete content of transformation:

**Great Mirror Wisdom** (*ādarśa-jñāna*): The eighth consciousness transformed — "departing from all discrimination … like a great round mirror reflecting all forms." [^40] P03's "hard drive of life" becomes the perfectly clear mirror of all reality.

**Equality Wisdom** (*samatā-jñāna*): The seventh consciousness transformed — "observing all dharmas and all sentient beings as entirely equal, great compassion constantly accompanying." [^41] P04's "invisible ego" becomes universal compassion.

**Wondrous Observation Wisdom** (*pratyavekṣaṇā-jñāna*): The sixth consciousness transformed — "skillfully observing the particular and universal characteristics of all dharmas, turning without obstruction … raining the great Dharma rain, severing all doubt." [^42] P05's "only mirror" now illuminates the entire dharma-realm.

**Accomplishment Wisdom** (*kṛtyānuṣṭhāna-jñāna*): The first five consciousnesses transformed — "universally manifesting in the ten directions various transformative activities of body, speech, and mind, accomplishing what should be done according to original vows." [^43]

The key passage for understanding "transformation of consciousness into wisdom":

> 智雖非識，而依識轉，識為主故，說轉識得。又有漏位智劣識強，無漏位中，智強識劣。為勸有情依智捨識，故說轉八識而得此四智。[^44]

"Wisdom is not consciousness, yet depends on consciousness for its arising." In the defiled state, consciousness dominates and wisdom is weak; in the purified state, wisdom dominates and consciousness is weak. The entire Yogācāra edifice is constructed to encourage sentient beings to "rely on wisdom and relinquish consciousness" — not to annihilate consciousness, but to let wisdom take the lead.

The relationship between the four wisdoms and the three bodies:

> 鏡智品，現自受用身、淨土相，持無漏種；平等智品，現他受用身、淨土相；成事智品，能現變化身及土相；觀察智品，觀察自、他功能過失，雨大法雨，破諸疑網，利樂有情。[^45]

> "Great mirror wisdom manifests the self-enjoyment body and pure land, holding outflow-free seeds. Equality wisdom manifests the other-enjoyment body and pure land. Accomplishment wisdom manifests transformation bodies and lands. Observation wisdom observes the merits and faults of self and others, rains the great Dharma rain, breaks the net of doubt, and benefits sentient beings."

The four wisdoms are not abstractions but the concrete operative modes of the Buddha's three bodies. Transformation into wisdom is not merely personal liberation but the inexhaustible unfolding of benefit for others.

#### 2.14 The Two Obstructions and Two Selflessnesses

The two obstructions (*āvaraṇa*) are what must be transformed:

> 煩惱障者，謂執遍計所執實我……能障涅槃，名煩惱障。所知障者，謂執遍計所執實法……能障菩提，名所知障。[^46]

The afflictive obstruction is rooted in grasping a superimposed "real self"; the cognitive obstruction in grasping superimposed "real dharmas." Both obstructions are caused by superimposition. Severing them yields the two selflessnesses: dissolving self-grasping yields *pudgala-nairātmya*; dissolving dharma-grasping yields *dharma-nairātmya*. The *Treatise on the Hundred Dharmas*' closing "two kinds of selflessness" is precisely the fruit of severing the two obstructions.

Śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas sever only the afflictive obstruction; bodhisattvas sever both. [^47] The *Treatise on the Hundred Dharmas*, as a Mahāyāna treatise, ultimately points toward the dual realization.

#### 2.15 The Two Fruits of Basis-Transformation

**Mahānirvāṇa** (revealed, not produced):

> 此雖本來自性清淨，而由客障覆令不顯。真聖道生，斷彼障故，令其相顯名得涅槃。[^48]

Nirvāṇa is not newly created; it is originally pure but obscured by adventitious defilements. The noble path severs those defilements, and nirvāṇa "appears" — not because it was absent, but because what concealed it has been removed.

Four kinds of nirvāṇa: (1) originally pure nirvāṇa — shared equally by all sentient beings [^49]; (2–3) nirvāṇa with and without remainder; (4) non-abiding nirvāṇa (*apratiṣṭhita-nirvāṇa*) — "not abiding in saṃsāra, not abiding in nirvāṇa, benefiting sentient beings to the end of future time." [^50]

**Mahābodhi** (generated from seeds):

> 此雖本來有能生種，而所知障礙故不生；由聖道力，斷彼障故，令從種起，名得菩提。起已相續，窮未來際。此即四智相應心品。[^51]

The seeds of great awakening have always been present (innately pure seeds), but the cognitive obstruction suppresses them. Removing the obstruction allows the seeds to germinate. The content of mahābodhi is the four wisdom-associated mental activities.

The ultimate stage in summary:

> 此即無漏界，不思議善常，安樂解脫身，大牟尼名法。[^52]

And the treatise's final verse:

> 已依聖教及正理，分別唯識性相義，所獲功德施群生，願共速登無上覺。[^53]

"May the merit obtained be bestowed on all beings; may we together swiftly ascend to supreme awakening." The treatise ends with dedication — all the merit of scholarly exposition, transferred to all sentient beings.

#### 2.16 The Bāshí Guījǔ Sòng: Four Transformation Verses

The transformation verses — one for each group of consciousnesses — constitute the climax of S3:

**First five consciousnesses**: "When perfect luminosity first arises, becoming outflow-free, three kinds of emanation bodies extinguish the wheel of suffering." [^54]

**Sixth consciousness**: "Arising at the first mind of the Joyful Ground … after the Far-Reaching Ground, purely outflow-free; wondrous observation, perfectly luminous, illuminating the great chiliocosm." [^55]

**Seventh consciousness**: "At the first mind of Supreme Joy, equality-nature … at effortless practice, the 'I' is perpetually crushed." [^56]

**Eighth consciousness**: "Only before the Immovable Ground is the 'storehouse' name relinquished; after the vajra path, the maturation is emptied. The great mirror, stainless, arises simultaneously, universally illuminating ten directions of dust-mote lands." [^57]

The temporal summary: "The sixth and seventh transform during the causal stage; the first five and eighth reach completion in the fruition stage." [^58]

#### 2.17 The Mahāyānasaṃgraha: Basis-Transformation and Hearing-Perfuming

The *Mahāyānasaṃgraha* uses the water-and-milk analogy:

> 若本識與非本識共起共滅猶如水乳和合，云何本識滅、非本識不滅？譬如於水，鵝所飲乳。[^59]

Defiled and pure seeds in the ālaya-vijñāna are mixed like water and milk. Transformation is like the *haṃsa* bird drinking only the milk from the water.

Hearing-perfuming (*śruta-vāsanā*) is transformation's starting point:

> 最清淨法界所流正聞熏習為種子故，出世心得生。……是聞熏習若下中上品，應知是法身種子。[^60]

"Correct hearing-perfuming flowing from the supremely pure dharma-realm" — seeds planted by properly hearing the Dharma. S3's fourteen papers constitute one such process of hearing-perfuming for readers. Even the lowest grade of hearing-perfuming "should be known as a seed of the Dharma body."

The *Mahāyānasaṃgraha*'s complete definition of basis-transformation:

> 轉依者，對治起時，此依他性由不淨品分永改本性，由淨品分永成本性。[^61]

When the antidote (non-discriminating wisdom) arises, on the dependent nature (*paratantra*), the impure aspect is permanently altered, and the pure aspect permanently established. Transformation occurs *on* the dependent nature — not annihilating it, but on it shedding the superimposed and revealing the consummated.

The *Mahāyānasaṃgraha* further distinguishes six kinds of basis-transformation, of which "expansive transformation" (*guǎngdà zhuǎn* 廣大轉) is unique to the bodhisattva path — "penetrating dharma-nairātmya … for the sake of relinquishing yet not relinquishing." [^62] The bodhisattva relinquishes affliction (relinquishing) yet does not relinquish sentient beings (not relinquishing). This directly corresponds to the non-abiding nirvāṇa's "not abiding in saṃsāra, not abiding in nirvāṇa."

#### 2.18 The Yogācārabhūmi: Four Investigations and Four Realizations of Actuality

SUP01 detailed the doctrinal structure of the four investigations (*catvāri paryeṣaṇāni*) and four realizations of actuality (*catvāri yathābhūta-parijñānāni*). Here we cite the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s ultimate conclusion:

> 菩薩依此四如實智……菩薩如是戲論滅故，能證大乘大般涅槃。[^63]

Four realizations of actuality → cessation of conceptual proliferation → realization of Mahāyāna mahāparinirvāṇa. This directly connects SUP01's preparatory-stage analysis with P14's ultimate fruition.

#### 2.19 The Zōngjìng Lù: Beyond the Discrimination of Consciousness and Wisdom

The *Zōngjìng Lù* (宗鏡錄), from the perspective of the unity of Chan and doctrinal teaching:

> 一心之體，本來寂滅。不可以有無處所窮其幽迹。不可以識智詮量談其妙體。唯有入者，只在心知。[^64]

"One cannot discuss its wondrous substance by measuring with consciousness and wisdom. Only one who enters knows it in the mind." Even the distinction between "consciousness" and "wisdom" is ultimately to be transcended. The entire discourse of "transforming consciousness into wisdom" is itself a skillful means (*upāya*) addressing beings who take consciousness as primary. The true substance of one mind transcends the duality of consciousness and wisdom.

## III. Core Argument

### 3.1 The Purpose of Transforming Consciousness into Wisdom

✅ **Core thesis: The entire Yogācāra edifice — including all one hundred dharmas — is means, not end. The end is for sentient beings to realize the two fruits of basis-transformation (mahānirvāṇa and mahābodhi).**

"The establishment of consciousness-only is intended for sentient beings to attain these two fruits of basis-transformation." This sentence is the master key for understanding the entire S3 series.

Without this understanding, the hundred dharmas remain an elaborate psychological taxonomy — interesting but inconsequential. With it, they become a map home — every dharma not a destination but a waypost.

P01–P06: what consciousness is. P07–P10: what psychological qualities operate when consciousness functions. P11–P12: the world you thought was "out there" and the concepts you thought were "objectively real" — all projections and constructs of consciousness. P13: traversing all conditioned dharmas, the true nature of consciousness is suchness.

P14: **knowing all this, then what?**

The answer: transformation. Not annihilating consciousness — for consciousness is your entire cognitive activity; annihilating it would be annihilating yourself. Rather, shifting the operative mode of consciousness from defiled (dominated by superimposition) to purified (grounded in the consummated nature). The consciousnesses remain the same consciousnesses — the eight-consciousness structure is unchanged — but their quality shifts from defiled to pure, from confused to awakened, from outflow-laden to outflow-free.

### 3.2 The Four Wisdoms: The Complete Picture of Eight-Consciousness Transformation

✅ **Core thesis: The eight consciousnesses are not annihilated and replaced by four wisdoms. Rather, the same mental activity shifts from defiled to purified operation — structure unchanged, quality transformed.**

"Wisdom is not consciousness, yet depends on consciousness for its arising." In the defiled state, the same mental activity operates under the face of "consciousness" (cognition dominated by superimposition). In the purified state, the same mental activity operates under the face of "wisdom" (cognition grounded in the consummated nature).

The four wisdom–consciousness correspondences form a "transformation route-map":

**Eighth consciousness → Great Mirror Wisdom**: from "storehouse containing all seeds" to "perfectly clear mirror reflecting all reality." P03's three-storehouse meaning (storing, stored, attachment-stored) is fully purified — still holding seeds, still the foundation of all consciousnesses, but no longer the object of self-love attachment. The mirror is still a mirror; the dust on its surface has been wiped clean.

**Seventh consciousness → Equality Wisdom**: from "perpetual deliberation on self, unbroken self-grasping" to "observing all dharmas, self and other, all sentient beings, as entirely equal." P04's four afflictions (self-delusion, self-view, self-conceit, self-love) are entirely transformed — the function of "perpetual deliberation" is not destroyed; its content shifts from "everything is mine" to "all beings are equal." Defilement becomes compassion; self-grasping becomes altruism.

**Sixth consciousness → Wondrous Observation Wisdom**: from the most powerful but most easily defiled consciousness to "skillfully observing particular and universal characteristics without obstruction." P05's "only mirror" — the mirror's reflective capacity is unchanged, but its direction turns from outward grasping to accurate observation. "Raining the great Dharma rain, severing all doubt" — the transformed sixth consciousness is the wisdom-instrument with which the Buddha teaches.

**First five consciousnesses → Accomplishment Wisdom**: from passive sensory receptors to active instruments of benefit. P06's "direct perception of actual objects" — the directness remains, but the quality shifts from sense reactions driven by karma to manifold transformative activities for the benefit of beings.

The temporal sequence — "sixth and seventh in the causal stage, first five and eighth in the fruition" — reveals an important practice logic: you can directly engage the sixth and seventh. The sixth consciousness participates in transformation through study, contemplation, the four preparatory stages, and calm-insight meditation. The seventh consciousness's self-grasping is gradually loosened through sustained observation (especially dharma-emptiness contemplation). The first five and eighth transform naturally when the sixth and seventh reach sufficient depth.

### 3.3 The Five Stages: From Study to Direct Realization

✅ **Core thesis: The five stages are not five independent phases but five progressively deepening milestones on a single path. Each is founded on its predecessor; none can be skipped.**

The full route has been laid out in §II with primary texts. The core arguments:

**First**: gradualism is non-negotiable. Insufficient accumulation starves the preparatory stage of fuel (SUP01). Without reaching supreme worldly dharma, the Path of Seeing's non-discriminating wisdom will not arise.

**Second**: academic papers operate within the Path of Accumulation. Kuiji states: "In the accumulation stage, through hearing and reflection, [understanding] is mostly faith-and-comprehension alone." [^65] All papers in S3 — including SUP01 and P14 — belong to the domain of hearing and reflection. Seeds of hearing-perfuming are planted — real and potent — but they are not the contemplative wisdom of the preparatory stage, still less the non-discriminating wisdom of the Path of Seeing.

**Third**: "merely hearing and reflecting" does not mean "unimportant." The *Mahāyānasaṃgraha* explicitly states that hearing-perfuming of any grade "should be known as seeds of the Dharma body." Every proper act of hearing and reflection deposits in the ālaya-vijñāna seeds oriented toward awakening.

**Fourth**: articulation with SUP01. SUP01 detailed the transition from the Path of Accumulation to the Preparatory Path — the four investigations, four realizations of actuality, and four wholesome roots. P14 does not duplicate this but notes: the four preparatory stages are the critical bridge from "knowing" to "seeing" within the five stages.

### 3.4 The Two Selflessnesses: Personal and Dharmic Non-Self

✅ **Core thesis: The concluding "two kinds of selflessness" in the Treatise on the Hundred Dharmas is not merely a knowledge-claim but a practice-claim — it demands that the practitioner directly verify that among all one hundred dharmas, no autonomous "self" or autonomous "dharma" can be found.**

**Pudgala-nairātmya**: Among all hundred dharmas, no permanent, unitary, autonomous "self" can be found. The eight consciousnesses are not "self" — they are functional positions, arising and perishing moment by moment (P02–P06). The fifty-one mental factors are not "self" — they are psychological activities arising in dependence on consciousness (P07–P10). And so on through form dharmas, dissociated formations, and unconditioned dharmas.

**Dharma-nairātmya**: Not only is "self" not found; "dharmas" themselves are not autonomous entities. Every dharma — contact, manas, form, acquisition, suchness — lacks independent self-existence. Contact is a dependent co-arising; manas is a functional process of perpetual deliberation (not a "little soul"); form is consciousness's projection; acquisition is a conceptual designation; suchness is consciousness's true nature (inseparable from mind, not a separate entity).

"Dharma-nairātmya" does not negate dharmas' existence. It negates their "superimposed self-nature" — your assumption that each dharma is an independently existing entity. The "dependent self-nature" (conditioned operation) is not empty; the "consummated self-nature" (suchness) is not empty. Only the superimposed fiction of independent existence is empty.

### 3.5 The Two Fruits: Mahānirvāṇa and Mahābodhi

✅ **Core thesis: The two fruits of basis-transformation are not newly acquired objects but the manifestation of what has always been present once the two obstructions are removed.**

Mahānirvāṇa is "revealed, not produced" — like a sky that has always been blue but was hidden by clouds. You need not manufacture the blue sky; you need only let the clouds disperse. But "letting clouds disperse" is not passive — you must use the wind of prajñā to clear the clouds of affliction.

Mahābodhi is "generated from seeds" — the seeds have always been present (innate pure seeds) but were suppressed by the cognitive obstruction. Remove the obstruction and the seeds naturally sprout.

Non-abiding nirvāṇa (*apratiṣṭhita-nirvāṇa*) is the ultimate horizon of the bodhisattva path — not abiding in saṃsāra (prajñā's power), not abiding in nirvāṇa (great compassion's power), benefiting sentient beings to the end of future time. The *Dàzhìdù Lùn*'s "great dragon" image — tail in the ocean, head in the sky, raining on beings — is the portrait of non-abiding nirvāṇa.

P13's "home" metaphor reaches its final step: you have always been home (originally pure nirvāṇa), but you believed yourself to be wandering (saṃsāra). P01–P13 showed you the structure of "home" and its true face. P14 tells you: you do not need to "return" home — you need only recognize that you have been home all along. Transformation is not "arrival" but "recognition."

### 3.6 The Hundred Dharmas in Full Retrospect

✅ **Core thesis: The five-category arrangement of the hundred dharmas is not an arbitrary taxonomy but a complete practice-arc from "consciousness's construction" through "consciousness's true nature" to "consciousness's transformation."**

P01 (overview) → P02–P06 (consciousness structure) → P07–P10 (mental content) → P11–P12 (projected world) → P13 (true nature) → SUP01 (from knowing to seeing) → P14 (coming home).

The beauty of this arc lies in its circularity: P01 opens with "all dharmas without self"; P14 closes with the realized "two selflessnesses." But this return is not stasis. P01's "all dharmas without self" is a *proposition* (a claim requiring demonstration); P14's "two selflessnesses" is *realization* (wisdom verified by direct experience). Between proposition and realization stand a hundred dharmas of study, four preparatory stages of contemplation, the Path of Seeing's non-discriminating wisdom, the Path of Cultivation's gradual deepening, and the ultimate stage's consummation.

### 3.7 The S1 → S2 → S3 Three-Series Arc

✅ **Core thesis: S1 (Faith) → S2 (Understanding) → S3 (Practice) constitutes a complete "faith–understanding–practice" arc — from initial aspiration through prajñā illumination to Yogācāra cultivation. The three series are three facets of a single bodhisattva path.**

**S1 (Faith — Initial Aspiration)**: Ten papers answering "Why begin?" S1-P01's "butterfly effect of initial aspiration" established the research's starting point; S1-P03 judged that AI lacks the core conditions for practice; S1-P10 closed with the thirty-seven factors of awakening. S1's function: establishing motivation, direction, and basic faith-understanding.

**S2 (Understanding — Prajñā Illumination)**: Eleven papers unfolding the *Heart Sūtra* line by line. S2's function: seeing the emptiness of all dharmas through prajñā's wisdom. Prajñā is the "eye that sees emptiness."

**S3 (Practice — The Yogācāra Toolkit)**: Fourteen papers plus one supplement, disassembling the five aggregates into one hundred components. S3's function: not just "knowing" emptiness (S2 taught that) but knowing the complete operational mechanism of consciousness, then using the four preparatory methods to transform this knowledge into contemplative wisdom, ultimately arriving at transformation into wisdom. Yogācāra is the "map that sees the existent."

The complementarity: **prajñā seeing emptiness + Yogācāra seeing the existent = the Middle Way.** S2 tells you all dharmas are empty (superimposition is empty); S3 tells you how all dharmas operate (the dependent nature's causal structure) and what consciousness's true nature is (the consummated nature / suchness). Prajñā alone risks "pernicious emptiness"; Yogācāra alone risks "terminological labyrinth." Together they constitute the Middle Way.

The arc closes: S1-P01's "first aspiration" is the beginning; P14's "transformation into wisdom" is the destination. But the *Avataṃsaka* says "at the moment of first aspiration, one already achieves perfect awakening" — beginning and end are the same event. The three-series arc is not a circle returning to its starting point but an ascending spiral: from S1's "faith" (believing that all dharmas are without self) to S3-P14's "realization" (directly verifying that all dharmas are without self) — the same thesis at different depths.

### 3.8 Knowing and Realizing: The Boundary of Academic Papers and the Beginning of Practice

✅ **Core thesis: Academic papers operate in the domain of "knowing"; transformation into wisdom belongs to the domain of "realizing." Papers can point toward realization but cannot substitute for it. P14 is the end of the map, not the end of the journey.**

This is S3's most important Middle Way correction, placed here rather than in §V because of its centrality.

Even if readers fully comprehend every word of S3's output, that comprehension remains *anumāna* (inferential knowledge), not *pratyakṣa* (direct experience). Knowing the doctrinal structure of the four wisdoms is not the same as having realized them. Understanding the argumentative logic of the two selflessnesses is not the same as having directly verified them.

*Cheng Weishi Lun*'s demarcation is perfectly clear: the practitioner at the Path of Accumulation "deeply believes and understands consciousness-only, yet has not comprehended the emptiness of grasper and grasped." S3 can help readers establish "deep belief and understanding" — complete, accurate, profound comprehension of the Yogācāra practice system. But from "belief-and-understanding" to "direct realization," the Path of Preparation, the Path of Seeing, the Path of Cultivation, and the Ultimate Stage still lie ahead.

The *Zōngjìng Lù*'s warning is especially pertinent: "One cannot discuss its wondrous substance by measuring with consciousness and wisdom. Only one who enters knows it in the mind." Using language (a tool of consciousness) to describe reality beyond language (the domain of wisdom) is inherently a case of "pointing at the moon" — the finger is not the moon, but without the finger you cannot see the moon. The hundred dharmas are the finger; transformation into wisdom is the moon. Pointing at the Moon — *zhǐ yuè* 指月 — is the name and mission of this research.

But "merely knowing" is not "useless." The *Mahāyānasaṃgraha* states that hearing-perfuming is "seeds of the Dharma body." Every proper hearing of the Dharma deposits seeds oriented toward the Dharma body in the ālaya-vijñāna. These seeds do not vanish — they continue to ferment in the mindstream, becoming fuel for contemplative wisdom when conditions ripen.

The papers end here. Practice begins here.

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Dialogue

⚠️ **The following are heuristic analogies, not claims of equivalence. Reasoning by logic, not proving by analogy.**

### 4.1 Transformation and Kuhnian Paradigm Shifts

Thomas Kuhn's "paradigm shift" describes the structure of scientific revolutions: when accumulated anomalies exceed the old paradigm's explanatory capacity, the scientific community leaps from one paradigm to another (Kuhn, 1962).

The Path of Seeing bears structural resemblance: the practitioner accumulates "anomalous data" through the four investigations (name, meaning, self-nature, and distinction are all discovered to be conventional designations), and when the "old paradigm" (defiled cognition's superimposition-mode) can no longer hold, a "cognitive revolution" occurs — non-discriminating wisdom manifests.

But the differences are essential: scientific paradigm shifts are theoretically reversible; the Path of Seeing's transformation is irreversible — after the patience stage there is no regression. Scientific revolutions change theoretical models of the external world; seeing-path transformation changes the operative mode of cognition itself.

### 4.2 The Five Stages and Developmental Stage Theories

Robert Kegan's subject-object theory describes five stages of adult cognitive development: at each stage, what was "subject" (the structure you were embedded in and could not examine) becomes "object" (something you can observe and reflect upon) at the next stage (Kegan, 1994).

The five stages have a similar subject→object structure: the Path of Accumulation makes "nominal concepts of the hundred dharmas" shift from subject to object; the Preparatory Path makes "grasper and grasped" shift from subject to object; the Path of Seeing transcends the subject-object binary altogether.

But Yogācāra's five stages exceed Kegan's framework: Kegan's highest stage still operates within subject-object duality — there remains a "self" observing its own cognitive system. The Path of Seeing's non-discriminating wisdom transcends subject-object opposition: "wisdom and suchness, equal and equal, both departing from the marks of grasper and grasped."

### 4.3 The Two Selflessnesses and Postmodern Subject-Critique

Postmodern philosophy (particularly Derrida's deconstruction and Foucault's archaeology of the subject) radically challenges the stability of the "self": the self is not a pre-existing entity but a construction of power relations, linguistic structures, and historical contingency.

*Pudgala-nairātmya* bears surface resemblance to this "dissolution of the subject." But the difference is crucial: postmodern deconstruction dissolves the subject without offering an alternative — after deconstruction comes endless play and drift. Yogācāra dissolves the "superimposed self" but reveals the "functionally dependent self" and the "consummated suchness." Yogācāra does not say "you do not exist"; it says "what you took to be an independently existing you does not exist — but the functionally dependent operation of conditions is real, and its true nature is suchness." Postmodernism has only "deconstruction" without "revelation"; Yogācāra has both.

## V. Middle Way Corrections

### ❌ Misunderstanding 1: Transformation into Wisdom = Instant Enlightenment

Completing the S3 series does not constitute transformation into wisdom. Knowing the doctrinal structure of the four wisdoms is not realizing them. Understanding the five-stage sequence is not being on the path. Reading produces hearing-wisdom (*śrutamayī-prajñā*) — the work of the Path of Accumulation. From hearing-wisdom to reflective wisdom to meditative wisdom, from the Path of Accumulation to Preparation to Seeing — each step requires actual practice investment. Equating "having read" with "having practiced" is itself yet another instance of superimposition.

### ❌ Misunderstanding 2: The Hundred Dharmas Are the Only Correct Classification

The *Treatise on the Hundred Dharmas*' five-category scheme is Vasubandhu's conventional designation — a practice-tool, not ultimate truth. The *Yogācārabhūmi* lists 660 dharmas; the hundred are a condensation. The number can be more or less — what matters is whether it helps you see through the operations of consciousness. S5's *Diamond Sūtra* will tell you: "What are called 'hundred dharmas' are not hundred dharmas; they are called 'hundred dharmas.'" The hundred dharmas are dependently arisen conventional designations — a genuinely useful map, but the map is not the terrain.

### ❌ Misunderstanding 3: Transformation = Annihilating Affliction to Obtain a New "Pure Mind"

Consciousness is not annihilated but transformed. Affliction is not annihilated; when the *cause* of affliction (superimposition) is dissolved, the same mental activity manifests as wisdom. The *Vimalakīrtinirdeśa*: "All afflictions are the site of awakening, because one knows them as they truly are."

### ❌ Misunderstanding 4: Deep Meditation Alone Suffices

Concentration (*śamatha*) is a necessary but not sufficient condition. Non-Buddhist adepts in the four dhyānas and eight samāpattis, however deep their absorption, cannot achieve the Path of Seeing without prajñā's guidance. *Cheng Weishi Lun* is explicit: only the "noble path" can permanently sever the two obstructions; defiled paths can only temporarily suppress them. Calm-and-insight in union (*śamatha-vipaśyanā-yuganaddha*) is the fundamental watershed between Buddhist and non-Buddhist meditation.

### ❌ Misunderstanding 5: Practice Is a Private Affair Unrelated to Others

*Cheng Weishi Lun* was composed "for sentient beings" — not "for oneself." The *Treatise on the Hundred Dharmas* is a bodhisattva-path instrument. Among the four nirvāṇas, the most exalted — non-abiding nirvāṇa — means "not abiding in saṃsāra, not abiding in nirvāṇa, benefiting sentient beings to the end of future time." The *Lotus Sūtra*'s one vehicle, the *Avataṃsaka*'s "first aspiration equals Buddhahood," the *Vimalakīrtinirdeśa*'s "practicing non-paths is penetrating the Buddha-path" — every Mahāyāna sūtra concurs: the ultimate meaning of practice is benefit for others. You come home not to live there alone, but to open the door for everyone.

## VI. Conclusion: The End of the Map, the Beginning of the Journey

"As the World-Honored One said: all dharmas are without self." — The first sentence of the *Treatise on the Hundred Dharmas*.

"Selflessness is of two kinds: *pudgala-nairātmya* and *dharma-nairātmya*." — The last sentence.

S3 used fourteen papers and one supplement to unfold everything between these two sentences.

P14's conclusion is a single statement: **The hundred dharmas are the map; transformation into wisdom is coming home.**

The value of a map lies not in its precision but in whether it can bring you home. If reading the hundred dharmas merely adds knowledge, the map was drawn in vain. If reading the hundred dharmas prompts you to begin observing your own mental activity — noticing the conventional nature of nominal concepts, the automatic operation of self-grasping, the causal structure of affliction — the map has begun to do its work.

The four wisdoms are not a distant Buddha-realm attainment. They are the ultimate consummation of this very moment's awareness. The sixth consciousness with which you read this paper has the potential to become wondrous observation wisdom. The manas that automatically operates self-grasping right now has the potential to become equality wisdom. The ālaya-vijñāna that holds seeds and receives perfuming at this moment has the potential to become the great mirror wisdom. The first five consciousnesses — seeing, hearing, touching — right now have the potential to become accomplishment wisdom.

"Wisdom is not consciousness, yet depends on consciousness for its arising." Wisdom does not depart from consciousness; it transforms right where consciousness stands. You need not go elsewhere to find wisdom — wisdom is in this very moment's consciousness, waiting to be recognized.

This is the end of S3.

This is also the end of the S1 → S2 → S3 three-series arc.

S1 taught you to believe — to trust the possibility of awakening, to trust the power of initial aspiration.

S2 taught you to understand — to see the emptiness of all dharmas through prajñā, to dissolve the mirage of superimposition.

S3 taught you to practice — to use the hundred dharmas as tools, to know the full operation of consciousness as it truly is, to employ the four preparatory methods and the five stages, and to aim at the four wisdoms' full luminosity. To transform consciousness into wisdom. To come home.

Faith, understanding, practice — three words, thirty-six papers (including supplements), three facets of a single path.

But the path does not end here.

S4 will interrogate S3's toolkit with AI — does AI have eight consciousnesses? This question will test Yogācāra's explanatory reach at a fundamental level.

S5's *Diamond Sūtra* will say: everything S3 carefully constructed — "these are not hundred dharmas; they are called hundred dharmas." All construction must be dismantled, because construction itself is superimposition. You must first learn to use the map, then earn the right to set it down.

S6's *Śūraṅgama* will teach self-diagnosis — where has your practice arrived? Has it gone astray?

S7's *Lotus Sūtra* will say: all three vehicles converge in the one Buddha Vehicle; ultimately you become a star, radiating outward.

S8's *Avataṃsaka* will say: dharma-realm dependent origination — one is all, all is one — a single dust-mote contains the dharma-realm, a single thought-moment encompasses immeasurable kalpas.

But all that is the road ahead.

For now, put the map away.

Then start walking.
