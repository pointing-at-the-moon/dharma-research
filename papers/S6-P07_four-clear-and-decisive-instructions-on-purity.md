## Abstract

The latter half of fascicle 6 of the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* (大佛頂首楞嚴經, T945) records the Buddha's instruction to Ānanda on the **Four Clear and Decisive Instructions on Purity** (四種決定清淨明誨): the cutting-off of lust, killing, stealing, and gross false speech. These constitute four absolute boundaries that no practitioner of *samādhi* may cross. This paper argues that the Four Instructions should not be read as precepts of moral law but as **technical boundary conditions for meditative practice**. Their placement in the sūtra's textual sequence — fascicle 6, *before* the establishment of the *maṇḍala* platform (fascicle 7) and *before* the Three Gradual Stages of purification (fascicle 8) — is not accidental. The Buddha is teaching that the moral perimeter must be erected first; both the physical ritual space and the purification of mind unfold *within* this perimeter. The paper develops six arguments: (1) the Four Instructions have the rhetorical structure of a *checklist* rather than a *manual*, with four independent invocations of the Buddha-versus-Māra criterion-formula making the whole non-skippable; (2) the placement of the lust-precept first has a two-layered meditative logic — a surface layer of autonomic embodiment (the hormonal and nervous-system cascade triggered by lust disrupts the somatic baseline of meditation for a long time after any single arising), and a deeper layer of cognitive structure (lust is the only affliction that dissolves the subject-object boundary, and since the Ear-Faculty Practice consists of tracing the hearing-subject back to its nature, any habitual inclination toward subject-object fusion corrupts its structural precondition); (3) the character 心 (*xīn*, mind) in the repeated phrase 其心不 ("their mind does not...") bears the full weight of the difference between Mahāyāna *mind-containment* precepts and Hīnayāna *act-based* precepts — this is not a stricter standard but a *more precise* one; (4) the five characters 斷性亦無 (*duàn xìng yì wú*, "even the nature of cutting-off is not") articulate the perfect-sudden three layers of precept-observance: bodily cutting → mental cutting → dissolution of the concept of cutting; (5) the Fourth Instruction's stylistic rupture from the first three is a deliberate bridge to the Fifty Skandha-Māras (五十陰魔) of fascicles 9–10; (6) the four repetitions of the criterion-formula constitute a *recursive* structure of Buddha-versus-Māra discrimination. The cross-disciplinary section departs from the usual convention by drawing its structural analogy from Buddhism's own tradition — the preparation of the ordination altar (戒壇) — rather than from modern science. The mirror of the Four Instructions is already given in Buddhism's own ritual heritage; any external analogy would dilute rather than illuminate.

**Keywords:** Four Clear and Decisive Instructions on Purity (四種清淨明誨), cutting off lust, cutting off killing, cutting off stealing, cutting off gross false speech, mind-containment as precept (攝心為戒), Three Superior Trainings (三無漏學), Buddha-versus-Māra criterion, Śūraṅgama Sūtra (楞嚴經), Ear-Faculty Practice (耳根圓通)

## §I Introduction: Three Perimeters

In Paper 06 of this series, we noted an observation we did not fully develop at the time. Examining the structural sequence of the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra*, we pointed out that after Mañjuśrī's selection of the Ear-Faculty Practice (耳根圓通) at the end of fascicle 6, the Buddha does not proceed directly to the Three Gradual Stages. An entire fascicle intervenes: fascicle 7 is devoted to establishing the *maṇḍala* platform and transmitting the Śūraṅgama *dhāraṇī*. We wrote:

> You do not begin a sterilization protocol in a dirty operating room — you must first have a clean space before the decontamination work can be done inside it. Fascicle 7 is the preparation of space; the Three Gradual Stages of fascicle 8 are the preparation of mind.[^p06-ref]

The analogy was correct as far as it went, but it was incomplete. Pushing the same logic one step further back reveals something the earlier paper did not fully see. *Before* the establishment of the *maṇḍala* platform in fascicle 7, one more thing must occur. In the latter half of fascicle 6 — after Ānanda's request for instructions on "establishing a place of practice" (安立道場) — the Buddha does not proceed immediately to the ritual layout. He first articulates the general framework of the **Three Superior Trainings** (三無漏學) — mind-containment as precept, precept as the basis of *samādhi*, *samādhi* as the basis of wisdom — and then unfolds the Four Clear and Decisive Instructions on Purity in full detail. The actual structural sequence of the sūtra is therefore:

- **Fascicle 6, first part**: Twenty-Five Avenues of Perfect Penetration → Mañjuśrī's verse (**choosing the gate**)
- **Fascicle 6, latter part**: Ānanda's request → Three Superior Trainings → **Four Clear and Decisive Instructions on Purity** (**moral perimeter**)
- **Fascicle 7**: *Maṇḍala* platform + Śūraṅgama *dhāraṇī* (**spatial perimeter**)
- **Fascicle 8**: Three Gradual Stages + Ground of Dry Wisdom (**purification of mind**)
- **Fascicle 8 onwards**: The fifty-five stages of progression

This yields a clear three-layered perimeter structure: **moral guardrails → spatial guardrails → purification of mind**. The sequence is not interchangeable. The moral guardrails must be erected first, because without moral commitment there is no meaningful sense in which a ritual space exists at all. The spatial guardrails must be erected before mental purification, because without a clean perimeter the purification would be continuously contaminated by incoming defilement. The Three Gradual Stages sit at the innermost layer, where the finest work of the mind can finally unfold.

This means the prospective framing in Paper 06 — that Paper 07 would be about "protection while walking on the path" — was inaccurate. The Four Instructions are not guardrails for someone already inside the door. They are **the outermost perimeter, erected before one has even entered**. This paper therefore treats the outermost layer of the three-perimeter structure: the moral precondition that must be in place before any practice can begin.

The consequence of this reframing is substantial. We cannot read the Four Instructions as *regulations governing the behavior of a practitioner already engaged in practice*. They are not regulations. They are **entry conditions**. If you do not accept them, you have not yet started; if you accept them but violate them at will, you remain perpetually at the threshold, never inside. This distinction shapes the reading of everything that follows.

## §II Scriptural Basis

### A. The Sūtra: *Śūraṅgama* Fascicle 6

#### 2.1 Ānanda's Request and the Three Superior Trainings

The occasion for the Four Instructions is Ānanda's practical question. Having heard Mañjuśrī's selection of the Ear-Faculty as the ideal gate, he does not rush into practice. Instead, he raises a pragmatic concern framed by the consciousness of *mofa* (末法), the age of declining Dharma:

> Most compassionate World-Honored One! I have now awakened to the dharma-gate of Buddhahood, and in its practice I have no further doubt. [Yet] these sentient beings are gradually far from the Buddha; false teachers proclaiming the Dharma are as numerous as the sands of the Ganges. For those who would gather their minds and enter *samādhi*, how should they establish a place of practice, distance themselves from the deeds of Māra, and ensure that the *bodhicitta* does not retreat?[^1]

Three features of this request deserve attention. First, Ānanda is not asking for himself alone — he explicitly frames the question on behalf of "all sentient beings in the age of the final Dharma" in a world where "false teachers proclaiming the Dharma are as numerous as the sands of the Ganges." Second, he asks about *establishing a place of practice* — how to build a location in which cultivation can actually occur. Third, he pairs "distancing from the deeds of Māra" with "ensuring *bodhicitta* does not retreat" — that is, he asks simultaneously for outer protection and inner stability.

The Buddha's response is layered. The first layer is the general framework of the Three Superior Trainings:

> The Buddha told Ānanda: You have often heard me in my *Vinaya* proclaim the three decisive meanings of cultivation, namely: **mind-containment constitutes precept (攝心為戒); precept gives rise to *samādhi* (因戒生定); *samādhi* gives rise to wisdom (因定發慧)**. These are called the Three Superior Trainings (三無漏學).[^2]

The phrasing here is crucial. The Buddha does not say "ceasing evil constitutes precept"; he says "mind-containment constitutes precept." The Song-dynasty commentator Zǐxuán (子璿), in his *Śūraṅgama Yìshū Zhùjīng* (首楞嚴義疏注經), identifies this phrasing as the marker of a fundamental difference between the Mahāyāna and Hīnayāna understandings of *śīla*. The Hīnayāna approach is "precepts violated through the enacted deed, with severity corresponding to the act" (事遂緣成，輕重隨戒). The Mahāyāna approach is "guarding each individual mental movement inwardly, with all offenses held equally" (一一內防心念，輕重等持) — any arising of the defiled thought is already a violation, with no gradation.[^3] The Ming-dynasty commentator Jiāoguāng Zhēnjiàn (交光真鑑), in his *Zhèng-mài-shū* (正脈疏), pushes this further still: "Here we take 'no single thought arising' (一念不生) as the precept."[^4] The operative definition of mind-containment as precept, in other words, is the non-arising of a single discursive thought — the precept is already enacted before a thought has begun. This definition establishes the entire interpretive framework for the Four Instructions. When the Buddha says "their mind does not lust," "their mind does not kill," he does not mean "they do not do these things" — he means "the thoughts themselves do not arise."

#### 2.2 The First Clear and Decisive Instruction: Cutting Off Lust

The Buddha begins with the precept against lust:

> If any sentient being of the six realms, throughout all worlds, has a mind that does not lust (其心不婬), then they will not follow the continuity of birth-and-death. Your cultivation of *samādhi* aims at escape from defilement, but if the lustful mind is not eliminated, there is no escape from defilement. Even if one has abundant intelligence and *samādhi* manifests before one, if one does not cut off lust one must fall into the realm of Māra: at best, a Māra king; at middle, a Māra multitude; at worst, a Māra-woman.... After my *parinirvāṇa*, in the age of the final Dharma, many such Māra-multitudes will flourish in the world, widely practicing lust and claiming to be "good spiritual friends," causing sentient beings to fall into the pit of desire-view and lose the path to enlightenment. You must teach the people of the world who cultivate *samādhi* that **they must first cut off lust at the mind** (先斷心婬). This is what the Tathāgatas, the Buddhas of old, call the **First Clear and Decisive Instruction on Purity**.[^5]

The structure of this passage deserves careful parsing. It has six fixed components, and all six will be repeated in the three following instructions: (1) the positive statement "their mind does not X → they will not follow birth-and-death"; (2) the inverse statement "the X-mind not eliminated → no escape from defilement"; (3) the karmic consequence in three grades; (4) the prophecy of *mofa*; (5) the sealing phrase naming the Nth Clear and Decisive Instruction; (6) the parable and the Buddha-versus-Māra formula.

The parable that follows is the famous image of "steaming sand to make rice":

> If you cultivate *samādhi* without cutting off lust, it is like steaming sand and gravel hoping to produce rice. Even after a hundred thousand *kalpas*, it will only be called hot sand. Why? Because rice was never its substance — it is made of stone.... **You must make both body and mind cut off lust completely, and then cut off even the nature of cutting-off itself** (斷性亦無); only then can the enlightenment of the Buddha be hoped for. If I speak this way, this is the speech of the Buddha; if one does not speak this way, this is the speech of Pāpīyas (Māra).[^6]

The five characters *duàn xìng yì wú* — "even the nature of cutting-off is not" — constitute the perfect-sudden (圓頓) extreme of this instruction. Zǐxuán's commentary explains: "One who truly observes precepts does not even have the concept of 'observing' — how could one then, in body or mind, commit a grave violation?"[^7] The full cultivation of precepts is not merely non-violation; it is the eventual dissolution of the very notion that "I am observing" and "I have cut off something." Bodily cutting → mental cutting → the dissolution of the concept of cutting: these are the three depths.

#### 2.3 The Second Clear and Decisive Instruction: Cutting Off Killing

The second instruction mirrors the structure of the first precisely:

> If any sentient being of the six realms has a mind that does not kill, they will not follow the continuity of birth-and-death.... If one does not cut off killing, one must fall into the realm of spirits (神道): at best, a powerful *preta*-king; at middle, a flying *yakṣa* or chief of ghosts; at worst, an earth-dwelling *rākṣasa*....[^8]

An important clarification follows, addressing the question of the "five pure meats" (五淨肉) that the historical Buddha permitted monastics to eat:

> Ānanda! When I allowed bhikṣus to eat the five pure meats, these meats were all produced by my spiritual power — they had no life-root. Your Brahmanical land is hot and damp, with stones and sand, where vegetation cannot grow; through my great compassionate spiritual power, I provided what was nominally "meat" so you could sustain yourselves. How then, after the Tathāgata's *parinirvāṇa*, can those who eat the flesh of sentient beings still be called disciples of Śākyamuni?[^9]

Jiāoguāng's judgment on this passage is unambiguous: "Any instance in which the Buddha permitted the eating of meat was a skillful expedient (權變) in his lifetime, a gradual compassionate accommodation. After his *parinirvāṇa*, it is the flesh of beings whose lives were actually taken — can one still eat it? The deep sūtras that abrogate the expedient do so precisely to prevent confusion in the age of decline."[^10] He further argues that "eating meat is killing": "Although one does not slaughter directly, one causes the killing through payment — the karma is identical. Therefore eating meat is killing."[^11]

The parable section gives us "stopping one's ears and shouting":

> If one cultivates *samādhi* without cutting off killing, it is like a person who stops their own ears and shouts loudly, seeking not to be heard. This is what is called "trying to hide but becoming more exposed."[^12]

#### 2.4 The Third Clear and Decisive Instruction: Cutting Off Stealing

The third instruction:

> If any sentient being of the six realms has a mind that does not steal, they will not follow the continuity of birth-and-death.... If one does not cut off stealing, one must fall into the realm of deviance (邪道): at best, a sprite; at middle, a demon; at worst, a deviant person possessed by spirits.... They hide their deceit and call themselves "good spiritual friends," each claiming to have attained supramundane dharmas, deceiving the ignorant, causing loss of mind, so that the families they visit are ruined.[^13]

Jiāoguāng's definition of stealing is striking in its breadth: "This kind of stealing is not only the taking of another's physical property. It includes every instance of deceptive speech and conduct, every act of intimidation — extending even to **a single thought of hoping for offerings**."[^14] The mere arising of a single hope for gain from another is already theft. The most egregious form, particularly relevant to *mofa*, is "borrowing the Tathāgata's robes to peddle the Tathāgata" — wearing Buddhist garb to sell the Dharma for profit.

The parable is "filling a leaking vessel with water":

> If one cultivates *samādhi* without cutting off stealing, it is like a person who pours water into a leaking vessel hoping to fill it — even after *kalpas* as numerous as dust, it will never be level.[^15]

Zǐxuán's commentary is precise: "Pouring the water of *samādhi* into a broken precept-vessel, hoping to fulfill the fruit — through dust-*kalpas* it will not fill. Whose fault is this? How can one not be careful! Inner virtue is empty, outer appearance deceives others; the precept-vessel is pierced, and wholesome dharmas leak away."[^16] *Samādhi* is the water; *śīla* is the vessel. A cracked vessel cannot hold water, no matter how much is poured in. This parable connects directly to the "cup" metaphor of Paper 06: the Three Gradual Stages clean the cup; the Four Instructions ensure the cup itself has no cracks. The two metaphors describe the same structure from its two sides.

#### 2.5 The Fourth Clear and Decisive Instruction: Cutting Off Gross False Speech

The Fourth Instruction exhibits a striking structural departure from the first three, and the departure is itself the teaching:

> Ānanda! Even if sentient beings of the six realms have bodies and minds free from killing, stealing, and lust — even if these three acts are already perfected — if they commit gross false speech (大妄語), their *samādhi* cannot attain purity. **They become "love-view Māras" (愛見魔), losing the seed of the Tathāgata.** The "gross false speech" here refers to claiming attainments one has not attained, claiming realizations one has not realized, or seeking worldly supremacy — saying to others: "I have now attained the fruit of *srotāpanna*, of *sakṛdāgāmin*, of *anāgāmin*, of arhatship, of *pratyekabuddha*hood, of the Ten Stages or the stages preceding them"... This is an *icchantika*, destroyer of the Buddha-seed.[^17]

Note the stylistic shift. The first three instructions began with "their mind does not X, then they do not follow birth-and-death." The Fourth begins instead with "even if their bodies and minds are free from killing, stealing, and lust — if they commit gross false speech, their *samādhi* cannot attain purity." Why? Jiāoguāng's answer is precise: "There are two kinds of false speech: minor false speech consists in saying things untruly; **gross false speech consists in falsely claiming spiritual realization**. What is cut off here is gross false speech. **This must be spoken aloud (此須出口) — it is unlike the mind-based violations [of the previous three]**."[^18] The Fourth Instruction has a fundamentally different structure from the other three: it requires actual *utterance* to be committed, whereas the other three are violated at the level of mental arising alone. This is why the Fourth must have a different rhetorical shape.

The passage that follows is also unique to the Fourth:

> After my *parinirvāṇa*, I command bodhisattvas and arhats to take birth in the age of the final Dharma, manifesting in various forms — as *śramaṇas*, lay Buddhists, rulers, officials, children, prostitutes, widows, thieves, butchers and merchants — to liberate those in *saṃsāra*. They dwell alongside such beings, praising the Buddha-vehicle, causing their minds to enter *samādhi*. **But they will never say "I am truly a bodhisattva" or "I am truly an arhat," never revealing the Buddha's secret causation, never casually speaking to the unlearned — except at the moment of their own death, when they may leave a hidden indication.**[^19]

Jiāoguāng's judgment of this passage gives the iron rule for distinguishing true from false spiritual teachers in *mofa*: "**If they remain, they do not reveal; if they reveal, they do not remain.** This is an invariable rule, as seen in the three holy ones — Fēnggān, Hánshān, and Shídé [of Tang-dynasty Chan legend]. If they reveal their identity and yet continue to remain, this clearly shows they are deviant people seeking offerings — do not trust them."[^20] The story of these three sages is the classic exemplification of the rule: Fēnggān, upon being recognized by the magistrate Lǚ-Qiū Yìn as an emanation of Amitābha, immediately departed Guóqīng Temple and was never seen again; Hánshān and Shídé, similarly recognized, fled into the cliffs and vanished. The rule governs the age of decline: **any person who openly proclaims their own realization while continuing to teach is by that very act disqualified as an authentic teacher**.

The parable is "carving human excrement into sandalwood shape," and there is a unique positive conclusion:

> If bhikṣus have minds as straight as a lute-string (心如直絃), entirely truthful, entering *samādhi* they will permanently have no demonic affair. I certify that such a person has accomplished the unsurpassed awareness of a bodhisattva.[^21]

The four characters *xīn rú zhí xián* — "mind as straight as a lute-string" — constitute the summation of all four instructions: at the farthest depth, the precept is no longer a precept at all, but the intrinsic condition of the mind.

#### 2.6 The Fourfold Buddha-versus-Māra Criterion Formula

Each of the four instructions concludes with the same formula:

> If I speak this way, this is the speech of the Buddha; if one does not speak this way, this is the speech of Pāpīyas.[^22]

This formula occurs four times in the latter half of fascicle 6. The repetition is not rhetorical redundancy — it is structural insistence. **Each of the four instructions is an independent act of Buddha-versus-Māra discrimination.** One cannot observe three and violate the fourth; one cannot use an exemption in one precept to justify violation of another. Every violation is an immediate relocation to the side of Māra. This formula is the concrete application, at the level of *śīla*, of the overall Tathāgatagarbha-versus-heterodoxy discrimination that structures the entire *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* (as discussed in Paper S6-P01).

#### 2.7 The Direct Prophecy of the Fifty Skandha-Māras

In the Fourth Instruction, the Buddha uses a critically important term: *ài-jiàn mó* (愛見魔), "love-view Māra" — "they become love-view Māras, losing the seed of the Tathāgata."[^23] This term directly anticipates the chapters on the **Fifty Skandha-Māras** (五十陰魔) in fascicles 9 and 10. The Four Clear and Decisive Instructions on Purity are the **preventive medicine** for the Fifty Skandha-Māras; the Fifty Skandha-Māras are the **pathology report** of what happens when the Four Instructions fail. The anticipation is not subtle — it is explicit: if you cannot hold even these four boundaries, the later chapters on demonic states are your clinical photograph.

### B. The Commentarial Tradition: Zǐxuán and Jiāoguāng

#### 2.8 Zǐxuán: The Root Difference in Precept-Substance Between the Vehicles

Zǐxuán's *Śūraṅgama Yìshū Zhùjīng* makes a crucial doctrinal point about the Four Instructions. The difference between Mahāyāna "mind-containment as precept" and Hīnayāna *vinaya* is not a difference in the precepts themselves but in the **precept-substance** (戒體). The Hīnayāna treats the precepts as "arising with the act, with severity corresponding to the deed" (事遂緣成，輕重隨戒). The Mahāyāna treats them as "guarding each mental movement inwardly, with severity held equally" (一一內防心念，輕重等持) — every arising of the defiled thought is already a violation, and there is no gradation of severity.[^24] Zǐxuán cites the *Awakening of Faith in the Mahāyāna* (大乘起信論) as doctrinal support: "As the treatise says, 'When mind arises, all dharmas arise; when mind ceases, all dharmas cease.' Therefore [the Mahāyāna treatment of precepts] is entirely distinct from Hīnayāna precept-observance."[^25]

This distinction is the interpretive key for the Four Instructions. If one reads "their mind does not lust" through Hīnayāna act-based categories, it appears to demand the impossible — no human can fully control the arising of mental states. But understood as the Mahāyāna standard, "their mind does not lust" is not a demand for perfect achievement; it is a **statement of the appropriate standard for the context of *samādhi* cultivation**. The object of meditative concentration is the mind; therefore the object of the precepts must also be the mind. Body-and-speech precepts govern social ethics; mind-precepts govern *samādhi*. The contexts are different, and the standards accordingly differ.

#### 2.9 Jiāoguāng: Why the Lust-Precept Comes First

Jiāoguāng's *Zhèng-mài-shū* contains what we may call the "lust-first adjudication" (淫戒居首判教), addressing a question any comparatively-minded reader of Buddhist *vinaya* will raise: why does the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* place lust first, when other *vinaya* texts (notably the *Brahmajāla Sūtra*) begin with killing? Jiāoguāng's answer, citing the earlier commentator Wēnlíng:

> In other sūtras, the precept against killing comes first — this is because **establishing teaching with compassion as the primary principle** (設化以慈悲為先). In this sūtra, the lust-precept comes first — this is because **authentic cultivation takes renunciation of desire as the root** (真修以離欲為本). The energy of desire is coarse and turbid, defiling the wondrous clarity; the habit of desire is maddeningly deluding, easily causing the loss of right reception [of *samādhi*]. In perpetuating birth-and-death and destroying the true permanence, nothing exceeds this. Therefore [lust] must be the first precept, the First Clear and Decisive Instruction on Purity.[^26]

Jiāoguāng goes on to connect this to the overall structure of the sūtra:

> Observe that Ānanda's entire request arose from having been subjected to false romantic entrapment [by the *Mātaṅga* woman], and therefore his very first aspiration was to recoil from the turbidity of desire. At every level of the Three Gradual Stages, lust is addressed first. In the Ten Faiths, the very first is "desire and love becoming dry." Among worldly beings, it is by "the mind not flowing loose" that they ascend to the six heavens of desire. Therefore **authentic cultivation by inward containment must first renounce desire**.[^27]

This ties the entire sūtra together: Ānanda's arising of aspiration, the Three Gradual Stages, the Ten Faiths, even the worldly ascent to the heavens — every level of the *Śūraṅgama*'s structure places desire-renunciation at the foundation of authentic cultivation.

#### 2.10 Jiāoguāng: The Four Instructions as Preparatory Practice for Ear-Faculty

Jiāoguāng's framing of the Four Instructions is decisive for the overall argument of this paper:

> What is cultivated here is still the Ear-Faculty Practice. But for beginners of the dullest capacity, three special additions are made — precepts, the *maṇḍala* platform, and *dhāraṇī* — and therefore the broad classification (大科) calls this "preparatory practice" (加行). The *samādhi* and wisdom that arise from these precepts are still the wondrous *samādhi* of forgetting sense-object and exhausting the sense-faculty, and the threefold-emptiness wisdom attained in that *samādhi*. The sūtra treats precepts in detail here, and only briefly mentions *samādhi* and wisdom — this is clearly visible in the passage.[^28]

Jiāoguāng is explicit: the Four Instructions are **not an independent practice**. They are the **preparatory layer** (加行) for the Ear-Faculty Practice, specifically designed for practitioners "of the dullest capacity — beginners." He goes on to explain why sharper practitioners do not need this preparatory layer:

> Middle-capacity practitioners have slight afflictions; they do not spontaneously give rise to thoughts of lust or the others. Furthermore, during their "forgetting sense-objects" stage, their inward vigilance is already powerful, so worldly turbidities do not even enter — how could they spontaneously give rise to the four kinds of thoughts?... Therefore this gate [the Four Instructions + *maṇḍala* + *dhāraṇī*] is specifically for those whose defilements are deeply habituated (塵勞素重), who do not even require external stimulus to spontaneously generate thoughts of lust and the rest — for whom even bodily and verbal manifestation cannot be ruled out. For such beings, **the four precepts are added as the preparatory layer before the inward turning, and the *maṇḍala* and *dhāraṇī* are added as the supportive practice during the inward turning itself**.[^29]

This passage is the direct textual basis for the three-perimeter framework of this paper. The Four Instructions (precept), the *maṇḍala* and *dhāraṇī* (space), and the Three Gradual Stages (mind) are not three independent practices but the three layers of preparatory cultivation for the Ear-Faculty Practice. And Jiāoguāng explicitly states that the Four Instructions are for practitioners whose defilements are "deeply habituated" — which explains why the sūtra's textual sequence must place the outermost moral perimeter *first*: for such practitioners, whose body and speech are not yet under control, an unbreachable outer boundary must exist before anything else.

#### 2.11 Jiāoguāng: The Operative Definition of Mind-Containment, and the Uniqueness of the Fourth Instruction

Jiāoguāng gives the operative definition of "mind-containment as precept" in the context of the Four Instructions:

> "Their mind does not lust" means: with respect to all forms of lust, **not only does one not move body or speech, one does not give rise to even a single thought of ideation** — only then is one not in violation, only then is one in accord with mind-containment as precept. The same applies to the other three.[^30]

"Not only does one not move body or speech, one does not give rise to even a single thought" — this is the unified standard for the first three instructions. But for the Fourth, Jiāoguāng specifically notes: "This [gross false speech] must be spoken aloud; it is unlike the mind-based violations."[^31] The first three are violated at the moment of mental arising; the Fourth requires actual utterance. This stylistic difference is not a textual oversight — it reflects the fact that **gross false speech is the only affliction that inherently involves another**. You must make the claim *to someone* for it to constitute *dà wàng-yǔ*. This feature also sets up the special connection between the Fourth Instruction and the Fifty Skandha-Māras: the demonic states are all internal experiences; the problem arises at the moment you interpret those experiences as "my realization" and begin telling others — that is the precise moment the "love-view Māra" comes into being.

### C. Comparative Sources: *Brahmajāla Sūtra* and *Yogācārabhūmi*

To locate the *Śūraṅgama*'s Four Clear and Decisive Instructions in their full comparative context, we need to set them alongside two other major Mahāyāna precept traditions: the Ten Major Precepts of the *Brahmajāla Sūtra* (梵網經), representing the ritual-legal (*vinaya*) tradition, and the Four *Pārājika*-Equivalents (四種他勝處法) of the *Yogācārabhūmi* (瑜伽師地論) bodhisattva-precept chapter, representing the bodhisattva-path tradition.

#### 2.12 *Brahmajāla Sūtra*: The Vinaya Tradition's Kill-Steal-Lust-Falsehood Sequence

The *Brahmajāla Sūtra* (T1484), fascicle 2, lists the Ten Major Bodhisattva *Pārājika* Precepts in the following order: **killing → stealing → lust → lying** → selling alcohol → speaking of the faults of the fourfold assembly → self-praise and denigration of others → stinginess → anger without accepting apology → slander of the Three Jewels.[^32] The first precept is killing:

> "If a Buddha's child kills by their own hand, instructs another to kill, praises killing as an expedient, rejoices on witnessing killing, or even kills through incantation... the bodhisattva should give rise to the permanent mind of compassion and filial reverence, skillfully protecting and saving all sentient beings. To indulge in killing for one's own satisfaction is a bodhisattva *pārājika* offense."[^33]

Note the *Brahmajāla*'s framing: "the permanent mind of compassion and filial reverence," "skillfully protecting and saving all sentient beings." The framework is **ethical altruism**. The killing-precept comes first because killing most egregiously violates the root of compassion. This differs entirely from the *Śūraṅgama*'s lust-first sequencing driven by "authentic cultivation takes renunciation of desire as the root" — but the two are not in conflict. They correspond to different teaching contexts. The *Brahmajāla* operates in the context of *establishing teaching* (設化): the foundational ethical standards to which all who vow to follow the Mahāyāna must adhere. The *Śūraṅgama* operates in the context of *authentic cultivation* (真修): the technical conditions for those who have specifically committed to cultivating *samādhi*.

#### 2.13 *Yogācārabhūmi*: The Bodhisattva-Path Tradition's Four *Pārājika*-Equivalents

The *Yogācārabhūmi* (T1579), fascicle 40, lists four *tā-shèng-chù-fǎ* (他勝處法, "dharmas that defeat one") for the bodhisattva. The order is: **self-praise and denigration of others → stinginess refusing gifts → unforgiving anger → slander of the *bodhisattva-piṭaka***.[^34] This sequence differs from both *Brahmajāla* and *Śūraṅgama*:

> "If a bodhisattva, for the sake of desire for gain and veneration, praises themselves and denigrates others — this is called the first *dharma that defeats one*."[^35]

The *Yogācārabhūmi* takes as its foundational concern **the primary obstacles to bodhisattva altruism**: self-praise and denigration (harming fellow bodhisattvas), stinginess (refusing to give), anger (harming sentient beings), and slander of the true Dharma (destroying the transmission of the Dharma). None of the four directly names killing, stealing, lust, or false speech. What is even more striking is that the *Yogācārabhūmi* contains a "skillful expedient" (善權方便) passage explicitly listing conditions under which a compassionate bodhisattva may commit acts that would otherwise be killings, thefts, lusts, or lies:

> "If bodhisattvas, abiding in the bodhisattva's pure precept-*saṃvara*, by skillful expedient and for the sake of benefiting others, engage in a small portion of what are by nature offenses — for this reason, their bodhisattva precepts are not violated, and much merit is produced."[^36]

This stands in sharp contrast to the *Śūraṅgama*'s uncompromising "both body and mind must cut off lust completely; even the nature of cutting-off must be eliminated." The *Śūraṅgama* is absolute prohibition; the *Yogācārabhūmi* allows conditional openings.

#### 2.14 The Three-Text Comparison: Not Contradiction, But Three Contexts

The three sequences, set side by side:

| Dimension | *Brahmajāla* (T1484) | *Śūraṅgama* Four Instructions | *Yogācārabhūmi* (T1579) |
|-----------|---------------------|-------------------------------|-------------------------|
| **Fundamental offenses** | kill → steal → lust → lie | lust → kill → steal → lie | self-praise → stinginess → anger → slander |
| **First in order** | killing (compassion-ethical ground) | lust (obstacle to *samādhi* and wisdom) | self-praise and denigration (obstacle to altruism) |
| **Attitude toward exceptions** | strict; limited opening in minor precepts | absolute prohibition, no opening | systematic exceptions via skillful expedient |
| **Precept-substance concept** | permanent Buddha-nature scroll | intrinsically pure Tathāgatagarbha | *bodhicitta* vow + wholesome-pure intention |
| **Primary concern** | behavioral regulation (*vinaya*) | obstacle to meditative cultivation | effectiveness of altruism (bodhisattva-path) |
| **Primary audience** | all who take bodhisattva precepts | cultivators of *samādhi* | aspirants on the bodhisattva-path |

These three sequences are **not mutually contradictory**. They correspond to three different teaching contexts. The *Brahmajāla* is a *vinaya* tradition: precepts exist to regulate behavior and preserve communal integrity, so killing comes first (the most grave violation of the compassionate ground). The *Śūraṅgama* is a cultivation tradition: precepts exist to protect the technical conditions of *samādhi*, so lust comes first (the most direct structural interference with meditative concentration). The *Yogācārabhūmi* is a bodhisattva-path tradition: precepts exist to preserve the effectiveness of altruistic action, so self-praise and denigration come first (the most fundamental corruption of the bodhisattva's work).

The comparison also reveals an important warning: **one must not use the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s skillful-expedient openings to justify violations in the *Śūraṅgama*'s context**. If a person cultivating *samādhi* invokes "bodhisattva compassion" as an excuse for acts of lust, killing, stealing, or falsehood, what they are actually doing is committing the very offense forbidden by the Third Instruction — theft, in its most insidious form. Zǐxuán's commentary on the Third Instruction puts this with precision: "Not to take the Tathāgata's teachings of provisional meaning and twist them into one's own private interpretation, misleading beginners... grasping the expedient while slandering the real is of this same kind."[^37] Taking the skillful-expedient meaning the Buddha offered in one context and turning it into a self-serving exemption in another context — this is **theft at the level of Dharma**, and it is more serious than theft of material goods.

## §III Core Argument

### 3.1 Why Checklist, Not Manual

The Four Clear and Decisive Instructions have a peculiar stylistic feature: the four precepts share a highly repetitive structure, each independently complete — each with its positive formulation, its negative formulation, its karmic consequence, its *mofa* prophecy, its parable, and its Buddha-versus-Māra criterion. This repetition is not a literary redundancy. It is a deliberate structural feature.

Consider the difference between a *manual* and a *checklist*. A manual is a continuous narrative: you read chapter one to understand chapter two, chapter two to understand chapter three — the whole forms an organic argument. A checklist is categorically different: **each item is independent, closed, and non-omissible**. You cannot skip item four because you have already done items one through three; you cannot merge item two with item one because they seem related; each item must be checked off on its own.

The Four Instructions are a checklist, not a manual. The Buddha repeats the same syntactic pattern four times, independently declaring four times "if I speak this way, this is the speech of the Buddha; if not this way, this is the speech of Pāpīyas." He is telling us that **each instruction is an independent life-or-death threshold**, that none may be passed over, none may be skipped, and the exemption of any one cannot excuse the violation of another. If you observe three and violate the fourth, the entire perimeter has broken — because the defining feature of a perimeter is that *a single gap is equivalent to having no perimeter at all*.

This stylistic feature echoes Jiāoguāng's framing of the Four Instructions as *preparatory practice for those of dullest capacity*. The characteristic of dull-capacity affliction is precisely the tendency toward self-deception at the point of specific weakness: "This one time is just..."; "This case doesn't count..."; "This is different from that..." The checklist structure exists to block this self-deception: four items, independent, formally repetitive, identical in adjudication — there is nowhere to hide.

### 3.2 The Meditative Logic of Lust-First: From Somatic Mobilization to Subject-Object Structure

Jiāoguāng provides the classical reason for placing lust first: "The energy of desire is coarse and turbid, defiling the wondrous clarity; the habit of desire is maddeningly deluding, easily causing loss of right reception."[^38] This passage, though it reads as literary, is in fact a concrete description. Anyone who has seriously practiced meditation knows exactly what it describes. The moment lust arises, the body is mobilized: heart rate accelerates, breathing becomes shallow, pupils dilate, blood is redistributed, the face flushes, perspiration begins, and the hormonal system releases its cascade. This is an autonomic mobilization — a full-body reaction, not a purely mental event.

This mobilization has two features that are devastating for the meditator.

**First, it is not switch-like; it decays slowly.** A thought of greed can be dropped in an instant; a thought of anger can be turned in an instant. But once the body's mobilization system has been triggered, the hormones require time to metabolize, the heart rate requires time to return to baseline, the breath needs time to settle, and the body needs time to return to the stable somatic ground on which meditation can unfold. This process cannot be completed by an act of will alone — it is physiological, not under direct voluntary control. What meditation most requires is a stable somatic baseline, and lust leaves behind precisely an extended disruption of that baseline.

**Second, it damages the current session more comprehensively than the other three afflictions.** One can continue a sitting session through mild anger — the quality of concentration drops, but the session does not collapse entirely. One can continue through a mild impulse of theft or through the memory of a past false word. But when lust arises, the session is essentially over, and whether the next session can re-enter *samādhi* depends on how quickly the body settles. A practitioner who frequently generates lustful thoughts in daily life is effectively cycling between mobilization, decay, and re-mobilization — never arriving at the stable ground from which real practice can begin.

This is why "if the lustful mind is not eliminated, there is no escape from defilement" (婬心不除，塵不可出) is not an abstract moral judgment. It is a very concrete description of a somatic mechanism. The "defilement" (*chén*, 塵) here is not metaphysical affliction — it is the physical residue left by mobilization: the sweat, the altered heart rate, the breathing pattern, the hormonal levels. "No escape from defilement" means: as long as the mobilization-decay cycle is being repeatedly triggered, the practitioner never escapes it.

This is the first-order explanation for why lust must come first — the comprehensiveness and persistence of its somatic disruption. But there is a second, deeper structural reason specific to the Ear-Faculty Practice.

The central operation of the Ear-Faculty Practice is *fǎn wén wén zì-xìng* — "turning the hearing back to hear its own nature." One moves from the hearing-subject (that which perceives sound) back to the hearing-nature (the intrinsic awareness by which hearing is possible). This operation presupposes a specific structure: that *subject* and *object* are distinguishable, and that the practitioner's work consists in moving from the object-side back toward the subject-side. The entire chapter on Avalokiteśvara's Ear-Faculty Practice describes this process of reverse flow.

Among all afflictions, killing, stealing, and false speech all preserve the structure of "I am doing something to some object." The killer and the killed remain distinct; the thief and the stolen item remain distinct; the liar and the deceived remain distinct. Even in the midst of these acts, the agent retains the "I / object" differentiation. Lust alone is different. The structural signature of lust is subject-object fusion — at its peak, the boundary between "I" and "object" momentarily dissolves, and the two temporarily become one. This is not a literary description but a structural definition of the affliction.

For the practitioner of the Ear-Faculty Practice, this structural feature is a second level of danger on top of the first. Because the reverse-flow practice is built on the premise that the "hearing-subject" can be identified and traced backward, any habitual tilt of the subject-object structure — even at the level of mental movement only — means that when the practitioner attempts to turn back, the "subject" they are trying to return to is no longer stably in place. It has been trained by habit to lean toward the "object" side, ready at any moment to fuse again. **The surface-layer somatic mobilization disrupts the baseline ground of meditation; the deeper-layer habit of subject-object fusion disrupts the cognitive precondition of reverse-flow hearing. These two effects compound, and together they constitute the double lethality of lust for the practitioner of the Ear-Faculty Practice.**

So "the lustful mind not eliminated, no escape from defilement" admits of two readings: the surface reading — the body has been mobilized and the hormones have not yet withdrawn, so *samādhi* cannot unfold — and the deeper reading — the subject-object structure has been chronically tilted, so the center of reverse-flow is no longer stable. Jiāoguāng's phrase "authentic cultivation takes renunciation of desire as the root" gathers both meanings: it is not that renunciation is moral, but that renunciation is **possible-making**. Without somatic non-mobilization the baseline is unstable; without structural non-fusion the center of reverse-flow is unstable.

This analysis also explains why killing, stealing, and falsehood — though their karmic consequences are also grave — rank below lust in the *Śūraṅgama*'s sequence. They are obstacles to meditation, but not structural destructions. One can continue sitting with the mild residue of any of the other three, accepting a diminished quality; lust, once mobilized, essentially ends the session and takes time to recover from. One is a wound one can fight through; the other directly removes you from the field. That is why the *Śūraṅgama*'s cultivation-oriented sequencing places lust at the front.

### 3.3 The Threefold Weight of "Their Mind" (其心)

Each of the four instructions opens with "their mind does not X" (其心不 X). The two characters *qí xīn* — "their mind" — bear the entire interpretive weight of the Four Instructions. They tell us that the object of these precepts is mind, not body. This is not a raising of the standard but a *matching* of the standard to the domain.

Body-and-speech precepts are for communal ethics — a monastic community needs observable, adjudicable rules of conduct. You cannot treat "I had no intent to kill but my hand slipped and killed someone" as innocent; the community needs enforceable boundaries. But the scene of *samādhi* cultivation is entirely different. The object of meditation is mind; therefore the object of the precept that protects meditation must also be mind. If you use body-and-speech standards to protect *samādhi*, you are using a metric irrelevant to the domain — and that protection will fail precisely when you most need it.

Zǐxuán puts this clearly: Hīnayāna is "arising with the act, severity corresponding to the deed" — violations judged by observable behavior, gradations possible. Mahāyāna is "guarding each mental movement inwardly, with severity held equally" — mental movement is the boundary, and there is no gradation.[^39] This is not a Mahāyāna that is more severe; it is a Mahāyāna that is more *precise* — because the Mahāyāna's cultivation scene (including *samādhi*) has the mind itself as its object.

Jiāoguāng pushes the standard to its extreme: "Not only does one not move body or speech, one does not give rise to even a single thought of ideation — only then is one not in violation, only then is one in accord with mind-containment as precept."[^40] This standard is staggering — who can fully prevent the arising of a single thought? But this is not a demand that you *reach* the standard; it is a *measuring stick*. When you find that a thought has arisen, you know — not yet; train further; turn again. If the standard were "body and speech not in violation," it would be easy to assume you had arrived; but if the standard is "not a single thought," you will always know you are still on the road. This is why *qí xīn* — "their mind" — is the entire interpretive coordinate of the Four Instructions.

### 3.4 The Perfect-Sudden Three Layers of "Even the Nature of Cutting-Off Is Not"

The parable section of the First Instruction contains the five characters that constitute the perfect-sudden (圓頓) extreme of the entire treatment: "You must make both body and mind cut off lust completely, and then **even the nature of cutting-off itself must be eliminated** (斷性亦無); only then can the enlightenment of the Buddha be hoped for."[^41]

This phrase describes the three depths of precept-observance:

**First depth: bodily cutting.** No longer violating in action. This is the outermost layer — not killing, not stealing, not committing acts of lust, not speaking falsely. It is the minimum of Hīnayāna *vinaya* and the baseline of communal ethics. When most people hear "precept-observance," this is what they imagine.

**Second depth: mental cutting.** The thought itself does not arise. Not merely non-action, but the non-arising of the mental inclination. This is the Mahāyāna standard of mind-containment as precept, and Jiāoguāng's "no single thought arising." At this depth, the precept has been interiorized — one is not "resisting" the impulse, because the impulse is no longer generated.

**Third depth: even the nature of cutting-off is not.** Even the thought "I am observing precepts" or "I have cut off this affliction" is laid down. This is the perfect-sudden extreme — the full cultivation of precepts is not perfect execution but the dissolution of the very distinction between executor and executed. Zǐxuán's commentary: "One who truly observes precepts does not even have the concept of 'observing' — how could one then, in body or mind, commit a grave violation?"[^42] A genuine precept-observer has no "I am observing" — if that concept is still present, the third depth has not been reached.

Why does the third depth matter? Because in the context of *samādhi* cultivation, if you continue to grasp the thought "I am observing precepts," that thought is itself a subtle self-grasping — and in the most refined states of concentration it will become an obstacle. What is needed is that at the furthest depth, the precept becomes the *intrinsic condition* of the mind rather than an externally imposed regulation. This is why the positive conclusion of the Fourth Instruction is "mind as straight as a lute-string" (心如直絃) — the final form of precept-observance is not "I observe the precept diligently" but that the mind has become straight in itself.

### 3.5 The Stylistic Rupture of the Fourth Instruction and the Bridge to the Skandha-Māras

The structure of the first three instructions is perfectly parallel: positive statement → inverse statement → karmic consequence → *mofa* prophecy → sealing phrase → parable. The Fourth Instruction clearly departs from this pattern:

1. No "their mind does not falsify, then they do not follow birth-and-death" — instead: "even if their bodies and minds are free from killing, stealing, and lust — if they commit gross false speech, their *samādhi* cannot attain purity."
2. No "the falsifying mind not eliminated, no escape from defilement."
3. The karmic consequence is not "must fall into some realm" but "becoming a love-view Māra, losing the seed of the Tathāgata... an *icchantika*, destroying the Buddha-seed."
4. A unique passage on the manifestation of true bodhisattvas and arhats in *mofa*.
5. The parable is "carving excrement into sandalwood shape," but the conclusion adds a positive section on "mind as straight as a lute-string."

Why these structural departures? Jiāoguāng's explanation: "There are two kinds of false speech: minor false speech consists in saying things untruly; gross false speech consists in falsely claiming spiritual realization. What is cut off here is gross false speech. This must be spoken aloud — it is unlike the mind-based violations."[^43] The Fourth Instruction has a fundamental structural difference from the other three: it requires actual *utterance* to be committed.

The root of this difference is the peculiar structure of gross false speech: **it is the only affliction that necessarily involves another**. Lust, killing, and stealing can in extremity be entirely self-contained acts — a thought of lust arises in the mind alone, a person can kill alone, a person can steal alone. But gross false speech cannot — you must claim *to another* that "I have realized" for gross false speech to be committed. Without an audience there is no gross false speech.

This "necessarily involves another" feature both distinguishes gross false speech from the other three and links it directly to the Fifty Skandha-Māras. For each skandha-māra state, the trap is not the state itself (states arise during any serious practice), but the practitioner's **outward claim about the state** — "each saying to himself that he has attained the unsurpassed Way" (各各自謂成無上道). The state itself is not the problem; the moment you interpret it as "my realization" and begin to tell others, the māra takes form.

This is why the Fourth Instruction uses the phrase "love-view Māra" (愛見魔) — it is a direct anticipation of the Fifty Skandha-Māras of fascicles 9 and 10. The role of the Fourth Instruction in the overall checklist is to block the final step by which a demonic state becomes a demon: by preventing gross false speech, one prevents the outward claim, and therefore prevents the crystallization of "love-view Māra." Even if one experiences a demonic state in meditation, as long as one does not claim "I have realized," the love-view Māra has not yet formed and one still has the chance to turn back. But once the claim is uttered — especially in *mofa*, to unknowing listeners — it marks the beginning of the *icchantika*-style severing of wholesome roots.

Also relevant here is the rule for distinguishing true from false teachers in *mofa*, what Jiāoguāng calls "if they remain, they do not reveal; if they reveal, they do not remain": a genuine bodhisattva manifesting in *mofa* either immediately departs upon being recognized (like the three sages Fēnggān, Hánshān, and Shídé) or never reveals the identity at all; anyone who reveals and continues to remain is by that fact disqualified as authentic.[^44] This rule forms a complete protective structure alongside the Fourth Instruction: true sages never self-declare; any self-declarer is by that very act non-sage. In *mofa*, facing any person who claims spiritual realization, this rule is the most direct mirror for detecting demons.

### 3.6 The Recursive Structure of the Buddha-versus-Māra Formula

The concluding formula of each instruction — "if I speak this way, this is the speech of the Buddha; if one does not speak this way, this is the speech of Pāpīyas" — occurs four times in the latter half of fascicle 6.[^45] This repetition is not emphasis but **recursion**.

Recursion means that a complete structure repeats itself at every layer. The Buddha-versus-Māra formula works exactly this way. Each individual instruction is itself a complete act of Buddha-versus-Māra discrimination, and the four discriminations taken together constitute a larger Buddha-versus-Māra discrimination at the level of the Four Instructions as a whole. One can observe three and violate the fourth — in which case one has stood on the Māra side with respect to the fourth. One can observe all four in act but only at the level of outward form and not at the level of mind — in which case one has stood on the Māra side with respect to the whole. At every layer there is a discrimination to be made; at no layer can it be fudged.

This recursive structure echoes the overall framework of the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* discussed in S6-P01 — the Tathāgatagarbha-versus-heterodoxy discrimination that runs through the entire text. In the latter half of fascicle 6, this discrimination takes concrete form as the four moral perimeters; in fascicles 9 and 10, it will take concrete form as the fifty layers of demonic states. Different levels, same criterion: *on which side do you stand?*

## §IV Internal Structural Echo: The Preparation of the Ordination Altar

The cross-disciplinary section of this paper departs slightly from the convention of other papers in this series. Rather than drawing an analogy from modern disciplines (psychology, neuroscience, complexity theory, and so on), we return to Buddhism's own ritual tradition — **the preparation of the ordination altar** (戒壇) — to display the structural echo of the Four Clear and Decisive Instructions on Purity. The choice is deliberate: the deepest mirror of the Four Instructions is already given within Buddhism's own tradition, in the ritual of building the *maṇḍala*-altar, and any external analogy would dilute rather than illuminate.

The traditional construction of an ordination altar is a rigorous procedure. Before the ordination ceremony can begin, the following steps must be completed in order: *sprinkling and cleansing* (灑淨, purifying the site with consecrated water) → *boundary-setting* (結界, drawing unbreachable lines beyond which no defilement may pass) → *invitation of the holy ones* (請聖, inviting the buddhas and bodhisattvas of the ten directions to witness) → *raising the banners* (立幡, marking the limits of the altar) → *arranging the offerings* (佈壇, installing the ritual implements) → *recitation of mantras* (誦咒, empowering the entire field) → *the actual reception of the precept-substance*. This procedure has three features:

**First, steps cannot be skipped.** If boundary-setting is not complete, there is no pure field in which to invite the holy ones; if the holy ones have not been invited, the altar arrangement is meaningless; if the entire field has not been empowered, the formal reception of the precept-substance is theoretically invalid. Each step depends on the completion of the previous.

**Second, each step has the character of a Buddha-versus-Māra criterion.** If the sprinkling is incomplete, the boundary has gaps, or the holy ones are not truly invited — these finer deviations render all subsequent steps baseless. An ordination altar cannot be "more or less correct" — a single hole means the entire field is invalid.

**Third, the altar is not an end in itself** — it exists to provide a valid field for the ordination ceremony that follows. The altar's construction is preparatory, not the culmination.

Mapping these three features back onto the Four Clear and Decisive Instructions yields a precise correspondence:

What are the Four Instructions? **They are the boundary-setting of the altar of mind**. What the Buddha demands in the latter half of fascicle 6 is precisely that the boundary of the altar of mind be drawn clearly first — four lines, no gaps. The *maṇḍala* platform and Śūraṅgama *dhāraṇī* of fascicle 7 are the **sprinkling and empowerment of the altar of mind** — purifying the physical space as well. The Three Gradual Stages of fascicle 8 are **the ordination ceremony proper, unfolding inside the altar** — the formal reception of the precept-substance of *samādhi*.

In other words, from the latter half of fascicle 6 through fascicle 8, the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* is teaching you to build an **altar of mind**. And the order of construction is: first the boundary-setting (the Four Instructions) → then the sprinkling and empowerment (the *maṇḍala* and *dhāraṇī*) → finally the formal reception (the Three Gradual Stages). This is not three independent sections but a single complete ordination ritual — only this altar is not in the physical world but in the practitioner's mind.

This correspondence explains why the Four Instructions must come first: because boundary-setting is the first step of any altar construction. Without boundary-setting, there is no enclosure for sprinkling to contain; without enclosure, there is no scope for the invitation of the holy ones. The textual position of the Four Instructions — the latter half of fascicle 6 — corresponds precisely to the position of boundary-setting in the ordination ritual — the very first step.

This correspondence also explains why the Four Instructions must be a checklist rather than a manual: because boundary-setting's defining feature is that it cannot have even one point of gap. You cannot say "I did only half the boundary-setting in this area, but the whole thing still counts" — boundary-setting is not a manual (partially executable) but a checklist (each item must be complete).

⚠️ **The limit of the analogy**: The ordination altar is a physical and ritual structure; the Four Instructions are a structure of mental movement and behavior. Although the two are isomorphic in structure, they are not at the same level. The sprinkling of the altar uses water; the "sprinkling" of the altar of mind is the moment-to-moment vigilance of awareness. The boundary-markers of the altar are banners; the boundary-markers of the altar of mind are the four cutting-offs of the Instructions. This analogy is structural, not operational — we cannot use the steps of altar-building to "operationally" construct the altar of mind. The altar of mind can only be built through slow understanding and practice; there is no shortcut.

Even so, this internal analogy is more precise than any external analogy could be — because Buddhism's own ritual tradition has already physicalized and visualized the doctrine that "practice must be preceded by the establishment of boundaries." Two thousand years of monastic life have enacted this doctrine every day: every formal practice, every formal ordination, every formal communal rite begins with boundary-setting. The Four Instructions are not a new requirement, but the internalization of this ancient ritual — from the external physical altar into the mind of every practitioner.

## §V Middle-Way Corrections

After the foregoing arguments, five common misreadings of the Four Clear and Decisive Instructions must be explicitly corrected.

### 5.1 ❌ The Four Instructions are not pre-modern moralism

The most common misreading reads the Four Instructions as traditional ethical instruction — "the Buddha is telling us not to do bad things." This reading misses the point entirely.

The Four Instructions are not ethics but **technical boundary conditions for meditative cultivation**. They address not "how a good person should behave" but "what conditions must be met for someone who wishes to cultivate *samādhi*." To put it metaphorically: this is not "don't steal because stealing is wrong" but "a laboratory cannot have bacterial contamination because contamination corrupts the experiment." The first is an ethical judgment; the second is a technical precondition — entirely different in nature.

Does this mean someone who does not cultivate *samādhi* is free from the Four Instructions? From a general perspective on *śīla*, that is a separate question (the ordinary *vinaya* applies to all Buddhist followers). But from within the context of the Four Instructions themselves, the teaching is: **if you wish to cultivate *samādhi*, these four are unsidesteppable preconditions**. This is not a moral demand; it is a feasibility condition.

Why does this distinction matter? Because if you read the Four Instructions as morality, you will naturally begin to discuss "situations," "exceptions," "degrees" — as all ethics does. But if you read them as technical preconditions, you will not — just as one does not ask "can the engine check before takeoff have exceptions," because one understands that the check is not morality but physics. In the domain of meditation, the Four Instructions are physics of exactly this kind.

### 5.2 ❌ "As long as I don't do it, I'm fine"

The second misreading treats the Four Instructions as behavioral precepts — "as long as I don't actually do it, I haven't violated." This reading completely misunderstands the weight of the character *qí xīn* (their mind).

As argued above, the object of the Four Instructions is mind, not behavior. Zǐxuán says "guarding each mental movement inwardly, with severity held equally"; Jiāoguāng says "no single thought arising" — these are not exaggerations but precise standards matched to the meditation context. If you use "behavior not in violation" to read the Four Instructions, you have already missed the whole point — because the object of meditation is mind, and therefore the object of the precepts must also be mind.

The concrete form of this misreading is: "I was just thinking about it"; "I didn't actually do it"; "a thought that flashed through doesn't count." Such self-justifications have some validity in body-and-speech precept contexts, but none in mind-containment precept contexts. Because when cultivating *samādhi*, what you face *is* precisely those "just thinking" thoughts — if you indulge them in daily life, they will become the main actors in meditation.

### 5.3 ⚠️ The order lust → kill → steal → lie is not a ranking of severity

The third misreading treats the sequence lust → kill → steal → lie as a "ranking of severity" — imagining that lust is the gravest and gross false speech the least grave. This completely misunderstands the sūtra's teaching logic.

Jiāoguāng's lust-first adjudication has already shown that lust comes first not because lust is more evil than killing, but because in the **context of cultivation**, lust most directly disrupts meditation (as argued at length in §3.2 — both at the somatic-mobilization level and at the subject-object-structural level). The *Brahmajāla Sūtra* places killing first because in the **context of establishing teaching**, killing most fundamentally violates the compassionate ground. The *Yogācārabhūmi* places self-praise and denigration first because in the **context of the bodhisattva path**, harming fellow bodhisattvas most thoroughly destroys altruism. Three orderings, three contexts — not an absolute ranking of evil.

A specific warning is needed: one must absolutely not use the *Śūraṅgama*'s lust-first ordering to conclude that "lust is graver than murder," which would be a grotesque distortion. The *Śūraṅgama*'s order is a cultivation priority order, not an ethical severity order. A person who observes three and violates one (say, observing lust-kill-steal but committing gross false speech) has, in the checklist structure of the Four Instructions, failed entirely — not because gross false speech is graver than the others, but because each item in the checklist is an independent life-or-death threshold.

### 5.4 ⚠️ *Yogācāra*'s skillful expedient is not a loophole for the *Śūraṅgama*'s absolute prohibition

The fourth misreading is the most dangerous — it typically occurs among practitioners who have some familiarity with the *Yogācārabhūmi*: using the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s "skillful expedient allowing killing, stealing, lust, and falsehood" as a justification for violating the *Śūraṅgama*'s precepts.

The *Yogācārabhūmi* does indeed allow a bodhisattva, under specific conditions, to "engage in a small portion of what are by nature offenses — for this reason, their bodhisattva precepts are not violated, and much merit is produced."[^46] But this opening has extraordinarily strict preconditions: (1) the actor must already be a bodhisattva abiding in pure bodhisattva *saṃvara*; (2) the action must be purely for the benefit of others, with no trace of self-interested motive; (3) there must be a clear karmic calculation (e.g., the bodhisattva would rather kill a bandit and fall into hell himself than let the bandit commit the karma of mass murder); (4) this operates in the **context of bodhisattva altruistic action**, not in the **context of the meditator's self-cultivation**.

When a practitioner cultivating *samādhi* uses the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s opening to justify a violation, what they are actually committing is not the opening but **the theft forbidden by the Third Instruction** — twisting a provisional teaching the Buddha gave in one context into one's own interpretive license to back one's own behavior. Zǐxuán's definition of this theft is exact: "Not to take the Tathāgata's teachings of provisional meaning and twist them into one's own private interpretation, misleading beginners... grasping the expedient while slandering the real is of this same kind."[^47] Taking provisional as definitive, taking expedient as ultimate, bringing an opening from one context to exonerate oneself in another — this is theft at the level of Dharma, and it is more serious than theft of material goods.

This correction is especially important because it addresses an error that more knowledge makes more possible — a person who knows nothing of the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s opening cannot make this mistake at all; only someone who has read the *Yogācārabhūmi* can. It must be said plainly here: one reads the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s skillful-expedient passages in order to understand the breadth of the bodhisattva path, not to find loopholes for oneself in the *Śūraṅgama*'s context.

### 5.5 ❌ The Four Instructions are not Christian-style original sin

The fifth misreading comes from cross-cultural confusion — reading the Buddhist concept of "precept" as analogous to the Christian concept of "sin," assuming the Four Instructions say "human nature contains these four original sins that must be suppressed." This completely misses the basic character of Buddhist *śīla*.

Buddhist *śīla* is not the opposite of sin — it is a **technical enabling condition**, not a **prohibitive repression**. The Buddha established precepts not because human nature is essentially evil and must be curbed, but because practice has its physical conditions — in the same way that chess has rules. The rules exist not because you are a bad person to be controlled, but because without rules there is no chess.

The correct reading of the Four Instructions is: "If you want to cultivate *samādhi*, these four conditions make it possible; without these four conditions, *samādhi* physically cannot arise." This is an *enabling* statement — it makes something possible. Reading it as "four original sins of human nature" imports the Christian category of sin into Buddhist precept, and is a cross-cultural category error.

This correction is especially important for modern readers, because modern culture tends to have an instinctive reaction against concepts like "precept," "taboo," and "regulation" — they feel like repressive legacies of ancient religion. But the Four Instructions are not of that kind. They are more like an athlete's training discipline, a scientist's laboratory protocols, or a pilot's pre-flight checklist — technical preconditions established so that something highly precise can occur, not chains placed on human nature.

## §VI Conclusion: The Three Perimeters Considered Together

Returning to the correction of Paper 06 with which this paper opened: the Four Clear and Decisive Instructions on Purity are not "protection while walking on the path" but "the first perimeter before entering." This correction is not merely a re-ordering; it is a re-understanding of the entire structure of the preparatory package.

From the latter half of fascicle 6 through fascicle 8, the Buddha is teaching the construction of an **altar of mind**. This altar has three perimeters:

**The outermost perimeter: moral guardrails** (latter half of fascicle 6 — the Four Instructions). Cutting off lust, killing, stealing, and gross false speech — these constitute the boundary-setting of the altar of mind. They must be erected first, because boundary-setting is the precondition for all subsequent steps. The defining feature of this layer is the **checklist structure** — four independent items, no skipping, each a complete Buddha-versus-Māra discrimination.

**The middle perimeter: spatial guardrails** (fascicle 7 — the *maṇḍala* platform and Śūraṅgama *dhāraṇī*). Establishing the physical altar and reciting the Śūraṅgama mantra — these are the sprinkling and empowerment of the altar of mind. Only after the moral boundaries have been erected does the purification of physical space have meaning; without the moral perimeter, even a magnificent physical altar is merely a worldly building.

**The innermost perimeter: purification of mind** (fascicle 8 — the Three Gradual Stages and the Ground of Dry Wisdom). Cutting off the assisting causes, carving out the primary nature, reversing the present karma — this is the formal purification procedure unfolding inside the altar of mind. Paper 06 examined this layer in detail: the poisoned-honey metaphor, the double-exit structure of the Ground of Dry Wisdom, the recursive rhythm of the Four Preparatory Stages.

Together, the three constitute the actual preparatory package (加行) for the Ear-Faculty Practice. Without any one layer, the subsequent Ear-Faculty work is theoretically impossible. And the order of the three is not interchangeable: moral precedes spatial, spatial precedes mental. This is why the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* places the Four Instructions in the latter half of fascicle 6, the *maṇḍala* and *dhāraṇī* in fascicle 7, and the Three Gradual Stages in fascicle 8 — this ordering *is* the teaching.

From this three-perimeter framework, we can better understand the overall structure of the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra*: Fascicles 1 through the first part of 6 destroy delusion and reveal the real (Seven Locations of Mind, Ten Verifications of Seeing, Twenty-Five Avenues of Perfect Penetration, Mañjuśrī's selection of the Ear-Faculty) — this is **choosing the gate**. The latter half of fascicle 6 through fascicle 8 is the three-layered preparatory practice — this is **erecting the perimeters**. Fascicles 9 and 10 are the Fifty Skandha-Māras — this is **the demon-revealing mirror** (what traps await when you actually begin to practice). Finally there is the transmission section. The Four Instructions, as the outermost of the three perimeters, function to *make the subsequent demon chapters meaningful*: if you have not even erected the Four Instructions, the demon-revealing mirror of the Fifty Skandha-Māras has no one to reflect — it cannot see a practitioner who has not yet entered.

This understanding sets the context for Papers 08 through 10 on the Fifty Skandha-Māras. The demonic states are not an independent "demonology" but a pathology report of what happens when the Four Instructions fail. Every demonic state's trap point can be traced to a subtle violation of one of the Four Instructions — a theme for the subsequent papers. Especially relevant is the "love-view Māra" anticipated by the Fourth Instruction — this term will recur throughout the Fifty Skandha-Māras, referring to the māra that comes into being at the moment a practitioner, experiencing a meditative state, claims outwardly "I have realized." The fourth item of the Four Instructions checklist is the first gate of the entire structure of the Fifty Skandha-Māras.

Finally: the very existence of this paper (and the fact that you have read to this point) says something important — the Four Instructions are not the remote *śīla* of antiquity but the technical preconditions any serious practitioner today must face. In the age of declining Dharma, false teachers proclaiming the Dharma are as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, and the true is difficult to distinguish from the false. But the Buddha gave us, two thousand years ago, the most direct criterion: "If I speak this way, this is the speech of the Buddha; if one does not speak this way, this is the speech of Pāpīyas." This criterion is still operative today — not as moralistic preaching, but as the technical boundary of the work of meditation.

May all who read this far be able to erect these four perimeters within their own minds — not as repression, but as the technical conditions that make *samādhi* possible. May the moral altar be first erected, the physical space then built, and the purification of mind at last unfold safely within these perimeters.
