## Abstract

The doctrine of the *Six Characteristics in Perfect Fusion* (*liù xiàng yuán róng* 六相圓融)—totality (*zǒng xiàng* 總相), distinction (*bié xiàng* 別相), sameness (*tóng xiàng* 同相), difference (*yì xiàng* 異相), formation (*chéng xiàng* 成相), and decay (*huài xiàng* 壞相)—is the simultaneous-dialectical instrument by which the Huayan school (*Huáyán zōng* 華嚴宗) installs three pairs of relations onto the structure of any single dharma: whole and part, homogeneity and heterogeneity, establishment and self-restraint. This paper argues that the six characteristics are not six independent categories but six simultaneously co-present facets of one and the same dharma; remove any single characteristic and the remaining five cannot stand alone.

The paper traces the textual genealogy of this instrument across four centuries and two continents. The six terms themselves already appear in the sūtra text of the *Daśabhūmika*'s "Great Vow" passage (preserved in fascicle three of Vasubandhu's *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna*, T1522), and so are not a later commentarial imposition but a hermeneutic vocabulary supplied by the scripture itself. Vasubandhu's fascicle one provides the first systematic exegesis and establishes the *chú shì* 除事 ("setting aside the particulars") principle that fixes the six as hermeneutic rather than ontological. The *Dilun* school transmits and refines the doctrine through Jingying Huiyuan's four-gate framework (preserved in Chengguan's *Yǎnyì chāo* 演義鈔, T1736 fascicle 52). Zhiyan, the second Huayan patriarch, deploys the six characteristics at the application layer without doctrinal innovation. Fazang's *Treatise on the Five Doctrines* (*Huáyán wǔjiào zhāng* 華嚴五教章, T1866 fascicle 4) completes the perfect-teaching doctrinalization through the famous house-and-rafter parable, the eternalism-and-nihilism exclusion procedure, the eight-line summary verse, and the distinction between the *six meanings* (*liù yì* 六義) and the *six characteristics* (*liù xiàng* 六相). Chengguan's *Huáyán jīng shū* 華嚴經疏 (T1735) and *Yǎnyì chāo* (T1736) further systematize the doctrine into a seven-gate structure that explicitly inherits and extends Huiyuan's four gates—a cross-school transmission that links the Yogācāra-affiliated *Dilun* tradition to the Huayan school proper.

The paper further argues that Fazang's distinction—"the six meanings rest on the self-nature (*zì tǐ* 自體) of dependent origination; the six characteristics rest on the modal-aspect (*yì mén* 義門) of dependent origination"—is the single most important doctrinal bridge for readers of the S3 series (*Cheng weishi lun* and the Yogācāra texts) entering Huayan. The *six meanings* face is ontological (*things-themselves*); the *six characteristics* face is hermeneutic (*modes-of-viewing*). Both unfold from the same dependent origination but along distinct paths that cannot be collapsed into one another. This historical lineage—Vasubandhu, Huiyuan, Zhiyan, Fazang, Chengguan—itself crosses the Yogācāra and Huayan traditions, so the bridge is not merely structural analogy but a real seven-century lineage of cross-school transmission.

The paper corresponds to the eighth of Samantabhadra's *Ten Great Vows*—*Constantly Following the Buddha's Learning* (*cháng suí fó xué* 常隨佛學)—and to the *Mutual Containment Without Confusion* (*yī duō xiāng róng bù tóng mén* 一多相容不同門) of the Ten Profound Gates. S8-P11 has already articulated this correspondence from the Ten Gates angle; §VI of the present paper redeploys it from the six-characteristics angle, showing that learning-from-one-buddha is itself an event in which all six characteristics operate simultaneously, and that the "constant" of *Constantly Following* refers not to temporal duration but to the simultaneous co-presence of the six. Fazang's culminating sentence in the *Wujiao zhang* fascicle four six-characteristics passage—"At the moment of first arousing the aspirational mind, full enlightenment is already accomplished. This is precisely because the six characteristics of *dharmadhātu* dependent origination are perfectly fused, causes and effects simultaneously inter-pervading and self-mastered in both forward and reverse direction"—is here taken up from its structural face; S8-P05 will later take up the same sentence from the positional face of the *xìn mǎn wèi* 信滿位 (faith-completion stage) doctrine.

**Keywords 關鍵詞:** Six Characteristics in Perfect Fusion 六相圓融, totality 總相, distinction 別相, sameness 同相, difference 異相, formation 成相, decay 壞相, *dharmadhātu* dependent origination 法界緣起, Vasubandhu 世親, Fazang 法藏, Chengguan 澄觀, *yì mén* (modal-aspect) 義門, *zì tǐ* (self-nature) 自體, "first-aspiration-equals-enlightenment" 初發心時便成正覺, Constantly Following the Buddha's Learning 常隨佛學, Mutual Containment Without Confusion 一多相容不同門

## I. Introduction: The Instant You See a House

The instant your gaze falls upon a wooden house, try answering this question: in that single instant, which entered your awareness first—*the house*, or *the rafters and tiles*?

If you say "the house first"—were the rafters and tiles a delayed second arrival? When you saw the house, were the rafters and tiles somehow not there?

If you say "the rafters and tiles first"—did the house come along afterward? When you saw only scattered rafters and tiles, did the house not yet exist?

If you say "they came simultaneously"—you are already standing at the threshold of *liù xiàng yuán róng*.

This simple observation is the starting point of Fazang's entire treatment of the *Six Characteristics in Perfect Fusion* in fascicle four of the *Wujiao zhang*. Fazang is not posing a philosophical riddle. He is pointing to a fact that most readers of the *Avataṃsaka* have never paused to examine: **whole and part, homogeneity and heterogeneity, establishment and self-restraint—these three relations are not a temporal dialectical progression but the simultaneous co-presence of one and the same dharma seen along six observational facets**. When you see the house, the rafters and tiles are already there; when you see only the rafters and tiles, the house has already, at that very instant, been established. There is no before and after, only simultaneity—and this simultaneity is not coincidence but the basic structure of dependent origination viewed along its hermeneutic face.

### 1.1 Why the Six Characteristics Must Be Installed Early

The published order of the S8 series follows the sequence of Samantabhadra's Ten Great Vows (P01 through P10). But within the writing order, this paper—P08—falls at the head of the "basic instrument" stage, for a single reason: the six characteristics are a doctrinal tool that every subsequent paper in S8 will need to draw upon. When P03 expands the *unobstructed mutual interpenetration of phenomena*, when P05 expands the doctrine of *the faith-completion stage subsuming all subsequent stages*, when P09 treats Sudhana's pilgrimage of fifty-three good friends, when P10 advances the formal argument that the Ten Profound Gates correspond to the Ten Great Vows—the three relations of whole-and-part, one-and-many, homogeneity-and-heterogeneity will recur over and over. Without first installing the six characteristics as a shared toolbox, every later paper would have to re-explain the same dialectic at every point of contact—a writing-economic cost the series cannot bear.

But the word "tool" should not be taken lightly here. The six characteristics are not S8's scaffolding; they are its mirror. When you have learned to use the six characteristics to behold a house, a golden lion, or the act of bowing to a buddha, you have also learned to use them to behold the *dharmadhātu* itself. **The six characteristics are an instrument, but they are an instrument by which the *dharmadhātu* sees itself**—and this is the difference between the present treatment and a typical "introduction to Huayan doctrine."

### 1.2 Four Structural Theses

The argument that follows turns on four structural theses, each of which corresponds to a sub-section of §III.

*First*, **the six characteristics arise from the great scripture itself**. The six terms—totality, distinction, sameness, difference, formation, decay—appear directly in the sūtra text of the *Daśabhūmika*'s "Great Vow" passage at the first ground (a fact directly evidenced by the scriptural citation that opens fascicle three of Vasubandhu's *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna*). Vasubandhu is not the *inventor* of the six characteristics; he is their first systematic commentator. This fact dispels a popular misreading: the six characteristics are not a Chinese Huayan invention but a hermeneutic vocabulary that the Indian Mahāyāna scripture itself already supplied.

*Second*, **the six characteristics are "modal-aspect" rather than "self-nature" from the very beginning**. When Vasubandhu defines the six in fascicle one of the *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna*, he explicitly inscribes the *chú shì* 除事 principle—"the particulars are the aggregates, the bases, the elements, and so on"—meaning that the six characteristics treat not the *dravya*-level facts of the five aggregates and the eighteen elements but their meaning-level dimensions. Five centuries later, Fazang refines this principle into the foundational distinction "the six meanings rest on the self-nature of dependent origination; the six characteristics rest on the modal-aspect of dependent origination." This paper argues that this distinction is the single most important bridge for S3 Yogācāra readers entering Huayan: it tells the S3 reader that the six characteristics are not a parallel ontological model competing with *trisvabhāva*, but the unfolding of the same dependent origination along a different path.

*Third*, **the simultaneous co-presence structure of the six characteristics is not Hegelian dialectic**. To a twenty-first-century reader, the six categories "totality, distinction, sameness, difference, formation, decay" may look like the Hegelian "thesis–antithesis–synthesis" triad has spawned a near relative. §V will need to make explicit that this resemblance is purely surface-level. Hegelian dialectic is a temporal *Aufhebung*; the six-characteristics dialectic is a synchronic co-presence. To read the six as a temporal sequence "first totality, then distinction; first formation, then decay" is to commit one of the most damaging distortions of Huayan doctrine.

*Fourth*, **"At the moment of first arousing the aspirational mind, full enlightenment is already accomplished" is possible precisely because of the perfect fusion of the six characteristics**. Fazang's culminating sentence in the *Wujiao zhang* fascicle four passage on the six characteristics—"At the moment of first arousing the aspirational mind, full enlightenment is already accomplished. This is precisely because the six characteristics of *dharmadhātu* dependent origination are perfectly fused, causes and effects simultaneously inter-pervading and self-mastered in both forward and reverse direction"—is not rhetorical encouragement but a structural argument-proposition. §3.6 will take up this sentence from its *structural* face: the moment of first aspiration and the moment of full enlightenment, under the simultaneous co-presence structure of the six characteristics, already share the same six-fold structure within a single thought-moment. The proposition is not "if you arouse the aspirational mind, you will gradually become a buddha"—it is "the event of arousing the aspirational mind already possesses the entire structure of the event of full enlightenment." S8-P05 will later take up the same sentence from the positional face of the *faith-completion-stage* doctrine.

### 1.3 The Vow and the Gate to Which This Paper Corresponds

This paper corresponds to the eighth of Samantabhadra's Ten Great Vows, *Constantly Following the Buddha's Learning*, and to the Profound Gate of *Mutual Containment Without Confusion*. S8-P11 §3.8 has already given the structural statement from the Ten Profound Gates angle: to learn from one buddha is to learn from all buddhas (*mutual containment*), yet Śākyamuni remains Śākyamuni, and his particular teaching skillful means remain his alone (*without confusion*). The crux of *mutual containment without confusion* lies in the *without-confusion*: the one does not become the many, the many do not become the one, yet the one and the many are mutually containing in their very nature.

§VI of the present paper redeploys *Constantly Following the Buddha's Learning* from the six-characteristics angle. At the moment of learning from a single buddha, six characteristics operate simultaneously: *totality-as-sameness* (the dharma is the same dharma), *distinction-as-difference* (each buddha's specific skillful means remains his own), *sameness-as-formation* (all buddhas jointly disclose the same reality), *difference-as-formation* (each buddha responds to his own audience), *formation-establishes* (the act of teaching genuinely takes place), *decay-abides* (each facet remains itself rather than collapsing into something else). **"Constantly Following the Buddha's Learning" is not the temporal duration of "always following"; it is the moment-by-moment opening of the six characteristics in simultaneity**.

### 1.4 Notice of Failure Points

This paper carries five failure points, each of which §V will address in turn:

1. **The Hegelian misreading**: reading the six characteristics as a Hegelian temporal progression (the principal teaching-danger of this paper).
2. **The "six-independent-categories" misreading**: reading the six as six separable items.
3. **The *six meanings* / *six characteristics* confusion**: especially common among S3 Yogācāra readers.
4. **The cheapening of "first-aspiration-equals-enlightenment"**: reading the sentence as a personal psychological achievement.
5. **The "*trisvabhāva* equals six characteristics" unification claim**: reading the §4.1 correspondence table as a merger of the two systems.

A reader who finds any of the five suspicious during the body of the paper is encouraged to skip directly to the corresponding item in §V.

## II. Scriptural Foundations

In keeping with the fourth methodological principle of the S8 series (set out in S8-P01)—the radicalized "sūtras-first, treatises-second" structure—this section is divided into three parts: (A) the sūtra grounding within the *Avataṃsaka*; (B) the Indian commentarial source, Vasubandhu's *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna*; (C) the Chinese patriarchal lineage, from the *Dilun* school through Chengguan. This paper is the first in the S8 series to display the full Indian-treatise-to-Chinese-classification lineage in its complete form, and the three-part division is itself a methodological declaration: the six characteristics are not the isolated invention of any single patriarch but a mature instrument matured through the double sedimentation of Indian exegetical tradition and Chinese doctrinal classification, beginning from prompts already present in the scripture itself.

### 2.1 (A) The *Avataṃsaka* Source

#### 2.1.1 The Tenth-Ground Chapter, First Ground: The Scriptural Position to Which the Six Characteristics Are Attached

The scriptural source-position of the six characteristics is the first ground—the "Ground of Joy" (*pramuditā*)—of the *Daśabhūmika* (*shí dì pǐn* 十地品) of the *Avataṃsaka*. Fascicle 34 of the eighty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* gives the enumeration of the ten grounds and the entrance description of the first ground, providing the scriptural substrate from which Vasubandhu would extract the six characteristics:

> What are the ten? The first is the Ground of Joy, the second the Ground of Stainlessness, the third the Ground of Radiance, the fourth the Ground of Blazing Wisdom, the fifth the Ground of Difficult-to-Conquer, the sixth the Ground of Manifestation, the seventh the Far-Reaching Ground, the eighth the Immovable Ground, the ninth the Ground of Excellent Wisdom, the tenth the Ground of the Dharma Cloud. Buddha-sons, these ten bodhisattva grounds have been spoken, will be spoken, and are now spoken by the buddhas of the three times.[^1]

> ...his position is to be born into the lineage of the Tathāgatas, with no flaw of family or descent, having departed the worldly destinies and entered the supramundane path; he attains the bodhisattva's dharma, abides in the bodhisattva's place, enters into the equality of the three times, and is decisively destined for unsurpassed enlightenment within the lineage of the Tathāgatas. A bodhisattva who abides in dharmas of this sort is said to abide in the bodhisattva's *Ground of Joy*, by reason of conformity with the unwavering mark.[^2]

The passages that follow at the first ground—the "ten joys," the "perfections of the great relinquishment," the "great vow" passage, and so on—together constitute a complete description of the bodhisattva's entry into the first ground. When Vasubandhu undertakes the systematic exegesis of this passage in the *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna*, he extracts the structural unit that he calls "the ten phrases" (*shí jù* 十句): every paragraph of the *Daśabhūmika*'s discursive content unfolds in groups of ten, and the six characteristics are precisely the hermeneutic tool for analyzing the internal structure of any such ten-phrase unit.

#### 2.1.2 The Great Vow Passage: The Six Names Appear in the Scripture Itself [Critical Finding]

The present paper must single out a fact that most Huayan readers have overlooked: **the six terms themselves—totality, distinction, sameness, difference, formation, decay—appear directly within the scriptural text of the *Daśabhūmika*'s "great vow" passage at the first ground**. This is directly evidenced by the sūtra passage cited in fascicle three of Vasubandhu's *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna*:

> The sūtra says: he also arouses a great vow, namely, "all the practices of all bodhisattvas, vast and immeasurable, unmixed, encompassed by the perfections, purified by the grounds, generating all dharmas auxiliary to the path—**characteristics of totality, characteristics of distinction, characteristics of sameness, characteristics of difference, characteristics of formation, characteristics of decay**—articulating all the practices of all bodhisattvas as the path of the grounds in actuality, together with the skillful activity of the perfections, in order to teach all beings, causing them to receive the practices and grow in mind."[^3]

This passage is the content of the bodhisattva's ninth great vow at the first ground—the vow to take the six characteristics as a *skillful means* (*upāya*) by which to encompass all bodhisattva practice. **Within the scriptural passage itself, the six characteristics figure as one of the items in the great vow's list of skillful means; they are not a hermeneutic category that Vasubandhu added afterward**. This finding has decisive consequences for the discussion in §3.1: the six characteristics are not Vasubandhu's invention but were already in the scripture that Vasubandhu was glossing. Vasubandhu's contribution was to provide systematic commentarial treatment for these six already-existing terms and to elevate them from "a list-item within the great vow at the first ground" into "the universal instrument for analyzing all ten-phrase structures throughout the *Daśabhūmika*."

Fazang's later historical placement of the source of the six characteristics—captured in the eleventh-century gloss "the Master Gangzang took the six characteristics from the source of the great scripture" (*gāngzàng liù xiàng yuán hū dà jīng* 剛藏六相源乎大經, in fascicle one of the *Yúnjiān lèijiě* 雲間類解 commentary on the *Jīn shīzi zhāng*)—thereby acquires its documentary basis.[^4]

### 2.2 (B) The Indian Commentarial Source: Vasubandhu's *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna*

#### 2.2.1 The Earliest Systematic Definition (Fascicle One)

Vasubandhu's *Shí dì jīng lùn* 十地經論 (*Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna*) is the earliest systematic treatment of the six characteristics in the entire Buddhist canon. The defining passage in fascicle one is the headwaters of all subsequent six-characteristics hermeneutics:

> In every ten-phrase unit of all that is spoken, there are six gates of differentiated characteristics. As to this verbal-explanation, one should know that the particulars are set aside; "the particulars" means the aggregates, the bases, the elements, and the like. The six characteristics are: totality, distinction, sameness, difference, formation, decay. Totality is the *root entry*; distinction is the *remaining nine entries*. Distinction depends upon the root and fulfills it. Sameness is by virtue of being entries; difference is by virtue of incremental marking. Formation is the *brief statement*; decay is the *expanded statement*—as in the formation and decay of a world-system. With respect to all other ten-phrase units, recognize them according to their context.[^5]

This text has four key features.

*First*, **"verbal-explanation"** (*yán shuō jiě shì* 言說解釋): the six characteristics are explicitly positioned as instruments of *verbal-explanation*—that is, as exegetical-hermeneutic categories, not as ontological or metaphysical categories of being.

*Second*, **the "set aside the particulars" principle** (*chú shì* 除事): "the particulars are the aggregates, the bases, the elements, and so on." The six characteristics do not treat the *dravya*-level objects of the five *skandhas*, the eighteen *dhātus*, or the twelve *āyatanas*—they treat the meaning-level dimension of these objects. When you analyze a discursive passage with the six characteristics, you are not asking "to which aggregate does this passage refer"; you are asking "how does the internal discursive structure of this passage unfold along six observational facets simultaneously?" This *chú shì* principle is the earliest Indian basis for what Fazang would later refine into "the six meanings rest on the self-nature; the six characteristics rest on the modal-aspect"—Vasubandhu himself had already placed the six characteristics on the *modal-aspect* path.

*Third*, **the "root entry / remaining nine entries" structure**. The discursive form of the *Daśabhūmika* unfolds in units of "ten entries" (i.e., ten phrases), and the six characteristics are designed to analyze the internal structure of each ten-entry unit. *Totality* corresponds to the root entry (the first); *distinction* corresponds to the remaining nine; *sameness* and *difference* correspond to the homogeneity and heterogeneity among the ten entries; *formation* and *decay* correspond to the brief and expanded modes of statement. This structural correspondence shows that the six characteristics are bound tightly to the discursive form of the *Daśabhūmika* itself—they are not categories deduced from some abstract dialectic but a hermeneutic instrument inducted from the scripture's own discursive form.

*Fourth*, **the "world-formation-and-decay" simile**. Vasubandhu compresses the meaning of the formation and decay characteristics into the single phrase "as in the formation and decay of a world-system." This compressed simile, however terse, is the distant ancestor of Fazang's elaborate house-and-rafter parable five hundred years later.

#### 2.2.2 Range of Application and General Rule

Other passages in the same fascicle of Vasubandhu's commentary show that the application of the six characteristics extends well beyond the single position of the "first-ground great vow." The line "with respect to all other ten-phrase units, recognize them according to their context" makes explicit that the six characteristics may be applied to every ten-phrase unit of discursive material in the *Daśabhūmika*:

> Among these, "well-decided" is the totality; the rest are distinction. *Sameness* lies in being well-decided; *difference* lies in being distinct. *Formation* is the brief statement; *decay* is the expanded statement, as in the formation and decay of a world-system.[^6]

> In this verse, "mutually beholding one another" is the totality; "all together pay reverence" is the distinction. So for the remaining verses: the first phrase is totality, the rest are distinction; sameness, difference, formation, and decay follow as above.[^7]

Vasubandhu even applies the six characteristics to the relation between the opening phrase and the remaining phrases of a *gāthā*. This shows that, in Vasubandhu's hands, the six characteristics already constitute a mature, broadly applicable exegetical instrument—but their status remains at the level of "verbal-explanation," not yet elevated to the level of an "observational gate for beholding the *dharmadhātu*." That elevation would have to wait six or seven hundred years to be completed in China.

### 2.3 (C) The Chinese Patriarchal Lineage

The Chinese patriarchal treatment of the six characteristics, from Vasubandhu down to Fazang, constitutes the main axis of §III. This subsection lists only the documentary basis; the detailed exposition is reserved for §III.

#### 2.3.1 The *Dilun* School: Jingying Huiyuan's Four Gates

The *Dilun* school (the school devoted to the *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna*) represents the middle stretch of this lineage during the sixth century. The most important *Dilun* treatment of the six characteristics is the "four-gate exegesis" of Jingying Huiyuan (淨影慧遠, 523–592—not to be confused with the earlier Huiyuan of Mount Lu, 334–416). Huiyuan's own writing on this could not be located directly in the present project's RAG vaults, but its structure is preserved within Chengguan's *Yǎnyì chāo* fascicle 52:

> Master Yuan's four-gate exegesis: first, *establishing the intention*—to break fixed grasping; second, *establishing the basis*—glossing by way of the practice of the first ground; third, *general application to all dharmas*; fourth, *exegesis of the textual passages*.[^8]

The four gates are: (i) *establishing the intention*—the discursive purpose of the six characteristics is to break "fixed grasping" (the grasping at any single characteristic as substantially real); (ii) *establishing the basis*—using the practice of the first ground as the demonstrative example; (iii) *general application to all dharmas*—extending the six characteristics to the universal interpretation of all dharmas; (iv) *exegesis of the textual passages*—item-by-item analysis of specific scriptural lines. Jingying Huiyuan's four gates are the direct precursor of Chengguan's later seven gates—a historical thread whose methodological significance §3.4 will treat in detail.

#### 2.3.2 Sui-Tang Transition: Zhiyan's Application-Layer Treatment

Zhiyan (智儼, 602–668), the second patriarch of the Huayan school, residing at Yunhua Monastery and known to posterity as the "Great Master of Yunhua," uses the six characteristics in his writings as a working hermeneutic instrument applied to actual exegesis—not as the topic of a dedicated doctrinal chapter. His *Huáyán jīng wèndá* 華嚴經問答 (T1873) directly invokes the six characteristics to interpret the ten-entry structure of the ten grounds:

> Question: Is this only with respect to the ten grounds, or does it pervade the prior stages? Answer: When the ten grounds are spoken, the prior positions are encompassed within them, **for the ten entries are explained by means of the six characteristics**, as may be known.[^9]

> Once one has, with respect to the realization of the first ground, set up the ten entries and **displayed them by means of the six characteristics**, then one may know: the first ground is the initial arousal of the mind of faith.[^10]

Zhiyan's use of the six characteristics is purely instrumental: he inherits Vasubandhu's definition and applies it to his own exposition of the doctrine of the ten grounds, but he does not undertake any systematic doctrinalization of the six characteristics themselves. He writes no dedicated chapter, proposes no new structural framework, deploys no independent simile (no house, no golden lion) to demonstrate the six characteristics, and does not elevate them from "verbal-explanation" to "observational gate for beholding the *dharmadhātu*." All of these tasks would await Fazang.

The historical division of labor between Zhiyan and Fazang is captured with great precision by a later commentarial verdict. Fascicle one of the *Huáyán jīng jīn shīzi zhāng yúnjiān lèijiě* 華嚴經金師子章雲間類解 (Song-dynasty commentary by Jingyuan):

> **The ten profound gates of Master Yunhua are rooted in the gate of contemplation; the six characteristics of Master Gangzang issue forth from the source of the great scripture.** Sūtra and contemplation interfuse, characteristics and gates mutually penetrate—and so the eighth section gathers the six characteristics together.[^11]

"Master Yunhua" is Zhiyan (after Yunhua Monastery, where he resided); "Master Gangzang" is Fazang under another honorific. The verdict establishes that, within the Huayan division of labor, Zhiyan's central work is the Ten Profound Gates and Fazang's central work is the six characteristics—and that the source of the six characteristics is "the great scripture" (i.e., the *Avataṃsaka* itself, specifically the first-ground great-vow passage that §2.1.2 has just exhibited). This verdict provides documentary support for §3.2's positioning: in the lineage of the six characteristics, Zhiyan is an *applier* rather than a *systematic constructor*—a positioning that is sustained, not invented, by later historical judgment.

#### 2.3.3 The Tang Huayan School: Fazang's Perfect-Teaching Doctrinalization

Fazang (法藏, 643–712), the third patriarch of the Huayan school, known as Master Xianshou (賢首), treats the six characteristics in three works:

- The *Huáyán yī chéng jiào yì fēn qí zhāng* 華嚴一乘教義分齊章 (commonly cited as the *Huáyán wǔjiào zhāng* 華嚴五教章), T1866 fascicle four, "the fourth: the meaning of the six characteristics in perfect fusion"—the principal source for §3.3, containing the complete three-gate structure (enumeration of the names with brief glosses; statement of the discursive intention; question-and-answer exposition), the house-and-rafter parable, the eternalism-and-nihilism exclusion procedure, the eight-line summary verse, the culminating "first-aspiration-equals-enlightenment" sentence, and the foundational "six meanings rest on self-nature; six characteristics rest on modal-aspect" distinction.
- The *Huáyán jīng tànxuán jì* 華嚴經探玄記 T1733 fascicles three and nine—a six-gate structural exegesis of the six characteristics, including the distinctive placement of the six characteristics as one of three kinds of skillful means (the *gathering-and-establishing skillful means*, *jí chéng fāng biàn* 集成方便, alongside the *initiating skillful means* and the *non-abiding skillful means*).
- The *Huáyán jīn shīzi zhāng* 華嚴金師子章 T1880 and the *Huáyán jīng yìhǎi bǎi mén* 華嚴經義海百門 T1881—the golden-lion parable as the most concise demonstration, paired with the house-and-rafter parable in T1866 to form the two classic six-characteristics demonstrations.

Fazang's treatment marks the *standardization* phase of the lineage: the six characteristics are elevated from Vasubandhu's verbal-explanation instrument into the central display-instrument of the Huayan perfect-teaching doctrine of the *dharmadhātu*. The relevant passages from all three works will be cited in full in §3.3.

#### 2.3.4 Mid-Tang: Chengguan's Systematic Seven Gates

Chengguan (澄觀, 738–839), the fourth patriarch of the Huayan school, known as the National Teacher Qingliang, further systematizes Fazang's treatment of the six characteristics. The *Dà fāng guǎng fó huáyán jīng shū* 大方廣佛華嚴經疏 T1735 and its self-commentary, the *Yǎnyì chāo* 演義鈔 T1736, contain Chengguan's seven-gate structure. His treatment is found at two locations:

- *Huáyán jīng shū* fascicle 31 (the prefatory section of the *Daśabhūmika* commentary): "thus Master Gangzang awaited the fivefold request before he would speak; Vasubandhu rendered all things perfectly fused by means of the six characteristics"—a single sentence that fixes the historical positioning of Vasubandhu's six characteristics.[^12]
- *Yǎnyì chāo* fascicle 52: an explicit listing of the seven-gate structure—"first, *general indication of the great intention*; second, *citation that displays the universality*; third, *clarification of the discursive intention*; fourth, *enumeration of the names with brief glosses*; fifth, *general illustration through simile*; sixth, *exhaustive gloss through cited examples*; seventh, *pointing to the elaborated treatment in other works*."[^13]

Chengguan's seven gates explicitly inherit and extend Jingying Huiyuan's four gates. This cross-school historical link—from the *Dilun* school to the Huayan school proper—elevates the six characteristics into a mature hermeneutic instrument that spans seven centuries, two continents, and the *Yogācāra* and *Huayan* doctrinal traditions. The detailed treatment is reserved for §3.4.

## III. The Doctrinal Structure and Historical Lineage of the Six Characteristics

This section is the principal framework of the paper. §3.1 through §3.4 trace the formation of the six-characteristics doctrine in temporal order, from the Indian source down to its mid-Tang systematization. §3.5 extracts the dialectical structure of simultaneous co-presence as the doctrinal core; §3.6 takes up Fazang's culminating "first-aspiration-equals-enlightenment" sentence in continuity with §3.5, and serves as the bridge to §IV (cross-series articulations) and §VI (the practice-correspondence).

### 3.1 The Indian Source: The Scriptural Text Itself, and Vasubandhu's Systematic Commentary

#### 3.1.1 The Six Characteristics Issue Forth from the Great Scripture

§2.1.2 has already disclosed a fact with decisive consequences for the history of six-characteristics interpretation: the six terms—totality, distinction, sameness, difference, formation, decay—appear directly within the scriptural text of the *Daśabhūmika*'s first-ground "great vow" passage; they are not a hermeneutic category that Vasubandhu added afterward. This fact requires that the discursive axis of §3.1 be reset: the six characteristics are **not** "Vasubandhu's hermeneutic invention" but rather "Vasubandhu's systematic commentary upon six terms that were already present within the scripture itself."

This reset is not a minor philological adjustment; it carries three consequences of doctrinal weight.

*First*, **the six characteristics possess scriptural status from the beginning**. Later debates about whether the six characteristics were Vasubandhu's personal innovation, a Chinese Huayan post-imposition, or the result of some non-Buddhist dialectical influence—none of these debates can be sustained on the documentary evidence. The six characteristics are listed within the scriptural passage itself as one of the *upāya*-items in the great vow; the work of the later commentators consisted only in unfolding these six scriptural terms at successive levels of doctrinal articulation.

*Second*, **the *upāya* context of the scriptural occurrence settles the hermeneutic identity of the six characteristics from the outset**. Within the scripture, the six characteristics figure as items in the list of "skillful means... generating all dharmas auxiliary to the path"—that is, as instruments for "teaching all beings, causing them to receive the practices and grow in mind," not as ontological categories describing the substance of things. This scriptural context fixes the identity of the six characteristics as *modal-aspect* (hermeneutic) from the very start, rather than *self-nature* (ontological). Fazang's foundational distinction five centuries later—"the six meanings rest on the self-nature of dependent origination; the six characteristics rest on the modal-aspect of dependent origination"—is in fact only the explicit articulation of a direction that the scriptural context had already implied.

*Third*, **Vasubandhu's contribution receives its proper historical placement**. Vasubandhu did not create the six characteristics; he elevated them from a localized list-item in the great vow at the first ground into a general hermeneutic instrument capable of analyzing every ten-phrase structure throughout the *Daśabhūmika*. This is itself a substantial achievement—it transforms the six characteristics from concrete vocabulary in a single scriptural passage into a universally applicable exegetical method—but it should not be misread as the *invention* of the six characteristics.

#### 3.1.2 Vasubandhu's Systematic Commentary and the *Chú Shì* Principle

The defining passage of Vasubandhu's *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna* fascicle one (already cited at §2.2.1) establishes three matters whose later importance cannot be overstated.

*First*, **the six characteristics are an instrument of "verbal-explanation."** "In every ten-phrase unit of all that is spoken, there are six gates of differentiated characteristics. As to this verbal-explanation, one should know that the particulars are set aside"—this sentence directly positions the six characteristics as instruments of *verbal-explanation*, that is, of exegesis. They do not address what things themselves are; they address how a ten-phrase discursive unit is to be expounded by means of language.

*Second*, **the *chú shì* principle excludes any ontological misreading**. "The particulars are the aggregates, the bases, the elements, and the like"—the six characteristics treat not the *dravya*-level objects of the five aggregates, the eighteen elements, and the twelve bases, but the meaning-level dimension of these objects. To put the matter sharply: when you analyze a discursive passage with the six characteristics, you are not asking "to which aggregate does this passage refer"; you are asking "how does the internal discursive structure of this passage unfold along six observational facets simultaneously?"

The *chú shì* principle has been continuously reinforced throughout the later interpretive history. Fazang's *Tànxuán jì* fascicle nine treats the same point explicitly:

> Concerning the explanation of the six characteristics here: this is not the scriptural text itself but the commentator's explanatory intent—thus one must understand. As for "*chú shì*, that is, the aggregates, bases, and elements," this is a determination of the meaning: namely, the six characteristics speak of fusion *with respect to principle* and not *with respect to the particulars of the aggregates and the like*; therefore the particulars are set aside.[^14]

Fazang explicitly states that what the six characteristics treat is *principle* (the meaning-level), not *particulars* (the *dravya*-level)—this is the precise inheritance of Vasubandhu's *chú shì* principle. Chengguan's later "clarification of the discursive intention" (the third of his seven gates) likewise takes "to break fixed grasping, to display the perfectly fused dharma of dependent origination" as its statement of intent; behind this lies the same *chú shì* principle.

*Third*, **the six characteristics are tightly bound to the "ten-entry" form of the *Daśabhūmika***. In Vasubandhu's definition, every characteristic corresponds to a specific position within the ten-entry structure: totality is the *root entry* (the first), distinction is the *remaining nine entries*, sameness corresponds to the common nature of the ten entries (all called "entries"), difference corresponds to their distinct natures ("incremental marking"), formation corresponds to "brief statement," decay corresponds to "expanded statement." This correspondence shows that the six characteristics are not deduced from some abstract dialectical schema but inducted from the discursive form of the *Daśabhūmika* itself.

#### 3.1.3 The "World-Formation-and-Decay" Simile: The Distant Ancestor of Fazang's House Parable

The closing line of Vasubandhu's defining passage—"Formation is the brief statement; decay is the expanded statement, as in the formation and decay of a world-system"—compresses formation-and-decay into the single phrase "world-formation-and-decay." However terse this simile, it is the distant ancestor of Fazang's elaborate house-and-rafter parable five hundred years later. Vasubandhu's meaning is: when a world-system "comes to be," it is briefly stated as a single integral whole (formation = brief statement); when this same world-system is "expanded out" in description, its constituent parts (mountains and rivers, the sentient and insentient) are listed item by item (decay = expanded statement).

In other words, "formation" and "decay" in Vasubandhu are not the *temporal opposition* of arising and perishing but the *hermeneutic opposition* of "integral brief statement" and "item-by-item expansion." This meaning would be refined by Fazang five centuries later into the house-and-rafter version of "the dependent-origination-completed assembly" and "each meaning abides in its own dharma without moving"—but its root meaning was already planted in Vasubandhu.

### 3.2 The Sui-Tang Transition: Zhiyan's Application-Layer Treatment

Zhiyan is the second patriarch of the Huayan school (602–668), the bridge between Du Shun and Fazang and the pivotal mediating figure as the school's doctrine moved from inception to maturity. Zhiyan's principal work is on the Ten Profound Gates—he composed the *Huáyán yī chéng shíxuán mén* 華嚴一乘十玄門 (T1868), completing the systematization of the school's most central doctrinal channel. With respect to the six characteristics, however, Zhiyan's treatment remains at the application layer rather than the construction layer.

#### 3.2.1 Documentary Evidence: How Zhiyan Uses the Six Characteristics

Zhiyan's direct use of the six characteristics is found mainly in the *Huáyán jīng wèndá* T1873, fascicles one and two:

> Question: Is this only with respect to the ten grounds, or does it pervade the prior stages? Answer: When the ten grounds are spoken, the prior positions are encompassed within them, **for the ten entries are explained by means of the six characteristics**, as may be known.[^15]

> Question: Then how is this matter to be characterized? Answer: With respect to the ten grounds: in the section on the entrances, "the root-entry, the foundational portion, the well-decided vow"; in the section on the explanation, "arousing the *bodhicitta*"—this is precisely the case. The *body* itself contains the **totality, distinction, and the rest**; the speaking of the ten grounds is named "characteristic"; the section of explanation merely demonstrates the practice-virtues at large by means of the gate of characteristics.[^16]

> Once one has, with respect to the realization of the first ground, set up the ten entries and **displayed them by means of the six characteristics**, then one may know: the first ground is the initial arousal of the mind of faith.[^17]

These three passages show that Zhiyan uses the six characteristics as a working exegetical instrument: "the ten entries are explained by means of the six characteristics," "displayed by means of the six characteristics," "the body itself contains the totality, distinction, and the rest." He inherits Vasubandhu's definition and applies it directly to his own exposition of the doctrine of the ten grounds. His treatment is purely *instrumental*—the inheritance and application of an already-mature hermeneutic method.

But Zhiyan does not undertake any systematic doctrinal development of the six characteristics themselves. He writes no dedicated chapter on them, proposes no new structural framework, deploys no independent simile (no house, no golden lion) to demonstrate them, and does not elevate them from "instrument of verbal-explanation" to "observational gate for beholding the *dharmadhātu*." All of these tasks would await Fazang.

#### 3.2.2 The Later Verdict: "Yunhua's Ten Gates Are Rooted in the Gate of Contemplation; Gangzang's Six Characteristics Issue Forth from the Source of the Great Scripture"

The division of labor between Zhiyan and Fazang within the doctrinal construction of the Huayan school is captured by an extraordinarily clear later verdict, in fascicle one of the *Huáyán jīng jīn shīzi zhāng yúnjiān lèijiě*:

> **The ten profound gates of Master Yunhua are rooted in the gate of contemplation; the six characteristics of Master Gangzang issue forth from the source of the great scripture.** Sūtra and contemplation interfuse, characteristics and gates mutually penetrate—and so the eighth section gathers the six characteristics together.[^18]

The two key claims of this verdict are: *first*, "Yunhua's Ten Gates are rooted in the gate of contemplation"—Zhiyan's central work is the Ten Profound Gates, and the source of that work is Du Shun's contemplative practice (the "gate of contemplation" referring to Du Shun's three contemplations of the *dharmadhātu*); *second*, "Gangzang's Six Characteristics issue forth from the source of the great scripture"—Fazang's central work is the six characteristics, and the source of that work is "the great scripture" (the *Avataṃsaka* itself, specifically the first-ground great-vow passage that §2.1.2 has already disclosed).

This verdict provides documentary support for the positioning of §3.2: in the Huayan school's doctrinal division of labor, Zhiyan was responsible for the contemplative-system (the Ten Profound Gates), and Fazang was responsible for the scriptural-system (the six characteristics). In the lineage of the six characteristics, Zhiyan is an inheritor (from Vasubandhu) without being a forerunner (he laid no new foundation for Fazang's perfect-teaching doctrinalization). This historical placement is documentarily clear.

#### 3.2.3 Two Honest Limits

The present paper's treatment of Zhiyan on the six characteristics carries two limits that must be honestly marked.

*First*, **T1870, the *Huáyán jīng nèizhāng mén děng zá kǒngmù zhāng* 華嚴經內章門等雜孔目章, was not verified within this project's RAG vaults**. This is another major work of Zhiyan, included in CBETA. If it should turn out that Zhiyan offers a more systematic treatment of the six characteristics in this work, the present description ("application layer rather than construction layer") would need revision. The limit is honestly marked here, to be revisited when verification becomes possible.

*Second*, **the claim that "Zhiyan elevated the six characteristics into an observational gate" cannot be sustained on the available documentary evidence**. An earlier draft hypothesis had it that "Zhiyan elevated the six characteristics from a verbal-explanation instrument into an observational gate," but the documentary evidence presented in §3.2.1 and §3.2.2 shows that Zhiyan's use of the six characteristics is purely instrumental—he inherits Vasubandhu's definition and applies it without substantive doctrinal upgrading. The present paper therefore adopts the conservative "application-layer" description and no longer claims any "contemplative-gate-elevation" work for Zhiyan. Should contrary evidence be discovered in T1870 or other Zhiyan works, the present treatment will be correspondingly revised.

The honest marking of these two limits is itself a concrete enactment of the S8 series' "evidence-driven" methodology: never inflating the descriptive scope beyond what the documentary record can support, for the sake of an aesthetically tidier exposition.

### 3.3 The Tang Huayan School: Fazang's Perfect-Teaching Doctrinalization

Fazang's treatment of the six characteristics is the *standardization* phase of the lineage. Across three principal works, he undertakes three complementary developments: the *Wujiao zhang* T1866 fascicle four offers the principled doctrinal exposition within a doctrinal-classification framework (the house-and-rafter parable, the eternalism-and-nihilism exclusion, the *liù yì* / *liù xiàng* distinction); the *Tànxuán jì* T1733 fascicle nine offers the applied exposition within a commentarial framework (the placement of the six characteristics as the *gathering-and-establishing skillful means*); the *Jīn shīzi zhāng* T1880 and *Yìhǎi bǎi mén* T1881 offer the most concise demonstration—the golden-lion parable composed for the Empress Wu Zetian. The present sub-section is organized by theme rather than by work.

#### 3.3.1 The House-and-Rafter Parable: A Concrete Demonstration of the Six Characteristics

The third gate of the three-gate structure of the *Wujiao zhang* fascicle four "Six Characteristics in Perfect Fusion"—the gate of "question-and-answer exposition"—takes a worldly wooden house as its example and unfolds the six characteristics one by one:

> The dharma of dependent origination pervades everywhere. For now, let us briefly take a dependently-formed house as our discussion. Question: what is the totality? Answer: the house. Question: but these are merely conditions like rafters and the rest—how is any of them the house? Answer: a rafter is itself the house. Why? Because the rafter, fully and on its own, can make the house. Apart from the rafter, the house is not formed. When you obtain the rafter, you have already obtained the house.[^19]

Fazang's house parable has one feature that requires careful attention: he does not say "the rafters and tiles assemble together to form the house." He says, **"the rafter is itself the house"**—"because the rafter, fully and on its own, can make the house." On a first reading this is bewildering: how could a single rafter be a whole house? Fazang's argument runs as follows. If this rafter is not in this house, then this house simply does not exist—"apart from the rafter, the house is not formed." Conversely, when you obtain this rafter, you have *ipso facto* obtained this house—"when you obtain the rafter, you have already obtained the house." Within the structure of dependent origination, then, rafter and house do not stand in the linear "part–whole" relation but in the simultaneous "mutual containment" relation: to obtain any one characteristic is to obtain all six.

**Totality = the house**: the integral house.
**Distinction = the rafters and other conditions**: the individual constituents of the house (rafters, tiles, beams, columns, and so on). But this "distinction" is not separation: "distinction depends upon the root and fulfills it" (Vasubandhu's original phrase from fascicle one)—distinction depends upon totality for its standing and at the same time fulfills totality.

Fazang treats the remaining four characteristics along the same logic:

> The second, distinction. The rafters and other conditions stand distinct from the totality. If they did not stand distinct, the meaning of totality would not be established—because in the absence of distinction there is no totality. ... Totality and distinction in mutual identity may be considered here. Question: if they are mutually identical, how can one speak of distinction? Answer: it is precisely *because* they are mutually identical that distinction is established. If they were not mutually identical, then totality would lie outside distinction and so would not be totality, and distinction would lie outside totality and so would not be distinction.[^20]

> The third, sameness. The rafters and other conditions, harmoniously together, make the house; not opposing one another, they are all called conditions of the house. Because they do not make some other thing, they are called *the same characteristic*.[^21]

> The fourth, difference. The rafters and other conditions, considered with respect to their respective forms and types, are distinct from one another. Question: if they are distinct, ought they not to be the same? Answer: it is precisely *because* they are distinct that they are the same. If they were not distinct—if a rafter is twelve feet, then the tile would have to be twelve feet as well—the original conditioned dharmas would be ruined, and the meaning of "uniformly-equal in making the house" would be lost.[^22]

> The fifth, formation. By means of these conditions, the meaning of the house is established. ... Question: we presently see that the rafters and other conditions each abide in their own dharma and do not, on their own, *make* a house. By what cause does the meaning of the house come to be established? Answer: it is precisely because the rafters and other conditions do *not* make—that the meaning of the house comes to be established. The reason: if the rafter were to go off and make a house, then it would lose its original "rafter-dharma," and the meaning of the house could not be established. Now, because they do not make, the rafters and other conditions present themselves; and because they present themselves, the meaning of the house comes to be established.[^23]

> The sixth, decay. The rafters and other conditions each abide in their own dharma and do not, on their own, make. ... Precisely because they do not make, the dharma of the house is established. If they were to make—if they did not abide in their own dharma—then the meaning of the house would not be established.[^24]

These six paragraphs are the doctrinal core of the *Wujiao zhang*'s Six-Characteristics chapter. The most distinctively Huayan move is the argument concerning *formation* and *decay*: the structure of Fazang's argument is counter-intuitive—**precisely because the rafter does not "make" the house (decay), the house can be established (formation)**. If the rafter were to "make a house," it would no longer be a rafter, and so the house could not be established—because the house is established precisely by "rafters that remain rafters" together with "tiles that remain tiles" together with the other conditions that each remain themselves. **The precondition of establishment is that each characteristic remain itself; and this is the simultaneity of *decay* and *formation***.

#### 3.3.2 The Exclusion of Eternalism and Nihilism

Fazang inserts into the house parable a critically important argument: the exclusion of the two errors of eternalism and nihilism. This is the central argument by which the six-characteristics doctrine prevents the two extreme misreadings:

> Question: if each of the rafters and other conditions only contributes a small portion of effort, and none contributes the whole—what error follows? Answer: the errors of nihilism and eternalism. If they do not fully establish but only contribute small portions, then each condition contributes only a small portion. This yields nothing more than "many small portions"; it does not yield a single complete house—and this is *nihilism*. If, while no condition contributes the whole, one nevertheless grasps that there is a complete house—then "an effect without a cause" arises, and this is *eternalism*.

> Therefore: if a dependently-originated dharma is not formed, that is the end of it; once formed, the dharma is *mutually identical*, perfectly fused without obstruction, sovereign and freely complete, beyond ordinary thought. Take the dependent origination of the dharma-nature in this way wherever it pertains.[^25]

The structure of the argument:

**The nihilism extreme**: if you say "each rafter contributes a small bit of effort, and the house is the sum of all these small contributions," you have *disassembled* the house into the sum of the small contributions of individual rafters. What you end up with is not a complete house but "many small portions" ("this is merely many small portions; it does not constitute one complete house, and so it is nihilism"). This is the nihilism extreme—decomposing the whole into the sum of the parts and losing wholeness in the process.

**The eternalism extreme**: if you say "regardless of what the rafters do, there is a complete house in any case," you have treated the house as a permanently-abiding substance independent of the rafters: "an effect without a cause—and so it is eternalism." This is the eternalism extreme—treating the whole as a permanently-abiding existence independent of the parts.

Fazang's conclusion: "if a dependently-originated dharma is not formed, that is the end of it; once formed, the dharma is *mutually identical*." A dependently-originated dharma either fails to come into being at all, or, once it comes into being, the whole and the parts are necessarily *mutually identical*—not "assembled" and not "independent." **The exclusion of eternalism and nihilism gives the six-characteristics doctrine its Middle-Way structure**: the six characteristics are neither the nihilism-extreme "doctrine of part-assembly" nor the eternalism-extreme "doctrine of substantial wholes"; they are the Middle-Way "doctrine of mutual identity and mutual containment."

This argument is the direct corrective for both §V.❌1 (the dialectical-misreading of the six characteristics) and §V.❌2 (reading the six characteristics as six separable categories). When the reader has finished the eternalism-and-nihilism passage, it should be clear why the six characteristics cannot be read as six separable categories—because "separation" is precisely the nihilism extreme.

#### 3.3.3 The Eight-Line Summary Verse

At the end of the *Wujiao zhang*'s Six-Characteristics-in-Perfect-Fusion chapter, Fazang sums up the entire exposition in an eight-line verse:

> The one that contains the many is named *totality*;
> The many not collapsing into one is *distinction*.
> Many kinds, each itself, jointly fulfill the totality;
> Each body distinct in difference appears within sameness.
> The one-and-many of dependent origination is wondrously fulfilled;
> Decaying-while-abiding-in-its-own-dharma, never *making*.
> This is the realm of wisdom and not of *eventful consciousness*;
> By this skillful means, one encounters the One Vehicle.[^26]

The density of these eight lines is extreme; every two lines correspond to a paired characteristic.

- "The one that contains the many is named totality; the many not collapsing into one is distinction"—the *totality–distinction* pair. "The one contains the many" means "the integral whole already contains all the parts"; "the many not collapsing into one" means "the multiple parts each remain themselves and do not fuse into uniformity."
- "Many kinds, each itself, jointly fulfill the totality; each body distinct in difference appears within sameness"—the *sameness–difference* pair. "Many kinds, each itself, jointly fulfilling the totality" means "characteristics of various kinds are homogeneous in jointly accomplishing the totality"; "each body distinct in difference" means "each retains its distinct individual nature."
- "The one-and-many of dependent origination is wondrously fulfilled; decaying-while-abiding-in-its-own-dharma, never making"—the *formation–decay* pair. "The one-and-many of dependent origination wondrously fulfilled" is *formation* (the conditions skillfully assembled); "decaying-while-abiding-in-its-own-dharma, never making" is *decay* (each abides in its own dharma, never doing what is another's).

The final two lines are the Middle-Way correction at the heart of the entire exposition: **"This is the realm of wisdom and not of eventful consciousness; by this skillful means, one encounters the One Vehicle."** The realm of the six characteristics in perfect fusion is the realm of *wisdom* (*prajñā*'s direct illumination), not the realm of the *sixth consciousness* (the conceptual-discriminating activity of the *manovijñāna*). The six characteristics are skillful means by which one encounters the One Vehicle (the perfect teaching of the *ekayāna*). These two lines are the foundational basis of §V's Middle-Way corrections: **the six characteristics are not an object for the consciousness to conceptualize; they are a realm for wisdom to behold directly**.

#### 3.3.4 The Golden-Lion Parable: The Other Classic Demonstration

When teaching the empress Wu Zetian, Fazang took the golden lion in the palace as his example and composed the *Jīn shīzi zhāng* 金師子章. Its eighth section, "Encompassing the Six Characteristics," is the most concise demonstration of the doctrine:

> Eighth, encompassing the six characteristics: namely, the lion is the *totality*; the differentiation of the five sense-organs is the *distinction*; together they share one dependent origination, which is the *sameness*; eyes and ears do not reach one another, which is the *difference*; the various organs assembling together is the *formation*; each condition abiding in its own position is the *decay*. This displays that within the *dharmadhātu* there is no isolated dharma. Whichever single characteristic one raises, the dependent origination of all six characteristics is contained within it: assembled-and-established, each is without self-nature; in every single characteristic, inexhaustible characteristics are contained; in every single dharma, inexhaustible dharmas are contained.[^27]

The correspondences in the golden-lion parable:

| Six Characteristics | Golden Lion |
|---|---|
| Totality | the lion (as a whole) |
| Distinction | the differentiation of the five organs (eyes, ears, nose, tongue, body, each different) |
| Sameness | sharing one dependent origination (all are made of gold) |
| Difference | eyes and ears not reaching one another (each remains itself) |
| Formation | the organs assembling together (the six organs together compose the lion's form) |
| Decay | each condition abiding in its own position (each retains its own original place) |

The golden-lion parable has two advantages over the house parable. First, it is more directly intuitive—the reader needs no specialized knowledge of timber-frame architecture to grasp it. Second, the simultaneity of *formation* and *decay* is more visible: the lion's eye must remain "the eye of the golden lion" (decay = abiding in its own position) for the entire golden lion to be established (formation = the organs assembling). If the lion's eye were to begin "becoming" the lion itself, neither the eye nor the lion would remain.

Fazang appends a sentence to the end of the golden-lion parable: **"This displays that within the *dharmadhātu* there is no isolated dharma. Whichever single characteristic one raises, the dependent origination of all six characteristics is contained within it."** This sentence discloses the actual purpose of the six-characteristics parables: it is not to make you remember six categories but to make you understand that "within the *dharmadhātu* there is no isolated dharma"—any single thing, the moment it is raised up for view, brings the other six characteristics with it. The six characteristics are not six concepts; they are the very fact that "any single thing has six observational facets simultaneously."

The *Yìhǎi bǎi mén* T1881 places the golden-lion parable in side-by-side correspondence with the *Wujiao zhang*'s eight-line summary verse:

> The lion is the *totality*: **"the one that contains the many is named *totality*."**
> The differentiation of the five organs is the *distinction*: **"the many not collapsing into one is *distinction*."**
> Together sharing one dependent origination is the *sameness*: **"many kinds, each itself, jointly fulfill the totality."**
> Eyes and ears not interfering with one another is the *difference*: **"each body distinct in difference appears within sameness."**
> The organs assembling—there is the lion—is the *formation*: **"the one-and-many of dependent origination is wondrously fulfilled."**
> Each organ abiding in its own position is the *decay*: **"decaying-while-abiding-in-its-own-dharma, never *making*."**[^28]

The correspondence shows that, within Fazang's doctrinal architecture, the golden-lion parable and the house-and-rafter parable are two demonstrations of the same argument—they are not two different sets of "six characteristics" but the same six characteristics displayed simultaneously upon two different empirical objects. The reader may enter the doctrine through whichever parable suits her temperament; the doctrinal content delivered by the two parables is identical.

#### 3.3.5 The "Gathering-and-Establishing Skillful Means": Another Placement in the *Tànxuán jì*

In fascicle nine of the *Huáyán jīng tànxuán jì*, Fazang treats the six characteristics through a route somewhat different from that of the *Wujiao zhang*. The *Tànxuán jì* is a commentary on the eighty-fascicle *Avataṃsaka*, so the six characteristics here are placed within the context of *upāya* (skillful means):

> A general account of skillful means yields three kinds: *first*, the *initiating skillful means*—as in the way that preliminary practices initiate the root, namely the seven skillful means; *second*, the *non-abiding skillful means*—wherein compassion and wisdom mutually guide one another so that one abides neither in *saṃsāra* nor in *nirvāṇa*, as in the perfection of skillful means; *third*, the *gathering-and-establishing skillful means*—the various dependently-originated conditions are skillfully gathered together and established, as in the skillful means of the six characteristics. The present case belongs to the *gathering-and-establishing* skillful means; therefore the text speaks of "this skillful," and so on.[^29]

Fazang here divides skillful means into three kinds:

- **The *initiating* skillful means**: faith and preparatory practice (a startup-level skillful means).
- **The *non-abiding* skillful means**: led by compassion and wisdom, abiding neither in *saṃsāra* nor in *nirvāṇa* (a bodhisattva-path-level skillful means).
- **The *gathering-and-establishing* skillful means**: the skillful means by which the conditions of dependent origination are skillfully gathered together as a whole.

The six characteristics are placed under the third kind, the *gathering-and-establishing skillful means*—they are the skillful means by which the conditions of a dependently-originated dharma are skillfully assembled into an integral whole. This placement has two important implications.

*First*, **the six characteristics are *gathering*, not *analytic***. If you take the six characteristics as analytic instruments (decomposing the house into rafters and tiles, decomposing the lion into the six organs), you have already misused the doctrine—the function of the six characteristics is to let you see "how the conditions, simultaneously, are *gathered into* an integral whole," not "how the whole is *decomposed into* its conditions."

*Second*, **the *upāya* status of the six characteristics is reinforced**. Vasubandhu in fascicle one had already placed the six characteristics as instruments of "verbal-explanation" (i.e., as *upāya* in the exegetical sense); Fazang in the *Tànxuán jì* further classifies them as a *gathering-and-establishing skillful means* (i.e., as *upāya* in the soteriological sense). From Vasubandhu to Fazang, the six characteristics extend from "*upāya* of explanation" into "*upāya* of practice and realization"—not only an instrument for reading the scripture but an instrument for directly seeing the assembled structure of dependently-originated dharmas.

#### 3.3.6 "The Six Meanings Rest on the Self-Nature of Dependent Origination; the Six Characteristics Rest on the Modal-Aspect of Dependent Origination" [The Critical Distinction]

In another passage of the *Wujiao zhang* fascicle four, Fazang gives a doctrinal distinction whose importance for S3 Yogācāra readers entering Huayan can hardly be overstated. At the end of the section on "the six meanings of the cause-gate of dependent origination," Fazang gathers the six meanings (*liù yì* 六義) under the six characteristics, and then offers the following:

> The fifth: *fusing-and-encompassing*. These six meanings are gathered through the fusion of the six characteristics. Namely: fusing the six meanings into a single *cause* is the *totality*; opening the single *cause* into the six meanings is the *distinction*; the six meanings being uniformly named "*cause*" is the *sameness*; the six meanings each not knowing one another is the *difference*; on account of these six meanings, *cause* and the rest are established—this is the *formation*; the six meanings each abiding in their own positions is the *decay*.

> Question: how do the six characteristics and the six meanings divide their ranges? Answer: **the six meanings rest on the self-nature of dependent origination; the six characteristics rest on the modal-aspect of dependent origination. As the dharma-essence enters the modal-aspect, differentiation thereby comes about**.[^30]

This distinction is Fazang's doctrinal innovation, and it is the central citation for §4.1 of the present paper. Its argumentative structure runs as follows.

**The six meanings**: "empty, existent, having power, lacking power, dependent on conditions, independent of conditions"—six pairs of oppositions that constitute the *ontological* structure of a dependently-originated dharma in its position as a *cause*. The six meanings treat the *self-nature* of the dependently-originated dharma itself (*things-themselves*)—what the thing is in its dependently-originated structure.

**The six characteristics**: "totality, distinction, sameness, difference, formation, decay"—six observational facets that constitute the *hermeneutic* structure of a dependently-originated dharma in its *modal-aspect* position. The six characteristics treat *how to view* the dependently-originated dharma (*modes-of-viewing*)—how the thing simultaneously presents itself along six observational facets.

Fazang's pivotal sentence is: **"As the dharma-essence enters the modal-aspect, differentiation thereby comes about."** The same dharma-essence (the dependently-originated dharma itself), when entering the modal-aspect, gives rise to the differentiation of the six observational facets. The six meanings and the six characteristics are not two separable systems but two *paths of unfolding* of the same dependently-originated dharma: the six meanings travel the *self-nature* path (the ontological face); the six characteristics travel the *modal-aspect* path (the hermeneutic face).

This distinction is decisively important for S3 Yogācāra readers entering Huayan. Readers of the S3 series are habituated to *self-nature discourse*—the seed-actuality production of the *ālayavijñāna*, the ontological construction of *trisvabhāva*, the categorial structure of the five-position-hundred-dharmas (*wǔ wèi bǎi fǎ* 五位百法). All of these are discussions on the *self-nature* path. When such a reader first encounters the six characteristics, the most natural reaction is: "Are these six characteristics another ontological model?" The answer is: **no**. The six characteristics are not another ontological model; they are the unfolding of the same dependent origination on the *modal-aspect* path. An S3 reader may take the six meanings (or, more broadly, the *trisvabhāva*, the *ālayavijñāna* dependent origination, the five-position-hundred-dharmas) as the mature paradigm of the *self-nature* path, and the six characteristics as the mature paradigm of the *modal-aspect* path. The two paths discuss the same dependently-originated dharma but unfold it in different directions.

§4.1 will use this distinction as the basis for constructing a structural correspondence table between the six characteristics and *trisvabhāva*. **The purpose of that table is not to reduce Yogācāra to Huayan, nor to identify Huayan with Yogācāra, but to let S3 readers see that the two systems travel two paths of unfolding of the same dependently-originated dharma—each may corroborate the other but neither may replace the other.**

### 3.4 Mid-Tang: Chengguan's Systematic Seven Gates

Chengguan (738–839), the fourth patriarch of the Huayan school known as the National Teacher Qingliang, brought Fazang's treatment of the six characteristics into a more systematic form. Chengguan's systematization is found in fascicle 31 of the *Huáyán jīng shū* T1735 and fascicle 52 of his self-commentary, the *Yǎnyì chāo* T1736.

#### 3.4.1 The Seven-Gate Structure

In *Yǎnyì chāo* fascicle 52 Chengguan explicitly enumerates his seven-gate structure for the six characteristics:

> Master Yuan's four-gate exegesis: first, *establishing the intention*—to break fixed grasping; second, *establishing the basis*—glossing by way of the practice of the first ground; third, *general application to all dharmas*; fourth, *exegesis of the textual passages*. With respect to the third: as in the case where, in a single instance of "form," there are simultaneously gathered together "form, suffering, impermanence" and the rest as a single complex of dharma-meanings—this is the totality of "form." "Impermanent form" and the rest are the distinction. The fact that "impermanence" and the rest are all spoken of as "form" is the sameness. That "impermanent" is not "suffering" and so on is the difference. Formation and decay—the two gates—turn upon body-and-meaning, sameness-and-difference, fulfilling the prior two gates...

> **In the present commentary there are altogether seven gates: first, *general indication of the great intention*; second, *citation that displays the universality*; third, *clarification of the discursive intention*; fourth, *enumeration of the names with brief glosses*; fifth, *general illustration through simile*; sixth, *exhaustive gloss through cited examples*; seventh, *pointing to the elaborated treatment in other works*.** The first gate: namely, the desire is for the six characteristics to be perfectly fused, so that root and branch are unobstructed.[^31]

Chengguan's seven gates, in order:

1. **General indication of the great intention**—stating the root purpose of the perfect fusion of the six characteristics: that root and branch be without obstruction.
2. **Citation that displays the universality**—quoting Vasubandhu's "in every ten-phrase unit of all that is spoken, there are six gates of differentiated characteristics" to display the universal applicability of the six characteristics.
3. **Clarification of the discursive intention**—stating the discursive purpose of the doctrine: to break fixed grasping, to display the perfectly fused dharma of dependent origination.
4. **Enumeration of the names with brief glosses**—listing the six characteristic-names with brief definitions.
5. **General illustration through simile**—employing an integral simile (the house, the golden lion) to demonstrate the six characteristics.
6. **Exhaustive gloss through cited examples**—glossing each characteristic in turn through multiple cited examples.
7. **Pointing to the elaborated treatment in other works**—directing the reader to the more detailed exposition (especially in Fazang's *Wujiao zhang*).

The structural feature of the seven gates is this: the first three gates form the "intention-establishing" stage (why are we discussing the six characteristics?); the middle three form the "exposition" stage (what are they, and how are they demonstrated?); the final gate forms the "redirection" stage (where should the reader go for more detailed treatment?). The seven gates together compose a complete pedagogical unit—a reader who follows them in order has gone through one full traversal of learning the doctrine.

#### 3.4.2 "Master Yuan's Four Gates" and the Cross-School Transmission

The distinctive historical significance of Chengguan's seven gates lies in this: he explicitly inherits and extends "Master Yuan's four gates." **The "Master Yuan" here is Jingying Huiyuan** (淨影慧遠, 523–592), not Mount Lu's Huiyuan (廬山慧遠, 334–416). Jingying Huiyuan was a major figure of the *Dilun* school—the school devoted to the study of Vasubandhu's *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna*—and so belongs to the Yogācāra lineage.

The methodological significance of this historical thread is considerable.

**First, the formation of the six-characteristics hermeneutic crosses two schools, *Yogācāra* and *Huayan***. Vasubandhu (Indian *Yogācāra*) → Jingying Huiyuan (*Dilun* school, the early Chinese *Yogācāra* tradition) → Zhiyan (second Huayan patriarch) → Fazang (third Huayan patriarch) → Chengguan (fourth Huayan patriarch). This lineage spans India and China and traverses both the *Yogācāra* tradition and the Huayan school. Jingying Huiyuan's four gates being inherited and extended into Chengguan's seven gates means that the Huayan school's six-characteristics hermeneutic carries, in its middle portion, a *Dilun*-school bloodline.

**Second, this cross-school transmission supplies the historical basis for §4.1's "six-characteristics × trisvabhāva" correspondence—the correspondence is not merely structural analogy.** When the present paper, in §4.1, proposes a structural correspondence between the six characteristics and *trisvabhāva*, this correspondence is not pulled from thin air—it has a real historical lineage line: the six-characteristics doctrine departs from Vasubandhu (*Yogācāra*), passes through the four-gate exegesis of Jingying Huiyuan (*Dilun* school, the *Yogācāra* tradition), and is finally systematized in the seven gates of Chengguan (fourth Huayan patriarch). If anyone asks, "why may the six characteristics be put into correspondence with *trisvabhāva*?", the answer is not merely "because they are structurally similar," but: "because the two systems share the same Vasubandhu source, and have undergone substantive interchange in the Chinese commentarial transmission."

#### 3.4.3 "Root-and-Branch Without Obstruction" as the Goal of Systematization

Chengguan's systematization of the six characteristics has a single core purpose: **to render root and branch without obstruction**. These four characters appear in the first of the seven gates, the "general indication of the great intention": "the desire is for the six characteristics to be perfectly fused, so that root and branch are unobstructed."

"Root-and-branch without obstruction" is Chengguan's central placement of the six-characteristics doctrine: the purpose of the six characteristics is to transform the relation between the *root* (the totality) and the *branches* (the distinctions) from a relation of *master-and-subordinate* into a relation of *unobstructed mutual identity*. Common-sense understanding takes the whole (the root) as primary and the parts (the branches) as secondary; the whole has priority over the parts, and the parts depend upon the whole. The six-characteristics doctrine breaks this hierarchical structure: when you see the house, the rafters and tiles are not "appendages" of the house—they are the very mode in which the house exists; when you see the rafters and tiles, the house is not the "after-the-fact assembly result"—the house is already simultaneously established within every single rafter. The relation between root and branches is one of *simultaneous mutual identity without obstruction*, not of *hierarchical subordination*.

Chengguan's "root-and-branch without obstruction" and Fazang's "mutual identity without obstruction" are the same doctrine in different terminologies. Fazang stresses *mutual identity*—one is all, all is one; Chengguan stresses *unobstruction*—no barrier and no hierarchy between root and branches. The convergence of the two terminologies marks the maturity of the six-characteristics doctrine within the Huayan school.

#### 3.4.4 Chengguan's Placement of "Vasubandhu Rendering All Things Perfectly Fused by Means of the Six Characteristics"

In fascicle 31 of the *Huáyán jīng shū*, in the prefatory section to the *Daśabhūmika* commentary, Chengguan offers a single distilled sentence on the historical position of Vasubandhu's six characteristics:

> Thus Master Gangzang awaited the fivefold request before he would speak; Vasubandhu rendered all things perfectly fused by means of the six characteristics—herein lies the intention. Moreover, this single assembly contains only one chapter, lacking *skillful means* and *advancing-victory*: this precisely displays this very meaning.[^32]

"Master Gangzang [the bodhisattva Vajragarbha, here a different *gāng zàng* than Fazang's later honorific] awaited the fivefold request before he would speak" refers to the prefatory structure of the *Daśabhūmika*, in which the bodhisattva Vajragarbha refrains from speaking until requested five times. "Vasubandhu rendered all things perfectly fused by means of the six characteristics" is Chengguan's distilled summary of Vasubandhu's entire hermeneutic enterprise: Vasubandhu used the six characteristics to *fuse perfectly* all the ten-phrase structures of the *Daśabhūmika*. Chengguan's phrasing makes a key point: Vasubandhu's six characteristics are not a localized instrument applied to a single specific scriptural passage—they are a *fusing* instrument applied to the entire *Daśabhūmika*. They are the hermeneutic method by which the discursive structure of the entire *Daśabhūmika* is brought into a state of "perfect fusion" at the meaning-level.

This placement constructs a bridge between §3.4 and §3.5: Chengguan's "perfect fusion" and the "simultaneous co-presence" that §3.5 will unfold are the same doctrine. To say that Vasubandhu used the six characteristics to render the *Daśabhūmika*'s discursive structure perfectly fused is to say: when a reader uses the six characteristics to read the *Daśabhūmika*, the six observational facets of every ten-phrase unit are simultaneously present—and this is the dialectical structure of simultaneous co-presence.

### 3.5 The Dialectical Structure of Simultaneous Co-Presence

§3.1 through §3.4 have traced, in temporal order, the formation of the six-characteristics doctrine from its Indian scriptural source down to its mid-Tang systematization. The present sub-section extracts from this lineage a single doctrinal core that pervades every stage: **the six characteristics are not a temporal dialectical process; they are the simultaneous co-presence of one and the same dharma along six observational facets.**

#### 3.5.1 The Simultaneity of the Three Pairs

Structurally, the six characteristics are three pairs of opposition:

- **Totality vs. distinction**—whole and part.
- **Sameness vs. difference**—homogeneity and heterogeneity.
- **Formation vs. decay**—establishment and self-restraint.

The crucial feature of these three pairs is that the two characteristics within each pair are *simultaneously established*, not *sequentially appearing*. When you see a house, totality (the whole) and distinction (the rafters and tiles) are simultaneously established—not "first the house, then the rafters and tiles," and not "first the rafters and tiles, then the house." When you see a golden lion, sameness (all of one gold) and difference (eyes and ears each distinct) are simultaneously established—not "first homogeneity, then differentiation." When you see any dependently-originated dharma, formation (the conditions assembled) and decay (each abiding in its own position) are simultaneously established—not "first establishment, then maintenance," but rather: at the very instant of establishment, every characteristic is already maintaining its own position.

These three simultaneities are the foundational structure of the six-characteristics doctrine. Fazang's house parable in *Wujiao zhang* fascicle four is the concrete demonstration of this simultaneity; Chengguan's "root-and-branch without obstruction" is its doctrinal summation; Vasubandhu's defining passage in fascicle one of the *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna* is its earliest Indian basis. The entire lineage points to this single doctrinal core.

#### 3.5.2 Why Simultaneity: The Structural Necessity of "Mutual Identity"

The simultaneity of the six characteristics is not accidental; it is the necessary consequence of the structure of *mutual identity* (*xiāng jí* 相即). The decisive sentence in *Wujiao zhang* fascicle four reads: "**If a dependently-originated dharma is not formed, that is the end of it; once formed, the dharma is mutually identical.**" The meaning: a dependently-originated dharma either fails to come into being at all (the empty extreme as a possibility), or, once it comes into being, it is necessarily *mutually identical*—the whole and the parts mutually identical, homogeneity and heterogeneity mutually identical, establishment and self-restraint mutually identical.

*Mutual identity* is the central doctrinal term of the Huayan school. It refers to the *simultaneous mutual entry* of two categories—A is in B and B is in A; A is B and B is A. When totality and distinction are mutually identical, this means: the whole does not merely *contain* the parts; the whole *is* the way the parts exist. The parts do not merely *compose* the whole; the parts *are* the concrete actuality of the whole. For this reason, "totality-and-distinction simultaneous" is no accident—it is the necessary consequence of the *mutual identity* structure.

If you ask, "why must totality and distinction be simultaneous?", the answer is: **because they are mutually identical. If they were not simultaneous, they would not be mutually identical; if they were not mutually identical, no dependently-originated dharma would come to be at all.** This is a structural argument, not a rhetorical pronouncement.

#### 3.5.3 The Fundamental Difference from Hegelian Dialectic

To a twenty-first-century reader, the six categories "totality, distinction, sameness, difference, formation, decay" may appear to bear a family resemblance to Hegel's "thesis–antithesis–synthesis." This resemblance is purely surface-level. There are four fundamental differences.

**First, temporality vs. synchronicity**. Hegelian dialectic is temporal: the thesis appears first, the antithesis appears later, the synthesis is the final *Aufhebung* that supersedes both predecessors. The six-characteristics dialectic is synchronic: totality, distinction, sameness, difference, formation, and decay are all co-present simultaneously, with no sequential order. To read the six as "first totality, then distinction" or "first formation, then decay" is to have already temporalized them—the most common and most damaging misreading of the doctrine.

**Second, *Aufhebung* vs. preservation in place**. The central operation of Hegelian dialectic is *Aufhebung*—the synthesis cancels-and-preserves the thesis and antithesis, lifting their opposition to a higher level. The six-characteristics dialectic has no *Aufhebung*: distinction does not cancel totality, difference does not cancel sameness, decay does not cancel formation. Each characteristic remains *in its own position* (this is precisely the meaning of *decay*—"abiding in its own dharma, never *making*"). The six characteristics are not an ascending spiral; they are a flat, lateral simultaneity.

**Third, teleology vs. dependent origination**. Hegelian dialectic is teleological: it has a historical direction running from thesis to synthesis, ultimately oriented toward Absolute Spirit's self-realization. The six-characteristics dialectic has no teleology—it does not develop in any direction, because the six characteristics are an *already-simultaneous* structure with no "future" to advance toward. The six characteristics are not a process; they are a structure.

**Fourth, the consciousness-subject vs. the *dharmadhātu* itself**. Hegelian dialectic is the dialectic of a consciousness-subject—Spirit, in the process of self-recognition, completes its self-realization through thesis, antithesis, and synthesis. The six-characteristics dialectic is not the dialectic of a consciousness-subject—it is the structure of the *dharmadhātu* itself, independent of the cognizing subject. When you behold a house through the six characteristics, you are not "actively dialectizing" the house; you are merely *seeing* the six-characteristics structure that the house already possesses.

The sum of these four differences is this: **the six characteristics are not the eastern version of Hegelian dialectic; they are an entirely different doctrinal structure**. To read them as Hegelian is to miss the doctrinal core entirely. §V.❌1 will return to this misreading and address it in the form of a direct corrective.

#### 3.5.4 Six-Characteristics Simultaneity and the Unobstructed Interpenetration of Phenomena

A reader who has finished S8-P03 ("The Four Dharma-Realms: The Stratified Unfolding from *Phenomenon* to the *Unobstructed Interpenetration of Phenomena*") will ask: is the simultaneous co-presence structure of the six characteristics the same as the "one-is-all, all-is-one" structure of the *unobstructed interpenetration of phenomena*?

The answer is: **they are the same doctrine described at different levels, but they cannot be directly equated**. *Unobstructed interpenetration of phenomena* is the *state-description* of the dharma-realm structure—it describes the state of mutually-identical unobstruction in which the *dharmadhātu* presents itself at maximum resolution. The six characteristics are the *observational-angle description* of the same state—they describe how the observer holds six facets simultaneously in observing this mutually-identical unobstructed state. *Unobstructed interpenetration of phenomena* is what is observed; the six characteristics are the method of observing. The two are matched within Huayan doctrine: only the *dharmadhātu* of unobstructed phenomenal interpenetration calls for an observational instrument like the six characteristics; only an instrument like the six characteristics can match the *dharmadhātu*'s structure of unobstructed phenomenal interpenetration.

But this does not mean that the six characteristics may be *reduced to* the unobstructed interpenetration of phenomena, or vice versa. Each retains its own discursive position: the unobstructed interpenetration of phenomena is the principal axis of P03 (the dharma-realm structure face); the six characteristics are the principal axis of P08 (the observational instrument face). A reader who has finished both papers will see that they are two facets of the same doctrine—but within the discursive system of the Huayan school they each carry distinct functions.

### 3.6 The Perfect Fusion of the Six Characteristics and "First-Aspiration-Equals-Enlightenment"

#### 3.6.1 The Position of the Culminating Sentence

The "statement of intent" (*jiào xìng yì* 教興意) section of Fazang's *Wujiao zhang* fascicle four "Six Characteristics in Perfect Fusion" closes the entire exposition with a culminating statement:

> This teaching is given in order to display the dependently-originated dharma-realm of the perfect teaching of the One Vehicle—inexhaustible, perfectly fused, sovereign, mutually identical, unobstructed, melted-and-fused, on into the inexhaustible Indra's-net of principle and phenomenon. When this meaning is presented, all delusion-obstacles are at a stroke present: cut one and you cut all, and the delusions of the nine times and ten times are extinguished. The merits of practice are: complete one and you complete all. The principle-nature is: display one and you display all. **The "universal" and the "particular" are jointly fully-present, beginning and end uniformly even. At the moment of first arousing the aspirational mind, full enlightenment is already accomplished. This is precisely because the six characteristics of *dharmadhātu* dependent origination are perfectly fused, causes and effects simultaneously inter-pervading and self-mastered in both forward and reverse direction.**[^33]

The structure of the passage: first, the six characteristics are said to display the integral dharma-realm structure of "the perfect teaching of the One Vehicle, inexhaustibly perfectly fused"; then, once this structure is present, it has the mutually-identical effects of "cut one and cut all, complete one and complete all, display one and display all"; finally, the culminating statement: "The 'universal' and the 'particular' are jointly fully-present, beginning and end uniformly even. At the moment of first arousing the aspirational mind, full enlightenment is already accomplished. This is precisely because the six characteristics of *dharmadhātu* dependent origination are perfectly fused, causes and effects simultaneously inter-pervading and self-mastered in both forward and reverse direction."

"At the moment of first arousing the aspirational mind, full enlightenment is already accomplished" is itself the famous line from the *Brahmacaryā* chapter (*fàn xíng pǐn* 梵行品) of the *Avataṃsaka*. Fazang quotes this line, but he is doing more than quoting—he *structurally grounds* the line in the perfect fusion of the six characteristics: "**precisely because the six characteristics of *dharmadhātu* dependent origination are perfectly fused**." That is: "first-aspiration-equals-enlightenment" is possible precisely because the perfect fusion of the six characteristics in *dharmadhātu* dependent origination guarantees that first-aspiration and full enlightenment are jointly fully-present within a single thought-moment.

#### 3.6.2 The Structural Face

The present paper takes up this sentence from its *structural* face. By "structural face" I mean: "first-aspiration-equals-enlightenment" is not established because the person who arouses the aspirational mind "advances quickly in cultivation" (this is the *positional* face), but because the structure of perfect fusion of the six characteristics renders the events of "first-aspiration" and "full enlightenment" jointly fully-present within a single thought-moment.

To unfold this concretely:

**The six characteristics of "first-aspiration":**
- Totality = the integral *bodhicitta*
- Distinction = the specific content of this single thought-moment of arousing
- Sameness = the homogeneity of this arousing with that of all bodhisattvas
- Difference = the individual form of this single thought-moment of arousing
- Formation = the present-moment manifestation of this arousing
- Decay = this arousing remaining as arousing rather than becoming something else

**The six characteristics of "full enlightenment":**
- Totality = integral buddha-awakening
- Distinction = the specific content of this single thought-moment of awakened-illumination
- Sameness = the homogeneity of this illumination with that of all buddhas
- Difference = the individual form of this single thought-moment of illumination
- Formation = the present-moment manifestation of this illumination
- Decay = this illumination remaining as illumination rather than becoming something else

When you place these two sets of six characteristics side by side, you see that the event of arousing the first thought of aspiration and the event of full enlightenment are *structurally identical* in their six-characteristics structure. Their difference is not "structural" (the six-characteristics structures are identical) but "positional" (one is at the position of arousal, the other at the position of buddhahood). Yet because of the simultaneity of the six characteristics, this positional difference is already dissolved at the very moment of arousal—the moment of arousal already arouses through this same six-characteristics structure; the moment of full enlightenment likewise illuminates through this same six-characteristics structure. **"The same six-characteristics structure" is the structural basis of "first-aspiration-equals-enlightenment."**

To put it another way: if, at the moment of first arousing the aspirational mind, you can let the six characteristics open simultaneously—totality, distinction, sameness, difference, formation, decay all at work together—then this event of arousal already possesses the entire structure of the event of full enlightenment. The difference is only this: at the buddha-position, the six characteristics are a "mature simultaneous opening"; at the arousal-position, they are an "initial simultaneous opening." But the *fact of simultaneous opening itself* has, at the position of arousal, already taken place.

#### 3.6.3 The Division of Labor with the Positional Face

The sentence "first-aspiration-equals-enlightenment" has two faces. The present paper takes up the structural face; S8-P05 will later take up the positional face. The positional face proceeds along the following line: in the Huayan school's doctrine of cultivation-positions, the bodhisattva at the *faith-completion stage* (*xìn mǎn wèi* 信滿位, the completion of the ten faiths) is regarded as a "buddha-in-practice" (*xíng fó* 行佛)—not a "buddha-in-position" (*wèi fó* 位佛). That is, in the sense of "practicing the buddha-path," the bodhisattva is already a buddha; in the sense of "achieving the buddha-position," the bodhisattva is still in cultivation. This concept of the "buddha-in-practice" is the central thesis of S8-P05, where it will be unfolded as the "faith-completion stage subsuming all subsequent stages."

The division of labor between the two faces is clear:

| Face | Principal paper | Mode of exposition |
|---|---|---|
| Structural face | S8-P08 (the present paper, §3.6) | The perfect fusion of the six characteristics guarantees that first-aspiration and full enlightenment are jointly fully-present within a single thought-moment |
| Positional face | S8-P05 (forthcoming) | The faith-completion bodhisattva is already a "buddha-in-practice" within the Huayan position-system |

The two faces **mutually corroborate but do not replace each other**. The structural face answers "why is it possible" (because of the perfect fusion of the six characteristics); the positional face answers "at what position does it occur" (the faith-completion stage). After reading both papers, the reader will see "first-aspiration-equals-enlightenment" sustained from two directions at once—the structural necessity and the concrete positional location.

#### 3.6.4 Preventing the Cheapening of the Sentence

The sentence "first-aspiration-equals-enlightenment" carries a serious risk of misreading: being read as the personal-psychological declaration "since I have aroused the aspirational mind, I am already a buddha." This misreading is especially common within the twenty-first-century context of "express-lane spirituality"—it reduces the structural proposition of the perfect fusion of the six characteristics to a personal feeling of accomplishment.

The key to correcting this misreading is: **"first-aspiration-equals-enlightenment" is not a declaration of personal achievement; it is the event of perfect fusion of the six characteristics in the *dharmadhātu*'s dependent origination**. The sentence does not say "this person aroused the aspirational mind and so became a buddha"; it says "under the structure of the perfect fusion of the six characteristics in the *dharmadhātu*'s dependent origination, the event of arousing the aspirational mind itself possesses the entire structure of the event of full enlightenment." The person who arouses the mind is not a buddha *because* "she aroused the mind"; she shares the same structure with the event of full enlightenment because "the action of her arousal participates in the perfect fusion of the six characteristics in the *dharmadhātu*'s dependent origination." The emphasis is not on "her," but on "the structure of the event."

§V.❌4 will, on the basis of this corrective, address the cheapening of "first-aspiration-equals-enlightenment" directly. The present sub-section gives only the preview.

## IV. Cross-Series Articulations

This section establishes four concrete points of articulation between the six-characteristics instrument and other papers in the S8 series, as well as the S3 Yogācāra series. The purpose of each articulation is to make the present paper's instrument available for later papers to draw upon, and to make the present paper itself the bridge for S3 Yogācāra readers entering Huayan.

### 4.1 Articulation with the S3 Yogācāra Series: Six Characteristics × *Trisvabhāva*

#### 4.1.1 The Purpose and the Limits of the Correspondence Table

**Warning**: this sub-section will present a structural correspondence table between the six characteristics and *trisvabhāva*. Before reading the table, please first read the three statements of limit below; otherwise the table itself will be misread.

*First*, **the correspondence is at the instrumental level, not at the ontological level**. *Trisvabhāva* is the ontological construction of the *ālayavijñāna* dependent origination upon individual consciousness; the six characteristics are the hermeneutic observation of the *dharmadhātu* dependent origination upon integral things. The *objects* of the two systems differ; the *discursive levels* differ; the two cannot be simply unified.

*Second*, **the purpose of the table is to let S3 readers enter Huayan, not to reduce Yogācāra to Huayan**. If a reader takes the table to mean "the six characteristics of Huayan already contain the *trisvabhāva* of Yogācāra, so Yogācāra can be replaced by Huayan," she has completely misread the intent of the sub-section. The table is a bridge, not a destination.

*Third*, **the *liù yì* / *liù xiàng* distinction still stands**. Fazang's explicit formulation: "the six meanings rest on the self-nature of dependent origination; the six characteristics rest on the modal-aspect of dependent origination." The six meanings travel the *self-nature* path; the six characteristics travel the *modal-aspect* path. The correspondence table is a bridge demonstration constructed for S3 readers along the *modal-aspect* path; it is not an absorption of S3's *self-nature* discourse into the present paper.

#### 4.1.2 The Structural Correspondence Table

Within the limits stated above, the table is as follows:

| *Trisvabhāva* | Six-characteristics correspondence | The shared doctrinal structure |
|---|---|---|
| *Parikalpita-svabhāva* (the imagined nature) | distinction + difference | The simultaneous determination of "individual differentiation" |
| *Paratantra-svabhāva* (the dependent nature) | formation + decay | The simultaneous determination of "established by conditions / each abiding in its own dharma" |
| *Pariniṣpanna-svabhāva* (the perfectly accomplished nature) | totality + sameness | The simultaneous determination of "integral unity" |

A reading of the correspondences:

- ***Parikalpita-svabhāva* ↔ distinction + difference**. The imagined nature is the ordinary person's individual grasping of things—taking each thing as an independent, fixed, self-natured existence. This grasping corresponds to the *isolated* operation of the *distinction* (treating things as individual) and the *difference* (absolutizing the differences between things). But within the Huayan six-characteristics structure, distinction and difference never operate in isolation—they arise simultaneously with totality, sameness, formation, and decay. The "error" of the imagined nature is therefore not in "seeing distinction and difference" (which is not in itself an error), but in "seeing only distinction and difference and failing to see the other four"—and this latter is precisely grasping.

- ***Paratantra-svabhāva* ↔ formation + decay**. The dependent nature is the actual condition of dharmas arising in dependence on conditions—all dharmas arise through the harmonization of conditioning factors and not through independent existence. This condition corresponds to *formation* (the assembling of conditions) and *decay* (each abiding in its own dharma without moving) of the six characteristics. In Yogācāra, the dependent nature is the seed-actuality production of the *ālayavijñāna*; in Huayan, the dependent nature is the conditional gathering of the *dharmadhātu* dependent origination. The two articulate the same fact of *dependent origination* but in different descriptive vocabularies.

- ***Pariniṣpanna-svabhāva* ↔ totality + sameness**. The perfectly accomplished nature is the *real face* of the dependent nature: when one no longer regards the dependent nature with the imagined nature, what one sees of the dependent nature is the perfectly accomplished. This perfectly-fulfilled state corresponds to *totality* (integral unity) and *sameness* (the common dharma-nature) of the six characteristics. In Yogācāra, the perfectly accomplished is *tathatā*; in Huayan, the perfectly accomplished is the *dharmadhātu*. The doctrinal correspondence is tight.

#### 4.1.3 The Correct Use of the Table

The correct use of the correspondence table is not "memorize this correspondence." It is: **through this correspondence, see one thing—the *self-nature* description of Yogācāra and the *modal-aspect* description of Huayan are in fact discussions of the same dependently-originated dharma**. When an S3 reader first encounters the six characteristics, the table can help her find the corresponding positions within the *trisvabhāva* structure she already knows—"so totality and sameness correspond to the perfectly accomplished I am familiar with; formation and decay correspond to the dependent." The instant of "finding the corresponding position" *is* the instant in which the S3 reader enters Huayan.

But this entry is not a "leaving Yogācāra." Once the S3 reader has entered Huayan, she may continue to hold both readings simultaneously: using the *self-nature* discourse of Yogācāra to analyze the seed-actuality production of individual consciousness, and using the *modal-aspect* discourse of Huayan to behold the simultaneous co-presence of integral things along six facets. **The two readings do not contradict each other; they are two paths of unfolding of the same dependently-originated dharma**. This is the final import of "the six meanings rest on the self-nature, the six characteristics rest on the modal-aspect."

§3.4.2 has already noted that the formation of the six-characteristics hermeneutic, historically, traverses both *Yogācāra* and Huayan traditions (Vasubandhu → Jingying Huiyuan → Zhiyan → Fazang → Chengguan). The correspondence is therefore not merely structural analogy but a doctrinal correspondence supported by a real historical lineage. When an S3 reader uses the table to enter Huayan, she is in fact retracing a historical path that was already walked seven centuries ago.

### 4.2 Articulation with S8-P05 *Faith-Completion Subsumption*: A Two-Voice Reading of "First-Aspiration-Equals-Enlightenment"

§3.6 has already given the present paper's *structural-face* commitment to Fazang's culminating sentence and has previewed S8-P05's *positional-face* commitment. The present sub-section closes the coordination of the two papers in a single table:

| Paper | Same citation | Face committed | Core exposition |
|---|---|---|---|
| S8-P08 (the present paper, §3.6) | the same Fazang citation | Structural face | The perfect fusion of the six characteristics guarantees that first-aspiration and full enlightenment are jointly fully-present within a single thought-moment, in structural identity |
| S8-P05 (forthcoming) | the same Fazang citation | Positional face | The faith-completion bodhisattva is, within the Huayan position-system, already a "buddha-in-practice" (not a "buddha-in-position") |

The two papers share the same Fazang citation but commit to it from two different directions. After finishing the present paper, the reader may await S8-P05's continuation from the positional face; after finishing S8-P05, the reader may return to the present paper to deepen the structural-face understanding. **This is the first case of within-series two-paper articulation in S8**—a demonstration of how the S8 series allows the same citation to bear different doctrinal tasks across different papers without descending into repetition.

### 4.3 Articulation with S8-P11 §3.8 (*Mutual Containment Without Confusion*)

S8-P11 §3.8 has already unfolded *Constantly Following the Buddha's Learning* from the angle of the Ten Profound Gates' *Mutual Containment Without Confusion*. Its core exposition: to learn from one buddha is to learn from all buddhas (*mutual containment*); yet Śākyamuni remains Śākyamuni, and his particular teaching skillful means remain his alone (*without confusion*). The crux of *mutual containment without confusion* lies in the *without-confusion*: the one does not become the many, the many do not become the one, yet the one and the many are mutually containing in their very essence.

§VI of the present paper will redeploy *Constantly Following the Buddha's Learning* from the six-characteristics angle. The two papers do not duplicate each other; they are two facets of the same doctrine:

| Paper | Angle | Core structure |
|---|---|---|
| S8-P11 §3.8 | Ten Profound Gates | *Mutual containment without confusion* — one is many, many is one, yet the one does not become many and the many do not become one |
| S8-P08 §VI (the present paper) | Six characteristics | The simultaneity of totality, distinction, sameness, difference, formation, decay — at the moment of learning from one buddha, all six characteristics operate simultaneously |

The relation between the two angles: the Ten Profound Gates angle is *structural statement* (what is the dharma-realm structure of this learning event?); the six-characteristics angle is *observational unfolding* (how to behold this learning event simultaneously through six facets?). A reader may enter the vow of *Constantly Following the Buddha's Learning* through whichever angle she prefers; both angles ultimately converge on the same fact: **the moment of learning from one buddha is not a personal psychological event but the simultaneous co-presence of *dharmadhātu* dependent origination**.

§VI will take a typical scene—the offering of homage to an image of Śākyamuni—as its demonstrative object, displaying how the six characteristics operate simultaneously within this concrete action.

### 4.4 The Implicit Articulation with the S7 *Ekayāna* Series

One of the central theses of the S7 *Lotus* series is "gathering the three into the one" (*huì sān guī yī* 會三歸一)—the three vehicles are skillful means (the *distinction* of the one vehicle); the one vehicle is the ultimate (the *totality* of the three vehicles). This structure bears a family resemblance to the *totality / distinction* correspondence of the six characteristics—but the two are of different contexts. S7's "gathering the three into the one" operates at the level of *teaching-vehicle* (the three vehicles of *śrāvaka*, *pratyekabuddha*, and *bodhisattva* converging upon the one buddha-vehicle); S8's *totality / distinction* operates at the level of *dharma-realm structural observation* (the simultaneous co-presence of any thing's whole and parts).

The two cannot be directly unified, but there is one implicit doctrinal junction: **the Lotus's "gathering the three into the one" and the *Avataṃsaka*'s "totality-and-distinction in mutual identity" share the same fundamental structure of "one-is-many, many-is-one."** The Lotus enters this structure from the teaching-vehicle face (three vehicles → one vehicle); the *Avataṃsaka* enters it from the observational face (totality ↔ distinction). S7-P02 ("The One Great Cause") has already given the Lotus-face account; the present paper gives the Huayan-face account. The relation between the two papers is parallel, not dependent—the reader may enter this shared structure from either paper.

The present sub-section is only a marker; further unfolding is left to a future synthetic paper (such as S8-P12, the eight-series final closure) where the doctrinal junctions between the S8 and S7 series may be more thoroughly handled.

## V. Middle-Way Corrections

This paper carries five points of likely failure with respect to the six-characteristics doctrine, each of which corresponds to one item in the §1.4 notice. The present section addresses them in turn.

### ❌1 The Hegelian-Dialectic Misreading (the principal teaching-danger of this paper)

**The form of the misreading**: reading "totality, distinction, sameness, difference, formation, decay" as a Hegelian dialectical progression—totality the thesis, distinction the antithesis, sameness-difference-formation-decay intermediate moments, finally arriving at some higher-order synthesis. Or: reading the six characteristics as a temporal sequence "first totality then distinction; first formation then decay."

**Why the misreading occurs**: a twenty-first-century reader (especially one trained in Western philosophy) immediately associates the word *dialectic* with Hegel. The six terms "totality, distinction, sameness, difference, formation, decay" appear, on the surface, to share a family resemblance with "thesis, antithesis, synthesis." Adding to this, Fazang's name for the doctrine—"the meaning of the six characteristics in *perfect fusion*"—is read as "the integrated state finally achieved"; the six characteristics are then read as a dialectical progression from opposition to synthesis.

**The correction**:

*First*, **return to Fazang's own text**. The closing two lines of the *Wujiao zhang* fascicle four eight-line summary verse—"**this is the realm of wisdom and not of eventful consciousness; by this skillful means, one encounters the One Vehicle**"—explicitly state that the six characteristics are the realm of *wisdom* (*prajñā*'s direct illumination), not the realm of the *sixth consciousness* (the conceptual-discriminating activity of the *manovijñāna*). Hegelian dialectic is the dialectic of the eventful-consciousness realm—the self-movement of Spirit at the level of concepts; the six characteristics are not. The six characteristics are the six facets the observer simultaneously sees in directly beholding a dependently-originated dharma; they are not the inferential progression of concepts within thinking.

*Second*, **return to the structural necessity of "mutual identity"** (§3.5.2). Fazang's explicit formulation: "if a dependently-originated dharma is not formed, that is the end of it; once formed, the dharma is mutually identical." A dependently-originated dharma, once formed, is necessarily mutually identical; mutually identical, it is necessarily simultaneous. There is no "before and after." If you ask "why must the six characteristics be simultaneous?", the answer is not "because Fazang stipulated this," but "because of the necessity of the structure of mutual identity." Not simultaneous, not mutually identical; not mutually identical, not dependently originated.

*Third*, **test it instantly with the house parable**. When you see a house, ask yourself, in the very instant: in this single instant, which entered your awareness first—*the house* (totality) or *the rafters and tiles* (distinction)? The answer cannot be "one before the other"—they entered simultaneously in this single instant. The same test applies to the golden lion, and to any object before your eyes. **The simultaneity of the six characteristics is not deduced; it is observed**.

### ❌2 Reading the Six Characteristics as Six Independent Categories

**The form of the misreading**: treating the six characteristics as six separable items—imagining that "this thing is the totality, that thing is the distinction," or "this is the sameness aspect, that is the difference aspect." Using the six characteristics as a classification instrument: dividing the world into a totality-class, a distinction-class, a sameness-class, and so on.

**Why the misreading occurs**: human cognition is habitually classificatory—seeing six nouns, the first response is to assign each noun to a different object. Buddhist treatises (especially the *Abhidharma* tradition) employ extensive classification schemes (the five aggregates, the twelve bases, the eighteen elements, the five-position-hundred-dharmas), and the reader naturally treats the six characteristics as another such classificatory instrument.

**The correction**:

*First*, **return to the indivisibility of the house parable**. The crucial sentence in Fazang's house parable: **"because the rafter, fully and on its own, can make the house. Apart from the rafter, the house is not formed. When you obtain the rafter, you have already obtained the house."** A single rafter can make the entire house, because apart from this rafter there is no this house, and to obtain this rafter is to obtain this house. The structure of the argument is this: the six characteristics cannot be assigned to different objects—each characteristic *fully and on its own* bears the entire house. In other words: totality is not the object "house," distinction is not the object "rafters and tiles," sameness is not the object "homogeneity"—the six characteristics are the simultaneous co-presence of one and the same house along six observational facets, and *each characteristic is the entire house*.

*Second*, **return to the eternalism-and-nihilism exclusion**. Fazang's argument inserted into the house parable: "if they do not fully establish but only contribute small portions ... this is merely many small portions; it does not constitute one complete house, and so it is nihilism." If you take the six characteristics as instruments in which "each characteristic bears only a portion" (e.g., distinction bears the rafters and tiles, totality bears the outline, sameness bears the homogeneity), you have committed the nihilism extreme—you end with "many small portions," not a complete house. The corrective method is: each characteristic bears the whole; the six characteristics simultaneously bear the same object.

*Third*, **the counter-test of removing any single characteristic**. Try removing any one of the six characteristics—say, describe the house using only totality, distinction, sameness, difference, and formation, dropping decay. You will find: without decay (each abiding in its own dharma), the rafter would "become" the house instead of "remaining a rafter," and so the house could not be established. The same applies to any of the six: remove any one, and the remaining five cannot stand alone. **This non-removability is precisely the indivisibility of the six characteristics**.

### ❌3 The *Liù Yì* / *Liù Xiàng* Confusion

**The form of the misreading**: confounding Fazang's "six meanings" (the six meanings of the cause-gate of dependent origination: empty, existent, having power, lacking power, dependent on conditions, independent of conditions) with the "six characteristics." Or: treating the six characteristics as another ontological model, on a par with *trisvabhāva*, the eight-consciousness structure, and the five-position-hundred-dharmas.

**Why the misreading occurs**: readers of the S3 Yogācāra series are deeply familiar with *self-nature discourse*—the *ālayavijñāna*, seed-actuality production, *trisvabhāva* and the eight consciousnesses, all of these are discourses on the *self-nature* path. When such a reader first encounters the six characteristics, the most natural reaction is to read them as another *self-nature* discourse: "the six characteristics are Fazang's ontological construction of the dependently-originated dharma itself." This misreading is especially common in S3 readers, so the present paper must dwell on it.

**The correction**:

*First*, **return to Fazang's explicit text**. *Wujiao zhang* fascicle four: "**Question: how do the six characteristics and the six meanings divide their ranges? Answer: the six meanings rest on the self-nature of dependent origination; the six characteristics rest on the modal-aspect of dependent origination. As the dharma-essence enters the modal-aspect, differentiation thereby comes about**." This is Fazang's own explicit distinction between the relation of the six meanings and the six characteristics. The six meanings travel the *self-nature* path (*things-themselves*); the six characteristics travel the *modal-aspect* path (*modes-of-viewing*). The same dharma-essence unfolds along the self-nature path as the six meanings, and along the modal-aspect path as the six characteristics. They are not two separable systems but two paths of unfolding of the same dependently-originated dharma.

*Second*, **return to Vasubandhu's *chú shì* principle** (§3.1.2). Vasubandhu, in defining the six characteristics in fascicle one, explicitly stated: "as to this verbal-explanation, one should know that the particulars are set aside; 'the particulars' means the aggregates, the bases, the elements, and the like." The six characteristics are *verbal-explanation*; they *set aside* the *dravya*-level particulars of the aggregates, bases, and elements, and treat only the meaning-level dimension. From Vasubandhu down to Fazang, the *modal-aspect* path is the direction the six characteristics have always taken; this has not changed. The S3 reader needs to grasp: the six characteristics are not another set of eight consciousnesses, not another *trisvabhāva*, not another five-position-hundred-dharmas—they do not travel the *self-nature* path at all.

*Third*, **see the two-path structure from the historical lineage**. §3.4.2 has already noted: the formation of the six-characteristics hermeneutic crosses both the *Yogācāra* and Huayan traditions (Vasubandhu → Jingying Huiyuan → Zhiyan → Fazang → Chengguan). Jingying Huiyuan is a *Dilun*-school figure (the early Chinese *Yogācāra* tradition); his "four-gate exegesis" was inherited by Chengguan as the seven gates. This historical thread is itself the proof that the six characteristics were never "a Huayan-school instrument deployed against the Yogācāra school," but a hermeneutic instrument jointly cultivated by the two traditions. When an S3 reader enters Huayan, she does not need to "abandon" Yogācāra; she may hold both readings simultaneously—using Yogācāra's self-nature discourse to analyze individual consciousness, and Huayan's modal-aspect discourse to behold integral things.

### ❌4 The Cheapening of "First-Aspiration-Equals-Enlightenment"

**The form of the misreading**: reading "first-aspiration-equals-enlightenment" as a personal-psychological declaration, "since I have aroused the aspirational mind, I am already a buddha." Or: taking the sentence as encouragement to arouse the *bodhicitta* rather than as a structural argument-proposition.

**Why the misreading occurs**: the twenty-first-century context of "express-lane spirituality" makes this misreading especially easy. Readers want immediate accomplishment-feeling from the dharma, and "first-aspiration-equals-enlightenment" reads exactly as the legitimation of that accomplishment-feeling. Furthermore, the literal meaning of the sentence is striking on its own—an equation of the form "arousing the mind = full enlightenment" is exactly what any reader seeking rapid awakening most wants to hear.

**The correction**:

*First*, **return to the structure of Fazang's argument** (§3.6.1). Fazang's original sentence: "**This is precisely because the six characteristics of *dharmadhātu* dependent origination are perfectly fused, causes and effects simultaneously inter-pervading and self-mastered in both forward and reverse direction**." "First-aspiration-equals-enlightenment" is possible *precisely because* the six characteristics of *dharmadhātu* dependent origination are perfectly fused. In other words: the establishment of the sentence has a structural condition—it stands only under the *dharmadhātu* structure of perfect fusion of the six characteristics. Apart from this condition, the sentence is mere rhetoric.

*Second*, **understand the sentence from the angle of "event" rather than "individual"** (§3.6.4). "First-aspiration-equals-enlightenment" does not say "this person aroused the mind and so became a buddha"; it says "under the structure of perfect fusion of the six characteristics in the *dharmadhātu*'s dependent origination, the event of arousing the aspirational mind itself possesses the entire structure of the event of full enlightenment." The person is not a buddha *because* "she aroused the mind"; she shares the same structure with the event of full enlightenment because "the action of her arousal participates in the perfect fusion of the six characteristics in the *dharmadhātu*'s dependent origination." The emphasis is not on "her," but on "the structure of the event."

*Third*, **the "buddha-in-practice rather than buddha-in-position" distinction** (shared with S8-P05). The Huayan school carries an important position-doctrine concept: the bodhisattva at the faith-completion stage is a "buddha-in-practice" (in the sense of "practicing the buddha-path, she is a buddha"), not a "buddha-in-position" (in the sense of having reached the buddha-position). "First-aspiration-equals-enlightenment" speaks of the *buddha-in-practice*, not the *buddha-in-position*—the action of arousing the mind is already a buddha-action (buddha-in-practice), but this does not imply that the person has arrived at the buddha-position (buddha-in-position). This distinction will be formally argued in S8-P05; the present sub-section gives only the preview.

### ❌5 The "Six Characteristics = *Trisvabhāva*" Unification Claim

**The form of the misreading**: after reading the §4.1 correspondence table, concluding that "Huayan's six characteristics already contain Yogācāra's *trisvabhāva*," or that "Yogācāra can be replaced by Huayan." Or, in reverse: "Yogācāra's *trisvabhāva* already contains the six characteristics; Huayan's six characteristics are just another expression of the *trisvabhāva*."

**Why the misreading occurs**: the visual force of the correspondence table is strong—when a reader sees the assignments "imagined ↔ distinction-difference," "dependent ↔ formation-decay," "perfectly accomplished ↔ totality-sameness," she naturally assumes that the two systems are "the same thing." Twenty-first-century readers are particularly fond of "unification" readings—uniting different systems and obtaining the satisfaction of an "ultimate framework."

**The correction**:

*First*, **the table is at the instrumental level, not at the ontological level** (§4.1.1). The purpose of the table is to let S3 readers enter Huayan, not to reduce Yogācāra to Huayan. The objects of the two systems differ: *trisvabhāva* is the ontological construction of the *ālayavijñāna* dependent origination upon individual consciousness; the six characteristics are the hermeneutic observation of the *dharmadhātu* dependent origination upon integral things. The discursive objects differ; the discursive levels differ; the two cannot be simply unified.

*Second*, **the structural distinction "the six meanings rest on the self-nature, the six characteristics rest on the modal-aspect"**. This distinction has been treated in detail at §3.3.6. It tells us: *trisvabhāva* travels the *self-nature* path; the six characteristics travel the *modal-aspect* path. The two paths discuss the same dependently-originated dharma but unfold in different directions—one asks "what the thing itself is in its dependently-originated structure," the other asks "how the thing simultaneously presents itself along six observational facets." **Two descriptive paths of the same object cannot replace each other.**

*Third*, **maintain the tension between the two paths**. The most beneficial reading-posture for an S3 reader is to hold both readings simultaneously. When you analyze the seed-actuality production of individual consciousness, use Yogācāra's self-nature discourse; when you behold the simultaneous co-presence of an integral thing, use Huayan's six-characteristics observation. The two readings are not contradictory but complementary. **Complementarity is not unification**—maintaining the tension between the two paths comes closer to the doctrinal flavor of each system than a forced merger ever could.

## VI. Practice-Correspondence: A Perfect-Teaching Reading of *Constantly Following the Buddha's Learning* (the Six-Characteristics Version)

S8-P11 §3.8 has already unfolded *Constantly Following the Buddha's Learning* from the angle of the Ten Profound Gates' *Mutual Containment Without Confusion*. The present section redeploys it from the angle of the six-characteristics structure, taking as its demonstrative scene a single concrete action: **offering homage to an image of Śākyamuni**.

### 6.1 Why This Scene

"Offering homage to an image of Śākyamuni" is a concrete action with which any practitioner is familiar. It calls for no special cultivation conditions, no training in any particular dharma-gate—if you have ever clasped your palms and bowed before an image of Śākyamuni in a buddha-hall, in a monastery, or at a household shrine, you have had this experience. The aim of the present section is to let you see that this action of homage, which you may take to be "as ordinary as anything could be," is, under the structure of simultaneous co-presence of the six characteristics, already the perfect-teaching reading of *Constantly Following the Buddha's Learning*.

The reasons for choosing this scene rather than an abstract exposition are two.

*First*, **the simultaneity of the six characteristics can only be directly seen on a concrete event**. With abstract exposition alone, the reader easily takes the six characteristics for six conceptual categories; only when the six are unfolded upon a concrete event can the reader truly see what "the six characteristics simultaneously" actually means.

*Second*, **"Constantly Following the Buddha's Learning" must be a daily action, or it is empty talk**. If the practice required special cultivation conditions, the great majority of readers could not practice it in daily life. What this section means to display is this: in the most everyday action of homage, *Constantly Following the Buddha's Learning* is already taking place in the mode of the simultaneous co-presence of the six characteristics—you need do nothing extra for it; you need only see that it is already so.

### 6.2 The Simultaneous Six Characteristics of Offering Homage to Śākyamuni's Image

When, before a buddha-hall, you press your palms together and bow your forehead to an image of Śākyamuni Buddha—the physical duration of this action perhaps a few seconds—the following six characteristics operate simultaneously.

**Totality-as-sameness** (the same dharma): what you offer homage to is not merely "this single image of Śākyamuni" but "the dharma itself," which is the totality. The image of Śākyamuni is the concrete present-form of the dharma, but the dharma is not only the image of Śākyamuni—it is the same dharma jointly displayed by all the buddhas of the ten directions and the three times. At the instant your forehead touches the ground, this "same dharma" totality is already present—you do not "think of" it; it is already there in the object of your homage.

**Distinction-as-difference** (different teaching skillful means): yet what you offer homage to is still the individual form of Śākyamuni—the thirty-two marks, the eighty subsidiary marks, the eight phases of the buddha-life, the forty-nine years of teaching—all of these are his *distinction* as "Śākyamuni, this particular buddha." You are not offering homage to "a fused composite of all buddhas"; you are offering homage to Śākyamuni, this concrete historical person and his concrete teachings. **Distinction-as-difference does not dissolve totality-as-sameness; totality-as-sameness does not erase distinction-as-difference**—they are simultaneously established.

**Sameness-as-formation** (joint disclosure of reality): Śākyamuni and the buddhas of the ten directions are homogeneous in "jointly disclosing the same reality." At the instant of your homage to Śākyamuni, this homogeneity of "jointly disclosing reality" is already present—you do not "afterward infer" that the buddhas of the ten directions are also disclosing the same reality; the concrete form of Śākyamuni and the universal form of the buddhas of the ten directions are already, from the very beginning, the same matter at the level of "disclosing reality."

**Difference-as-formation** (each responding to the audience's capacities): yet the buddhas of the ten directions each respond to different audiences with different teaching skillful means. Śākyamuni's skillful means for the beings of *Sahā*—the four noble truths of the *Hīnayāna*, the *prajñāpāramitā* of the *Mahāyāna*, the three turnings of the dharma-wheel—differ from the Medicine Master Buddha's skillful means for the beings of the Eastern Lapis Lazuli World, and from Amitābha's skillful means for the beings of *Sukhāvatī*. The *difference* of each buddha—the particular teaching for the particular audience—is established. **Difference-as-formation does not contradict sameness-as-formation; rather, it is the very concrete manifestation of sameness-as-formation**.

**Formation-establishes** (the teaching-event takes place): the very action of your homage to Śākyamuni is established as a "dharma-learning event." This event is not vacuous—it has a concrete time, place, posture, and intention; it is a real action that genuinely takes place. At the instant of this action, the matter of "offering homage" really does "assemble together"—the body's prostration, the gesture of joined palms, the contemplating thought, the form of the buddha-image: all these conditions assemble simultaneously, so that the event of "offering homage" is established.

**Decay-abides** (the teaching dispels its own marks): yet, simultaneously, this very action of homage dispels grasping at homage. At the instant of homage, there is no fixated thought of "I am offering homage to an image"—the body remains the body, the buddha-image remains the buddha-image, the action of homage remains the action of homage; each characteristic *remains in its own characteristic* and does not transform into something else. If you become attached to the thought "I am offering homage," the homage will be replaced by your thought, and so will lose the genuineness of homage. **Precisely because each characteristic remains in its own characteristic (decay), homage is genuinely established (formation)**—this is the specific cultivation-face unfolding of Fazang's house-parable formula "precisely because they do not make, the dharma of the house is established."

### 6.3 The True Meaning of "Constantly Following the Buddha's Learning"

When the above six characteristics operate simultaneously at the instant of this action of homage, the vow of *Constantly Following the Buddha's Learning* is already fully realized—not as the temporal commitment "in the future I will often follow Śākyamuni in study," but as: "at this instant of homage, learning from Śākyamuni *is* learning from the buddhas of the ten directions (totality-as-sameness + sameness-as-formation), yet Śākyamuni remains Śākyamuni (distinction-as-difference + difference-as-formation); this dharma-learning event is genuinely established (formation-establishes) and simultaneously dispels grasping at the dharma-learning (decay-abides)."

The true meaning of the word "constant" is not "always" (temporal duration) but "simultaneous" (the six-characteristics structure). At every instant of offering homage to Śākyamuni, the six characteristics are simultaneously at work—and so every such instant is already *Constantly Following the Buddha's Learning*. **"Constantly Following the Buddha's Learning" is not a future goal you must work toward; it is the present-moment event already underway each time you offer homage to a buddha**.

S8-P11 §3.8's exposition from the Ten Profound Gates' *Mutual Containment Without Confusion*—"learning from one buddha is learning from all buddhas, yet Śākyamuni remains Śākyamuni"—and the six-characteristics version of the present section are two facets of the same doctrine. The Ten Profound Gates are *structural statement*; the six characteristics are *observational unfolding*; the two converge upon the same fact: the moment of learning from one buddha is not a personal psychological event but the simultaneous co-presence of the *dharmadhātu*'s dependent origination.

### 6.4 A Method for Carrying the Six Characteristics into Daily Practice

Having read through this section, the reader may attempt the following exercise. The next time you offer homage to a buddha, recite a buddha-name, chant a sūtra, sit in meditation, or simply stand before a buddha-hall looking at the image, silently open these six windows in your mind:

> **Totality-as-sameness** (the same dharma)
> **Distinction-as-difference** (the individual form of this particular buddha)
> **Sameness-as-formation** (joint disclosure of reality)
> **Difference-as-formation** (each responding to the audience)
> **Formation-establishes** (this action is genuinely established)
> **Decay-abides** (each characteristic remains itself)

Do not try to *think through* each characteristic—merely allow them to be *simultaneously present*. You will find that, when you no longer try to deduce the six characteristics through the conceptual discrimination of the sixth consciousness, the six characteristics arise simultaneously in the most natural way—and this is the cultivation-face of the closing two lines of Fazang's eight-line summary verse, "this is the realm of wisdom and not of eventful consciousness; by this skillful means, one encounters the One Vehicle." The six characteristics are not an object for your thinking; they are a realm for your direct beholding.

When you can open these six windows at the instant of offering homage to a buddha, *Constantly Following the Buddha's Learning* is no longer a distant cultivation goal—it is something you are doing right now. Every offering of homage is a simultaneous opening of the six characteristics; every simultaneous opening of the six characteristics is a present-moment realization of *Constantly Following the Buddha's Learning*.

## VII. Conclusion

### 7.1 The Six Characteristics Are Not "the Instrument of an Instrument"

This paper has unfolded the Huayan school's six-characteristics doctrine across five faces—the documentary, the doctrinal-structural, the cross-series-articulative, the Middle-Way-corrective, and the practice-correspondence. The title of the paper calls the six characteristics "a basic instrument of Huayan doctrine," but, having read through to the end, the reader should now understand: **the six characteristics are not "an instrument of an instrument"; they are an observational facet of the *dharmadhātu* itself**.

The word *instrument* is easily misread to mean "a means by which a subject achieves some end"—the reader picks up the six-characteristics tool and goes off to "analyze" a house or "behold" a buddha-image. But the six characteristics are not that kind of instrument. When you use the six characteristics to behold a house, you are not "actively dialectizing" the six facets of the house; you are merely *seeing* the six observational facets that the house already possesses. The six characteristics are not a tool in your hand; they are the structure in which the house itself is simultaneously co-present along the *modal-aspect* path. What the observer does is only this: cease to seal off this simultaneous co-presence with the *imagined nature*, and let it disclose itself as it already is.

In this sense, the "instrumental status" of the six characteristics is itself a *upāya*-statement. The §I of this paper called the six characteristics "a doctrinal instrument that every subsequent paper of S8 will draw upon"; this is true—but the "instrument" here is one that "lets you see the observational facets of the *dharmadhātu* itself," not one that "lets you construct some discourse." When the six characteristics are correctly used, they will eventually transcend the identity of "instrument"—after the reader has used them to fluency, she will discover that the six characteristics are in fact the way the *dharmadhātu* sees itself, with little to do with her "use."

### 7.2 The True Meaning of "Constant"

This paper corresponds to the eighth of Samantabhadra's Ten Great Vows, *Constantly Following the Buddha's Learning*. Most readers take the word "constant" temporally—"forever," "without ceasing," "every day." The exposition of §VI seeks to convert this understanding into a structural one: "constant" is not temporal duration but the present-moment "as-such" of the simultaneous opening of the six characteristics.

When you can let the six characteristics open simultaneously at the instant of offering homage to a buddha, you are already *Constantly Following the Buddha's Learning*—you need not wait for "forever," and you need not "accumulate every day." Each present moment of simultaneous opening of the six characteristics is the concrete realization of "constant."

This conversion is not a denial of temporal practice—daily liturgy, long-term accumulation, gradual deepening, all of these still have meaning. But their meaning is not "the accumulation of quantity"; it is "the increasing fluency with which each present moment opens the six characteristics simultaneously." When your offering of homage moves from "thinking of the buddha-image while joining your palms" to "the simultaneous co-presence of the six characteristics at the instant of joined palms," your practice rises from the *behavioral layer* to the *dharma-realm layer*—precisely what S8-P11 stated in its introduction as "the same physical action, with the inner structure of time, space, and agency transforming from linear to interfusing."

### 7.3 Preview of S8-P07 *Sāgaramudrā-Samādhi*

The present paper is the first paper of the "basic instrument" stage of the S8 series. The next paper, S8-P07, will unfold "Vow 7, *Requesting Buddhas to Remain in the World* × *Establishment of Subtle Mutual Containment*"—using Chengguan's "tenfold gloss on the *sāgaramudrā*" as its principal framework, treating the *sāgaramudrā-samādhi* (the ocean-seal *samādhi*) as the *samādhi* discourse on "imprinting the three times within a single thought."

The relation between the *sāgaramudrā-samādhi* and the present paper's six characteristics is close: the simultaneous-co-presence structure of the six characteristics is *directly realized* in the *sāgaramudrā-samādhi* in the form of *samādhi*. When the six characteristics open at the instant of offering homage to a buddha, this opening is itself an embryonic form of the *sāgaramudrā*—"the exhaustion of delusive thought and the clarity of mind" arises at the instant of the simultaneous opening of the six characteristics; "all phenomena present at once" takes place within the structure of the simultaneous co-presence of the six characteristics. After finishing the present paper, the reader may carry the instrument of the six characteristics into S8-P07, where she will find that the *sāgaramudrā-samādhi* is not another doctrinal system but the deepening of the simultaneous-co-presence structure of the six characteristics on the cultivation face.

### 7.4 Closing

From Vasubandhu to Chengguan, from Indian exegetical tradition to Chinese doctrinal classification, from the house parable to the golden-lion parable, from Fazang's "mutual identity without obstruction" to Chengguan's "root-and-branch without obstruction"—four centuries, two continents, five generations of patriarchs, countless sūtras and treatises—all point toward the same fact:

**Within the *dharmadhātu* there is no isolated dharma**. Whichever single thing is raised up for view, the other six characteristics arrive with it. The six characteristics are not six concepts; they are the very fact that one and the same thing is simultaneously co-present along six observational facets.

The next time you offer homage to a buddha, recite a buddha-name, chant a sūtra, sit in meditation—the next time your gaze falls upon any single thing—silently ask yourself: in this single instant, are the six characteristics not all simultaneously present? You will find that, when you stop using the sixth consciousness to "think" the six characteristics, the six characteristics are already there—and have always been there. What you do is only to cease sealing them off.

> The one that contains the many is named *totality*;
> The many not collapsing into one is *distinction*.
> Many kinds, each itself, jointly fulfill the totality;
> Each body distinct in difference appears within sameness.
> The one-and-many of dependent origination is wondrously fulfilled;
> Decaying-while-abiding-in-its-own-dharma, never *making*.
> This is the realm of wisdom and not of eventful consciousness;
> By this skillful means, one encounters the One Vehicle.
> ——Fazang, *Huáyán wǔjiào zhāng*, fascicle four
