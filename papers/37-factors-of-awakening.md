## Abstract

本文提出「雙層論」：三十七道品（saptatriṃśad-bodhipakṣa-dharmāḥ）不僅是佛教出世間的解脫修行路線圖，更是一套適用於任何生命領域的完整自我精進系統。論證路線如下：（一）以佛陀涅槃前三月的最後教誡為起點，確立三十七道品在整體佛法中的核心地位；（二）系統梳理經典依據，從《增壹阿含經》「一偈含攝三十七品」到《維摩詰所說經》「三十七道品是菩薩淨土」，建立從小乘到大乘的完整教證線索；（三）提出核心論證——七科次第（觀察→行動→穩固→生根→成力→覺醒→整合）捕捉了任何「有意識改變」的通用邏輯，因此同一套結構在出世間能用於解脫煩惱，在入世間能用於減肥、考試、戒癮、創業等任何目標；（四）以行為科學（習慣迴路）、正向心理學（性格優勢）、自我決定理論作為跨學科會通（⚠️ 結構對應，非理論等價），指出現代 self-help 各自發現了三十七道品的碎片——mindfulness 是四念處、habit science 是四正勤、grit 是五力、flow 是七覺支——但沒有任何一個現代框架把 37 步串成完整系統；（五）中道校正防止四種過度詮釋：不是心靈雞湯、佛法不離世間法不等於佛法等於世間法、結構對應不是理論等價、次第不是線性。本文是系列第十篇，接續論文 #9（法器準備），為第一季「初發心」系列的收官之作：器準備好了，這就是完整的方法。

This paper proposes a "dual-layer thesis": the 37 bodhipakkhiya-dhamma are not only a transcendent liberation path but a complete self-mastery system applicable to any life domain. The argument proceeds: (1) establishing the centrality of the 37 factors through the Buddha's final teaching three months before parinirvana; (2) tracing scriptural evidence from the Ekottara Agama's "one verse contains all 37" to the Vimalakirti Sutra's "the 37 factors ARE the bodhisattva's pure land"; (3) the core thesis -- the seven-category progression (observe, act, stabilize, root, strengthen, awaken, integrate) captures the universal logic of intentional change, therefore the same structure applies to both liberation from suffering and mastery of any life domain; (4) cross-disciplinary parallels (heuristic, not reductive) with behavioral science, positive psychology, and self-determination theory, noting that modern self-help has independently discovered fragments -- mindfulness = four foundations, habit science = four right efforts, grit = five powers, flow = seven awakening factors -- but no modern framework integrates all 37 steps; (5) four Middle Way corrections against over-interpretation. Paper #10 follows Paper #9 (preparing the vessel) as the culmination of the "initiating the aspiration" series: the vessel is ready -- here is the complete method.

## I. The Problem: What Is Your Methodology?

You want to lose weight. You buy a book called *Atomic Habits*. It says: build good habits, break bad ones. Sounds right. You start following the program.

A month later you have lost two kilograms. But something feels off -- you do not know *why* this method works, or *when* it will stop working. You are just following instructions.

So you buy another book: *Peak* (on deliberate practice). This one tells you: do not just repeat -- practice with purpose. Also sounds right. You layer it on top of the first method.

A while later, you pick up a third: *Flow*. This one teaches you to enter states of total absorption. Fine, you try that too.

Now you have three books, three methods, three vocabularies fighting in your head. Each one is correct on its own terms, but they have no connection to one another. You do not know when to use which. You have fragments. You do not have a system.

2500 years ago, someone already assembled all these fragments into a single framework.

His name was not James Clear. It was not Angela Duckworth. It was not Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi. His name was Siddhartha Gautama. He articulated a 37-step system that begins with observation, proceeds through action, stabilization, rooting, strengthening, and awakening, and culminates in a fully integrated way of life. Seven stages. Thirty-seven factors. One complete methodology.

He was not teaching "religion." He was teaching **how to change yourself** -- starting from the most basic act of seeing the problem, ending with the full integration of a correct way of living.

This system is called the 37 factors of awakening (saptatrimsad-bodhipaksa-dharmah / bodhipakkhiya-dhamma).

This paper does not attempt yet another explanation of what the 37 factors *are* -- any Buddhist textbook can do that. What this paper argues is: **the reason this system works for spiritual liberation is precisely that it captures the universal logic of "change" -- and that logic is identical whether you are trying to overcome suffering, lose weight, pass an exam, break an addiction, or build a business.**

In the last paper (#9) we asked: is your vessel ready?

Now the question is: what do we pour into it?

## II. Scriptural Basis: What the Buddha Said about the 37 Factors

### 2.1 The Buddha's Final Teaching -- His Last Instructions Three Months before Passing

If you had only three months to live, what would you say to the people closest to you?

You would say whatever matters most.

Three months before his parinirvana, the Buddha gave his disciples a final teaching. He did not discourse on the subtleties of emptiness. He did not paint a grand cosmology. He taught the 37 factors of awakening -- all seven categories, from first to last, not one omitted:

> 「三十七道品法，所謂四念處、四正勤、四如意足、五根、五力、七覺支、八聖道分，汝應修習精勤思惟，此法能令到解脫處。」[^1]
>
> "The 37 factors of the path -- namely the four foundations of mindfulness, the four right efforts, the four bases of spiritual power, the five faculties, the five powers, the seven factors of awakening, and the noble eightfold path -- you should diligently practice and reflect upon them, for this Dharma can lead you to the place of liberation."

He then continued: "All conditioned phenomena are impermanent. Life is as precarious as a flash of lightning. You must not be negligent."

A person's final words are the distillation of a lifetime. The Buddha chose to make the 37 factors his last message to the world. That was no accident.

### 2.2 One Verse Contains All -- The 37 Factors Are Not 37 Separate Things

You might think: 37 steps? Is that not too many?

The Buddha's attendant Ananda was asked this very question. When Mahakasyapa (Mahakassapa) challenged him -- "You say the Ekottara Agama gives rise to all 37 factors. How is that possible?" -- Ananda's reply was striking:

> 「且置增一阿含，一偈之中，便出生三十七品及諸法。」[^2]
>
> "Set the Ekottara Agama aside -- within a single verse, all 37 factors and all dharmas are born."

Which verse? "Refrain from all evil, cultivate all good, purify your own mind -- this is the teaching of all Buddhas."

Ananda was saying: the 37 factors are not 37 separate tasks. They are **37 facets of a single diamond.** "Refrain from all evil" maps onto the first two branches of the four right efforts (abandon existing unwholesomeness, prevent new unwholesomeness). "Cultivate all good" maps onto the latter two (generate new wholesomeness, strengthen existing wholesomeness). "Purify your own mind" is the entire progression from the four foundations of mindfulness through the noble eightfold path.

One verse contains all 37 factors. This means: you do not need to learn all 37 steps at once. You need only remember one thing -- **stop doing what is harmful, start doing what is beneficial, and continually purify your mind** -- and the 37 factors will unfold naturally.

The reverse is equally true.

"Refrain from all evil" as a single undertaking can itself be accomplished through the complete sequence of the 37 factors: first observe (four foundations of mindfulness -- what is unwholesome? where in your body, speech, and mind does it arise?), then act (four right efforts -- abandon existing unwholesomeness, prevent new unwholesomeness), then stabilize (four bases of power), take root (five faculties), strengthen (five powers), awaken (seven factors of awakening), and finally integrate into a complete way of living (noble eightfold path). "Cultivate all good" works the same way -- first observe what is wholesome, then use the four right efforts to generate and strengthen it, then stabilize, take root, all the way to the eightfold path. "Purify your own mind" goes without saying -- purifying the mind is the core project of the 37 factors from beginning to end.

The relationship between the verse and the 37 factors is therefore **bidirectional**: the verse subsumes all 37 factors (upward compression), and the 37 factors unfold each line of the verse (downward expansion). What makes this remarkable is not just Ananda's statement -- it is that the structure of the system itself permits this bidirectional operation. **Any correct objective can be realized through the complete sequence of the 37 factors.** This is precisely the preview of the "dual-layer thesis" argued in Chapter III.

### 2.3 The Bodhisattva's Pure Land -- The 37 Factors Are Not a Hinayana Practice

Some will say: "The 37 factors belong to the Hinayana. They are the Arhat's path. Mahayana bodhisattvas do not need them."

Kumarajiva's translation of the *Vimalakirtinirdesa Sutra* addresses this directly:

> 「三十七道品是菩薩淨土——菩薩成佛時，念處、正勤、神足、根、力、覺、道眾生來生其國。」[^3]
>
> "The 37 factors of the path ARE the bodhisattva's pure land -- when the bodhisattva attains Buddhahood, beings who have practiced the foundations of mindfulness, right efforts, bases of spiritual power, faculties, powers, awakening factors, and the path will be born in that land."

This means: beings who practice the 37 factors will take rebirth in that bodhisattva's pure land. In other words, the 37 factors **are** the blueprint for the pure land -- not a tool for escaping the world, but a method for building a better one.

Zhi Qian's earlier translation (the *Vimalakirti Sutra*) states it even more starkly: the Tathagata's body "is born from the 37 factors of the path." [^4] The Buddha's dharma-body grows out of the 37 factors.

If the Buddha's body is born from the 37 factors, and the bodhisattva's pure land *is* the 37 factors -- can you still call this "Hinayana only"?

### 2.4 Two-Phase Structure -- The Foundation for Seeing and the Support for Walking

The seven categories of the 37 factors are not a flat checklist. They contain an internal phase transition.

The *Yogacarabhumi-sastra略纂* (Abbreviated Commentary) records a key insight from Master Tai:

> 前三——念住、正斷、神足等——為「所依事」，在見道故；後——根、力、覺道支等——為「能依事」。[^5]
>
> "The first three -- foundations of mindfulness, right efforts, bases of spiritual power -- are the 'supporting basis,' belonging to the path of seeing. The latter -- faculties, powers, awakening factors, and the path -- are the 'supported practices.'"

In plain language: the first three categories (four foundations of mindfulness, four right efforts, four bases of spiritual power) are **the foundation for seeing the path** -- you must first observe, act, and stabilize before you can see where you are going. The latter four categories (five faculties, five powers, seven factors of awakening, noble eightfold path) are **the support for walking the path** -- once you see it, you must root down, build strength, awaken, and integrate.

This ordering is not arbitrary. It represents a structural turning point from "seeing" to "walking" -- and that turning point operates identically whether you are on the path of liberation or navigating a challenge in daily life.

### 2.5 Daily Practice -- Not Theory, but Every Single Day

The 37 factors are not an abstract theory written on paper. The *Ekottara Agama* describes a practitioner's daily reality:

> 「竟日經行，未曾捨離意遊三十七道品之法。若眼見色，不起色想，亦無流馳之念，爾時眼根則應清淨。」[^6]
>
> "Walking in meditation throughout the day, never abandoning the mind's engagement with the 37 factors of the path. When the eye sees form, no conceptual elaboration arises, nor any rushing of thought -- at that time the eye faculty is purified."

Thinking about the 37 factors while walking. While eating. When the eye perceives an object, using the awareness cultivated by the 37 factors to see clearly. This is a **system that operates in every waking moment**, not a weekend temple visit.

And what is the foundation for all of this? The same text states it directly:

> 「諸善功德三十七道品之法，無放逸行為第一。」[^7]
>
> "Of all meritorious qualities and the 37 factors of the path, non-negligence (appamada) is foremost."

Non-negligence -- not being lazy, not being scattered, not deceiving yourself -- is the bedrock of the 37 factors. Without this foundation, you cannot take even one of the 37 steps.

## III. Core Argument: The Dual-Layer Thesis

### 3.1 The Logic of Sequence -- Why These Seven Categories in This Order

The *Mahaprajnaparamita-sastra* makes this sequence crystal clear:

> 「如行者到師所，聽道法時，先用念持是法，是時名念處。持已，從法中求果，故精進行，是時名正懃。多精進故心散亂，攝心調柔故，名如意足。心調柔已，生五根。是五根增長，能遮煩惱……是名為力。得力已，分別道法有三分……修道用，故名覺。見道用，故名道。」[^18]
>
> "When a practitioner comes to the teacher and listens to the Dharma, first using mindfulness to hold the teaching -- this is called the foundations of mindfulness. Having retained it, seeking fruition from the Dharma through diligent effort -- this is called the right efforts. Due to excessive effort the mind becomes scattered; collecting and softening the mind -- this is called the bases of spiritual power. Once the mind is softened, the five faculties arise. As these five faculties grow, they can obstruct the afflictions ... this is called the powers. Having gained power, one discerns three aspects of the path ... what is used for cultivating the path is called the awakening factors. What is used for seeing the path is called the [eightfold] path."

Let us set aside the Buddhist terminology for a moment and examine the **logical structure** of these seven categories:

```
Observe -> Act -> Stabilize -> Root -> Strengthen -> Awaken -> Integrate
4 Foundations  4 Right    4 Bases of    5 Faculties  5 Powers  7 Awakening  8-fold
of Mindfulness Efforts    Spiritual Power                      Factors      Path
```

| Stage | Category | What You Do | Why at This Position |
|-------|----------|-------------|---------------------|
| 1 | Four Foundations of Mindfulness (satipatthana) | Observe body, feelings, mind, phenomena | You cannot change what you cannot see |
| 2 | Four Right Efforts (sammappadhana) | Abandon evil, prevent evil, generate good, strengthen good | Once you see the problem, begin acting |
| 3 | Four Bases of Spiritual Power (iddhipada) | Stabilize through desire, effort, concentration, investigation | Action needs a stable motivational foundation |
| 4 | Five Faculties (indriya) | Faith, effort, mindfulness, concentration, wisdom take root | After stabilization, capacities begin to root |
| 5 | Five Powers (bala) | Same five, now unshakable forces | Deep roots enable resistance to adversity |
| 6 | Seven Factors of Awakening (bojjhanga) | Mindfulness, investigation, effort, joy, tranquility, concentration, equanimity | When strength matures, awakening unfolds in seven dimensions |
| 7 | Noble Eightfold Path (ariya atthangika magga) | Right view, intention, speech, action, livelihood, effort, mindfulness, concentration | Full integration into every aspect of life |

This sequence **is not arbitrary.** You cannot skip observation and jump straight to action -- that is blindness. You cannot skip action and jump to rooting -- that is wishful thinking. You cannot skip rooting and jump to awakening -- that is self-deception.

Each step is the prerequisite for the next. That is why the 37 factors form a **system**, not a checklist.

### 3.2 Layer 1 -- The Transcendent Path of Liberation

In the traditional Buddhist context, the 37 factors constitute the core of the path to liberation:

The **four foundations of mindfulness** observe: the body as impure, feelings as ultimately unsatisfactory, the mind as impermanent, and phenomena as devoid of self. This is not checking whether you look good in the mirror -- it is perceiving that this body is an impure aggregate, that all feelings ultimately entail suffering, that mental states arise and pass in each instant without permanence, and that all phenomena are empty of inherent nature because they arise through conditions.

The **four right efforts** operate on: abandoning afflictions already arisen, preventing those not yet arisen, generating wholesome qualities not yet arisen, and strengthening those already present. This is not giving up junk food -- it is giving up greed, hatred, and delusion.

From the **five faculties** to the **five powers**, faith, effort, mindfulness, concentration, and wisdom mature from seedlings into full strength.

The **seven factors of awakening** are the seven dimensions of moving toward enlightenment -- mindfulness, investigation of phenomena, effort, joy, tranquility, concentration, and equanimity.

The **noble eightfold path** is the complete way of life the Buddha taught at the very first turning of the wheel -- right view, right intention, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, right concentration.

This is a path from ordinary being to noble one. The *Madhyantavibhaga* (Distinguishing the Middle from the Extremes) states that when a bodhisattva reaches the fourth ground (Arcismati, the Radiant Ground), the 37 factors reach their fullest accomplishment. [^8] Yet even at this height, a trap remains: one can become attached to wholesome dharmas themselves -- a "love of virtuous practice." You enjoy cultivation so much that the enjoyment itself becomes an obstacle. The remedy? The text says: "Without the most excellent right effort, this attachment cannot be extinguished." [^8] One must apply a deeper layer of effort to break free from attachment to effort itself.

The transcendent depth of the 37 factors encompasses even its own side effects. That is the completeness of the system.

The *Buddhagotra-sastra* (Treatise on Buddha-Nature) goes further: the 37 factors are the "foundation" (依止) of the nirmanakaya Buddha -- all the merit and qualities of any Buddha are established on the basis of the 37 factors. [^9]

### 3.3 Layer 2 -- Worldly Self-Mastery

Now let us take the same structure and apply it to something everyone understands: **losing weight.**

#### Step One: Four Foundations of Mindfulness -- First, See Clearly

You want to lose weight. But have you actually observed yourself?

- **Mindfulness of body:** What is your body's current state? Not looking in the mirror and feeling ugly -- objectively seeing: what is your weight, your body fat percentage, where does it hurt, when did you start gaining.
- **Mindfulness of feeling:** Does being overweight actually bother you? Or do you not really care? If you do not feel the discomfort, you will not have the motivation to change. The Buddha said "observe that feeling is unsatisfactory" -- you must first honestly admit: this situation is causing me suffering.
- **Mindfulness of mind:** Is your mind ready? Do you genuinely want to lose weight, or are you just following a trend on social media? Is your intention stable, or will you want to lose weight today and forget about it tomorrow?
- **Mindfulness of phenomena:** Do you have a correct method? Do you understand caloric deficit? Do you know what basal metabolic rate is? Or are you planning to just skip dinner?

Most people fail at weight loss because **they skip this step.** They never observe -- they jump straight to action. They buy a gym membership, go three times, and never return.

The four foundations of mindfulness tell you: **do not rush into action. First, open your eyes.**

#### Step Two: Four Right Efforts -- Begin Acting

You have observed. You know where the problems are. Now begin acting:

- **Abandon existing unwholesomeness:** The bad habits you have already formed -- drinking sugary beverages every day, eating late-night snacks, ordering takeout constantly -- stop.
- **Prevent new unwholesomeness:** Bad habits you have not picked up yet -- do not start. Your friend invites you to an all-you-can-eat buffet? You can say no.
- **Generate new wholesomeness:** Start doing things you have never done -- walk 10,000 steps a day, learn to cook healthy meals, replace sugary drinks with water.
- **Strengthen existing wholesomeness:** The good things you are already doing -- sleeping early, exercising moderately -- keep doing them. Do more.

The structure of the four right efforts is remarkably precise: it is not simply "stop bad, do good." It divides into four quadrants -- existing/not-yet-existing x unwholesome/wholesome. Modern habit science (James Clear's *Atomic Habits*) essentially covers only two quadrants: breaking bad habits and building good ones. The Buddha added two more: **prevention** (prevent unwholesomeness not yet arisen) and **growth** (strengthen wholesomeness already present).

#### Step Three: Four Bases of Spiritual Power -- Getting on Track

You have started acting. But action is unstable -- you are motivated today, lazy tomorrow. You need a stable motivational foundation:

- **Desire (chanda):** You genuinely want to get healthy -- that desire itself is the fuel.
- **Effort (viriya):** You show up every day. No excuses.
- **Mind (citta):** Your mind settles. You stop wavering -- no longer wanting to lose weight today and wanting hot pot tomorrow.
- **Investigation (vimamsa):** You observe what works for you and what does not, adjusting your strategy accordingly.

The key at this stage: you are **on track.** You no longer need to fight yourself every day. You have found your rhythm.

#### Step Four: Five Faculties -- Taking Root

After a period of stability, five capacities begin to take root in your mind:

- **Faith (saddha):** You have seen results. You believe the method works.
- **Effort (viriya):** Effort is no longer gritting your teeth -- it has become a natural habit.
- **Mindfulness (sati):** You remember your goal. You do not forget why you started.
- **Concentration (samadhi):** You have the focus to resist temptation -- others eat cake, you can decline.
- **Wisdom (panna):** You increasingly understand which foods suit you, which exercises are effective, when to rest.

The five faculties are "roots" -- like the roots of a tree growing downward. With roots, a single storm will not knock you over.

#### Step Five: Five Powers -- Becoming Unshakable

As the five faculties continue to grow, they become the five powers -- forces that cannot be moved:

- **Power of faith:** Others can question your method all they want. You are steady as stone.
- **Power of effort:** Not three days of effort followed by three days of rest, but months of consistent output.
- **Power of mindfulness:** Constant awareness, moment by moment, of your eating and your body's condition.
- **Power of concentration:** Faced with the temptation of delicious food, the mind does not waver.
- **Power of wisdom:** You can now guide others -- because you truly understand the principles.

Angela Duckworth wrote an entire book called *Grit* describing exactly this stage -- but she only addresses "perseverance" without discussing the four preceding stages (observe, act, stabilize, root). Without the earlier foundation, talking about perseverance alone is hollow.

#### Step Six: Seven Factors of Awakening -- Awakening Unfolds

The weight loss has succeeded. But the brilliance of the 37 factors is that the system does not stop at "success." It continues:

- **Mindfulness (sati-sambojjhanga):** Your awareness of your body becomes extremely refined. Not just numbers on a scale, but a felt sense of your body's state.
- **Investigation (dhammavicaya):** You can discern what methods suit you -- too thin is unhealthy, too aggressive is unsustainable, find the middle way.
- **Effort (viriya):** The right method confirmed, you continue refining.
- **Joy (piti):** You feel genuine delight -- not because you look better, but because you have mastered the method of changing yourself.
- **Tranquility (passaddhi):** Body and mind are at ease. The old anxiety, restlessness, and self-doubt have faded.
- **Concentration (samadhi):** You can sustain a healthy lifestyle with stability.
- **Equanimity (upekkha):** You let go of attachment to the past -- no longer fixating on why you gained weight or why you failed so many times before.

Csikszentmihalyi's "flow" theory captures certain aspects of this stage -- particularly joy and concentration. But flow theory lacks equanimity -- the dimension of letting go.

#### Step Seven: Noble Eightfold Path -- Natural Integration

At this point, the right way of living is no longer something you must "work to maintain." It is simply natural:

- **Right view:** You have a correct understanding of health, nutrition, and the body.
- **Right intention:** Your thinking has shifted -- no longer "what should I eat to reward myself?" but "what does my body need?"
- **Right speech:** You no longer complain that "losing weight is so hard," because it is not hard anymore.
- **Right action:** Your behavior naturally aligns with healthy principles.
- **Right livelihood:** Your entire lifestyle is healthy.
- **Right effort:** You continue to refine, but it is not endurance -- it is enjoyment.
- **Right mindfulness:** You are aware of your physical and mental state at all times.
- **Right concentration:** You are settled. External food temptations do not move you.

**The key insight:** Once right view is established -- you genuinely understand what is correct -- it does not matter if someone tells you "you need this diet pill" or "you should fast for three days." Your mind is unmoved. The eightfold path unfolds naturally, because the first factor (right view) is in place.

---

That is the 37 factors applied to weight loss in their complete unfolding.

But weight loss is only one example. The same seven steps work anywhere:

**Preparing for university entrance exams:** The four foundations -- are you actually studying? Is your body at the desk or on your phone? Do you feel that getting into a good school matters? Is your mind on it? Do you have a correct study method? The four right efforts -- stop scrolling your phone, do not pick up new distractions, start studying seriously, keep studying. Then the five faculties and five powers unfold naturally -- faith, effort, focus, concentration, discernment.

**Breaking an addiction:** The four foundations -- observe what state your body is in. The four right efforts -- the existing addiction must be abandoned, new addictive substances must not be touched, build healthy replacement habits, strengthen them. Here the right effort of "preventing unwholesomeness not yet arisen" is particularly powerful -- it is essentially what modern addiction treatment calls "relapse prevention." The Buddha arrived at this concept 2500 years earlier.

**Starting a business:** The four foundations -- where is your business? Is there market demand? Is your mindset ready? Do you have a methodology? The four right efforts -- stop wasting resources, avoid repeating mistakes, do what is right, keep doing it. The five faculties -- faith (believe in your product), effort (do not slack), mindfulness (remember the mission), concentration (do not be shaken by competitors), wisdom (make sound business decisions).

**Repairing a relationship:** The four foundations -- honestly observe your actual state in this relationship. The four right efforts -- stop controlling the other person, stop emotional manipulation, learn to listen, keep practicing. Then rooting, strengthening, and finally the eightfold path unfolds naturally -- your speech (right speech), your behavior (right action), your way of living (right livelihood) all transform.

### 3.4 Structural Identity -- Why Both Layers Work

Why can the same 37 factors work both for liberation from suffering and for losing weight or passing exams?

Because **the underlying logic is the same.**

Any "intentional change" -- regardless of whether the object of change is mental affliction or body weight -- must proceed through: first observe (where you are) -> begin acting (abandon the unwholesome, generate the wholesome) -> stabilize motivation -> root capacities -> mature into strength -> awaken across multiple dimensions -> fully integrate.

This logic does not belong to Buddhism. It does not belong to psychology. It is **the structure of change itself.** The Buddha discovered it. Modern scientists have independently rediscovered its fragments in different corners.

The *Commentary on the Four-Part Vinaya* recognized this long ago. It distinguishes worldly causation (ten wholesome deeds -> three favorable realms) from transcendent causation (37 factors -> the fruit of the three vehicles), then states: "Though the scale of Mahayana and Hinayana differs, the logic of practice is the same." [^10] -- the scope differs between the greater and lesser vehicles, but the operational logic is identical.

Worldly and transcendent -- "though the scale differs, the logic of practice is the same." This is the classical basis for the dual-layer thesis.

"The Dharma does not depart from worldly phenomena" -- this statement does not mean the Dharma *is* worldly phenomena. It means: **the operational logic of the Dharma is also visible in worldly phenomena.** Because the mind you use for spiritual practice and the mind you use for losing weight are **the same mind.** The way the mind operates does not switch to one system for practice and another for weight loss.

Where is the difference? **In the object, not in the structure.**

The transcendent four foundations of mindfulness observe: the nature of the body, the nature of feeling, the nature of mind, the nature of phenomena. The worldly four foundations of mindfulness observe: your weight, your feelings about it, your motivation, your method.

The structure is identical. The depth is entirely different.

### 3.5 What Modern Self-Help Got Right -- and What It Missed

Let us make an honest comparison:

| Modern Framework | What It Discovered | Corresponding Category in the 37 Factors | What It Missed |
|------------------|--------------------|------------------------------------------|----------------|
| MBSR (Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction) [^11] | Observe the present moment without judgment | Four Foundations of Mindfulness | What comes after observation? No subsequent action system |
| Atomic Habits [^12] | Break bad habits, build good ones | Four Right Efforts (partial) | Missing observational prerequisite; missing "prevention" dimension |
| Grit [^13] | The power of long-term perseverance | Five Powers | Missing the "rooting" stage of five faculties -- perseverance without foundation is hollow |
| Flow [^14] | The optimal experience of total absorption | Seven Factors of Awakening (partial) | Missing the ethical foundation (eightfold path) and letting go (equanimity) |
| CBT (Cognitive Behavioral Therapy) [^15] | Change cognition -> change behavior | Noble Eightfold Path (right view -> right intention) | Covers only the first two steps of the eightfold path |

Every modern framework **got something right** -- each captured one or two categories of the 37 factors. But none of them **strung all 37 steps together.**

You would need to buy five books at the bookstore to piece together the full picture of the 37 factors. And those five books do not know one another exist.

**Integration itself is the missing piece.** The Buddha completed that integration 2500 years ago.

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Dialogue

> ⚠️ All cross-disciplinary parallels below are **structural analogies** (heuristic analogy), not theoretical equivalences (theoretical equivalence). The Buddhist seven categories operate within a distinct ontological framework (causation, karma, liberation); modern science operates within an empirical-positivist framework. The value of these parallels lies in helping modern readers find an entry point into Buddhist thought -- not in reducing one side to the other.

### 4.1 Behavioral Science and the Four Right Efforts

Charles Duhigg proposed the "habit loop" (cue -> routine -> reward). James Clear developed the "Four Laws of Behavior Change." Both fundamentally address: how to break bad habits and build good ones. [^12]

This directly corresponds to the four quadrants of the four right efforts. But the four right efforts include an additional dimension: **preventing unwholesomeness not yet arisen.**

Modern habit science primarily treats "existing bad habits" (abandon existing unwholesomeness) and "not-yet-formed good habits" (generate new wholesomeness). The Buddha additionally emphasized **prevention** -- bad habits you have not yet acquired should not be allowed to arise. This is a higher-order awareness: not merely treating existing problems, but preventing future ones.

### 4.2 Positive Psychology and the Five Faculties / Five Powers

Peterson and Seligman (2004) established the VIA Classification of Character Strengths -- 24 character strengths. [^16] The five faculties and five powers list only five: faith, effort, mindfulness, concentration, and wisdom.

But the critical difference is not in quantity -- it is in **structure.** VIA is a flat classification table -- you take a test and discover your "signature strengths." The five faculties and five powers form a **developmental sequence** -- first rooting, then strengthening; moreover, the five have a clear progressive relationship. The Buddhist system states: you need all five, and they develop **in sequence.**

### 4.3 Self-Determination Theory and the Four Bases of Spiritual Power

Deci and Ryan (1985) proposed Self-Determination Theory with three basic psychological needs: autonomy, competence, and relatedness. [^17] The four bases of spiritual power's "desire" (chanda, aspiration/autonomy) and "effort" (viriya, exertion/competence) show structural correspondence.

But the four bases include an additional factor: "investigation" (vimamsa) -- wisdom-based examination as a foundation. Self-Determination Theory lacks this reflective dimension. The Buddha's system does not merely require "having motivation" -- it requires "observing whether the motivation is correct."

### 4.4 Systems Thinking -- The Missing Integration

All three comparisons above point to the same conclusion: modern science has, at the **fragmentary level**, independently rediscovered individual components of the 37 factors. But no modern framework has completed the **integration.**

This is the unique contribution of the 37 factors: they are not 37 scattered concepts, but a **complete system with internal logic, sequential relationships, and structural turning points.**

The earliest known complete self-mastery system in human history. 2500 years old. Still operational.

## V. Middle Way Corrections

### 5.1 This Is Not Self-Help Soup for the Soul

This paper argues that the 37 factors can be applied to weight loss, exam preparation, and entrepreneurship. Some might therefore conclude: "So Buddhism is just a premium version of self-help!"

It is not.

The worldly application is an **entry point**, not a destination. Using the 37 factors to lose weight is fine -- you learn to observe, act, and persevere. But if you stop there, you have used a diamond blade to cut paper.

The full power of the 37 factors lies in the **transcendent** -- liberation from affliction, transcendence of cyclic existence. Using them to optimize worldly goals is only their shallowest layer of application. It is like a supercomputer: you can use it to play games, but it was designed to model the universe.

### 5.2 "The Dharma Does Not Depart from the World" Does Not Mean "The Dharma Equals the World"

The saying "the Dharma does not depart from worldly phenomena" has been severely misread.

It does not mean: the Dharma and worldly phenomena are the same thing.

It means: the operational logic of the Dharma is also visible in worldly phenomena -- because the mind is the same mind. But worldly goals remain within cyclic existence (becoming thinner, wealthier, more successful), while the Dharma's goal points beyond cyclic existence (liberation, awakening).

Shared structure does not imply ontological equivalence. A water pipe and a blood vessel share a similar structure -- both are tubes, both transport fluid. But you would not connect a water pipe to your heart.

### 5.3 Structural Correspondence Is Not Theoretical Equivalence

*Atomic Habits* is not the four right efforts. *Grit* is not the five powers. MBSR is not the four foundations of mindfulness.

The cross-disciplinary parallels in this paper are **signposts** -- they help you find the direction. But a signpost is not the destination. You cannot look at a signpost and declare, "I have arrived."

The Buddhist four right efforts operate within a framework of causation and karma. James Clear's Four Laws operate within a framework of neurological habit loops. The two show structural correspondence, but their explanations for *why it works* are entirely different. Acknowledge the correspondence. Respect the difference.

### 5.4 Sequence Is Not Linear

By this point you might assume: first complete the four foundations of mindfulness, check the box, then enter the four right efforts, check the box, then the four bases of spiritual power ... like clearing levels in a video game.

That is not how it works.

The seven categories of the 37 factors form a **spiral**, not a straight-line staircase. While cultivating the five powers, you may need to return to the four foundations of mindfulness for renewed observation. While practicing the noble eightfold path, the four right efforts of "abandoning and preventing unwholesomeness" are still operating every day.

The *Ekottara Agama*'s description of "walking in meditation throughout the day, the mind engaged with the 37 factors" captures exactly this -- the mature practitioner **holds all seven categories simultaneously**, never "graduating" from the earlier ones. [^6]

The sequence of the seven categories is the **order of learning** (where to begin), not the **order of lived experience** (practicing only one category at a time). Entry has stages. Practice is simultaneous.

---

## VI. Conclusion: The Vessel Is Ready -- Here Is the Path

In the last paper (Paper #9), we asked: is your vessel ready?

In this paper, we answer: the vessel is ready. **Now, here is the complete method.**

The 37 factors of awakening are not 37 scattered rules. They are an organic system -- beginning with observation, proceeding through action, stabilization, rooting, strengthening, and awakening, culminating in a complete way of life. On the transcendent level they can lead you to liberation. On the worldly level they can help you change anything you want to change. Not because the Dharma has lowered itself to the level of worldly phenomena, but because **the logic of change is universal** -- the mind is the same mind.

Modern self-help has each grasped one leg of the elephant: mindfulness is the four foundations of mindfulness, habit science is the four right efforts, grit is the five powers, flow is the seven factors of awakening. Only the 37 factors see the whole elephant.

You do not need to master all 37 steps at once. Begin with the four foundations of mindfulness.

**Pick one area of your life you want to change. Observe what state your body is in. Observe what you are feeling. Observe whether your mind is ready. Observe whether you have a correct method.**

That is it. Four questions. That is the starting point.

The remaining 33 steps will come find you.

---

The 37 factors are a methodology -- they teach you **how to walk.**

But anyone walking the path will eventually ask a deeper question: **What am I walking toward? What is it that I am seeing?**

The Buddha answered that question in 260 characters.

Those 260 characters are called the *Heart Sutra* (*Prajnaparamita Hrdaya*).

Series 2 -- we will meet there.
