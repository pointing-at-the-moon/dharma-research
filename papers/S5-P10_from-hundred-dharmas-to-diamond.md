## Abstract

Series 3 devoted fourteen papers to constructing the Yogācāra toolkit of the *Śatadharmaprakāśamukha* (Treatise on the Hundred Dharmas) — from the eight consciousnesses through fifty-one mental factors, eleven form dharmas, twenty-four dharmas not associated with mind, to the six unconditioned dharmas, governed by the principle "all dharmas are without self" (*sarvadharma-anātman*) and culminating in the conversion of consciousness into wisdom. Series 5 devoted nine papers to cutting through the reified existence of all dharmas with the prajñā blade — from the *jífēi* tripartite structure to the kōan-mirror to the six similes of illusion. This paper bridges the two series by answering a core question: after the cutting, what remains?

The paper argues that the hundred dharmas undergo dimensional ascent from "substantial tools" to "illusory designations" — the toolkit does not vanish, but the practitioner's relationship to it is fundamentally transformed. The *Śatadharmaprakāśamukha*'s "all dharmas are without self" and the Diamond Sūtra's "what is called all dharmas is not all dharmas, and therefore is called all dharmas" are two paths to the same truth: S3 arrives at *anātman* through analytical method (dismantling all dharmas into one hundred components and finding no self among them), while S5 arrives through prajñā method (*jífēi*-negating all dharmas). Using the *Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra*'s "five dharmas and three natures" — name (*nāma*), mark (*nimitta*), discrimination (*vikalpa*), right wisdom (*samyagjñāna*), and suchness (*tathatā*) — as the bridging framework, this paper locates the hundred-dharma contemplation within "name, mark, and discrimination" (paratantra) and Diamond prajñā within "right wisdom," with "suchness" as their shared destination. The *Cheng weishi lun*'s definition of *āśraya-parāvṛtti* — "abandoning parikalpita superimposed on paratantra, and attaining pariniṣpanna within paratantra" — provides the precise Yogācāra mechanism for the S3-to-S5 dynamic.

**Keywords:** *Śatadharmaprakāśamukha*, Diamond Sūtra, *anātman* of all dharmas, *jífēi* structure, *paratantra*, illusory designation, five dharmas and three natures, *vijñāna*-to-*jñāna* conversion, *āśraya-parāvṛtti*, bridge

## I. Introduction

### 1.1 What Remains After the Cutting?

S5-P09 concluded with the Diamond Sūtra's final teaching: "All conditioned phenomena are like dreams, illusions, bubbles, shadows, like dew and like lightning — contemplate them thus." Six similes illuminated from six angles that all ninety-four conditioned dharmas within the hundred-dharma framework are dependently arisen and illusory-yet-real — neither substantially existent nor nihilistically void.[^1]

This conclusion raises an immediate question: if all conditioned phenomena are like dreams and illusions, is the Yogācāra toolkit that S3 painstakingly built across fourteen papers — the eight consciousnesses, fifty-one mental factors, eleven form dharmas, twenty-four dharmas not associated with mind — still of any use?

This is not a rhetorical question. It is the genuine perplexity every serious practitioner encounters in the course of study and reflection: I first spend considerable time learning a precise analytical system (S3's hundred dharmas), and then you tell me it is all illusory (S5's six similes) — so was my prior study a waste?

This paper's answer is: no.

The hundred-dharma toolkit has not been destroyed by the Diamond Sūtra. What has occurred is a more precise transformation: the hundred dharmas have undergone dimensional ascent from "substantial tools" to "illusory designations." The tools remain the same — you can still use them to contemplate the operations of mind — but you no longer treat them as entities.

Imagine you have been navigating a forest with a map. S3 taught you to read the map — which symbols represent rivers, which represent mountains, how to interpret contour lines. S5 tells you: the map is not the forest. The rivers on the map will not drown you, the mountains on the map need not be climbed. But this does not mean you discard the map. Until you have emerged from the forest, the map remains your only navigational instrument — only now you know: the map is the map, and the forest is the forest.

### 1.2 Positioning of This Paper

This paper is the tenth and final paper of the S5 series (writing order: P01→P02→P07→P03→P04→P05→P06→P08→P09→P10). It had to be written last, because its bridging function requires all papers in both S3 and S5 to be finalized — you cannot draw an engineering diagram before the parts have been fully assembled.

Functionally, this paper is not only the conclusion of S5 but the integration paper for both S3 and S5. S5-P01 established the micro-level framework of the *jífēi* structure (trisvabhāva mapping), S5-P07 revealed the macro-level structure of the Diamond Sūtra (dual-layer obstruction mapping), and this paper addresses a question that S5 alone cannot answer: after the prajñā blade has finished cutting, what is the state of the toolkit that was cut — the hundred dharmas?

The answer to this question simultaneously prepares the ground for subsequent series. S4 (AI × Buddhadharma) requires this bridging framework: AI is a dependently arisen tool (S3's positioning), the Diamond Sūtra teaches non-attachment to tools (S5's cutting), and only after integrating both can we answer "How would the Buddha use AI?" S6 (Śūraṅgama Sūtra) will push the inquiry further: the one who uses the hundred-dharma map and wields the Diamond blade — who is that?

## II. Scriptural Foundations

### A. Sūtra Sources

#### 2.1 The Hundred Dharmas: All Dharmas Are Without Self

The opening line of the *Śatadharmaprakāśamukha* serves as its governing principle:[^2]

> 如世尊言：「一切法無我。」何等一切法？云何為無我？一切法者略有五種：一者心法、二者心所有法、三者色法、四者心不相應行法、五者無為法。
>
> "As the World-Honored One has said: 'All dharmas are without self.' What are all dharmas? In what way are they without self? All dharmas are broadly of five kinds: (1) mind dharmas, (2) mental factors, (3) form dharmas, (4) dharmas not associated with mind, and (5) unconditioned dharmas."

The treatise closes with two forms of selflessness:

> 言無我者，略有二種：一補特伽羅無我、二法無我。
>
> "Selflessness is broadly of two kinds: (1) the selflessness of persons (*pudgala-nairātmya*), and (2) the selflessness of dharmas (*dharma-nairātmya*)."

This is the doctrinal skeleton of the hundred dharmas: beginning from the Buddha's own words, unfolding a five-category classification (eight mind dharmas, fifty-one mental factors, eleven form dharmas, twenty-four dharmas not associated with mind, six unconditioned dharmas — one hundred in total), and culminating in the twofold selflessness. The fourteen papers of S3 examined each dharma within this skeleton — not to build an encyclopedia, but to experientially realize "selflessness" through the concrete analysis of each component.

#### 2.2 The Diamond Sūtra: "What Is Called All Dharmas Is Not All Dharmas"

Diamond Sūtra §17 directly responds to the *Śatadharmaprakāśamukha*'s governing principle:[^3]

> 所言一切法者，即非一切法，是故名一切法。
>
> "What is called all dharmas is not all dharmas, and therefore is called all dharmas."

In the same section, the Buddha further declares:

> 是故，佛說：「一切法無我、無人、無眾生、無壽者。」須菩提！若菩薩通達無我、法者，如來說名真是菩薩。
>
> "Therefore the Buddha says: 'All dharmas are without self, without person, without sentient being, without life-span.' Subhūti, if a bodhisattva penetrates selflessness and dharma-selflessness, the Tathāgata calls this one a true bodhisattva."

"All dharmas are without self" — these four characters appear in both the opening of the *Śatadharmaprakāśamukha* and §17 of the Diamond Sūtra. The hundred dharmas arrive there through analytical method: dismantling all dharmas into one hundred components and finding no "self" in any of them. The Diamond Sūtra arrives through prajñā method: *jífēi*-negating all dharmas, revealing that no dharma possesses substantial selfhood. Two paths, one destination.

§28 further specifies:[^4]

> 若復有人知一切法無我，得成於忍，此菩薩勝前菩薩所得功德。
>
> "If a person knows that all dharmas are without self and attains to patience (*kṣānti*), this bodhisattva surpasses the former bodhisattva's merit."

"Knowing all dharmas are without self and attaining to patience" — this is not mere conceptual knowledge (which S3's analysis can achieve), but the prajñā-realization that only S5's contemplative insight can reach. The *kṣānti* here is *anutpattika-dharma-kṣānti* — patient acceptance of the non-arising of all dharmas — not endurance but abiding.

#### 2.3 The *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra*: Three Natures and Provisional Designation

The *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra* provides the crucial framework for understanding the nature of the hundred-dharma classification. The three natures are defined as follows:[^5]

> 云何諸法遍計所執相？謂一切法名假安立自性差別，乃至為令隨起言說。云何諸法依他起相？謂一切法緣生自性。云何諸法圓成實相？謂一切法平等真如。
>
> "What is the imagined character of all dharmas? It is the nominal designation of self-nature and distinction in all dharmas, established so that discourse may arise. What is the dependent character? It is the conditioned-arising nature of all dharmas. What is the consummated character? It is the equal suchness of all dharmas."

The classificatory operation of the hundred dharmas — naming all dharmas (mind, mental factors, form…) and identifying their distinctions — is precisely the process of superimposing *parikalpita* upon *paratantra*. Classification is a necessary designation ("so that discourse may arise"), but it is not an intrinsic self-nature.

The sūtra's most critical statement for understanding the hundred dharmas' cognitive structure:[^6]

> 於依他起自性及圓成實自性上，增益遍計所執自性。
>
> "Upon paratantra and pariniṣpanna, [sentient beings] superimpose parikalpita."

When practitioners use the hundred-dharma toolkit, they easily commit the error of treating "the hundred-dharma classification" as the actual structure of mind — as though mind were truly partitioned into eight consciousnesses and fifty-one mental factors. This is superimposing *parikalpita* upon *paratantra*. The hundred dharmas are tools for contemplation, not blueprints of mind.

Yet the sūtra simultaneously warns against the opposite danger:[^7]

> 撥一切相皆是無相，誹撥諸法遍計所執相、依他起相、圓成實相。
>
> "To deny all marks as markless is to repudiate parikalpita, paratantra, and pariniṣpanna alike."

Denying the contemplative function of the hundred dharmas amounts to repudiating all three natures — because you need the dependently arisen tool (hundred-dharma classification) to establish contemplation and arrive at *pariniṣpanna*. This is precisely the position this paper defends: the Diamond Sūtra negates not the hundred dharmas themselves, but the reified attachment to the hundred dharmas.

#### 2.4 The *Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra*: Five Dharmas and Three Natures — The Bridging Framework

The *Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra* provides the core framework connecting the hundred dharmas to the Diamond Sūtra — the five dharmas and three natures:[^8]

> 五法、自性、識、二無我分別趣相者，謂名、相、妄想、正智、如如。
>
> "The five dharmas, self-natures, consciousnesses, and two selflessnesses that differentiate and tend toward marks are: name, mark, discrimination, right wisdom, and suchness."

The correspondence between the five dharmas and three natures:[^9]

> 彼名及相，是妄想自性。依彼妄想生心心法，名俱時生，如日光俱。種種相各別分別持，是名緣起自性。正智如如者，不可壞，故名成自性。
>
> "Name and mark are the imagined nature. When mind and mental factors arise dependent on this discrimination, names co-arise simultaneously, like sunlight with the sun. The various marks held by distinct discriminations — this is the dependent nature. Right wisdom and suchness, being indestructible, are the consummated nature."

| Five Dharmas | Three Natures | S3/S5 Correspondence |
|-------------|---------------|---------------------|
| Name + Mark | Parikalpita | The hundred dharmas' "labels" — mind, mental factors, form, etc. |
| Discrimination (dependent on name and mark) | Paratantra | The hundred dharmas' "contemplative operation" — analyzing mental operations |
| Right Wisdom | — | The Diamond Sūtra's prajñā — non-discriminative wisdom |
| Suchness | Pariniṣpanna | The shared destination of hundred-dharma contemplation and Diamond prajñā |

The hundred-dharma toolkit operates within name, mark, and discrimination: naming all dharmas (name), identifying their distinctive characteristics (mark), and analyzing mental operations using the sixth consciousness (discrimination). The Diamond Sūtra's prajñā operates within right wisdom — without depending on name, mark, or discrimination, directly illuminating the true character of all dharmas. Suchness is their shared destination — hundred-dharma contemplation brought to its fullest becomes right wisdom illuminating suchness, which is exactly the Diamond Sūtra's "suchness unmoved."[^10]

Notably, the four investigative contemplations (*catvāri paryeṣaṇāni*) of the preparatory stages examine precisely these three: name-investigation examines "name" as mere linguistic convention, meaning-investigation examines "mark" as a mental construct, and the investigations of provisionally established self-nature and distinction examine the definitional frameworks underlying "discrimination." The four contemplations are not a separate practice external to the hundred dharmas — they are a systematic illumination of provisional designation operating on the hundred dharmas as their very material, across the dimensions of name, mark, and discrimination.

#### 2.5 The Diamond Sūtra's Complete *Jífēi* Structure

The Diamond Sūtra contains thirty-five instances of the "is not X… is called X" or "not X… is called X" formula, covering virtually every category — physical marks, merit, Buddhadharma, atoms, worlds, all dharmas, sentient beings, mind, material body, wholesome dharmas, ordinary beings, dharma-marks.[^11]

What does this mean? Every category of the hundred dharmas — mind dharmas, mental factors, form dharmas, dharmas not associated with mind — can be *jífēi*-negated:

"The Buddha speaks of mind dharmas; they are not mind dharmas; they are called mind dharmas."
"The Buddha speaks of mental factors; they are not mental factors; they are called mental factors."
"The Buddha speaks of form dharmas; they are not form dharmas; they are called form dharmas."

*Jífēi* does not destroy the hundred dharmas — it reveals their three-nature structure. S5-P01 demonstrated: "is not" negates *parikalpita* (the belief that mind dharmas are substantially real entities), while "is called" re-establishes conventional naming within *pariniṣpanna* (mind dharmas can still serve as provisional contemplative tools). After *jífēi*-negation, each dharma undergoes dimensional ascent from "substantially real classificatory item" to "illusory contemplative designation."

### B. Treatise Sources

#### 2.6 *Cheng Weishi Lun*: The Real/Provisional Analysis of the Hundred Dharmas

The *Cheng weishi lun* provides a precise real/provisional analysis of paratantra within the hundred dharmas:[^12]

> 依他起性，有實有假：聚集、相續、分位性故，說為假有；心、心所、色，從緣生故，說為實有。
>
> "Paratantra includes both real and provisional: aggregates, continuities, and positional natures are said to be provisionally existent; mind, mental factors, and form, arising from conditions, are said to be really existent."

This means paratantra within the hundred dharmas has two levels: the mind-kings (eight consciousnesses) and mental factors are real paratantra — they arise from conditions and possess individual characteristics. Form dharmas and dharmas not associated with mind are provisional paratantra — they are positional designations established upon the basis of real dharmas.[^13]

But whether real or provisional, they are all paratantra — all conditioned, all illusory-yet-real. The *Cheng weishi lun* states explicitly:[^14]

> 眾緣所引自心、心所，虛妄變現，猶如幻事、陽焰、夢境、鏡像、光影、谷響、水月、變化所成，非有似有。
>
> "Mind and mental factors, drawn by conditions, falsely manifest like magical illusions, mirages, dreams, mirror-images, light-shadows, echoes, moon-in-water, and magical transformations — non-existent yet appearing to exist."

"Non-existent yet appearing to exist" — these four characters describe the existential status of the hundred dharmas: not substantially real (non-existent), yet indeed presenting as phenomena (appearing to exist). This is precisely the Diamond Sūtra's six similes (dream, illusion, bubble, shadow, dew, lightning) stated in *Cheng weishi lun* terminology.

#### 2.7 *Cheng Weishi Lun*: Basis-Transformation — The Dynamic from Consciousness to Wisdom

The dynamic process from S3 to S5 has a precise name in Yogācāra: *āśraya-parāvṛtti* (basis-transformation).[^15]

> 依謂所依，即依他起，與染、淨法為所依故。染謂虛妄遍計所執；淨謂真實圓成實性。轉謂二分，轉捨、轉得。由數修習無分別智，斷本識中二障麁重，故能轉捨依他起上遍計所執，及能轉得依他起中圓成實性。
>
> "Basis means that which is depended upon, namely paratantra, because it serves as the basis for both defiled and pure dharmas. Defiled means the falsely imagined parikalpita; pure means the truly real pariniṣpanna. Transformation has two aspects: abandoning and attaining. Through repeated cultivation of non-discriminative wisdom, one severs the coarse-and-heavy seeds of the two obstructions in the foundational consciousness, and thereby can abandon parikalpita superimposed on paratantra, and attain pariniṣpanna within paratantra."

Every word in this passage points to the S3-to-S5 bridge:

"Paratantra" is the hundred dharmas — it is the shared basis for defiled dharmas (*parikalpita*) and pure dharmas (*pariniṣpanna*). The hundred dharmas themselves are not abandoned; they are the site of transformation. What is "abandoned" is *parikalpita* superimposed on paratantra — your reified attachment to the hundred dharmas. What is "attained" is *pariniṣpanna* within paratantra — seeing suchness within the illusory hundred dharmas.

What drives the transformation? "Repeated cultivation of non-discriminative wisdom" — prajñā. Diamond prajñā does not arrive from outside the hundred dharmas to destroy them; it illuminates the hundred dharmas' true face from within.

Among the six stages of basis-transformation,[^16] the most relevant are "cultivation transformation" (through repeated cultivation of the ten grounds) and "fruit-consummation transformation" (when the vajra-like samādhi arises and one permanently severs all coarse-and-heavy seeds). S3's learning belongs to "weakening-and-strengthening transformation" (the first two stages), while S5's contemplation belongs to "penetration transformation" and "cultivation transformation."

The fruit of basis-transformation is the four wisdoms — great mirror wisdom, equality wisdom, wondrous observation wisdom, and accomplishment wisdom.[^17] The sixth consciousness's discriminative contemplation (S3's hundred-dharma analysis) transforms into wondrous observation wisdom (S5's prajñā contemplation); the seventh consciousness's self-grasping transforms into equality wisdom. S3-P14 detailed this mechanism; this paper confirms: converting consciousness into wisdom is not discarding consciousness, but seeing wisdom within consciousness. Hundred-dharma analysis does not disappear — it transforms into prajñā contemplation.

This transformation has a concrete practice path in Yogācāra. The *Cheng weishi lun* defines the boundary with two verses:[^37]

Preparatory stage: "Establishing something before oneself, / calling it the nature of consciousness-only — / because there is still something apprehended, / one does not truly abide in consciousness-only."

Stage of penetration: "When, regarding the object of cognition, / wisdom has nothing whatsoever apprehended, / then one abides in consciousness-only, / having left behind the marks of both apprehender and apprehended."

The preparatory practitioner "establishes something" — constructing the cognition "this is consciousness-only." S3's hundred-dharma contemplation is the complete content of this "establishing something." But because there is still "something apprehended" (a hundred-dharma system being cognized, a subject doing the cognizing), one has not yet truly abided in consciousness-only. The four preparatory stages (warmth, summit, patience, supreme worldly dharma) progressively deepen the hundred-dharma contemplation from "analysis with apprehension" to "contemplation without apprehension" — until "wisdom has nothing whatsoever apprehended," and subject-object duality dissolves into the path of seeing. S3-SUP01 detailed this mechanism: warmth and summit apply the four investigative contemplations to the hundred dharmas' name, meaning, self-nature, and distinction as provisional designations; patience confirms the emptiness of the apprehended through the four wisdoms of actuality; supreme worldly dharma confirms the emptiness of the apprehender — and in the next instant, prajñā dawns.

#### 2.8 *Cheng Weishi Lun*: The Unconditioned Dharmas — The Only Non-Conditioned Category

Among the five categories of the hundred dharmas, the first four (mind dharmas, mental factors, form dharmas, dharmas not associated with mind) total ninety-four, all conditioned. The fifth category — six unconditioned dharmas — occupies a fundamentally different position:[^18]

> 此五皆依真如假立，真如亦是假施設名。
>
> "These five are all provisionally established in dependence on suchness, and 'suchness' too is a provisionally designated name."

Among the six unconditioned dharmas, the first five (unconditioned emptiness of space, unconditioned cessation through analysis, unconditioned cessation not through analysis, unconditioned motionlessness, unconditioned cessation of perception and feeling) are all provisionally established in dependence on suchness — they are not six things external to suchness, but six perspectives describing suchness. True suchness (*tathatā*) is the sixth, but it is not simply one among six — it is the ground upon which the other five are established.[^19]

The Diamond Sūtra's six similes negate "all conditioned phenomena" — all ninety-four conditioned dharmas fall within the scope of negation. But the six unconditioned dharmas do not. The ultimate orientation of the hundred dharmas lies not in the ninety-four conditioned dharmas, but in the six unconditioned dharmas — especially true suchness. This is the position of *pariniṣpanna* within the hundred-dharma classification.

Yet the *Cheng weishi lun* simultaneously notes:[^20]

> 遮撥為無，故說為有；遮執為有，故說為空。
>
> "To prevent denial, it is said to exist; to prevent grasping, it is said to be empty."

Suchness too is "a provisionally designated name" — it is not a substantial entity that can be grasped. You cannot treat "suchness" as the terminal station of hundred-dharma contemplation and buy a ticket there. Suchness is not a destination; suchness is what the forest itself looks like the moment you let the map fall from your hands.

### C. Commentary Sources

#### 2.9 Vasubandhu's *Vajracchedikā Treatise*: The Doubt-Severing Structure

Vasubandhu structured the entire Diamond Sūtra as "three questions and three answers" plus "twenty-four doubt-severings."[^21] Among these, the sixth doubt — "The bodhisattva adorns the buddha-field; how can this be non-graspable?" — receives a verse of extraordinary significance:

> 智習唯識通，如是取淨土。
>
> "Wisdom-practice, consciousness-only, penetration — thus one takes up the pure land."

Kuiji glosses this in the *Zànshù*:[^22] "'Wisdom-practice' means cultivating non-discriminative wisdom; 'consciousness-only' means the pure consciousness corresponding to wisdom; 'penetration' means reaching true reality."

These five characters — "wisdom-practice, consciousness-only, penetration" — are precisely the bridging formula for S3→S5: "wisdom" is prajñā (S5), "practice" is cultivation (S3's hundred-dharma study), "consciousness-only" is the contemplative framework (S3's toolkit), and "penetration" is reaching true reality (S5's prajñā arriving at *pariniṣpanna*). Not "first consciousness-only, then wisdom," but "wisdom-practice-consciousness-only-penetration" — the cultivation of prajñā uses consciousness-only as its medium to penetrate reality.

#### 2.10 Kuiji's *Zànshù*: Reading the Diamond Sūtra Through Yogācāra

Kuiji systematically interprets the Diamond Sūtra through the Yogācāra framework. The most essential passage:[^23]

> 心之所現名曰依他，執之為實乃名遍計，依計既泯即是圓成。如繩依麻有蛇託繩生，繩蛇既亡則麻著矣！
>
> "What appears to mind is called paratantra; grasping it as real is called parikalpita; when both dependent and imagined are extinguished, this is pariniṣpanna. As a rope depends on hemp and a snake arises dependent on the rope — once the rope-snake both vanish, the hemp stands revealed."

The rope analogy precisely expresses the relationship between the hundred dharmas and the Diamond Sūtra: hemp (reality / *pariniṣpanna*) is twisted into rope (paratantra / hundred-dharma classification), and you see the rope and think it is a snake (*parikalpita* / reified attachment). The Diamond Sūtra's prajñā does not sever the rope — it lets you see that "the rope is not a snake." The rope remains (the hundred-dharma toolkit does not vanish), but the snake disappears (reified attachment is demolished). Then you discover: the rope too is not ultimately rope — it is hemp. The hundred dharmas too are not ultimately hundred dharmas — they are the provisional designations of suchness.

Kuiji's two-truth interpretation of the "is not… is called" structure:[^24]

> 即非佛法，是勝義諦遮增益邊，是名佛法，是世俗諦遮損減邊。
>
> "'Is not Buddhadharma' is ultimate truth preventing superimposition; 'is called Buddhadharma' is conventional truth preventing denial."

"Is not" prevents superimposition — prevents you from reifying the Dharma. "Is called" prevents denial — prevents you from nihilating the Dharma. All instances of "is not… is called" follow this pattern. This is precisely the description of the hundred dharmas' dimensional ascent: "is not hundred dharmas" prevents superimposition (the hundred dharmas are not the real structure of mind), while "is called hundred dharmas" prevents denial (the hundred dharmas remain useful as contemplative designations).

Kuiji further positions the Diamond Sūtra within the "dharma-mark-deconstructing school" (*pòxiāng zōng*):[^25]

> 般若宗於破相。
>
> "The Prajñā [tradition] belongs to the mark-deconstructing school."

But immediately clarifies that the purpose of deconstructing marks is not to negate everything:[^26]

> 大意皆破眾生遍計情執。
>
> "The overarching intent is simply to deconstruct sentient beings' *parikalpita*-based emotional grasping."

"Deconstructing sentient beings' *parikalpita*-based emotional grasping" — not deconstructing paratantra, not deconstructing *pariniṣpanna*, only deconstructing *parikalpita*. The blade's edge of prajñā is always aimed at attachment, never at existence.

#### 2.11 *Yogācārabhūmi*: From 660 Dharmas to One Hundred

The original source of the *Śatadharmaprakāśamukha* is the *Yogācārabhūmi*:[^27]

> 差別分別有六百六十。
>
> "Differentiated and discriminated, there are six hundred and sixty."

The *Yogācārabhūmi* enumerates 660 dharmas; Vasubandhu condensed them to one hundred. This is itself a pedagogical design — not merely because 660 dharmas are too numerous to memorize (though they are), but because the purpose of teaching lies not in exhaustive classification but in establishing an entry point for contemplation. One hundred dharmas represent the minimum necessary contemplative map — sufficient for the practitioner to see the operating structure of mind, without becoming lost in a labyrinth of categories.

The *Yogācārabhūmi* lists fifty-three mental factors; the hundred dharmas condense to fifty-one. The unconditioned dharmas are "eight in the expanded form, six in the condensed form."[^28] These condensations are not arbitrary deletions but removal of analytical redundancy while preserving contemplative necessity.

That the hundred dharmas were condensed from 660 — this fact itself demonstrates: the hundred dharmas are tools, not ontology. Tools can be more condensed or more expanded, depending on pedagogical need. The Diamond Sūtra's prajñā is precisely telling you: do not mistake the specific form of a teaching tool for ultimate truth.

## III. Core Argument

### 3.1 Surveying the Hundred Dharmas: Conditioned and Unconditioned

The five-category classification of the hundred dharmas is a precise structure:

| Category | Type | Count | Nature | Diamond Sūtra Six Similes |
|----------|------|-------|--------|--------------------------|
| I | Mind dharmas | 8 | Conditioned; real paratantra | Like dreams, illusions... |
| II | Mental factors | 51 | Conditioned; real/provisional paratantra | Like dreams, illusions... |
| III | Form dharmas | 11 | Conditioned; provisional paratantra | Like dreams, illusions... |
| IV | Dharmas not associated with mind | 24 | Conditioned; provisional paratantra | Like dreams, illusions... |
| V | Unconditioned dharmas | 6 | Unconditioned; pariniṣpanna-side | Outside the scope of the six similes |

The first four categories, totaling ninety-four dharmas, are all conditioned and all belong to paratantra. The Diamond Sūtra's "all conditioned phenomena are like dreams and illusions" covers all ninety-four without exception. Even the eight consciousnesses (mind dharmas), which might appear most "real," are illusory. Even the eleven wholesome mental factors, which might appear most "good," are illusory.

But the fifth category — six unconditioned dharmas — falls outside this scope of negation, because the six similes negate "all conditioned phenomena," and unconditioned dharmas are not conditioned. This is a critical element of pedagogical design: the structure of the hundred dharmas itself already points toward a destination that transcends the conditioned. The ninety-four conditioned dharmas are the map; the six unconditioned dharmas indicate the direction the map points toward — not another point on the map, but the real terrain you see when you set the map down.

### 3.2 *Jífēi*-Negating the Hundred Dharmas: Revealing Their Three-Nature Structure

S5-P01 established the trisvabhāva mapping of the *jífēi* structure: "X" refers to paratantra (conditionally arisen designation), "is not X" negates *parikalpita* (reification), and "is called X" re-establishes conventional naming within *pariniṣpanna*.[^29]

Applying this framework systematically to all five categories:

**Mind dharmas:** "The Buddha speaks of mind dharmas; they are not mind dharmas; they are called mind dharmas." The eight consciousnesses are dependently arisen contemplative categories — they help you see the operations of mind. But if you believe the eight consciousnesses are eight really existing "things" running inside your head, that is *parikalpita*. Knowing that the eight consciousnesses are contemplative designations, and continuing to use the eight-consciousness framework without attachment, is establishment within *pariniṣpanna*.

**Mental factors:** "The Buddha speaks of mental factors; they are not mental factors; they are called mental factors." The fifty-one mental factors are tools for observing mental states — you use the label "craving" to identify a certain psychological tendency, the label "mindfulness" to identify another. But believing "craving" is an independently existing entity residing inside your mind is *parikalpita*. Mental factors are windows for contemplation, not components of mind.

**Form dharmas:** "The Buddha speaks of form dharmas; they are not form dharmas; they are called form dharmas." All eleven form dharmas are provisionally existent paratantra — they are "reflections" of mind-kings and mental factors (the *Śatadharmaprakāśamukha*'s ordering rationale: "because they are the reflected images of the first two"). They are useful as contemplative objects but are not entities existing independently of mind.

**Dharmas not associated with mind:** "The Buddha speaks of dharmas not associated with mind; they are not dharmas not associated with mind; they are called dharmas not associated with mind." The twenty-four dharmas not associated with mind are the most "provisional" of the conditioned dharmas — positionally designated, lacking even their own substance. Acquisition, life-faculty, species-similarity, birth, aging, abiding, impermanence — all are concepts designated upon the basis of mind and form dharmas. Precisely because they are pure designations, they most clearly display the nature of "classification as designation" — it is easiest to see *jífēi* at work in the dharmas not associated with mind.

**Unconditioned dharmas:** "The Buddha speaks of unconditioned dharmas; they are not unconditioned dharmas; they are called unconditioned dharmas." This is the deepest *jífēi*. The six unconditioned dharmas point toward *pariniṣpanna* — but the moment you treat "suchness" as a goal to pursue, it instantly becomes *parikalpita*. The *Cheng weishi lun* states that "suchness too is a provisionally designated name" — even the name "suchness" is a designation. *Jífēi*-negating the unconditioned dharmas does not negate suchness; it prevents you from grasping the concept of "suchness."

### 3.3 The Hundred Dharmas as the Proper Use of Paratantra: Extending the Raft Analogy

S5-P04 argued the core thesis of the Diamond Sūtra §6 raft analogy: the Dharma is a raft, used to cross the river, and should be let go upon reaching the far shore.[^30]

This paper extends the raft analogy from individual teachings to the entire hundred-dharma toolkit: the hundred dharmas are the raft.

S3 spent fourteen papers building the raft — the eight consciousnesses are the keel, the fifty-one mental factors are the planks, the form dharmas are the waterproofing, the dharmas not associated with mind are the ropes, the unconditioned dharmas indicate the direction of the far shore. This raft is precise, sturdy, and fully functional.

S5 spent nine papers teaching you to cross the river — the *jífēi* structure tells you the raft is not the shore (P01), the kōan-mirror lets you see whether you are still clutching the raft (P02), "all marks are false" tells you the raft's appearance is not the point (P03), "dharma should be let go" tells you to release the raft upon arrival (P04), the merit comparisons tell you not to calculate the raft's merit-value (P05), "giving rise to mind without abiding" tells you not to take root on the raft (P06), the dual-layer structure shows you the raft's two halves (P07), "the Tathāgata cannot be seen through marks" tells you the raft's appearance does not matter (P08), and the six similes tell you the raft itself is illusory (P09).

P10's question is: you have reached the shore (or at least you stand on the raft and see the shore) — now what?

The answer is not to discard the raft. When the bodhisattva reaches the far shore and sees people still in the water on the other side, what does one do? Build more rafts. Using what? Still using the hundred dharmas. But this time the manner of raft-building is different — you know the raft is a raft, not the shore. You use illusory hundred dharmas to build illusory rafts to ferry illusory sentient beings.

"Illusory" does not mean "useless." S5-P09 already demonstrated: illusory conditioned phenomena remain the practice map. The illusory raft still crosses rivers. Illusory teachings still enable beings to reach liberation. The difference lies in relationship — you are no longer bound by the raft.

### 3.4 From Consciousness to Wisdom: The Dynamics of S3→S5

S3's hundred-dharma contemplation operates primarily at the level of the sixth consciousness (*mano-vijñāna*) — the only consciousness possessing full discriminative function, capable of employing the hundred-dharma framework to analyze the operations of mind. S3-P05 argued that the sixth consciousness is "the only consciousness that can look in the mirror."[^31]

S5's prajñā contemplation operates at the level of non-discriminative wisdom (*nirvikalpaka-jñāna*) — transcending the sixth consciousness's discrimination, directly illuminating the true character of all dharmas.

From S3 to S5, then, is the transformation from "consciousness" (*vijñāna*) to "wisdom" (*jñāna*). The *Cheng weishi lun* states explicitly:[^32]

> 智雖非識，而依識轉，識為主故，說轉識得。
>
> "Wisdom is not consciousness, yet it depends on consciousness to turn; because consciousness is the principal, it is said to be 'attained by turning consciousness.'"

"Wisdom is not consciousness, yet it depends on consciousness to turn" — wisdom is not consciousness, but wisdom can only be brought forth through consciousness. Prajñā is not a superpower parachuted in from outside the hundred dharmas; it is the qualitative transformation that naturally emerges when hundred-dharma contemplation reaches its fullest intensity.

The sixth consciousness transforms into wondrous observation wisdom — "skillfully observing all dharmas' individual and shared characteristics, turning without obstruction." In S3, you used the sixth consciousness's discriminative function to study the hundred dharmas, where each dharma's individual characteristics and shared characteristics served as objects of discriminative contemplation. When this discriminative contemplation deepens to its limit, it does not remain at the level of discrimination but transforms into the "turning without obstruction" of wondrous observation wisdom — you no longer analyze one by one, but illuminate as a whole.

This transformation has a concrete practice path in Yogācāra. The *Cheng weishi lun* demarcates the boundary with two verses:[^37] the preparatory stage ("establishing something before oneself, calling it the nature of consciousness-only — because there is still something apprehended, one does not truly abide in consciousness-only") and the stage of penetration ("when, regarding the object of cognition, wisdom has nothing whatsoever apprehended, then one abides in consciousness-only, having left behind the marks of both apprehender and apprehended"). The practitioner at the preparatory stage "establishes something" — S3's hundred-dharma contemplation is the complete content of this "establishing." But because there is still "something apprehended," one has not yet truly abided. The four preparatory stages (warmth, summit, patience, supreme worldly dharma) progressively deepen the contemplation from "analysis with apprehension" to "contemplation without apprehension" — until subject-object duality dissolves and the path of seeing dawns. S3-SUP01 detailed this mechanism through the four investigative contemplations and four wisdoms of actuality.

This is the dynamics of the hundred dharmas' dimensional ascent: first you use consciousness (the sixth consciousness's discrimination) to study the hundred dharmas (the hearing-and-reflection of the path of accumulation), then apply the four investigative contemplations to observe the provisional-designation nature of the hundred dharmas (the contemplative wisdom of the preparatory path), and then consciousness transforms into wisdom (the unobstructed contemplation of wondrous observation wisdom). The hundred dharmas shift from analyzed objects to illuminated illusory designations. The map is still the same map, but your manner of reading it has changed from "decoding line by line" to "grasping at a glance."

### 3.5 The Five Dharmas and Three Natures as Bridge

The *Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra*'s five dharmas — name, mark, discrimination (*vikalpa*), right wisdom, and suchness — provide the most precise bridging coordinates for S3→S5.

**Where S3 operates within the hundred-dharma toolkit: name + mark + discrimination**

The fourteen papers of S3 were essentially performing three tasks:

First, naming (name): the *Śatadharmaprakāśamukha* names all dharmas — "this is attention, that is contact, that one is feeling." Name is the linguistic designation, the entry point for contemplation.

Second, identifying characteristics (mark): the definition of each dharma — "attention has the nature of alerting the mind," "contact has the nature of the threefold conjunction's discriminative transformation." Mark is how a dharma presents itself, the object of contemplation.

Third, discrimination: operating name and mark through the sixth consciousness to analyze mental operations — "craving is currently operating," "this is a manifestation of non-introspection." Discrimination is the cognitive activity of paratantra.

**Where S5 operates within Diamond prajñā: right wisdom**

The nine papers of S5 were essentially performing one task: liberation from name, mark, and discrimination, entering right wisdom.

The *Laṅkāvatāra* states: "Right wisdom is that in which name and mark are unattainable, like passing guests. The consciousnesses do not arise, neither cut off nor permanent."

"Name and mark are unattainable" — not that name and mark vanish, but that you no longer treat them as substantially real. You can still use "attention," "contact," "feeling" (S3's toolkit), but you know they are "like passing guests" — coming and going, not you, not belonging to you.

This is precisely what Diamond prajñā does: using the *jífēi* structure to illuminate the unattainability of name and mark. "Mind dharmas are not mind dharmas" = right wisdom illuminating that the name and mark of mind dharmas are unattainable.

**Shared destination: suchness**

The *Laṅkāvatāra* states: "The Bodhisattva Mahāsattva, with this right wisdom, neither establishes name and mark nor does not establish name and mark, abandoning the two views of establishment and repudiation, knowing that name and mark do not arise — this is called suchness."

"Neither establishing nor not establishing name and mark" — this is the *jífēi* structure operating at the level of suchness. You do not establish the names and marks of the hundred dharmas (no attachment to the classificatory tools), and you do not deny the names and marks of the hundred dharmas (no discarding of the contemplative function). "Abandoning the two views of establishment and repudiation" — neither superimposition nor denial.

Suchness is the shared endpoint of hundred-dharma contemplation (S3) and Diamond prajñā (S5). S5-P08 cited the Diamond Sūtra §17: "The Tathāgata means the suchness of all dharmas" — the Tathāgata is suchness. Hundred-dharma contemplation brought to its fullest (right wisdom illuminating suchness), Diamond prajñā brought to its fullest (*jífēi*-negating all dharmas and seeing suchness) — different routes, same destination.

### 3.6 The Destination of the Unconditioned Dharmas

The ultimate orientation of the hundred dharmas lies not in the ninety-four conditioned dharmas, but in the six unconditioned dharmas — especially true suchness.

The *Śatadharmaprakāśamukha* reveals this through an easily overlooked ordering rationale:[^33] mind dharmas are ranked first "because they are supreme above all"; mental factors second "because they correspond therewith"; form dharmas third "because they are the reflected images of the first two"; dharmas not associated with mind fourth "because they are positional distinctions of the first three" — and unconditioned dharmas fifth "because they are what the first four reveal."

"Because they are what the first four reveal" — unconditioned dharmas are not "produced" by conditioned dharmas but "revealed" by them. This is a subtle but critical verbal distinction: conditioned dharmas are produced by conditions (arise), while unconditioned dharmas are revealed by conditioned dharmas (display). You cannot manufacture suchness, but by contemplating the illusory nature of conditioned dharmas you can "reveal" suchness — just as you cannot manufacture space, but dismantling walls allows space to naturally appear.

S3 used fourteen papers to contemplate the structure of conditioned dharmas — dismantling the bricks of the wall. S5 used nine papers to illuminate the illusory nature of conditioned dharmas — discovering that each brick is not as solid as you thought. Then space appears. Not because you did something special, but because the obstruction is no longer there.

The *Cheng weishi lun* states:[^34] "Fully achieved as revealed by the twofold emptiness, the true nature of all dharmas is called pariniṣpanna. This is the suchness — upon paratantra, permanently freed from the preceding parikalpita — revealed by the twofold emptiness."

*Pariniṣpanna* is not a new thing — it is the suchness "revealed" upon paratantra once *parikalpita* has been cleared away. Hundred-dharma contemplation (S3) + prajñā cutting (S5) = clearing away *parikalpita* = *pariniṣpanna* revealed. This is the ultimate meaning of "construction and dimensional ascent": not building a structure and then demolishing it, but building a structure and then discovering — the structure has always stood upon the ground, and the ground is suchness.

### 3.7 Everyday Analogy: Learning to Drive

Imagine you are learning to drive.

**Phase One (S3: hundred dharmas):** You study traffic rules — red means stop, green means go, speed limit sixty, signal before turning. You learn engine principles — how cylinders operate, how the gearbox engages. You learn operating procedures — press the clutch, shift gears, release the clutch, accelerate. Each step requires deliberate memorization and conscious execution. While driving, your mind runs continuous calculations: "Accelerate now… check rearview mirror… three-second gap from the car ahead…" This is hundred-dharma contemplation — using conceptual tools to analyze the operations of mind.

**Phase Two (S5: prajñā):** After ten thousand kilometers, the rules have internalized. You no longer recall rules one by one — red lights stop you naturally, road conditions trigger automatic judgment. You no longer "know" you are using the clutch — your body has merged with the vehicle. The traffic rules have not disappeared, the engine principles have not changed, but you no longer read them item by item — they have undergone dimensional ascent from "conceptual knowledge" to "embodied memory" (more precisely, "intuitive contemplation").

The Diamond Sūtra is not telling you to forget traffic rules. It is allowing traffic rules to ascend from "a memorized checklist" to "naturally operating awareness." The hundred dharmas ascend from "item-by-item analytical tools" to "intuitively illuminating prajñā wisdom."

But note: if you have never learned traffic rules and take to the road, you will have an accident. S3's construction is the prerequisite for S5's dimensional ascent — you cannot skip construction and leap directly to ascent. At the same time, if after ten thousand kilometers you still pull out the traffic manual at every intersection for item-by-item verification, you have a different problem — the rules have become an obstruction. This is the pedagogical logic of S3→S5: first construct (learn the rules), then ascend (rules internalize), and finally: the rules are you, and you are the rules — but you would never say "I am the rules."

The tradition has a more apt analogy — kindling fire by friction.[^38] The path of accumulation is the long, sustained rubbing of wood against wood — nothing seems to happen. The preparatory path is the moment the wood begins to warm — you feel the heat (warmth), the friction reaches its peak (summit), you confirm the fire will surely come (patience). The supreme worldly dharma is the final instant before flame erupts. The path of seeing is the instant the flame appears. S3's fourteen papers are those seemingly eventless strokes of friction — but without them, the fire will never come.

### 3.8 Closing the Arc of the Entire Series

Standing at P10's vantage point and looking back, the first five series of the Pointing at the Moon octology form a complete pedagogical arc:

**S1 Faith (initial aspiration):** Finding the mind — you discover that you possess a mind capable of awakening. Ten papers, from the butterfly effect of initial aspiration to the thirty-seven factors of awakening, established the foundations of practice: generating bodhicitta, understanding causality, preparing the vessel.

**S2 Understanding (Heart Sūtra / Prajñā):** Seeing emptiness — "Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva, practicing deep prajñāpāramitā, illuminated the emptiness of all five aggregates." Eleven papers, from the title to the conclusion, established the basic framework of prajñā contemplation: the five aggregates are empty, form is emptiness, in emptiness there is no form.

**S3 Practice (Hundred Dharmas):** The Yogācāra toolkit — dismantling mind into one hundred components and examining each. Fourteen papers, from the general outline to the conversion of consciousness into wisdom, constructed the most precise contemplative map. S3 is the series with the greatest constructive force — without this toolkit, the subsequent cutting has no purchase.

**S5 Cutting (Diamond Sūtra):** The prajñā blade — ten papers, from the *jífēi* structure to the six similes to this bridging paper, systematically cutting through the reified existence of all dharmas. S5 does not destroy S3 — it upgrades S3 dimensionally.

The arc of four series can be captured in a single sentence: **You found the mind (S1), saw emptiness (S2), received the map (S3), and then learned that the map is not the territory (S5) — but you still carry the map, because you are still in the forest.**

What does this arc prepare for subsequent series?

**S4 Inquiry (AI × Buddhadharma):** Requires P10's bridging framework. AI is a dependently arisen tool (S3's positioning), the Diamond Sūtra teaches non-attachment to tools (S5's cutting), and P10 integrates both to answer: How would the Buddha use AI? The answer: use it as illusion, without abiding in marks.

**S6 Illumination (Śūraṅgama Sūtra):** Will pursue a question P10 has not answered — the one who uses the hundred-dharma map, who wields the Diamond blade, who reads this paper — who is that? The Śūraṅgama Sūtra's "seven locations searching for mind" begins here.

## IV. Cross-Disciplinary Dialogue

### 4.1 Map and Territory ⚠️

Alfred Korzybski's famous dictum "the map is not the territory" precisely captures the relationship between the hundred dharmas and reality. The *Śatadharmaprakāśamukha* is a map of mind — one hundred classificatory items precisely annotating the operations of mind. But the "rivers" on the map are not real rivers; the "mountains" on the map are not real mountains.

S3 teaches you to read the map — understanding what each symbol represents. S5 tells you the map is not the territory — symbols are not entities. P10's thesis: knowing "the map is not the territory," you still need the map — because you are still on the journey.

But this analogy has its limits. In Korzybski's framework, the territory is an objective reality existing independently of the map. In the Dharma, "the territory" (reality / suchness) is not something existing separately from "the map" (the hundred dharmas / paratantra) — suchness is within paratantra, not external to it. So the more precise formulation is: the map is not the territory, but the map is also not outside the territory — the map sits on the territory, and you have simply been staring at the map while forgetting the ground beneath your feet.

### 4.2 Expert Intuition ⚠️

Hubert Dreyfus's skill-acquisition model describes five stages from novice to expert: the novice relies on rules, the advanced beginner recognizes situational patterns, the competent performer plans deliberately, the proficient performer intuits while deciding consciously, and the expert acts entirely on intuition.

S3's hundred-dharma study corresponds to the stages from novice to proficiency: you first memorize rules (hundred-dharma classification), then recognize situations (contemplating mental operations), then practice with deliberate plans (antidote methods), then intuit through contemplative familiarity. S5's prajñā contemplation corresponds to the expert: you no longer apply rules one by one — the rules have internalized into intuition.

But there is a fundamental difference between the Dharma's "intuition" (prajñā) and Dreyfus's "expert intuition": expert intuition remains a conscious activity (the sixth consciousness's refined discrimination), while prajñā wisdom transcends discrimination as non-discriminative wisdom. An expert driver does not need to think about rules, yet still maintains the sense of "I am driving." Prajñā contemplation illuminates even this subject-sense as empty. Thus the Dreyfus model can heuristically describe the "rule internalization" process from S3 to S5 but cannot be equated with the qualitative transformation of prajñā.

### 4.3 Phenomenology of Tools ⚠️

Martin Heidegger's distinction between "ready-to-hand" (*Zuhandenheit*) and "present-at-hand" (*Vorhandenheit*) offers another heuristic reference.

When you smoothly use a hammer to drive nails, the hammer "vanishes" — you are not conscious of the hammer, only of the nail. This is "ready-to-hand." When the hammer breaks, you notice the hammer itself — this is "present-at-hand."

S3's hundred-dharma analysis shifts mental operations from "ready-to-hand" (automated, unaware functioning) to "present-at-hand" (aware, analytical objects). You had been automatically craving, aversing, and deluding; the hundred dharmas teach you to pause and see: "This is the mental factor of craving at work."

S5's prajñā contemplation is not a return to the "ready-to-hand" state of unawareness, but entry into a higher dimension: you neither treat mental operations as "present-at-hand" analytical objects (not attached to classification) nor allow them to slip back into "ready-to-hand" unawareness (not abandoning contemplation). You inhabit a third mode that Heidegger did not describe: using tools as illusion, while knowing that both the tools and the one using them are illusion.

## V. Middle Way Corrections

### ❌ Misreading One: "The Diamond Sūtra negates the hundred dharmas, so they are useless"

This is P10's greatest pedagogical risk — P09's nihilistic misreading concretized at the S3 level.

The Diamond Sūtra negates not the hundred dharmas but the reified attachment to the hundred dharmas. As dependently arisen contemplative designations, the hundred dharmas remain useful in practice — just as the raft is illusory, but the illusory raft still crosses rivers (S5-P04). If you discard the hundred dharmas because you have read the Diamond Sūtra, you are not practicing prajñā — you are practicing nihilism.

The *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra*'s warning: "To deny all marks as markless is to repudiate all three natures." Denying the contemplative function of the hundred dharmas amounts to repudiating the three natures.

### ❌ Misreading Two: "First learn the hundred dharmas, then learn the Diamond Sūtra" as strict sequence

S3 and S5 are not a strict sequential order in practice — they operate simultaneously. You contemplate mental operations using the hundred dharmas (S3) while knowing that this contemplation itself is paratantra (S5). It is not first-build-then-demolish, but build-while-knowing.

The Pointing at the Moon octology's numbering (S3→S4→S5) is the pedagogical order of exposition, not a strict practice sequence. At any given moment, a practitioner is simultaneously constructing and contemplating — the emphasis merely shifts at different stages.

However, "not a strict sequence" also does not mean "skip the foundations." The path of accumulation in the five Yogācāra stages — hearing-and-reflection wisdom, moral discipline and meditative stability, the genuine arising of bodhicitta and renunciation — is the fuel that ignites the contemplative wisdom of the preparatory path. Without a stable foundation in the hundred-dharma study-and-reflection, the four investigative contemplations are mere conceptual spinning; without the practical foundation of discipline and meditative stability, contemplating emptiness remains philosophical speculation. The three discouragements and three forms of tempering that S3-SUP01 discussed (discouragement upon hearing how vast bodhi is, how difficult the six perfections are, how remote basis-transformation seems) are the psychological passages that practitioners must traverse during the path of accumulation. "Not a strict sequence" means S3 and S5 can be interwoven, not that you can skip S3 and proceed directly to S5.

### ❌ Misreading Three: "The hundred dharmas are Hīnayāna, the Diamond Sūtra is Mahāyāna"

The *Śatadharmaprakāśamukha* is a Mahāyāna treatise — composed by Vasubandhu Bodhisattva, governed by "all dharmas are without self," including dharma-selflessness (not merely person-selflessness). The Diamond Sūtra is a Mahāyāna sūtra — centered on prajñā, oriented toward the bodhisattva path. Both are different facets of the same practice system — analysis and contemplation, construction and deconstruction, consciousness and wisdom.

Kuiji's doctrinal classification already indicated:[^35] the dharma-mark school (*Saṃdhinirmocana*, *Yogācāra*, *Vijñaptimātra*) and the mark-deconstructing school (*Prajñā*, *Madhyamaka*, *Śata*) differ in approach, yet "the overarching intent is simply to deconstruct sentient beings' *parikalpita*-based emotional grasping." S3 deconstructs *parikalpita* through analytical method; S5 deconstructs *parikalpita* through prajñā method — different methods, same objective.

### ❌ Misreading Four: "I no longer need the hundred dharmas"

This is the most covert form of dharma-grasping — the belief that having transcended tools, one no longer needs them. S5-P07 cited Vasubandhu's warning: "Harboring 'I am a bodhisattva' — this itself obstructs the mind."[^36] Believing "I no longer need the hundred dharmas" is "harboring 'I am one who has transcended'" — another self-image acquired.

Bodhisattvas walk the path within illusion and continue to use illusory tools. Letting go of the raft does not mean forgetting the raft — you place it by the riverbank for those who come after. You do not carry the raft on your back, but neither do you burn it.

Moreover, P10 itself must also be *jífēi*-negated: "From the hundred dharmas to the Diamond; is not from the hundred dharmas to the Diamond; is called from the hundred dharmas to the Diamond." If upon finishing this paper you say "I understand the relationship between the hundred dharmas and the Diamond" — you have acquired yet another concept. The "relationship" you understand is itself a dependently arisen designation.

## VI. Conclusion

### 6.1 Restating the Core Thesis

From the hundred dharmas to the Diamond is not from low to high, not from simple to complex, not from error to truth. It is dimensional ascent from analysis to contemplation, from construction to deconstruction, from consciousness to wisdom.

The *Śatadharmaprakāśamukha*'s "all dharmas are without self" and the Diamond Sūtra's "what is called all dharmas is not all dharmas, and therefore is called all dharmas" are two faces of the same truth. S3 arrives at "selflessness" through the precision of Yogācāra analysis — dismantling all dharmas into one hundred components and finding no "self" in any of them. S5 arrives at "selflessness" through the *jífēi* blade of prajñā — all dharmas are not all dharmas, and the name and mark of dharmas are not what you took them to be.

The two paths converge at the point of basis-transformation: abandoning *parikalpita* superimposed on paratantra, attaining *pariniṣpanna* within paratantra — this is the shared destination of the hundred dharmas and the Diamond. The hundred dharmas are the basis (paratantra), prajñā is the transforming agent (non-discriminative wisdom), suchness is what is attained (*pariniṣpanna*). These three are not three separate things, but three facets of a single process.

### 6.2 Closing the S5 Series

The Diamond Sūtra's prajñā blade, from P01's *jífēi* structure through P09's six similes to P10's bridge, has completed a full pedagogical arc.

This blade is not for destroying — it is for liberating.

Liberating attachment to marks (P03: all marks are false). Liberating attachment to teachings (P04: dharma should be let go, let alone non-dharma). Liberating attachment to merit (P05: the reward paradox of the merit comparisons). Liberating attachment to "non-abiding" (P06: giving rise to mind without abiding). Liberating identification with structure (P07: the dual-layer structure is itself a designation). Liberating attachment to the Tathāgata's marks (P08: the Tathāgata cannot be seen through marks). Liberating the reification of all conditioned phenomena (P09: like dreams, illusions, bubbles, shadows). And finally, liberating attachment to liberation itself (P10: even the bridge must be *jífēi*-negated).

The Diamond Sūtra's final teaching does not ask you to grasp something, nor to let go of something. It simply lets you see: what you have been grasping, and what you have been trying to let go of, were never what you thought they were.

### 6.3 Open Questions

S4 (AI × Buddhadharma): Now that the hundred dharmas have ascended dimensionally to illusory designations, how should AI — as a dependently arisen tool — be used illusorily on the bodhisattva path? How would the Buddha use AI — without attachment, yet skillfully?

S6 (Śūraṅgama Sūtra): The one who uses the hundred-dharma map, who wields the Diamond blade, who reads this paper — who is that? The Śūraṅgama Sūtra's "seven locations searching for mind" begins here.
