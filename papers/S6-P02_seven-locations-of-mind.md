## Abstract

Where is your mind? The question sounds trivially simple — yet in Fascicle One of the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* (T0945), the Buddha posed it to Ānanda, who offered seven answers, each systematically refuted. This paper reconstructs all seven exchanges, applying the Yogācāra four-aspect theory (catuṣkoṭi-bhāga) from the *Cheng weishi lun* (T1585) as an analytical framework. The analysis demonstrates that Ānanda consistently identified projections of mind — its image-aspect (nimitta-bhāga) or seeing-aspect (darśana-bhāga) — rather than mind's self-witnessing nature (svasaṃvitti-bhāga). The paper further draws on the Yogācāra category of "direction" (diś), classified among the dharmas dissociated from mind (citta-dharma-viprayukta-saṃskāra), to expose the structural presupposition shared by all seven attempts: that mind possesses spatial location. Since "location" is itself a conceptual designation (prajñapti) projected by mind onto material phenomena, searching for mind's position with mind is self-referentially incoherent. The Seven Locations thus constitute not a philosophical quiz but a diagnostic mirror — each answer reveals not where mind is, but what the questioner mistakes for mind.

**Keywords:** Seven Locations of Mind (七處徵心), Four-Aspect Theory (四分說), nimitta-bhāga, darśana-bhāga, svasaṃvitti-bhāga, citta-dharma-viprayukta-saṃskāra, diś (方), upādāna-citta (攀緣心), vijñāna-sthiti (識精元明)

## I. The Problem

Have you ever searched for your glasses?

You rummage through the desk, the bookshelf, the sofa cushions, even retrace your steps to the bedroom — growing more frantic by the minute — until someone taps your shoulder: "They're on your head."

This is not a joke. It is precisely what Ānanda did twenty-five centuries ago in the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra*. The Buddha asked him: where is your mind? Ānanda answered seven times — inside the body, outside the body, hidden behind the sense-organ, in the darkness behind closed eyelids, wherever contact occurs, between sense-faculty and sense-object, in non-attachment itself. Seven times, the Buddha replied: "There is no such place" (無有是處).[^1]

Ānanda was not unintelligent. As the Buddha's personal attendant and the monk "foremost in learning" (多聞第一), he could recite the teachings verbatim.[^2] But reciting and seeing are different things. He could reproduce every doctrine about mind without being able to point out where mind actually was — like knowing a pair of glasses by brand and prescription while they sat perched on his own face.

This is the first diagnostic mirror of the S6 series. S6-P01 established the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* as a practitioner's self-diagnostic manual;[^3] now we enter the first examination room. The Seven Locations is not a philosophical debate recorded in scripture. It is a mirror the Buddha holds up to you: **every time you point and say "my mind is here," you reveal what you have mistaken for mind.**

This paper reconstructs each of the seven exchanges in full, applies the Yogācāra four-aspect theory (nimitta-bhāga, darśana-bhāga, svasaṃvitti-bhāga, svasaṃvitti-saṃvitti-bhāga) as an analytical framework, and exposes the structural presupposition underlying all seven attempts — that mind has a location — by drawing on the Yogācāra classification of "direction" (diś) as a mere conceptual designation.

## II. Scriptural Foundations

### A. The Sūtra

#### 2.1 The Seven Exchanges

The entire Seven Locations dialogue occurs in Fascicle One of the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* (T0945). The Buddha opens with a military analogy that frames the urgency:

> "It is as when a king, besieged by bandits, dispatches troops to suppress them — the troops must first know where the bandits are. What causes you to drift in saṃsāra is the fault of mind and eyes. I now ask you: where are your mind and eyes?"[^4]
>
> 「譬如國王為賊所侵，發兵討除，是兵要當知賊所在。使汝流轉，心目為咎；吾今問汝，唯心與目，今何所在？」

The analogy establishes the fundamental logic: without knowing the bandit's location, one cannot quell the insurrection. Without knowing where mind is, one cannot subdue afflictions. Ānanda's seven attempts are seven failed identifications of the bandit.

**First Location — Mind Is Inside the Body.** Ānanda's first response is the most intuitive: "All beings of the ten categories in the world alike take the discriminating mind to reside within the body."[^5] The Buddha uses the lecture hall as a counter-example: sitting inside the hall, you first see the Tathāgata and the assembly, then look outward through the windows to the Jetavana grove. If mind truly resides inside the body, it should first perceive the internal organs — heart, liver, spleen, stomach — before perceiving external objects. Yet you have never seen your own viscera. "If you cannot know what is within, how could you know what is without?"[^6]

**Second Location — Mind Is Outside the Body.** Ānanda immediately reverses course: if mind is not inside (since it cannot perceive inside), it must be outside (since it does perceive outside). He offers a lamp analogy: a lamp inside a room illuminates the room first; a lamp outside cannot illuminate the room. The Buddha counters with a food analogy: when one monk eats, the others do not become sated. If body and mind are truly separate and non-interactive, then what the eyes see should have no connection to what the mind knows. But when the Buddha shows his hand, Ānanda's mind immediately discriminates its features. "If they mutually know, how can [mind] be outside?"[^7]

**Third Location — Mind Hides Behind the Sense-Faculty.** Ānanda now constructs his own analogy: mind is concealed within the eye-faculty, like looking through a crystal bowl (瑠璃椀) placed over the eyes — transparent, non-obstructing, perceiving outward without seeing inward. The Buddha turns Ānanda's own analogy against him: if mind is like a crystal covering the eyes, you should see both the mountains and the crystal itself. Can you see your own eye-faculty? If you can, the faculty becomes an external object (眼即同境) and can no longer serve as mind's vehicle. If you cannot, the analogy collapses.[^8]

**Fourth Location — Seeing Darkness Means Seeing Inside.** Ānanda shifts strategy from spatial location to functional directionality: the body's cavities are bright, its innards dark. Opening the eyes and seeing brightness means seeing outward; closing the eyes and seeing darkness means seeing inward. The Buddha responds with a four-layered reductio: if darkness-as-seen constitutes "inside," then the darkness of a room without any lamp would all be your viscera. If closing the eyes sees inward, why does opening them not show your own face? If it did show your face, mind and eyes would be located in empty space — one body would require two knowing subjects.[^9]

**Fifth Location — Mind Arises Wherever Contact Occurs.** Ānanda abandons fixed location entirely, citing the Buddha's own words — "From mind arise all dharmas; from dharmas arise all minds" — to argue that mind has no fixed abode but arises wherever contact happens. The Buddha pursues two lines: if mind has no substance of its own, "contact" cannot occur (the absurdity of a nineteenth dhātu merging with a seventh sense-object). If mind does have substance, does it emerge from within or enter from without? Is it singular or multiple? Does it pervade the whole body or not? If it pervades, touching the head should simultaneously be felt in the feet.[^10]

**Sixth Location — Mind Is in the Middle.** This exchange has two rounds. First, Ānanda proposes spatial middle — the Buddha demolishes it through the relativity of directional markers: "middle" on the body is either edge or interior; "middle" in space shifts depending on the observer's orientation. Second, Ānanda refines his claim: mind arises in the middle between sense-faculty and sense-object — "Eye and form as conditions produce eye-consciousness; consciousness arises between them." The Buddha replies: if mind partakes of both faculty and object, the material and the mental are categorically different and would produce chaos if mixed. If mind partakes of neither, it has no substance, and "middle" has no locus.[^11]

**Seventh Location — Non-Attachment Is Mind.** This is the most profound of the seven. Having exhausted all positions — inside, outside, behind the faculty, in darkness, wherever contact occurs, in the middle — Ānanda retreats to a seemingly unassailable claim: "All things are unattached to; name this non-attachment 'mind.'" The Buddha forecloses even "nothing": is this "non-attachment" something or nothing? If nothing, it is like turtle-hair or rabbit-horn — non-existent, and non-attachment is meaningless. If something, it has characteristics, and what has characteristics has location — which contradicts "non-attachment."[^12]

#### 2.2 The Two Roots — The Verdict on the Seven Locations

After the seventh exchange, the Buddha does not propose an eighth location. Instead, he directly reveals the root cause:

> "All sentient beings, since beginningless time, have undergone various inversions... What are the two [roots]? Ānanda, the first is the beginningless root of birth-and-death — namely, the grasping mind (攀緣心) that you and all beings presently take as your own nature. The second is the beginningless essence of bodhi and nirvāṇa, originally pure — namely, the bright essence of consciousness (識精元明) that presently can generate all conditions yet is overlooked by those very conditions."[^13]
>
> 「認賊為子，失汝元常，故受輪轉。」[^14]

"Mistaking the thief for one's own child" — this four-character phrase is the final judgment on the Seven Locations. The grasping mind that Ānanda kept pointing to is precisely the thing that perpetuates saṃsāra. The bright essence of consciousness — bodhi's originally pure ground — has been present all along, but Ānanda, busy chasing the grasping mind's projections, had overlooked it entirely.

#### 2.3 Guest and Dust — Host, Visitor, Space, and Particle

After the two roots and before the Ten Demonstrations of Seeing (P03), the Buddha introduced a transitional teaching through Kauṇḍinya, the first of the five monks to attain arhatship. Kauṇḍinya offered two analogies:

> "As a traveler lodges at an inn, eating and sleeping, then packs and departs — the traveler does not remain. The host, however, goes nowhere. Thus: what does not abide is 'guest'; what abides is 'host.' Again, when the sky clears after rain and sunlight enters through a gap, dust particles are seen dancing in the beam — the dust stirs, but space itself is utterly still."[^15]
>
> 「不住名客，住名主人……澄寂名空，搖動名塵。」

The guest comes and goes; only the host remains. Dust dances in light; space does not move. Within the scope of P02, the key insight is that every "mind" Ānanda identified in the Seven Locations exhibited the characteristic of a guest — it arrived, shifted, and departed with conditions. None was the "host."

### B. Commentarial Sources

#### 2.4 Zixuan's Commentary — "Seven Locations, but Only Five Places"

Song-dynasty commentator Zixuan (子璿), in the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra Commentary and Annotation* (T1799), provides a structural analysis:

> "The above seven sections complete the refutation of the false mind's supposed abode. Though ordinary beings' speculations are infinitely varied, the places they rely upon do not exceed these seven. Wishing to expose the false mind's substance, [the Buddha] first destroys its abode — as when a city falls the bandits perish, when a nest topples the eggs shatter. **Though the examinations are seven, the actual locations are only five** — the fourth and seventh have no separate location."[^16]
>
> 「徵雖有七，處則唯五，第四、第七無別處故。」

Zixuan's observation is precise: the fourth location (seeing darkness as inside) is functionally a variant of the first (inside the body), while the seventh (non-attachment) has no "location" at all. Seven rounds, five actual positions.

More critically, Zixuan comments on the seventh location:

> "Not inside, outside, or in the middle — therefore mind has no attachment, [Ānanda] thinks. But he does not understand that the Buddha's intent in refuting the false is to reveal its lack of substance and lead him to recognize the original true mind... He falsely establishes 'non-attachment' and considers it to accord with the teaching. **Practitioners throughout the world mostly make this very mistake.** Simply being unattached at all times — they take this as their true mind, not knowing that clinging to this non-attachment is itself false thinking."[^17]
>
> 「舉世修行多作此計。」

"Practitioners throughout the world mostly make this very mistake" — Zixuan identifies the seventh location as the single most common error among meditators. He cites the *Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra*: "To take no-mind as the measure of mind — I call this a measure of mind" (無心為心量，我說為心量). "Non-attachment" as a stance is still attachment in a subtler form.

#### 2.5 Jiaoguang's *Orthodox Interpretation* — "Not Seven Examinations, but Seven Refutations Following Each Attachment"

Ming-dynasty commentator Jiaoguang Zhenjian (交光真鑑), in the *Orthodox Interpretation of the Śūraṅgama Sūtra* (X0275), directly rejects the traditional name:

> "The ancients' term 'Seven Locations Examining Mind' is imprecise language... Examining below, there is no further such interrogation — why call it seven examinations? **The seven rounds below are simply following each attachment and refuting it in turn.** To call them 'seven rounds of refuting locations' would not be erroneous."[^18]
>
> 「向下七番，但是隨執隨破。」

Jiaoguang's correction matters: the Buddha was not interrogating Ānanda about mind's location (which would presuppose that mind has one), but was dismantling each attachment as it arose. The Seven Locations is not an inquisition; it is a diagnostic procedure.

More analytically potent is Jiaoguang's three-tier framework of delusion:

> "This false thinking has three kinds of non-reality, and beings develop three tiers of delusion from it: first, it is fundamentally not mind yet resembles mind; second, it fundamentally has no substance yet seems to have substance; third, it fundamentally has no location yet seems to have location. [The Buddha] does not directly refute the first two... but first seizes upon the third — the fixation on mind's location — having [Ānanda] examine each one and revealing that there is utterly no abode. **Only after the seven locations are exhausted and [Ānanda] still fails to awaken** does [the Buddha] then denounce it as not-mind and reveal its lack of substance."[^19]

Three tiers: (1) mistaking what is not mind for mind; (2) presuming the false thought has substance; (3) presuming mind has location. The Seven Locations specifically targets tier three. Only after all seven "locations" are demolished does the Buddha advance to tiers one and two — a matter of pedagogical sequence, not logical oversight.

### C. Supporting Treatises

#### 2.6 The *Cheng weishi lun* — Four-Aspect Theory

The *Cheng weishi lun* (T1585), Fascicle Two, establishes the fourfold structure of consciousness:

> "What resembles the object-of-cognition is called the image-aspect (相分); what resembles the cognizing-subject is called the seeing-aspect (見分)."[^20]

> "The self-body on which image and seeing depend is called the self-witnessing aspect (自證分). Without this, one could not recall one's own mental activities."[^21]

> "If consciousness is analyzed finely, there should be four aspects... Of these four, the first two are external, the latter two internal."[^22]

The four aspects: nimitta-bhāga (the projected image), darśana-bhāga (the cognitive act), svasaṃvitti-bhāga (mind's self-knowing of its own activity), and svasaṃvitti-saṃvitti-bhāga (the witness of the self-witness, preventing infinite regress). The first two are "external" — they face outward toward objects. The latter two are "internal" — they constitute mind's reflexive awareness of itself.

Dharmapāla's four-aspect theory represents the orthodox position of the *Cheng weishi lun*. This paper employs it as the analytical lens for reading the Seven Locations: at every turn, Ānanda identified mind at the level of nimitta-bhāga or darśana-bhāga, never reaching svasaṃvitti-bhāga.

#### 2.7 The *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra* — Mind ≠ Mentation ≠ Consciousness

The *Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra* (T0676), Chapter on Mind, Mentation, and Consciousness, distinguishes three dimensions:

> "This consciousness is also called ādāna-vijñāna [appropriating consciousness]... also called ālaya-vijñāna... also called 'mind' (citta). Why? Because it accumulates and nourishes form, sound, smell, taste, and touch."[^23]

> "With ādāna-vijñāna as support and foundation, the six consciousness-bodies turn."[^24]

> "Ādāna-vijñāna is exceedingly deep and subtle; all its seeds flow like a torrent. I do not teach this to the ordinary and foolish, lest they discriminate and grasp it as a self."[^25]

This passage exposes a critical equivocation: "mind" (citta, the accumulating function), "mentation" (manas, the deliberating function), and "consciousness" (vijñāna, the discriminating function) are not interchangeable. What Ānanda called "mind" throughout the Seven Locations was actually the discriminating activity of the sixth consciousness — he mistook one function's projections for the totality of mind.

#### 2.8 Dharmas Dissociated from Mind — "Direction" as Conceptual Designation

The *Abhidharmasamuccayavyākhyā* (T1606), Fascicle Two, defines "direction" (diś):

> "Direction means: with respect to the causal differentiation among east, west, south, north, the four intermediate points, above, and below, 'direction' is conceptually designated. **It should be understood that this refers solely to cause-and-effect within material dharmas (色法). Immaterial dharmas have no capacity to pervade spatial locations.**"[^26]

This is the single most critical textual support for this paper's core thesis. "Direction" is the twentieth among the dharmas dissociated from mind (citta-dharma-viprayukta-saṃskāra), conceptually designated on the basis of material cause-and-effect. Directional concepts — east, west, up, down — are meaningful only within the domain of form (rūpa). Mind (citta), as a non-material dharma, fundamentally lacks the capacity to "occupy a spatial location."[^27]

Every one of the Seven Locations presupposes an unexamined premise: that mind has a place. But "place" (diś) is itself a designation that mind projects onto material reality. To search for mind's location with mind is to use the measuring instrument as the thing measured — or to search for the glasses already perched on one's own head.

## III. Core Argument

### 3.1 The Internal Logic of the Seven Locations — A Systematic Elimination

The Seven Locations is not a random sequence. It follows a rigorous progression from coarse to subtle.

The first three locations represent spatial fixation: mind is inside the body (first), outside the body (second), or hidden behind the sense-faculty (third). These place mind directly within a spatial container. The fourth location shifts to functional directionality — seeing darkness "means" seeing inside — but essentially translates a functional observation back into a spatial claim. The fifth abandons fixed position for fluidity — mind arises wherever contact occurs. The sixth attempts to locate mind at the intersection of faculty and object. The seventh is the most radical: not even "no location" is spared.

Zixuan's metaphor captures the structure precisely: "When a city falls the bandits perish, when a nest topples the eggs shatter." The Buddha is not searching for mind; he is demolishing every nest in which the false mind might hide. After demolition, the bandit has nowhere to shelter — not because the bandit does not exist, but because every disguise has been stripped away.

Jiaoguang's three-tier framework explains the pedagogical strategy: the Buddha begins with tier three (the fixation on location) rather than directly announcing "that is not your mind" (tier one), because Ānanda's attachments are still too thick for a direct revelation. "Only after the seven locations are exhausted and he still fails to awaken" does the deeper teaching begin.

### 3.2 Locations One through Three — Spatial Fixation

The first three locations share a common structure: **placing mind inside a spatial container.**

In the first location, the container is the body. Ānanda's reasoning appears sensible — "all beings of the ten categories take the discriminating mind to reside within the body" — it is, after all, common sense. The Buddha does not debate whether common sense is correct. He runs a simple logical experiment: if mind is inside the body, it should know the interior before the exterior. Yet you never perceive your own viscera.

In the second, the container is inverted to the body's exterior. In the third, it is shrunk to the interior of the eye-faculty, with Ānanda's own crystal-bowl analogy.

Read through the four-aspect theory: all three locate mind at the level of **nimitta-bhāga** — the image-aspect, the projected spatial field. "Inside the body," "outside the body," and "behind the eye-faculty" are all objects of cognition, spatial images projected by mind. Seeking the projector within its own projection is like searching for the film projector on the cinema screen.

### 3.3 The Fourth Location — From Space to Pseudo-Observation

The fourth location marks a subtle transition. Ānanda no longer designates a container but infers location from observational function: seeing brightness means seeing outward; seeing darkness means seeing inward. He has shifted from "where mind is" to "where mind's function points." But the Buddha reveals that this merely repackages spatial fixation as functional description. The logic "darkness = interior obstruction = inside" still anchors mind to a spatial referent.

In four-aspect terms: Ānanda has moved from nimitta-bhāga (spatial images) to the **directionality of darśana-bhāga** — the "direction" in which the seeing-function operates. But he then projects this directionality back into nimitta-bhāga (a spatial claim about "inside"), effectively collapsing the seeing-aspect back into the image-aspect. He has glimpsed the "seeing" dimension of mind without recognizing that seeing's directionality is not the same as mind's location.

### 3.4 The Fifth Location — The Universal Adhesive

The fifth location represents Ānanda's most significant strategic shift. He abandons fixed location entirely, citing the Buddha's own teaching — "From mind arise all dharmas; from dharmas arise all minds" — to argue that mind has no fixed abode but arises wherever contact occurs.

On the surface, this resembles the Yogācāra principle that consciousness turns with conditions. But the Buddha's refutation strikes at the core: "If this mind has no substance, there is nothing to make contact with." A verb without a subject is incoherent — if mind is nothing in itself, what performs the "arising wherever contact occurs"? Conversely, if mind does have substance, the fourfold critique applies: does it emerge from within or enter from without? Is it one or many? Does it pervade the body or not?

The deeper issue: Ānanda has substituted fluidity for fixity, but his conception of "mind" remains that of a thing — a thing that travels rather than stays. Whether fixed or fluid, so long as mind is conceived as a thing, it necessarily has substance and characteristics, and necessarily falls into the fourfold interrogation.

### 3.5 The Sixth Location — Between Faculty and Object

The sixth exchange, unfolding in two rounds, shows both Ānanda's increasing sophistication and the Buddha's corresponding escalation.

In the second round, Ānanda reaches his closest approximation of technical Yogācāra language: "Eye and form as conditions produce eye-consciousness; consciousness arises between them — this is where mind is." This sounds remarkably like the Yogācāra formula of the three-part convergence (根塵識三和合) — faculty meets object, consciousness arises.

But Yogācāra "arising" is not a spatial event. When the tradition says consciousness arises from the convergence of faculty and object, it describes a temporal actualization of latent seeds — an event, not a place. To interpret "consciousness arises" as "consciousness sits between faculty and object" is to misread temporality as spatiality, process as position.

### 3.6 The Seventh Location — The Deepest Trap

The seventh exchange is the shortest and deepest. Ānanda, having exhausted all positions, retreats to a final line of defense: "All things are unattached to; name this non-attachment 'mind.'"

The Buddha's logic is clean to the point of ruthlessness. Is this "non-attachment" something or nothing? If nothing — turtle-hair, rabbit-horn — there is no subject to "not attach." If something, it has characteristics; what has characteristics has location; what has location contradicts "non-attachment."

Zixuan's assessment is devastating: "Practitioners throughout the world mostly make this very mistake." The most common error in meditation halls worldwide is precisely this: sitting in stillness, letting every thought pass without grasping, and believing this constitutes realization. But "not grasping" is itself an act; "non-attachment" is itself a mental posture. There remains a subtle observer and observed, a faint stance of "I am being non-attached." Zixuan cites the *Laṅkāvatāra*: "To take no-mind as the measure of mind — I call this a measure of mind." The standard itself is another thought.

In four-aspect terms: across the first six locations, Ānanda oscillated between nimitta-bhāga and darśana-bhāga. At the seventh, he attempts to leap beyond darśana-bhāga — to a place where nothing is seen, nothing grasped, nothing established. But this "nothing" is itself a projection of darśana-bhāga: the seeing-aspect casts "emptiness" as a new nimitta-bhāga and then presumes it can rest there. Svasaṃvitti-bhāga is not "non-attachment"; it is mind's self-knowing nature, requiring no act of "not attaching" to sustain itself.

### 3.7 "Direction" as Conceptual Designation — An Echo of S3-P12

A single thread now runs through all seven: **every location presupposes that mind has a place.**

Inside is a place. Outside is a place. Behind the faculty is a place. The darkness is a place. Each point of contact is a place. Between faculty and object is a place. Even the seventh location's "non-attachment" occupies a place in conceptual space.

But what is "place" itself? S3-P12 demonstrated the conceptual-designation status of "direction" (diś) among the dharmas dissociated from mind.[^27] The *Abhidharmasamuccayavyākhyā*'s definition is decisive: "Direction is conceptually designated solely on the basis of material cause-and-effect. Immaterial dharmas have no capacity to pervade spatial locations."

"Direction" is designated only in dependence upon form. Mind, as a non-material dharma, simply does not possess the function of "occupying a direction."

This is the structural paradox of the Seven Locations: **to search for mind's spatial location using mind is to attempt to capture the manufacturer using one of its own products.** The search fails not because mind does not exist, but because the search tool and the search target are identical. Like the glasses analogy: you are searching with the very eyes that the glasses enable — because you are using them, you cannot see them.

### 3.8 The Four-Aspect Summary Reading

The four-aspect theory now permits a structural summary of the entire Seven Locations:

| Location | What Ānanda Identified | Four-Aspect Position |
|---|---|---|
| First–Third | Spatial position of mind (inside/outside/behind faculty) | Nimitta-bhāga — mind's projected spatial images |
| Fourth | Functional direction of mind (seeing brightness/darkness) | Darśana-bhāga's directionality collapsed back into nimitta-bhāga |
| Fifth | Mind's dynamic fluidity (arising wherever contact occurs) | Darśana-bhāga's operational dynamism reified as substance |
| Sixth | Mind's point of origination (between faculty and object) | Darśana-bhāga's causal conditions spatialized |
| Seventh | Mind's transcendent state (non-attachment) | Darśana-bhāga projects "emptiness" as nimitta-bhāga, presuming transcendence |

Every location commits the same fundamental error: **seeking mind within the domain of the first two aspects (nimitta-bhāga and darśana-bhāga).** Nimitta-bhāga is mind's projected image-world; darśana-bhāga is mind's cognitive activity — but neither images nor activities constitute mind itself. Mind's self-knowing nature is svasaṃvitti-bhāga, and the reason it was never "found" in the Seven Locations is precisely that it is **not the kind of thing that is found** — it is the ground upon which "finding" becomes possible.

An immediate correction is necessary: the four-aspect theory is an analytical instrument, not an ontological map of mind. Svasaṃvitti-bhāga must not be understood as "a third place" — somewhere deeper than nimitta-bhāga and darśana-bhāga. It is not anywhere. The *Cheng weishi lun* explicitly states: "These four aspects may be subsumed into three... or into two... or into one, since their substance is not different."[^28] The four can collapse into one — they are not four separate entities but four analytical facets of a single operation.

### 3.9 The Two Roots — Bridge from P02 to P03

The ultimate closure of the Seven Locations is not Ānanda "finding his mind." It is the Buddha directly revealing the root of the problem: the mind you have been searching for (the grasping mind) is precisely the thing perpetuating your suffering. Your actual mind (the bright essence of consciousness) you have never searched for — because you have been searching with the grasping mind, and the grasping mind's nature is to grasp outward. It will never turn back to see itself.

"Mistaking the thief for one's own child, you have lost your original constancy and thus undergo the turning of saṃsāra."

This is the Seven Locations' pedagogical completion. All "locations" demolished, Ānanda now faces a naked question: that which kept searching, kept pointing, kept standing back up after each refutation to point again — what is it?

This is not a question P02 can answer. P02's function is **demolition** — stripping away what you mistake for mind. The next step is **revelation** — the Ten Demonstrations of Seeing (P03) will not ask where mind is, but directly display the seeing-nature that does not arise or perish with its objects.

The demolition of the Seven Locations is the prerequisite for the revelation of the Ten Demonstrations. Without first clearing every false answer, the practitioner who encounters "seeing-nature" in the Ten Demonstrations would immediately place it inside some location — looping back to the first location all over again.

## IV. Interdisciplinary Dialogue — Descartes' *Cogito* as Structural Parallel

### 4.1 Structural Similarity ⚠️

Descartes' methodological doubt in the *Meditations on First Philosophy* exhibits a striking structural parallel to the Seven Locations. Descartes progressively doubts the reliability of the senses, the body, and the external world, arriving at a single indubitable remainder: the fact of thinking itself — *Cogito, ergo sum*.[^29]

The Seven Locations follows a parallel arc: the Buddha progressively eliminates every proposed abode of mind — inside the body, outside, behind the faculty, in functional direction, in fluid contact, between faculty and object, in non-attachment — until no "location" remains.

Both are methods of elimination: neither positively defines the target but rather dismantles every incorrect option. Descartes eliminates the reliability of cognitive objects; the Buddha eliminates the positionality of mind.

### 4.2 Fundamental Difference — Where the *Cogito* Stops, the Buddha Continues

Descartes' method of elimination halts at "I think." He discovers an indubitable foundation — the self-evidence of thinking — and rebuilds his entire epistemology upon it. *Cogito, ergo sum* becomes the first principle of Western philosophy.

The Buddha's method does not halt. Had Ānanda declared at the seventh location, "I am thinking — the thinking itself is mind," the Buddha's response would follow the same structure as his refutation of "non-attachment": is this "thinking" something or nothing? If something, it has characteristics, characteristics imply location, and location has already been demolished. If nothing, it is turtle-hair and rabbit-horn.

Within the four-aspect framework, Descartes' *Cogito* corresponds roughly to **darśana-bhāga** — the cognitive activity of mind (I am thinking, I am doubting, I am judging). Descartes treats this activity as self-evident and foundational. The Buddha considers it precisely what must be penetrated further — darśana-bhāga is a function of mind, not mind's ground. Svasaṃvitti-bhāga is not "I am thinking" but the self-knowing basis upon which thinking occurs — it does not require an act of "I think" to certify itself.

### 4.3 Irreducibility Statement ⚠️

The above comparison is a **heuristic analogy**, not a theoretical equivalence. Descartes' methodological doubt and the Seven Locations serve fundamentally different purposes: Descartes seeks to rebuild an epistemological foundation for certain knowledge; the Buddha seeks to reveal the illusory nature of the grasping mind as a soteriological intervention. One arrives at the establishment of the *Cogito*; the other arrives at the direct recognition of svasaṃvitti-bhāga — structurally similar, directionally opposite.

The more fundamental divergence lies in ontological commitments: Descartes' "I" (ego) is a substantial thinking subject; the Buddha's "mind" (citta) is a conditioned process of dependent arising. Descartes finds a fixed point and builds upon it; the Buddha reveals that all fixed points are mind's own constructions and invites the practitioner to see the constructor directly.

The value of this comparison lies not in arguing for equivalence between Buddhist philosophy and Cartesian epistemology, but in helping readers familiar with the Western philosophical tradition appreciate the depth of the Seven Locations — not as a primitive religious catechism but as a rigorous exercise in the philosophy of consciousness whose precision matches Descartes' methodological doubt while advancing beyond it.

## V. Middle Way Corrections

### ❌ 5.1 Verbal Realization — Knowing Is Not Seeing

"I know that mind is not in any of the seven locations." Having read this far, you may think precisely this. But knowing and realizing are not the same.

You can reconstruct the logic of each exchange, explain why the Buddha refuted each answer, and deploy the four-aspect theory to analyze Ānanda's errors — this is "knowing" (知). But the next time someone angers you, your first reaction will still be "I feel upset inside." That "inside" is the first location — mind inside the body.

Knowing is intellectual comprehension; realization is the transformation of one's entire mode of being. S5-P02 established this distinction through the "kōan mirror" methodology:[^30] understanding a kōan's logic is not awakening. The Seven Locations is likewise a kōan mirror — reading it is only the beginning. The practice lies in catching yourself, in each daily moment of "my mind feels..." and illuminating what you have just located.

### ❌ 5.2 Nihilism — "Mind Is Nothing at All"

The refutation of all seven locations does not mean mind does not exist. The Buddha demolished the **abodes** of the false mind, not the **existence** of mind itself.

The Two Roots teaching is explicit: the grasping mind is false, but the bright essence of consciousness is real. The Seven Locations strips away the former's disguises; it does not negate the latter's presence. To conclude from the Seven Locations that "mind is nothing; everything is empty" is to fall into the annihilationist view (uccheda-dṛṣṭi) — a more serious error than any of Ānanda's seven.

### ❌ 5.3 Reifying the Four-Aspect Theory

This paper employs the four-aspect theory as an analytical tool for reading the Seven Locations, but the theory itself is not an ontological description of mind's internal architecture. One must not treat "nimitta-bhāga," "darśana-bhāga," and "svasaṃvitti-bhāga" as three rooms inside mind or three components of a mechanism.

The *Cheng weishi lun* explicitly states that the four aspects "may be subsumed into three... into two... into one, since their substance is not different." They are four analytical facets of a single mental operation, not four independently existing entities. To understand svasaṃvitti-bhāga as "the third location Ānanda should have looked" is to commit precisely Ānanda's error — searching for yet another "place."

### ❌ 5.4 Diagnosing Others, Not Oneself

The Seven Locations reveals Ānanda's errors, but it was not designed for you to judge how foolish Ānanda was, nor to test others. S6-P01 emphasized: the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* is a mirror for oneself, not for others.[^31]

If your response to reading the Seven Locations is "Teacher So-and-so claims mind is inside the body — he cannot even pass the first location" — then what you are doing departs further from the *Śūraṅgama*'s teaching than any of Ānanda's seven errors. At least Ānanda was honestly searching for his own mind. You are decorating your sense of superiority with someone else's mistake. "To point out that another is a māra is itself māra" — this is not rhetoric; it is the *Śūraṅgama*'s core stance.

## VI. Conclusion

### Summary

The Seven Locations constitutes a precision-engineered systematic elimination: from inside the body to outside, from behind the faculty to functional direction, from fluid contact to the middle ground between faculty and object, and finally to non-attachment itself — the Buddha layer by layer dismantles every refuge of the false mind. The Yogācāra four-aspect theory reveals their shared structure: Ānanda searched exclusively within the domains of nimitta-bhāga and darśana-bhāga, while mind's self-knowing nature (svasaṃvitti-bhāga) occupies no "location" at all — because "location" (diś) is itself mind's own conceptual designation. The verdict on the Seven Locations is "mistaking the thief for one's own child" — taking the grasping mind for the true mind is the root of all cyclic existence.

### Implications for Practice

The next time you catch yourself thinking "I feel in my heart that..." — pause for one second. Where is that "heart"? Inside the body? Inside the brain? Somewhere in the middle of a feeling? Or simply "my subjective experience, wherever that is"?

You do not need to answer correctly. What you need is **the question itself.** Each time genuine inquiry arises about "where is my mind?" — that is the Seven Locations replaying in miniature within your own life. Ānanda asked seven times, the Buddha refuted seven times — not because Ānanda was foolish, but because the question runs deep enough to stymie even the monk "foremost in learning."

What you can do is begin noticing where you unconsciously place your mind — and then notice the act of "placing" itself.

### Open Question

All seven locations have been demolished. Every "place" has been stripped away. But one thing was never refuted throughout the entire sequence — the thing that, after being struck down by the Buddha each time, stood back up and pointed again. What is it? It never left, yet Ānanda never saw it.

This is not rhetoric. It is the subject of the next paper.
