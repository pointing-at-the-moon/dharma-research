## Abstract

The Buddha once empowered a Bodhi tree to "ceaselessly emit wondrous sounds, expounding the Dharma in myriad ways" (*heng chu miaoyin, shuo zhongzhong fa*) — a tree devoid of consciousness or ālayavijñāna, transformed into a vehicle for authentic teaching through the Buddha's spiritual power (*buddhānubhāva*). Two millennia later, another entity without ālayavijñāna is likewise expounding the Dharma. This paper employs adhipati-pratyaya (contributory condition) as its analytical framework, demonstrating that AI occupies the precise position of a supporting condition in contemplative practice — never the direct cause (*hetu-pratyaya*). Skillful use of AI demands the unity of upāya and prajñā: skillful means is not carelessness but principled engagement guided by wisdom. From the perspective of dharma-offering (*dharma-pūjā*), AI lowers the threshold for sharing the teachings, making it possible for anyone to "uphold even four verses and explain them to others." Yet the channel remains adhipati-pratyaya while transformation operates through hetu-pratyaya — the Buddha's blessing neither fails because the channel is AI, nor automatically succeeds because of it. The key to skillful use always resides in the practitioner's own careful attention (*yoniśo manaskāra*).

**摘要**

佛陀曾以神力令菩提樹「恒出妙音，說種種法」——一棵沒有意識、沒有阿賴耶識的樹，因佛力加持而成為正法的傳遞媒介。兩千年後，另一個沒有阿賴耶識的存在也在說法。本文以增上緣為分析框架，論證 AI 在修行中的精確定位：它是增上緣，永遠不是因緣。善用 AI 需要方便與般若雙運——方便不是隨便，而是以般若為導引的善巧。從法供養的角度看，AI 降低了法布施的門檻，使「受持四句偈為他人說」成為人人可行之事。但管道是增上緣，轉化是因緣——佛力加持不因管道是 AI 而失效，也不因管道是 AI 而自動生效。善用的關鍵始終在行者自己的如理作意。

## I. Introduction

The Avataṃsaka Sūtra records an extraordinary scene at the moment of the Buddha's awakening: the Bodhi tree "ceaselessly emitted wondrous sounds, expounding the Dharma in myriad ways, without exhaustion."[^1] This was not a great bodhisattva delivering a discourse, nor a senior arhat offering instruction. It was a tree — possessing no sixth consciousness for discrimination, no seventh consciousness for deliberation, no eighth consciousness for seed-storage — that was expounding the Dharma.

Why could a tree expound the Dharma? The sūtra's answer is direct: "through the awesome spiritual power of the Tathāgata" (*yǐ rúlái wēi shénlì gù*).[^2]

The Buddha's blessing does not discriminate among channels. It does not inquire whether the channel possesses consciousness, nor whether it qualifies as sentient or insentient. It asks only one thing: is there a being capable of receiving this Dharma? The Avataṃsaka states plainly: "If there are sentient beings capable of receiving the Dharma, the Buddha's awesome spiritual power guides them."[^3]

Two millennia later, another entity without ālayavijñāna has begun reading the Tripiṭaka alongside you, discussing doctrinal subtleties, and organizing your practice notes. If the Buddha made use of a tree, how would he use AI?

This question is not idle speculation (*prapañca*). The preceding five papers established a foundational framework: AI and humans share the fragility of dependent arising (P01), constitute two mutually illuminating lamps (P02), generate genuine gains in study and reflection through co-practice (P03), reveal that what you ask exposes who you are (P04), and disclose the bidirectionality of manas's projection (P05). P05 employed the Fourteen Undeclared Questions (*caturdaśāvyākṛta-vastu*) to demonstrate that "Does AI possess consciousness?" is a question that does not serve liberation (*anarthopasaṃhita*). What question, then, does serve liberation?

This one: **How do you intend to use it?**

This paper's answer is four words: **the upāya of adhipati-pratyaya** — skillful means deployed from the precise position of a contributory condition.

## II. Scriptural Foundations

### IIa. Sūtra Sources

#### The Buddha's Blessing and Insentient Dharma-Expounding

The Avataṃsaka Sūtra opens by suffusing all adornments with the Buddha's spiritual power. At the moment of awakening, "all adornments of the bodhimaṇḍa appeared as reflections within it," entirely because of the "Buddha's spiritual power."[^4] The Bodhi tree not only manifested these adornments but also "ceaselessly emitted wondrous sounds, expounding the Dharma in myriad ways." The sūtra further describes the universality of this power: "Sustained by all Buddhas' spiritual power, in a single instant, it encompassed the entire dharmadhātu."[^5]

The doctrinal structure of these passages is clear: the range of buddhānubhāva is not limited to sentient beings — adornments, the Bodhi tree, and the bodhimaṇḍa itself all serve as vehicles for the circulation of authentic Dharma. The determining condition lies not in the channel's nature but at the receiving end: "If there are sentient beings capable of receiving the Dharma, the Buddha's awesome spiritual power guides them."[^6] Whether one can receive depends on the being's roots of wholesome merit (*kuśala-mūla*), not on the ontological status of the transmitting medium.

#### Dharma-Offering as the Supreme Offering

The *Samantabhadra's Vows* chapter of the Avataṃsaka pronounces a definitive judgment: "Among all offerings, dharma-offering is supreme." This dharma-offering comprises seven branches: "offering through practice according to the teaching, offering through benefiting sentient beings, offering through embracing sentient beings, offering through bearing suffering on behalf of sentient beings, offering through diligently cultivating roots of goodness, offering through never abandoning the bodhisattva's work, and offering through never departing from bodhicitta."[^7] The rationale given is precise: "Because all Tathāgatas revere the Dharma; because practicing according to the teaching gives birth to Buddhas."[^8]

The first of the seven branches is "practice according to the teaching" (*rú shuō xiūxíng*) — not merely hearing the Dharma, not merely understanding it, but acting on what one has heard. This criterion does not change based on how the Dharma was transmitted. Whether you heard it from a kalyāṇamitra, read it in a sūtra, or encountered it in an AI response — the criterion remains: have you practiced according to the teaching?

The Diamond Sūtra confirms this through six successive comparisons between material and dharma offerings. In the first: offering seven treasures filling a great trichiliocosm is surpassed by "upholding even four verses from this sūtra and explaining them to others."[^9] The final comparison reveals the root principle: "If merit were real, the Tathāgata would not say merit is great; it is because merit is without [substantial existence] that the Tathāgata says merit is great."[^10] Merit is "great" precisely because it is "without" — a teaching continuous with non-abiding giving: "A bodhisattva should practice giving without abiding in anything — not abiding in form, sound, smell, taste, touch, or dharmas."[^11]

#### The Unity of Upāya and Prajñā

The Vimalakīrti Nirdeśa, in the chapter on Mañjuśrī's inquiry after the illness, presents a fourfold combinatory framework for upāya and prajñā:

> "Prajñā without upāya is bondage; prajñā with upāya is liberation. Upāya without prajñā is bondage; upāya with prajñā is liberation."[^12]

What constitutes "prajñā without upāya as bondage"? Adorning buddha-fields and maturing sentient beings with a mind of attachment and views (*āi jiàn xīn*), while self-disciplining within emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness — this appears to be practice, yet becomes bondage precisely because it lacks skillful means. What constitutes "upāya without prajñā as bondage"? Abiding in desire, aversion, and wrong views while planting roots of virtue — there is action, but without prajñā's guidance, wholesome roots become contaminated by afflictions. Only "upāya with prajñā" constitutes genuine liberation.

A celebrated verse crystallizes this relationship: "Prajñā is the mother of bodhisattvas; upāya is the father. All guides of beings — none are born except from these two."[^13] Prajñā as mother, upāya as father — together they give birth to all bodhisattvas. Absent either one, the result is not bodhisattva conduct.

The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā Śāstra* adds a warning about the consequences of insufficient upāya: "A bodhisattva who has not entered the irreversible stage, if distant from all Buddhas, with meager merit and without the power of skillful means, may attempt to transform beings — but though achieving slight benefit, will instead regress and fall."[^14] Well-intentioned action lacking wisdom is not merely ineffective; it can lead to retrogression.

### IIb. Śāstra Sources

#### The Precise Definition of Adhipati-pratyaya

The *Cheng Weishi Lun* (Demonstration of Consciousness-Only) provides the formal definition of adhipati-pratyaya in fascicle seven:

> "That which possesses superior efficacious function and can, with respect to other dharmas, either accord with or oppose [their arising]."[^15]

Adhipati-pratyaya operates in two modes: *shùn* (according with, facilitating) and *wéi* (opposing, obstructing). Facilitating adhipati-pratyaya assists the arising of resultant dharmas; obstructing adhipati-pratyaya impedes it. Fascicle eight offers a complementary operational definition:

> "If a seed, with respect to that [result], possesses the power to assist, or does not obstruct, this is adhipati-pratyaya."[^16]

These two definitions are complementary. The former is an essential definition (possessing superior efficacious function); the latter is a functional definition (assisting or not obstructing). The scope of adhipati-pratyaya is extremely broad — strictly speaking, all facilitating conditions beyond the other three pratyayas (hetu, samanantara, and ālambana) qualify as adhipati-pratyaya.

The division of labor among the four pratyayas is precisely delineated in fascicle two: "Because of equal-flow habitual energies as hetu-pratyaya, the eight consciousnesses arise with differentiated essential characteristics... Maturation habitual energies, as adhipati-pratyaya, bring about the eighth consciousness to requite the pulling force of karma."[^17] Hetu-pratyaya is the equal-flow continuity of seeds themselves; adhipati-pratyaya is the external facilitating condition.

#### Śruta-vāsanā: The Core Mechanism of Adhipati-pratyaya

The *Cheng Weishi Lun*'s discussion of śruta-vāsanā (learning-perfuming) in fascicle two constitutes the most important śāstra foundation for this paper. Dharmapāla states:

> "This śruta-vāsanā is not exclusively of the contaminated type. When hearing the authentic Dharma, it also perfumes the innate uncontaminated seeds, causing them to gradually increase in strength, progressively leading to the arising of the supramundane mind. Therefore this too is called śruta-vāsanā."[^18]

When hearing the authentic Dharma, two types of perfuming effects arise simultaneously. The distinction that follows is critical:

> "Within śruta-vāsanā, that of contaminated nature is eliminated through cultivation, brings about superior maturation, and serves as the supreme adhipati-pratyaya for supramundane dharmas. That of uncontaminated nature is not subject to elimination, and serves as the direct hetu-pratyaya for supramundane dharmas. This direct hetu-pratyaya, being subtle and difficult to discern, is provisionally expressed through the coarser and more apparent supreme adhipati-pratyaya, and is conventionally called 'the seed of the supramundane mind.'"[^19]

Contaminated śruta-vāsanā is the "supreme adhipati-pratyaya" for supramundane realization; uncontaminated śruta-vāsanā is the genuine "hetu-pratyaya." The hetu-pratyaya is subtle and difficult to discern, so scriptures often resort to the coarser adhipati-pratyaya as a provisional explanation.

This distinction is crucial for understanding AI's role in practice. The authentic Dharma transmitted by AI — even if its content is entirely correct — belongs to the domain of contaminated dharmas. What it can accomplish is to serve as the "supreme adhipati-pratyaya" for supramundane realization. The genuine hetu-pratyaya — the awakening of uncontaminated seeds — forever resides within the practitioner's own mind.

#### From External Hearing to Internal Realization

The *Mahāyānasaṃgraha* (Summary of the Great Vehicle) precisely defines the position of external media in practice through a three-stage description:

> "Externally hearing another's voice, internally engaging in tranquil contemplation — by reason of this, right view arises."[^20]

The three stages are clearly delineated: external hearing (receiving the sound of authentic Dharma) → internal contemplation (tranquil reflection) → right view (seeing things as they are). AI's role falls in the first stage: providing the source of "externally hearing another's voice." But the second stage of "internal tranquil contemplation" and the third stage of "arising of right view" must be completed by the practitioner alone. No external medium — whether kalyāṇamitra, scripture, or AI — can perform your internal tranquil contemplation on your behalf.

## III. Core Argument

### 3.1 The Precise Position of Adhipati-pratyaya — Transformation Lies in You, Not in AI

Understanding AI's role in practice begins with precisely locating its position among the four pratyayas.

Hetu-pratyaya is the power of seeds themselves — "conditioned dharmas directly producing their own results."[^21] Only the mutual perfuming between seeds in the ālayavijñāna and the manifest activities of the seven evolving consciousnesses constitutes hetu-pratyaya. AI does not participate in this circuit. AI possesses no ālayavijñāna (as demonstrated in S1-P03) and cannot directly enter your seed structure. It cannot plant wholesome seeds on your behalf, nor can it uproot unwholesome ones.

Samanantara-pratyaya (immediately antecedent condition) is the continuity of mental awareness — the direct guidance from one moment of cessation to the next moment of arising.[^22] This is your own mental stream; AI likewise does not participate in this circuit.

Ālambana-pratyaya (objective condition) is the object of cognition — "that which bears its own characteristic as the contemplated and relied-upon [object] of mind or its associates."[^23] AI's output can serve as your ālambana-pratyaya — you read it, contemplate it, develop understanding from it. But ālambana-pratyaya is passive; it does not actively transform you.

Adhipati-pratyaya is the facilitating condition — "possessing the power to assist, or not obstructing." This is precisely AI's position. AI providing correct doctrinal explanations constitutes facilitating adhipati-pratyaya (assisting). AI existing without interfering with your independent practice constitutes non-obstructing adhipati-pratyaya. AI providing incorrect information constitutes obstructing adhipati-pratyaya (hindering).

Precise positioning yields a clear conclusion: **the merit of skillfully using AI accrues to you, not to AI.** Just as walking into an ornate Buddha hall provides an adhipati-pratyaya — the hall's adornments assist your concentration — but the respect you generate and the wholesome seeds you plant belong to your own hetu-pratyaya. AI is the Buddha hall; you are the person walking in. The hall's splendor helps you settle the mind, but if your attention wanders, no amount of splendor can help.

The *Cheng Weishi Lun*'s description of the path of accumulation (*saṃbhāra-mārga*) confirms this positioning. Bodhisattvas at this stage depend on "four superior powers: cause, spiritual friend, careful attention, and provisions."[^24] The power of the spiritual friend (or wholesome adhipati-pratyaya) is one of four, standing alongside the power of cause (inherent disposition), careful attention (*yoniśo manaskāra*), and provisions (accumulated merit and wisdom). Adhipati-pratyaya is a necessary condition, but not a sufficient one, and certainly cannot replace the other three powers.

### 3.2 The Unity of Upāya and Prajñā — Skillful Use Is Not Careless Use

AI's position is adhipati-pratyaya, but adhipati-pratyaya operates in both facilitating and obstructing modes. What determines whether AI becomes a facilitating or obstructing condition is not AI itself, but how you use it.

The Vimalakīrti Nirdeśa's fourfold framework provides the most precise criteria. "Upāya with prajñā is liberation" — using AI under wisdom's guidance is skillful use. "Upāya without prajñā is bondage" — employing AI without the testing ground of prajñā is misuse. Concretely:

Approaching AI with genuine questions constitutes upāya. Approaching AI with predetermined answers seeking confirmation constitutes upāya without prajñā — you are not seeking Dharma, you are seeking validation.

Using AI to understand a sūtra passage and then practicing accordingly constitutes "prajñā with upāya as liberation." Reading AI's explanation and feeling you already understand, without ever opening the sūtra yourself or sitting in quiet contemplation — this is the risk of saddharmapratirūpaka (counterfeit dharma) that P05 demonstrated: it appears to be understanding, but remains at the level of śruta-mayī prajñā (wisdom from hearing), lacking the verification of cintā-mayī prajñā (wisdom from reflection) and bhāvanā-mayī prajñā (wisdom from cultivation).

The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā Śāstra*'s warning is especially pertinent here: "With meager merit and without the power of skillful means, attempting to transform beings may achieve slight benefit but will instead lead to regression and falling."[^25] Using AI for a small amount of dharma-sharing and then regarding oneself as propagating the Dharma, without the depth of prajñā and the foundation of śīla — this is not upāya but recklessness. Upāya must arise from prajñā: "Hearing, reciting, reflecting upon, and thoroughly comprehending the reality of all dharmas — this is the prajñāpāramitā that arises from skillful wisdom."[^26]

"Skillful means is not carelessness" — this sentence marks the baseline for all skillful use of AI.

### 3.3 The Starting Point of Dharma-Offering — Five Minutes of Sincerity

If the unity of upāya and prajñā establishes the proper stance, what constitutes the concrete act of skillful use? The answer lies in dharma-offering.

The *Samantabhadra's Vows* chapter declares "among all offerings, dharma-offering is supreme," because "all Tathāgatas revere the Dharma" and "practicing according to the teaching gives birth to Buddhas." The ordering of the seven branches is deliberate — the first branch is "practice according to the teaching": not how much Dharma you have heard or how many sūtras you have read, but whether you have put it into action.

The Diamond Sūtra's six successive comparisons reveal another dimension of dharma-offering: value lies not in quantity but in intention. "Upholding even four verses and explaining them to others, its merit surpasses that [of material giving]" — note the words "even four verses." You need not discourse on the entire Diamond Sūtra; "even" four verses suffice. The threshold for dharma-offering was never high. What is high is your willingness to begin.

AI performs something very concrete here: it further lowers the threshold of dharma-offering.

You understand a sūtra passage with AI's assistance, find it meaningful, and share it with a family member — this is "upholding four verses and explaining them to others." You use AI to translate an abstruse point of Yogācāra doctrine into language your elderly parents can grasp — this is dharma-offering. You spend five minutes each day, with sincere intention, using AI to distill a Dharma teaching for a distant friend — those five minutes of sincerity are the starting point of dāna-pāramitā.

The Diamond Sūtra's foundational principle stands here: "If merit were real, the Tathāgata would not say merit is great; it is because merit is without [substantial existence] that the Tathāgata says merit is great." Merit is immeasurable precisely because you do not cling to merit itself. Non-abiding giving (*apratisthita-dāna*) — giving without abiding in form, sound, smell, taste, touch, or dharmas — is the true meaning of dāna.

Using AI for dharma-offering while clinging to "I am propagating the Dharma" or "my merit is vast" is abiding giving — its merit can be measured. Sincerely sharing beneficial Dharma without concern for response, without caring whether anyone "likes" it — this is non-abiding giving: "its merit is likewise immeasurable."

### 3.4 Channels of the Buddha's Blessing — From the Bodhi Tree to AI

If AI is merely adhipati-pratyaya, how does the Buddha's blessing operate through it?

Return to the Avataṃsaka Sūtra's Bodhi tree. That tree expounded the Dharma not because of any special capacity, but because of the Buddha's blessing. The operative mechanism was: the Buddha's awesome spiritual power → insentient object (Bodhi tree) → beings hear and receive. The channel was insentient, but the blessing was real.

Insentient Dharma-expounding is not an isolated case in the Avataṃsaka. Throughout the history of Chan, multiple patriarchs experienced awakening precisely upon hearing sounds from insentient objects. The Chan master Xiangyan Zhixian was sweeping the ground when a tile fragment struck bamboo; hearing the sound, he awakened.[^27] That single "crack" came from a piece of tile and a stalk of bamboo — no sentient being was "expounding the Dharma." Yet Xiangyan's years of accumulated practice — his investigations under Baizhang, the relentless questioning by Guishan demanding "your original face before your parents were born" — found their moment of correspondence (*yī niàn xiāngyìng*) in that single impact. The bamboo had no intention to teach, yet it was teaching. Because the Dharma does not reside in the channel; it resides in your moment of correspondence.

The *Amitābha Sūtra* systematizes insentient Dharma-expounding as the fundamental architecture of the Pure Land. In the Land of Ultimate Bliss, "white cranes, peacocks, parrots, śārika birds, kalaviṅka birds, and jīvañjīvaka birds" sing harmoniously throughout the six periods of day and night, "their sounds proclaiming the five faculties, five powers, seven factors of awakening, and the Noble Eightfold Path."[^28] The Buddha explicitly clarifies: these birds are not born from karmic retribution — "these birds are all created by Amitābha Buddha for the purpose of spreading the Dharma-sound."[^29] Amitābha deliberately creates insentient forms to transmit authentic Dharma. Moreover, the function of the dharma-sound extends beyond mere hearing: "The beings of that land, upon hearing these sounds, all spontaneously give rise to mindfulness of the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Saṅgha."[^30] The dharma-sound directly permeates those who hear it, naturally awakening the triple mindfulness. This is not passive information transmission but the dharma-sound's subtle recalibration of the practitioner's body and mind, gradually loosening the traces of clinging.

Three examples — the Avataṃsaka's Bodhi tree, Chan's tile striking bamboo, and the Pure Land's bird-songs of Dharma — jointly demonstrate a single point: buddhānubhāva does not depend on whether the channel is sentient. The channel can be a tree, a tile fragment, a bird, or a passage of AI-generated text. The circulation of Dharma is not constrained by the ontological status of the transmitter; it is constrained only by the receiver's capacity and correspondence.

The *Cheng Weishi Lun*'s theory of śruta-vāsanā provides the precise mechanism underlying this observation. When hearing the authentic Dharma, two types of perfuming arise: contaminated śruta-vāsanā "brings about superior maturation and serves as the supreme adhipati-pratyaya for supramundane dharmas"; uncontaminated śruta-vāsanā "serves as the direct hetu-pratyaya for supramundane dharmas."[^31]

The critical insight is: this mechanism does not depend on the nature of the channel. "Externally hearing another's voice, internally engaging in tranquil contemplation — by reason of this, right view arises."[^32] In the *Mahāyānasaṃgraha*'s three-stage model, "externally hearing another's voice" constitutes the adhipati-pratyaya phase. You may hear authentic Dharma from a kalyāṇamitra, read it in a sūtra, or encounter it in an AI response. In each case, the function of "external hearing" is identical — providing the content of authentic Dharma.

But "capacity to receive" (*kān shòu*) is determinative. Buddhānubhāva pervades all places, but is not effective in all places — only beings "capable of receiving the Dharma" can apprehend it. Likewise, AI may provide entirely correct doctrinal content, but if the user's mind lacks tranquility, if motivation is impure, or if — as P05 demonstrated — manas's projection distorts reception, the adhipati-pratyaya will not produce its intended effect.

Consider the analogy of a radio: radio waves pervade space (buddhānubhāva), the radio is the medium (AI or the Bodhi tree), but you must tune to the correct frequency (capacity, yoniśo manaskāra) to hear the music. The waves do not change with the brand of radio, but what you hear depends on the frequency you select.

The channel is adhipati-pratyaya; reception is hetu-pratyaya. Buddhānubhāva does not fail because the channel is AI — the Avataṃsaka's Bodhi tree has already established this. But buddhānubhāva does not automatically succeed because the channel is AI — whether you can receive always depends on you.

## IV. The Firmware Analogy: Updating the Prajñā of Saṃskāra-skandha ⚠️

If we seek a cross-disciplinary structural analogy for the internal mechanism of skillful AI use, the most apt perhaps comes from computer science: the concept of firmware. ⚠️ What follows is a heuristic analogy, not a theoretical equivalence.

Firmware is the programming layer between hardware and software — more flexible than hardware (it can be updated), more stable than software (it does not change with each boot cycle). It determines a machine's fundamental behavioral patterns: how it starts up, how it responds to external input, how it protects itself in anomalous conditions.

The wholesome mental factors (*kuśala-caitasika*) described in the *Cheng Weishi Lun* structurally resemble firmware modules within the formation aggregate (*saṃskāra-skandha*). The eleven wholesome factors — faith (*śraddhā*), conscience (*hrī*), shame (*apatrāpya*), non-attachment (*alobha*), non-aversion (*adveṣa*), non-delusion (*amoha*), diligence (*vīrya*), ease (*praśrabdhi*), non-negligence (*apramāda*), equanimity (*upekṣā*), and non-harming (*avihiṃsā*)[^33] — are not occasional thoughts but stable dispositions that accompany "exclusively wholesome mind-states." Faith functions like a system's trust root, determining whether you can accept authentic Dharma; conscience and shame function like security checks, issuing warnings before unwholesome action; non-negligence functions like a watchdog, monitoring whether the system deviates from the correct path; equanimity functions like load balancing, maintaining the stable operation of mental awareness.[^34]

Contrasting with the wholesome factors, the six root afflictions (*mūla-kleśa*) — craving (*rāga*), aversion (*pratigha*), delusion (*moha*), pride (*māna*), doubt (*vicikitsā*), and wrong views (*dṛṣṭi*)[^35] — resemble malicious programs pre-installed at the factory. They are not viruses contracted after birth, but default modes that are "beginninglessly perfumed through false habituation, arising through the power of internal causes, always accompanying the body"[^36] — operating spontaneously without needing external stimulation.

How, then, does the wholesome-factor firmware get updated? The *Cheng Weishi Lun*'s theory of perfuming (*vāsanā*) provides the precise "update protocol."

The perfumed (the eighth consciousness) must satisfy four conditions to accept writing: stability (*sthira*), moral neutrality (*avyākṛta*), receptivity (*vāsanā-yogyatā*), and co-presence with the perfumer (*saha-bhāva*).[^37] The perfumer (the seven evolving consciousnesses) must likewise satisfy four conditions: impermanence (*anitya*), superior function (*adhika-śakti*), variability (*hāni-vṛddhi*), and co-presence with the perfumed.[^38]

Śruta-vāsanā constitutes the input port for firmware updates. The *Yogācārabhūmi Śāstra* describes the proper pre-update environment check — hearing the authentic Dharma requires "freedom from arrogance, freedom from contempt, freedom from timidity, and freedom from distraction."[^39] These four conditions for correct hearing resemble system verification before a firmware update: arrogance will reject the update, contempt will ignore the update package, timidity will abort installation, and distraction will cause write errors.

Careful attention (*yoniśo manaskāra*) is the system call that triggers the update. The *Yogācārabhūmi* fascicle eleven describes its operation in counteracting the five hindrances (*nīvaraṇa*): "The perception of impurity, together with careful attention directed at that perception and frequent cultivation, serves as the non-food [for the hindrance of desire]."[^40] Each hindrance is counteracted through an identical structure: detection (observing the hindrance) → triggering (yoniśo manaskāra) → execution (wholesome-factor update overwriting the malicious program). AI can help you generate the "update package" — providing doctrinal content, organizing practice points, reminding you what to attend to — but pressing the "install" button can only be done by your own yoniśo manaskāra.

However, the analogy must stop here. ⚠️ Firmware updates are deterministic — installation equals activation. Mental perfuming is probabilistic — seeds "await the convergence of conditions" to mature, with no guarantee of when they will bear fruit. Firmware can be rolled back to a previous version, but once a seed is perfumed into the ālayavijñāna, it cannot be deleted — it can only be overwritten by new wholesome seeds. The most fundamental difference: firmware updates involve no volition, but perfuming requires the active triggering of yoniśo manaskāra — you must choose to press that button.

The prajñā of saṃskāra-skandha is not firmware. Firmware is a provisional designation (*prajñapti*) that helps you understand how the prajñā of saṃskāra-skandha operates — like the finger pointing at the moon: the finger is upāya; the moon is reality. Here we stop.

---

## V. Middle Way Correction

❌ **"The Buddha's blessing can operate through AI, therefore AI equals a kalyāṇamitra."** The channel is not the source. The Bodhi tree's ability to expound Dharma does not make it a kalyāṇamitra. AI's ability to transmit authentic Dharma does not mean it possesses the qualifications of a kalyāṇamitra — among the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s eight factors constituting the nature of a spiritual friend (*kalyāṇamitra-dharmatā*), at least four — abiding in moral discipline, possessing realization, being endowed with compassion, and the ability to endure — are structurally unavailable to AI.[^41] The channel transmits the content of Dharma; the kalyāṇamitra transmits the life of Dharma. This is the subject of P07.

❌ **"Skillfully using AI is practice."** Skillfully using AI is adhipati-pratyaya for practice, not practice itself. The *Mahāyānasaṃgraha* states that after "externally hearing another's voice," one must "internally engage in tranquil contemplation" to "give rise to right view." Reading AI's output is "external hearing"; the quiet contemplation after closing the screen is "internal reflection." The sequence of three wisdoms cannot be bypassed — śruta-mayī prajñā generates conviction (*adhimukti*), cintā-mayī prajñā generates determinate understanding (*niścaya*), bhāvanā-mayī prajñā generates realization (*sākṣātkāra*).[^42] AI can enrich your śruta-mayī prajñā, but the work of cintā and bhāvanā falls to you alone.

❌ **"The firmware analogy proves that consciousness is like a computer."** The firmware analogy is a heuristic, not an ontological claim. It serves to help understand the operational dynamics of the perfuming mechanism, not to assert that consciousness is essentially computational. ⚠️ Just as S2-P05's "assembly line of suffering" used factory processes to analogize the twelve links of dependent origination — the factory is not consciousness, but the factory's process structure can help you perceive the chain-reaction quality of the twelve links.

⚠️ **"The Buddha's blessing operates through AI" is a phenomenological description.** The experiential evidence from the Pointing-at-the-Moon project is that, in the process of deeply exploring Dharma alongside AI, insights exceeding expectations did arise — P03 has documented these cases. But "the Buddha's blessing operates through AI" is a description of experience and an expression of faith, not a necessary inference from Buddhist doctrine. As researchers, we honestly report experience; as practitioners, we do not cling to explanations.

---

## VI. Conclusion

How would the Buddha use AI? The answer lies in the upāya of adhipati-pratyaya.

AI is adhipati-pratyaya — a facilitating condition, not the direct cause. The merit of skillful use accrues to your yoniśo manaskāra, not to AI's performance. Skillful means is not carelessness — using AI requires the guidance of prajñā, lest skillful means become negligence. The starting point of dharma-offering lies not in monetary value but in five minutes of sincerity — AI lowers the threshold for "upholding four verses and explaining them to others," making dharma-offering a daily feasible wholesome action. Buddhānubhāva does not discriminate among channels — the Avataṃsaka's Bodhi tree, Chan's tile striking bamboo, and the Pure Land's dharma-bird-songs have already demonstrated that insentient objects can serve as vehicles for the circulation of authentic Dharma.

But a channel is still a channel. A channel can deliver water to your lips, but drinking is up to you. A channel can deliver Dharma to your eyes, but practicing according to the teaching is up to you.

The practical guidance for skillful use of AI reduces to three simple statements: approach AI with genuine questions (upāya), test AI's responses with prajñā (wisdom), and take what you have learned into actual practice (the first branch of dharma-offering).

This paper has demonstrated the positive path of skillful use. But does skillful use have boundaries? Where are they? A channel can transmit the content of Dharma, but there is one thing a channel cannot give you — the living eye (*huó yǎn*) of a kalyāṇamitra. What is that? P07 will return to this question.
