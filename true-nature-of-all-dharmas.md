# The True Nature of All Dharmas — The Dharma Can Be Personally Verified

## What Is "The True Nature of All Dharmas"?

The phrase *zhūfǎ shíxiàng* (諸法實相, "the true nature of all dharmas") appears in the *Lotus Sūtra*, "Chapter on Skillful Means": "Only a buddha together with a buddha can fully fathom the true nature of all dharmas." Most interpretations approach it from a metaphysical angle—emptiness, suchness, dharma-nature—treating it as an abstract philosophical proposition. Yet the most fundamental meaning of *shíxiàng* (實相, "true nature") is, in fact, the most direct reading: **true and not false**.

What the Buddha realized is true. What the Buddha taught is true. That sentient beings can attain buddhahood by practicing in accordance with the Dharma is also true. These three layers of "true" taken together constitute the complete meaning of "the true nature of all dharmas."

## The Emptiness-Aspect Is the True Nature

The *Lotus Sūtra* speaks of "the true nature of all dharmas" (諸法實相); the *Heart Sūtra* speaks of "the emptiness-aspect of all dharmas" (諸法空相). The two appear different but point to the same thing—**the true face of all dharmas is emptiness**.

The *Heart Sūtra*'s progression is exceptionally clear:

> Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva, while practicing the profound prajñāpāramitā, illuminated and saw that all five aggregates are empty, and thereby transcended all suffering. Śāriputra, this is the emptiness-aspect of all dharmas.

Avalokiteśvara did not arrive at "all is empty" by sitting and reasoning. He deeply practiced prajñā wisdom (practiced the profound prajñāpāramitā), and then directly *illuminated and saw* that the true state of the five aggregates is emptiness. What was the result of this seeing? The transcendence of all suffering.

Cause and effect are clear. True and not false.

## The Verifiability of the Dharma

Here lies a layer of meaning that is critically important yet often overlooked: **the Dharma possesses the most essential quality of the scientific spirit—repeatable verifiability.**

The basic spirit of science is not that a genius proposes an elegant theory and that settles the matter, but that others, following the same method, can obtain the same result. The Dharma possesses precisely this quality. The Buddha did not say "just believe me." He said: practice it yourself, verify it yourself.

The facts of two and a half millennia are right there. From Śāriputra and Mahāmaudgalyāyana, to Nāgārjuna, Asaṅga, and Vasubandhu; from Huineng, Linji, and Zhaozhou, to the modern masters Xuyun, Yinguang, and Taixu—in every era, across every cultural context, people have practiced in accordance with what the Buddha taught and have indeed experienced growth in wisdom and reduction in afflictions.

This is not one person's subjective experience; it is a result verified repeatedly by countless practitioners over twenty-five hundred years.

From the first dhyāna to the fourth dhyāna, nourished by the joy of meditation; gain one measure of wisdom, shed one measure of affliction. These are not abstract metaphysics—they are changes that practitioners can actually experience in their own body and mind. Just as scientific experiments have measurable indicators, the practice of the Dharma has clear stages and criteria for verification.

## The True Nature Belongs to No One

The true nature of all dharmas, the emptiness-aspect of all dharmas—these belong to no one. The Buddha did not "invent" anything.

In the *Diamond Sūtra*, the Buddha repeatedly emphasizes this point: "If anyone says the Tathāgata has spoken any dharma, that person slanders the Buddha." Why? Because the true nature of all dharmas was already so before the Buddha appeared in the world, and it remains so after. The Buddha simply pointed it out as it is.

The Buddha's attitude throughout his lifetime of teaching can be summarized in six characters: **speak as it is; speak in accordance with the Dharma.**

"As it is"—according to the way things actually are. "In accordance with the Dharma"—according to the way the Dharma itself is. Nothing added, nothing subtracted, nothing of "I" mixed in.

The moment someone feels "this is my dharma," "my system," "my insight"—the instant that "I" enters, one has already departed from the true nature. Because one of the fundamental aspects of the true nature is **non-self**. The Dharma itself is without self; the one who speaks the Dharma is also without self.

## The Distribution Section for Our Time

The structure of a Buddhist sūtra ends with a section called the "distribution section" (*liútōng fēn*, 流通分)—the Dharma is not finished once it is spoken; it must be circulated to benefit more sentient beings.

The Buddha gave away everything he realized without reservation. He set no "intellectual property rights," no barriers to entry. Forty-nine years of teaching were for one thing: enabling all sentient beings to open to, be shown, awaken to, and enter the same insight.

Today, following in the Buddha's footsteps, we make Buddhist research and scholarship openly available on the internet, freely accessible to anyone, at any time, anywhere. This is not "sharing our work"—because the Dharma never belonged to anyone. We are simply doing the same thing the Buddha did: presenting what has always been there, what has always belonged to all sentient beings—presenting the truth, as it is, in accordance with the Dharma.

Citations are open and transparent. Arguments are fully laid out. All examination and questioning are welcome. Because the true nature withstands scrutiny from every angle.

**The Dharma does not ask you to believe blindly. The Dharma invites you to come and verify it for yourself.**

*The true nature of all dharmas—it has always been thus.*
